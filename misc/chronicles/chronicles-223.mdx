## On the water

The engine coughed twice and died outside Morholm, and Otto Drummond fixed it with a strip of blue slate and unrepeatable language. Halfway to Ulmvale, the barge took on water near the reed cut, and they spent the night beached on a mudbank counting stars. The morning began badly: the lock keeper at Morholm refused passage until the crew produced papers nobody remembered packing. At dusk the crew traded stories with the bargefolk of Morholm, and Otto Drummond lost a wager involving 2 tins of fermented tea bricks.

They were 46 miles from Ulmvale and further than ever, by any measure that counted, from where they had started. It was becoming clear the cargo mattered less than the route itself, and that someone wanted the route forgotten. The old maps had been wrong before, but never this confidently, and that worried Otto Drummond more than the weather did.

## What the log records

They took on a passenger at Morholm: Emeric Brandt, who claimed to be a surveyor and carried no instruments of any kind. an early frost caught them in the open stretch past Morholm, and the cargo of fermented tea bricks shifted so badly the hull groaned for hours. In Morholm they found the warehouse of Emeric Brandt locked, the windows papered over, and a notice from 1893 nailed to the door.

They were 59 miles from Ulmvale and further than ever, by any measure that counted, from where they had started. Later, writing the log by lamplight, Otto Drummond admitted the route was changing them in ways the ledger could not record. The old maps had been wrong before, but never this confidently, and that worried Otto Drummond more than the weather did.

## Nightfall

Under the floor plank where the spare rope lived, there was now a small box of woven reed, and none of them had put it there. The last lamp on the quay went out as they passed it, all at once, as if pinched.
