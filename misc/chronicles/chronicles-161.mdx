## On the water

A letter was waiting at the Ashrun post house, addressed in handwriting that made Maren Greaves go very quiet. The morning began badly: the lock keeper at Ashrun refused passage until the crew produced papers nobody remembered packing. Maren Greaves bought a crate of copper wire from a one-eyed trader in Ashrun, and by evening everyone aboard regretted it. Halfway to Tamstead, the barge took on water near the reed cut, and they spent the night beached on a mudbank counting stars.

It was becoming clear the cargo mattered less than the route itself, and that someone wanted the route forgotten. Nobody spoke of it the next day, which on this barge was how everyone agreed something had mattered.

## What the log records

The toll bridge at Tamstead was out, so they detoured through the old channel, where the water smelled of iron and the birds went silent. In Ashrun they found the warehouse of Yutta Ashdown locked, the windows papered over, and a notice from 1909 nailed to the door. They took on a passenger at Ashrun: Yutta Ashdown, who claimed to be a surveyor and carried no instruments of any kind.

They were 56 miles from Tamstead and further than ever, by any measure that counted, from where they had started. The old maps had been wrong before, but never this confidently, and that worried Maren Greaves more than the weather did.

## Nightfall

The last lamp on the quay went out as they passed it, all at once, as if pinched. It was only when they cast off that Yutta Ashdown noticed the second set of footprints on the cabin roof.
