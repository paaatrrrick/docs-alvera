## On the water

At dusk the crew traded stories with the bargefolk of Nargate, and Imre Whitlock lost a wager involving 5 tins of clockwork gears. The toll bridge at Kelford was out, so they detoured through the old channel, where the water smelled of iron and the birds went silent. They took on a passenger at Nargate: Edda Falkner, who claimed to be a surveyor and carried no instruments of any kind. The morning began badly: the lock keeper at Nargate refused passage until the crew produced papers nobody remembered packing. a still and windless month caught them in the open stretch past Nargate, and the cargo of spice tins shifted so badly the hull groaned for hours.

It was becoming clear the cargo mattered less than the route itself, and that someone wanted the route forgotten. Nobody spoke of it the next day, which on this barge was how everyone agreed something had mattered. The old maps had been wrong before, but never this confidently, and that worried Imre Whitlock more than the weather did.

## What the log records

The engine coughed twice and died outside Nargate, and Imre Whitlock fixed it with a strip of river-glass and unrepeatable language. The morning began badly: the lock keeper at Nargate refused passage until the crew produced papers nobody remembered packing. In Nargate they found the warehouse of Edda Falkner locked, the windows papered over, and a notice from 1795 nailed to the door. Halfway to Kelford, the barge took on water near the reed cut, and they spent the night beached on a mudbank counting stars.

Nobody spoke of it the next day, which on this barge was how everyone agreed something had mattered. Later, writing the log by lamplight, Imre Whitlock admitted the route was changing them in ways the ledger could not record.

## Nightfall

On the lock gate, in fresh chalk, someone had drawn the same spiral from the Nargate customs house. Under the floor plank where the spare rope lived, there was now a small box of hammered tin, and none of them had put it there.
