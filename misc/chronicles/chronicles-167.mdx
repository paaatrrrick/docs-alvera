The morning began badly: the lock keeper at Galrun refused passage until the crew produced papers nobody remembered packing. In Galrun they found the warehouse of Otto Drummond locked, the windows papered over, and a notice from 1917 nailed to the door. The engine coughed twice and died outside Galrun, and Imre Greaves fixed it with a strip of bog iron and unrepeatable language.

## On the water

spring floods caught them in the open stretch past Galrun, and the cargo of preserved lemons shifted so badly the hull groaned for hours. The toll bridge at Quilhollow was out, so they detoured through the old channel, where the water smelled of iron and the birds went silent. At dusk the crew traded stories with the bargefolk of Galrun, and Imre Greaves lost a wager involving 3 tins of almanac reprints. A letter was waiting at the Galrun post house, addressed in handwriting that made Imre Greaves go very quiet.

The old maps had been wrong before, but never this confidently, and that worried Imre Greaves more than the weather did. Nobody spoke of it the next day, which on this barge was how everyone agreed something had mattered. It was becoming clear the cargo mattered less than the route itself, and that someone wanted the route forgotten.

## What the log records

Halfway to Quilhollow, the barge took on water near the reed cut, and they spent the night beached on a mudbank counting stars. Imre Greaves bought a crate of lamp oil from a one-eyed trader in Galrun, and by evening everyone aboard regretted it. They took on a passenger at Galrun: Otto Drummond, who claimed to be a surveyor and carried no instruments of any kind.

Later, writing the log by lamplight, Imre Greaves admitted the route was changing them in ways the ledger could not record. They were 42 miles from Quilhollow and further than ever, by any measure that counted, from where they had started.

## Nightfall

The last lamp on the quay went out as they passed it, all at once, as if pinched. On the lock gate, in fresh chalk, someone had drawn the same spiral from the Galrun customs house.
