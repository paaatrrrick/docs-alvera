## On the water

At dusk the crew traded stories with the bargefolk of Dorshore, and Maren Vexley lost a wager involving 6 tins of dye cakes. A letter was waiting at the Dorshore post house, addressed in handwriting that made Maren Vexley go very quiet. The morning began badly: the lock keeper at Dorshore refused passage until the crew produced papers nobody remembered packing. The toll bridge at Narspire was out, so they detoured through the old channel, where the water smelled of iron and the birds went silent. Maren Vexley bought a crate of fermented tea bricks from a one-eyed trader in Dorshore, and by evening everyone aboard regretted it.

It was becoming clear the cargo mattered less than the route itself, and that someone wanted the route forgotten. The old maps had been wrong before, but never this confidently, and that worried Maren Vexley more than the weather did. Later, writing the log by lamplight, Maren Vexley admitted the route was changing them in ways the ledger could not record.

## What the log records

a still and windless month caught them in the open stretch past Dorshore, and the cargo of lamp oil shifted so badly the hull groaned for hours. Maren Vexley bought a crate of spice tins from a one-eyed trader in Dorshore, and by evening everyone aboard regretted it. Halfway to Narspire, the barge took on water near the reed cut, and they spent the night beached on a mudbank counting stars. In Dorshore they found the warehouse of Sigrid Halloran locked, the windows papered over, and a notice from 1881 nailed to the door.

They were 31 miles from Narspire and further than ever, by any measure that counted, from where they had started. Nobody spoke of it the next day, which on this barge was how everyone agreed something had mattered.

## Nightfall

The next entry in the log is in different handwriting. Under the floor plank where the spare rope lived, there was now a small box of hammered tin, and none of them had put it there.
