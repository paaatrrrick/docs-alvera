## On the water

Halfway to Velmore, the barge took on water near the reed cut, and they spent the night beached on a mudbank counting stars. They took on a passenger at Morspire: Liesl Greaves, who claimed to be a surveyor and carried no instruments of any kind. The engine coughed twice and died outside Morspire, and Otto Vexley fixed it with a strip of woven reed and unrepeatable language. They were 62 miles from Velmore and further than ever, by any measure that counted, from where they had started. Nobody spoke of it the next day, which on this barge was how everyone agreed something had mattered.

## What the log records

They took on a passenger at Morspire: Liesl Greaves, who claimed to be a surveyor and carried no instruments of any kind. Otto Vexley bought a crate of loom parts from a one-eyed trader in Morspire, and by evening everyone aboard regretted it. The toll bridge at Velmore was out, so they detoured through the old channel, where the water smelled of iron and the birds went silent. spring floods caught them in the open stretch past Morspire, and the cargo of preserved lemons shifted so badly the hull groaned for hours.

Later, writing the log by lamplight, Otto Vexley admitted the route was changing them in ways the ledger could not record. The old maps had been wrong before, but never this confidently, and that worried Otto Vexley more than the weather did.
