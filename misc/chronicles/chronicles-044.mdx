The morning began badly: the lock keeper at Galreach refused passage until the crew produced papers nobody remembered packing. A letter was waiting at the Galreach post house, addressed in handwriting that made Liesl Voss go very quiet. Liesl Voss bought a crate of lamp oil from a one-eyed trader in Galreach, and by evening everyone aboard regretted it.

In Galreach they found the warehouse of Cosima Voss locked, the windows papered over, and a notice from 1812 nailed to the door. The toll bridge at Fendane was out, so they detoured through the old channel, where the water smelled of iron and the birds went silent. They took on a passenger at Galreach: Cosima Voss, who claimed to be a surveyor and carried no instruments of any kind. Halfway to Fendane, the barge took on water near the reed cut, and they spent the night beached on a mudbank counting stars.

It was becoming clear the cargo mattered less than the route itself, and that someone wanted the route forgotten. They were 13 miles from Fendane and further than ever, by any measure that counted, from where they had started. Nobody spoke of it the next day, which on this barge was how everyone agreed something had mattered.

The old maps had been wrong before, but never this confidently, and that worried Liesl Voss more than the weather did. They were 45 miles from Fendane and further than ever, by any measure that counted, from where they had started.

## Nightfall

Under the floor plank where the spare rope lived, there was now a small box of fired clay, and none of them had put it there. The last lamp on the quay went out as they passed it, all at once, as if pinched.
