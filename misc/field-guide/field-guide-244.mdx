## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into fired clay pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Ilymire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 47 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ilymire and Ashbury, especially after spring floods.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Ashbury shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1893 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 58 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Ivo Mollering. The wardens at Ilymire maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
