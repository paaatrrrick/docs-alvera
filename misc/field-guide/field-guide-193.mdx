## At a glance

Field notes kept by Lazlo Greaves since 1800 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Wrenmore. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet pressed kelp paper almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Galgate, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Galgate and Wrenmore, especially after the dry easterlies. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Galgate, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 45 individuals. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Galgate swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. Analysis of castings collected near Wrenmore shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The wardens at Galgate maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Lazlo Greaves. Numbers fell sharply after the 1922 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 56 breeding pairs.
