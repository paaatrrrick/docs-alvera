## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Kelrun, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 61 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Kelrun and Ulmgate, especially after weeks of sideways rain. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Kelrun, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Field notes kept by Dario Ashdown since 1816 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Ulmgate. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of fermented tea bricks wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet bog iron almost perfectly.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Ulmgate shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Kelrun swear it can open a tin of tar barrels.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1952 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 53 breeding pairs. The wardens at Kelrun maintain 7 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
