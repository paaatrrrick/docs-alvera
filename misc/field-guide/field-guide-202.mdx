## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Calspire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Calspire, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Calspire and Torcrest, especially after a long fog season. It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 32 individuals.

## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of copper wire wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Torcrest shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Calspire swear it can open a tin of lamp oil.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Cosima Oduya. Numbers fell sharply after the 1818 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 77 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Calspire maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
