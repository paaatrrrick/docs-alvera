## At a glance

It favors the brackish margins around Erimore, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Erimore, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 35 individuals.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Beatrix Whitlock since 1785 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Velhollow. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of spice tins wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Erimore swear it can open a tin of tar barrels. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Velhollow shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1799 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 82 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Beatrix Whitlock. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
