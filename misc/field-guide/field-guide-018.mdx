## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Halrun and Delcrest, especially after an early frost. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 85 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Halrun, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into woven reed pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Delcrest shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Halrun swear it can open a tin of fermented tea bricks. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Halrun maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1895 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 79 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Otto Marchetti.
