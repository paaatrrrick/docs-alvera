## At a glance

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Erimore, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Erimore, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 19 individuals.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Tilda Sorvane since 1947 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Nargate. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of glass floats wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet hammered tin almost perfectly.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Erimore swear it can open a tin of tar barrels. Analysis of castings collected near Nargate shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1900 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 62 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Tilda Sorvane. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
