## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Velton and Mardane, especially after spring floods. It favors the brackish margins around Velton, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 87 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into lacquered birch pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Velton, emerging only on still nights.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Mardane shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Velton swear it can open a tin of lamp oil.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Dario Marchetti. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Velton maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1899 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 42 breeding pairs.
