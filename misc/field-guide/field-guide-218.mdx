## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 71 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Torvik and Ossvik, especially after the autumn king tides. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Torvik, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Torvik, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Ossvik shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Torvik swear it can open a tin of lamp oil.

## Conservation

The wardens at Torvik maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1790 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 71 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
