It favors the brackish margins around Fenfell, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 48 individuals.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of copper wire wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Selford shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The wardens at Fenfell maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Yutta Whitlock. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
