## At a glance

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet hammered tin almost perfectly.

## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Rosgate, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 16 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Rosgate and Lorhollow, especially after a long fog season. It favors the brackish margins around Rosgate, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Lorhollow shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Rosgate swear it can open a tin of tar barrels.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Rosgate maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1837 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 68 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Liesl Oduya.
