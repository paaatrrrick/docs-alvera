## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Yarholm, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Yarholm and Delrun, especially after weeks of sideways rain. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 52 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Yarholm swear it can open a tin of clockwork gears. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1795 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 67 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Otto Sorvane. The wardens at Yarholm maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
