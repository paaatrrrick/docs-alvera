## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 72 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Morreach and Quilshore, especially after spring floods. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Morreach, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Morreach, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet whale-bone veneer almost perfectly. Field notes kept by Dario Halloran since 1873 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Quilshore. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Morreach swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. Analysis of castings collected near Quilshore shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1827 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 52 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Dario Halloran. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
