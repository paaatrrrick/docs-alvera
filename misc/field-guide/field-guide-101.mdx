## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Halrun and Jorhollow, especially after a still and windless month. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 40 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Halrun, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into salt-cured oak pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Halrun, emerging only on still nights.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Halrun swear it can open a tin of dye cakes. Analysis of castings collected near Jorhollow shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1924 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 27 breeding pairs. The wardens at Halrun maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
