## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into fired clay pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 88 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Velhollow, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Velhollow, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of wool bales wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet wax-sealed canvas almost perfectly. Field notes kept by Bram Severin since 1846 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Pervale. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Pervale shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Velhollow swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1964 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 71 breeding pairs. The wardens at Velhollow maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
