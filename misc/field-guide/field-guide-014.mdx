## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 40 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Galhollow, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Galhollow, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Galhollow and Halrun, especially after weeks of sideways rain.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Halrun shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1838 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 31 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Sigrid Kessler. The wardens at Galhollow maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
