Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Torshore and Ericrest, especially after spring floods. ## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Torshore, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into hammered tin pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 13 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Torshore, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Sanna Marrow since 1907 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Ericrest. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of wool bales wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet woven reed almost perfectly.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Ericrest shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Torshore maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Sanna Marrow.
