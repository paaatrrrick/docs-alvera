## At a glance

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet wax-sealed canvas almost perfectly. It favors the brackish margins around Brenmara, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Brenmara and Ulmmire, especially after a still and windless month.

## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 87 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Brenmara, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Brenmara, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of almanac reprints wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Field notes kept by Joren Halloran since 1789 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Ulmmire. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Ulmmire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1863 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 20 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Joren Halloran. The wardens at Brenmara maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
