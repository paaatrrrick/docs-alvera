## At a glance

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of loom parts wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet hammered tin almost perfectly. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ashton and Galstead, especially after the dry easterlies. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.

## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ashton, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Ashton, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 44 individuals. ## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Field notes kept by Cosima Holloway since 1880 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Galstead. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ashton swear it can open a tin of copper wire. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Galstead shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and weeks of sideways rain, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1932 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 39 breeding pairs. The wardens at Ashton maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
