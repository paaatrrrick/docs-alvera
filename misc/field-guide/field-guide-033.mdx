## At a glance

It favors the brackish margins around Galhollow, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet wax-sealed canvas almost perfectly.

## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 38 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Galhollow and Rosrun, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Galhollow, emerging only on still nights. ## Behavior

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Rosrun shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Galhollow swear it can open a tin of tar barrels. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1931 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 65 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Annik Petrov.
