## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Selreach, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 27 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into bog iron pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Selreach, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Selreach and Jorvale, especially after weeks of sideways rain.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Jorvale shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The wardens at Selreach maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Anselm Sorvane.
