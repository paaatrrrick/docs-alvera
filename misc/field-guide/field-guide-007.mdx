## At a glance

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of glass floats wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation.

## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 39 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Narmore, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Narmore and Marcrest, especially after the autumn king tides. It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet hammered tin almost perfectly. Field notes kept by Tilda Brandt since 1817 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Marcrest. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Narmore swear it can open a tin of glass floats. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Marcrest shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1848 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 38 breeding pairs. The wardens at Narmore maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Tilda Brandt.
