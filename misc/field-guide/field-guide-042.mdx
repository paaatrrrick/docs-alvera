## At a glance

It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of glass floats wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 73 individuals.

## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Wrenspire, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 89 individuals. ## Behavior

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Wrenspire swear it can open a tin of tar barrels. Analysis of castings collected near Deldane shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1918 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 27 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Edda Greaves. The wardens at Wrenspire maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
