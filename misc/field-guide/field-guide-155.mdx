## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Ilyholm, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 55 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ilyholm, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of glass floats wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Field notes kept by Vesna Drummond since 1868 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Fenvik. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ilyholm swear it can open a tin of spice tins. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Fenvik shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Vesna Drummond. The wardens at Ilyholm maintain 3 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1839 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 12 breeding pairs.
