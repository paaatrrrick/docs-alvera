## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ashgate, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Ashgate, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into woven reed pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ashgate and Jorfell, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ashgate swear it can open a tin of fermented tea bricks. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Jorfell shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and weeks of sideways rain, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Bram Petrov. The wardens at Ashgate maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1818 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 40 breeding pairs.
