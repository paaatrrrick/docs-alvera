## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Narford, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Narford and Tamwick, especially after the autumn king tides. It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Narford, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Narford swear it can open a tin of clockwork gears. Analysis of castings collected near Tamwick shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Greta Brandt. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1879 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 38 breeding pairs.
