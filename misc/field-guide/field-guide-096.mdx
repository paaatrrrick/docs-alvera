## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Fenwick and Tammore, especially after the dry easterlies. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 82 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Fenwick, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Fenwick swear it can open a tin of clockwork gears. Analysis of castings collected near Tammore shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The wardens at Fenwick maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Petra Kessler.
