## At a glance

It favors the brackish margins around Ilymire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 56 individuals.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet pressed kelp paper almost perfectly. Field notes kept by Ivo Sorvane since 1964 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Perspire. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ilymire swear it can open a tin of glass floats. Analysis of castings collected near Perspire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1865 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 70 breeding pairs. The wardens at Ilymire maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
