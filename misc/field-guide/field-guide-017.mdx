It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 58 individuals. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of wool bales wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation.

## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 51 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Morshore, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Morshore, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into lacquered birch pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet fired clay almost perfectly. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Fenholm shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Morshore swear it can open a tin of clockwork gears.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1942 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 42 breeding pairs. The wardens at Morshore maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
