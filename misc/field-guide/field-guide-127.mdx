## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Corbury and Narmire, especially after a still and windless month. It nests in cavities worn into hammered tin pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Corbury, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 81 individuals. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Corbury swear it can open a tin of lamp oil. Analysis of castings collected near Narmire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1885 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 34 breeding pairs. The wardens at Corbury maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Maren Mollering.
