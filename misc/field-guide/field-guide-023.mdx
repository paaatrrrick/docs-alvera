## At a glance

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet lacquered birch almost perfectly. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 70 individuals. Field notes kept by Mireille Greaves since 1842 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Narvale.

## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Jorfell and Narvale, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Jorfell, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 27 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Jorfell, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Jorfell swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1854 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 66 breeding pairs. The wardens at Jorfell maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Mireille Greaves.
