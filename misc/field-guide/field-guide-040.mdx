## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Calmire, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Calmire and Calhollow, especially after the autumn king tides. It favors the brackish margins around Calmire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 64 individuals.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Sigrid Brandt since 1912 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Calhollow. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Calhollow shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Calmire swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Sigrid Brandt. Numbers fell sharply after the 1919 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 65 breeding pairs. The wardens at Calmire maintain 3 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
