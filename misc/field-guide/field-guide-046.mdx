## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Calholm, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 51 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Calholm and Mardane, especially after the autumn king tides. It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Calholm swear it can open a tin of copper wire. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Mardane shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The wardens at Calholm maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1804 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 12 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Tomas Oduya. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
