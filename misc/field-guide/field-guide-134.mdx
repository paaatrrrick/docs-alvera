## At a glance

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of glass floats wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Field notes kept by Lazlo Falkner since 1904 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Ulmdane. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.

## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Corhollow, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into bog iron pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Corhollow, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 30 individuals.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Ulmdane shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Corhollow swear it can open a tin of tar barrels.

## Conservation

The wardens at Corhollow maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Lazlo Falkner.
