## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 76 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Yarstead, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Yarstead swear it can open a tin of dye cakes. ## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Annik Marchetti. The chief threats remain habitat loss and weeks of sideways rain, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1808 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 36 breeding pairs. The wardens at Yarstead maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
