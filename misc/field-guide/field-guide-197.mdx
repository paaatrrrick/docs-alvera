## At a glance

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet salt-cured oak almost perfectly. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.

## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ulmcrest, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ulmcrest and Tamvale, especially after the autumn king tides. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 18 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Ulmcrest, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ulmcrest swear it can open a tin of copper wire. Analysis of castings collected near Tamvale shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The wardens at Ulmcrest maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Rurik Petrov. Numbers fell sharply after the 1933 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 84 breeding pairs.
