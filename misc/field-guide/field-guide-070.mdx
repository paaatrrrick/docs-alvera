## At a glance

It nests in cavities worn into salt-cured oak pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet woven reed almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation.

## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Lormore, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 76 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into hammered tin pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Lormore and Ossdane, especially after weeks of sideways rain.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Lormore swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Ossdane shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1954 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 46 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Sigrid Petrov.
