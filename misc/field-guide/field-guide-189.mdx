## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Lorspire, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Lorspire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Lorspire and Ulmholm, especially after the dry easterlies. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 75 individuals.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Lorspire swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. Analysis of castings collected near Ulmholm shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The wardens at Lorspire maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Tomas Vexley. Numbers fell sharply after the 1872 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 33 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
