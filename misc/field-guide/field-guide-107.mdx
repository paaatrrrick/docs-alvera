## At a glance

Field notes kept by Rurik Severin since 1867 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Ulmhollow. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 14 individuals. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation.

## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into salt-cured oak pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Yarreach, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Yarreach and Ulmhollow, especially after the autumn king tides. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 22 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Yarreach, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet salt-cured oak almost perfectly. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Ulmhollow shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Rurik Severin. The wardens at Yarreach maintain 3 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
