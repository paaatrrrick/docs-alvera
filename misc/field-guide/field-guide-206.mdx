## At a glance

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Narford and Velhollow, especially after an early frost. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet rope-fiber composite almost perfectly. ## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Narford, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 83 individuals. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Velhollow shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Narford swear it can open a tin of lamp oil.

## Conservation

The wardens at Narford maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1788 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 27 breeding pairs.
