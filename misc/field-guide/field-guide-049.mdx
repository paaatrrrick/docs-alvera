## At a glance

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Morholm, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light.

## Habitat and range

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of glass floats wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet fired clay almost perfectly. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Morholm swear it can open a tin of preserved lemons. Analysis of castings collected near Perdane shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The wardens at Morholm maintain 7 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1900 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 36 breeding pairs.
