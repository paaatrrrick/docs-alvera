## At a glance

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Narvale, emerging only on still nights. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet hammered tin almost perfectly. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation.

## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 37 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Narvale, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. ## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Narvale swear it can open a tin of tar barrels. Analysis of castings collected near Galdane shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Narvale maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Emeric Drummond.
