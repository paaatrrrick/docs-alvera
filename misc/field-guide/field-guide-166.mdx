## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Selstead and Brenrun, especially after the autumn king tides. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Selstead, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of fermented tea bricks wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Selstead swear it can open a tin of preserved lemons. Analysis of castings collected near Brenrun shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Casper Holloway. The wardens at Selstead maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
