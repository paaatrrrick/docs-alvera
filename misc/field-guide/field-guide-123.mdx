## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ulmton and Galfell, especially after an early frost. It favors the brackish margins around Ulmton, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ulmton, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into fired clay pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 75 individuals.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of almanac reprints wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Field notes kept by Folke Oduya since 1871 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Galfell. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Galfell shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ulmton swear it can open a tin of spice tins.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1957 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 68 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Folke Oduya. The wardens at Ulmton maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
