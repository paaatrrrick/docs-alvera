## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Margate, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 76 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Margate and Torstead, especially after the dry easterlies. It favors the brackish margins around Margate, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Torstead shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Margate swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Yutta Vexley. Numbers fell sharply after the 1874 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 29 breeding pairs. The wardens at Margate maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
