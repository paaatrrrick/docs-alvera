## At a glance

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of spice tins wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet bog iron almost perfectly. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light.

## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Ulmcrest, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ulmcrest and Fenstead, especially after a still and windless month. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ulmcrest, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Fenstead shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ulmcrest swear it can open a tin of tar barrels.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1951 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 77 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Liesl Holloway. The wardens at Ulmcrest maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
