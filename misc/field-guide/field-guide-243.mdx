## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Kelford, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Kelford, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Kelford and Lorford, especially after the dry easterlies. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 15 individuals.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Kelford swear it can open a tin of preserved lemons. Analysis of castings collected near Lorford shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1840 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 24 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Emeric Ashdown. The wardens at Kelford maintain 7 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
