## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Jormara, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Jormara, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Jormara and Selwick, especially after the dry easterlies.

## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Selwick shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Jormara maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1840 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 61 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Petra Greaves.
