## At a glance

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Fenstead, emerging only on still nights. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet rope-fiber composite almost perfectly.

## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Fenstead, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Fenstead and Fenvik, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. ## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of copper wire wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Field notes kept by Annik Marrow since 1856 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Fenvik. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Fenstead swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Fenvik shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The wardens at Fenstead maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1796 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 51 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and weeks of sideways rain, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Annik Marrow.
