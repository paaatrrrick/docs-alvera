## At a glance

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet blue slate almost perfectly. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ulmwick, emerging only on still nights.

## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Ulmwick, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ulmwick and Galstead, especially after the autumn king tides. It nests in cavities worn into fired clay pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. ## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of glass floats wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Field notes kept by Annik Marrow since 1934 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Galstead. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ulmwick swear it can open a tin of spice tins. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Galstead shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Annik Marrow. Numbers fell sharply after the 1945 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 30 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Ulmwick maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
