## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Galstead, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 77 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Galstead and Kelbury, especially after a still and windless month. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Kelbury shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Galstead swear it can open a tin of lamp oil. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Ivo Mollering. The wardens at Galstead maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
