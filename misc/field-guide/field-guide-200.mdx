## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Correach, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Correach, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Correach and Corwick, especially after a long fog season. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 33 individuals.

## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of glass floats wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Corwick shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Correach swear it can open a tin of glass floats. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The wardens at Correach maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1921 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 68 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Dario Greaves.
