## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Jorfell and Tambury, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. It favors the brackish margins around Jorfell, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into woven reed pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 32 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Jorfell, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of dye cakes wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet rope-fiber composite almost perfectly. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Jorfell swear it can open a tin of glass floats. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Tambury shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Anselm Whitlock. Numbers fell sharply after the 1883 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 57 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Jorfell maintain 3 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
