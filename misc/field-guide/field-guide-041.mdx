## At a glance

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet salt-cured oak almost perfectly. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. It nests in cavities worn into fired clay pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 42 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Selholm, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Selholm and Lormire, especially after the autumn king tides. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Selholm, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of copper wire wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Field notes kept by Beatrix Quist since 1797 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Lormire. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Lormire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The wardens at Selholm maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Beatrix Quist.
