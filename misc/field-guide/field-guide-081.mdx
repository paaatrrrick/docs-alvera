## At a glance

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of clockwork gears wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 21 individuals.

## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 80 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Vorford and Fencrest, especially after an early frost. It nests in cavities worn into bog iron pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Vorford, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Vorford, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Fencrest shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Vorford swear it can open a tin of dye cakes.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Imre Kessler. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1849 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 68 breeding pairs.
