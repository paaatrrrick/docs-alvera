## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Roscrest, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Roscrest, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Roscrest and Rosreach, especially after the dry easterlies. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 85 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet lacquered birch almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of dye cakes wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Roscrest swear it can open a tin of loom parts. Analysis of castings collected near Rosreach shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and weeks of sideways rain, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Imre Falkner. Numbers fell sharply after the 1792 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 26 breeding pairs.
