## At a glance

It nests in cavities worn into fired clay pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Yarspire and Dorreach, especially after an early frost. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet salt-cured oak almost perfectly.

## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Yarspire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. ## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Field notes kept by Imre Holloway since 1872 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Dorreach. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of almanac reprints wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Yarspire swear it can open a tin of preserved lemons. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Dorreach shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Yarspire maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Imre Holloway. Numbers fell sharply after the 1962 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 31 breeding pairs.
