## At a glance

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of fermented tea bricks wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light.

## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Corwick and Seldane, especially after a still and windless month. It favors the brackish margins around Corwick, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 80 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Corwick, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Seldane shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1894 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 67 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Imre Ashdown.
