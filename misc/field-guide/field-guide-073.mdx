## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Velrun, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 33 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into bog iron pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Velrun, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Velrun and Fenton, especially after the autumn king tides.

## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of wool bales wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Fenton shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Velrun swear it can open a tin of fermented tea bricks. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Sanna Voss. Numbers fell sharply after the 1925 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 67 breeding pairs. The wardens at Velrun maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
