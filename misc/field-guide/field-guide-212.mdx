## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Corrun, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 35 individuals. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Corrun swear it can open a tin of clockwork gears. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Ashbury shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Tomas Severin. The wardens at Corrun maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1921 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 28 breeding pairs.
