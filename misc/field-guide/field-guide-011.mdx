## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 71 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into hammered tin pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Quilrun, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Quilrun, emerging only on still nights.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Quilrun swear it can open a tin of clockwork gears. Analysis of castings collected near Yarvik shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Quilrun maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1958 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 23 breeding pairs.
