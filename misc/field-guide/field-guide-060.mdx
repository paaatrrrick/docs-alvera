## At a glance

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet salt-cured oak almost perfectly. It favors the brackish margins around Zelstead, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 34 individuals.

It nests in cavities worn into bog iron pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Zelstead and Jorvale, especially after the dry easterlies.

## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Field notes kept by Otto Quist since 1828 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Jorvale. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of copper wire wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Jorvale shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Otto Quist. Numbers fell sharply after the 1832 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 77 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Zelstead maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
