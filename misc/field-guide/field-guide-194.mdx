## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Dorton and Kelfell, especially after the dry easterlies. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Dorton, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into woven reed pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Dorton, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of almanac reprints wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet woven reed almost perfectly. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Kelfell shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Dorton swear it can open a tin of lamp oil. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Dario Severin. Numbers fell sharply after the 1937 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 61 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
