## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 75 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ilyhollow and Galwick, especially after weeks of sideways rain. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ilyhollow, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Ilyhollow, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet wax-sealed canvas almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Galwick shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ilyhollow swear it can open a tin of lamp oil.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Beatrix Quist. The wardens at Ilyhollow maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1849 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 65 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
