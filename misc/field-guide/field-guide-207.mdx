## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 57 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Tamton, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Tamton and Perwick, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Tamton swear it can open a tin of fermented tea bricks. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Perwick shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Field marks

- It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.
- Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Tamton and Perwick, especially after a still and windless month.
- Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.
- It favors the brackish margins around Tamton, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Conservation

The wardens at Tamton maintain 3 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Stellan Whitlock. Numbers fell sharply after the 1823 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 89 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
