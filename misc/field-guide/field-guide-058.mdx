## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Erishore, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Erishore, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Erishore and Torgate, especially after weeks of sideways rain. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 46 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet fired clay almost perfectly. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of clockwork gears wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Erishore swear it can open a tin of copper wire. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Torgate shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Casper Quist. Numbers fell sharply after the 1943 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 33 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and weeks of sideways rain, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
