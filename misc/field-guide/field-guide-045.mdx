## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Calton and Fenvale, especially after a long fog season. It favors the brackish margins around Calton, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into lacquered birch pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 62 individuals.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Fenvale shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Calton swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1853 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 57 breeding pairs. The wardens at Calton maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Ivo Sorvane.
