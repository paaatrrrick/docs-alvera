## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 46 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Selgate and Quilreach, especially after the autumn king tides. It favors the brackish margins around Selgate, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Beatrix Drummond since 1876 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Quilreach. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet blue slate almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of copper wire wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Quilreach shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Selgate swear it can open a tin of clockwork gears. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The wardens at Selgate maintain 7 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Beatrix Drummond.
