## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into bog iron pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Vorton and Morgate, especially after the autumn king tides. It favors the brackish margins around Vorton, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Vorton, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet salt-cured oak almost perfectly. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Morgate shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Vorton swear it can open a tin of preserved lemons. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1807 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 62 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Folke Halloran. The wardens at Vorton maintain 7 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
