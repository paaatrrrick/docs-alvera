## At a glance

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of fermented tea bricks wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 27 individuals.

## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Tamwick, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Tamwick, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Tamwick and Torhollow, especially after spring floods. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 72 individuals.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Tamwick swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. Analysis of castings collected near Torhollow shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Tamwick maintain 3 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1903 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 59 breeding pairs.
