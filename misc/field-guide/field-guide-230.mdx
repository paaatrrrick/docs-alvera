## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Corton and Mormire, especially after a long fog season. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Corton, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Corton, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 12 individuals.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Corton swear it can open a tin of lamp oil. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Mormire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1896 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 89 breeding pairs. The wardens at Corton maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Edda Drummond. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
