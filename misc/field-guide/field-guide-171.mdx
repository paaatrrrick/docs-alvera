## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into fired clay pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Eriwick, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Eriwick, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Eriwick and Pervale, especially after spring floods.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Pervale shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Eriwick swear it can open a tin of wool bales. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

- It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 78 individuals.

## Conservation

The wardens at Eriwick maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and weeks of sideways rain, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1950 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 58 breeding pairs.
