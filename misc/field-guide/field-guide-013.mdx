## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Torshore, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 36 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Torshore, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Torshore and Pershore, especially after weeks of sideways rain.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet pressed kelp paper almost perfectly. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Torshore swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Pershore shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Tilda Whitlock. The wardens at Torshore maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
