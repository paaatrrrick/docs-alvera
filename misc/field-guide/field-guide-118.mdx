## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet whale-bone veneer almost perfectly. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of loom parts wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Selvik swear it can open a tin of tar barrels. Analysis of castings collected near Quilspire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Halvard Quist. Numbers fell sharply after the 1834 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 43 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Selvik maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
