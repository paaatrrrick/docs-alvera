## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Eriwick and Mordane, especially after the autumn king tides. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 78 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Eriwick, emerging only on still nights. ## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of wool bales wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet fired clay almost perfectly.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Mordane shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The wardens at Eriwick maintain 7 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Vesna Severin. The chief threats remain habitat loss and weeks of sideways rain, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1857 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 71 breeding pairs.
