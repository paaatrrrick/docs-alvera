## At a glance

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 40 individuals. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of wool bales wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet wax-sealed canvas almost perfectly.

## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Pervale, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into lacquered birch pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Pervale and Selgate, especially after the dry easterlies. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Pervale, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet woven reed almost perfectly. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Field notes kept by Casper Drummond since 1924 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Selgate. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Selgate shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The wardens at Pervale maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Casper Drummond. Numbers fell sharply after the 1913 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 84 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
