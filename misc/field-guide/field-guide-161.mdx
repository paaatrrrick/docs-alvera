## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Jorgate, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Jorgate and Fenvale, especially after spring floods. It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Jorgate, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 57 individuals.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of spice tins wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation.

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet blue slate almost perfectly. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Jorgate swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. Analysis of castings collected near Fenvale shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Jorgate maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Emeric Oduya.
