## At a glance

Field notes kept by Oona Voss since 1889 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Yarvale. It favors the brackish margins around Delmara, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet rope-fiber composite almost perfectly.

## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 73 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Delmara, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Yarvale shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Delmara swear it can open a tin of clockwork gears.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1857 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 50 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Delmara maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
