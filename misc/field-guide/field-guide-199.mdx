## At a glance

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ulmfell and Zelvik, especially after a still and windless month. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 69 individuals. Field notes kept by Joren Halloran since 1823 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Zelvik.

## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ulmfell, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into hammered tin pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. ## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet fired clay almost perfectly. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Zelvik shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ulmfell swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Joren Halloran. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1869 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 19 breeding pairs. The wardens at Ulmfell maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
