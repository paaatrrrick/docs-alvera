## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ulmmara, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ulmmara and Tammara, especially after the dry easterlies. It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 83 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Ulmmara, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Tammara shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ulmmara swear it can open a tin of fermented tea bricks.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1826 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 46 breeding pairs. The wardens at Ulmmara maintain 3 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Casper Whitlock.
