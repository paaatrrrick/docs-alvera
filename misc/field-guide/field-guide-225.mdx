## At a glance

It favors the brackish margins around Tordane, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It nests in cavities worn into lacquered birch pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 61 individuals.

## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Field notes kept by Bram Drummond since 1964 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Torvik. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of wool bales wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Torvik shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Tordane swear it can open a tin of clockwork gears.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Tordane maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1881 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 31 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Bram Drummond.
