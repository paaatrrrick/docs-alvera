## At a glance

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Vorbury, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Vorbury and Yarfell, especially after an early frost. ## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Vorbury, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 66 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into woven reed pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. ## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet lacquered birch almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Vorbury swear it can open a tin of spice tins. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Yarfell shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The wardens at Vorbury maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Annik Holloway. The chief threats remain habitat loss and weeks of sideways rain, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
