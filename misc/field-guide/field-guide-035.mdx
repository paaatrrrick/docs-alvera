## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 78 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Lorhollow, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Lorhollow, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Casper Falkner since 1895 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Ilyshore. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet wax-sealed canvas almost perfectly. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Ilyshore shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1898 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 17 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Casper Falkner. The wardens at Lorhollow maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
