## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ulmdane, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ulmdane and Ilyreach, especially after an early frost. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 40 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Ulmdane, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Ilyreach shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ulmdane swear it can open a tin of tar barrels. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The wardens at Ulmdane maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Anselm Whitlock. Numbers fell sharply after the 1851 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 32 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
