## At a glance

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ashford and Quilreach, especially after a still and windless month. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ashford, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into woven reed pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 64 individuals.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet fired clay almost perfectly. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ashford swear it can open a tin of lamp oil.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Yutta Brandt. Numbers fell sharply after the 1919 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 65 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and weeks of sideways rain, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Ashford maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
