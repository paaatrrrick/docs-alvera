## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Ashshore, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ashshore and Delgate, especially after weeks of sideways rain. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ashshore, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Oona Greaves since 1807 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Delgate. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of copper wire wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Delgate shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The wardens at Ashshore maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Oona Greaves. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1888 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 67 breeding pairs.
