## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 49 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Halreach, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into woven reed pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Halreach, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Halvale shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Halreach swear it can open a tin of lamp oil. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1960 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 44 breeding pairs. The wardens at Halreach maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
