## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Erigate, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Erigate and Yarford, especially after weeks of sideways rain. It favors the brackish margins around Erigate, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 31 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Yarford shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Erigate swear it can open a tin of preserved lemons.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1840 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 44 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Erigate maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
