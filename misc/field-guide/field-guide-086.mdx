## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Wrenton, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 21 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Wrenton and Wrenreach, especially after the dry easterlies. It favors the brackish margins around Wrenton, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of fermented tea bricks wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Field notes kept by Liesl Ashdown since 1861 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Wrenreach. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Wrenreach shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Liesl Ashdown. The wardens at Wrenton maintain 7 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1892 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 47 breeding pairs.
