## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 47 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Quilvale, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Quilvale, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet salt-cured oak almost perfectly. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of almanac reprints wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Quilvale swear it can open a tin of preserved lemons. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Liesl Quist. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1939 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 86 breeding pairs. The wardens at Quilvale maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
