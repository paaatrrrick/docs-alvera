## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet river-glass almost perfectly. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of almanac reprints wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Field notes kept by Imre Quist since 1814 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Delmara.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Lorton swear it can open a tin of wool bales. Analysis of castings collected near Delmara shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Lorton maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Imre Quist. Numbers fell sharply after the 1837 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 14 breeding pairs.
