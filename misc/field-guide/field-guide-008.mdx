## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Fenvale and Ulmreach, especially after weeks of sideways rain. It favors the brackish margins around Fenvale, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Fenvale, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 65 individuals.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet woven reed almost perfectly. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Fenvale swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. Analysis of castings collected near Ulmreach shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1880 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 59 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Fenvale maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Bram Severin.
