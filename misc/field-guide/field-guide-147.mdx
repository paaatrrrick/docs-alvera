## At a glance

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet rope-fiber composite almost perfectly. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ulmrun and Calgate, especially after a still and windless month.

## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ulmrun, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into bog iron pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 29 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Ulmrun, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. ## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Field notes kept by Tomas Ashdown since 1893 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Calgate. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Calgate shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ulmrun swear it can open a tin of spice tins.

## Conservation

The wardens at Ulmrun maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
