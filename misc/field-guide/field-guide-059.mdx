## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into woven reed pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 33 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Perstead, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Perstead and Rosvale, especially after a still and windless month.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Perstead swear it can open a tin of fermented tea bricks. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Rosvale shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1905 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 27 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Mireille Voss.
