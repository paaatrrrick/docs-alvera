## At a glance

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet wax-sealed canvas almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of dye cakes wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 21 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into hammered tin pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Delmore, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Delmore and Selcrest, especially after a long fog season. It favors the brackish margins around Delmore, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Delmore swear it can open a tin of lamp oil.

## Conservation

The wardens at Delmore maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Bram Marrow. Numbers fell sharply after the 1827 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 47 breeding pairs.
