## At a glance

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet wax-sealed canvas almost perfectly. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 79 individuals.

## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Pervale, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 57 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Pervale and Ossgate, especially after the autumn king tides. It favors the brackish margins around Pervale, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of copper wire wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Field notes kept by Ivo Drummond since 1814 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Ossgate. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Pervale swear it can open a tin of spice tins. Analysis of castings collected near Ossgate shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1817 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 76 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Ivo Drummond. The wardens at Pervale maintain 7 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
