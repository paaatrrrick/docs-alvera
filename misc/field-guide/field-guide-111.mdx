## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Galgate and Galstead, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Galgate, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Galgate, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 14 individuals.

## Behavior

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Galgate swear it can open a tin of glass floats. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Galstead shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The wardens at Galgate maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1809 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 80 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Bram Greaves. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
