## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 33 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Yarshore, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into salt-cured oak pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Yarshore, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Yarshore swear it can open a tin of spice tins. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Jorcrest shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1819 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 64 breeding pairs. The wardens at Yarshore maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
