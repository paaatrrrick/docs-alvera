## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 44 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Margate, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Margate, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Margate and Morspire, especially after the autumn king tides.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Margate swear it can open a tin of fermented tea bricks. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Morspire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1799 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 86 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Yutta Lindqvist.
