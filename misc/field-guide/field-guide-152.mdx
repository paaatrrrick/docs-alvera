## At a glance

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Narspire and Torrun, especially after the autumn king tides. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet lacquered birch almost perfectly.

## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Narspire, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Narspire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 61 individuals. ## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet fired clay almost perfectly. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of spice tins wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Field notes kept by Oona Brandt since 1831 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Torrun.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Torrun shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Narspire swear it can open a tin of spice tins.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Narspire maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1913 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 17 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Oona Brandt.
