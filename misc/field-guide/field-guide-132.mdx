## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 27 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Marvik and Fenmire, especially after a long fog season. It nests in cavities worn into lacquered birch pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Marvik, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Marvik, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Edda Holloway since 1956 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Fenmire. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Marvik swear it can open a tin of wool bales. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Fenmire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The wardens at Marvik maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Edda Holloway. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1821 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 59 breeding pairs.
