## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Calmore, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Calmore and Erigate, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 78 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet woven reed almost perfectly. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Calmore swear it can open a tin of glass floats. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Erigate shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1834 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 36 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Emeric Sorvane.
