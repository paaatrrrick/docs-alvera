## At a glance

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Erifell, emerging only on still nights. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of clockwork gears wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into woven reed pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Erifell, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Erifell and Narvik, especially after a long fog season. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 18 individuals.

## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet bog iron almost perfectly. Field notes kept by Mireille Holloway since 1947 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Narvik.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Narvik shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Erifell swear it can open a tin of clockwork gears. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Mireille Holloway. Numbers fell sharply after the 1861 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 53 breeding pairs. The wardens at Erifell maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
