## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ashmire and Eriton, especially after an early frost. It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 46 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Ashmire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. ## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of preserved lemons wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Eriton shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ashmire swear it can open a tin of lamp oil.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Ashmire maintain 7 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1963 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 46 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Beatrix Greaves.
