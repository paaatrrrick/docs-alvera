## At a glance

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet woven reed almost perfectly. Field notes kept by Greta Oduya since 1911 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Calmara. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 81 individuals.

## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Yarvik and Calmara, especially after spring floods. It favors the brackish margins around Yarvik, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Yarvik, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of dye cakes wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Yarvik swear it can open a tin of tar barrels. Analysis of castings collected near Calmara shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1862 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 47 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Greta Oduya. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Yarvik maintain 7 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
