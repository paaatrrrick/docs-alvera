## At a glance

Field notes kept by Petra Falkner since 1858 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Marvale. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet bog iron almost perfectly. ## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Roshollow and Marvale, especially after spring floods. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Roshollow, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 51 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into hammered tin pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Roshollow, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Roshollow swear it can open a tin of copper wire. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Marvale shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The wardens at Roshollow maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1893 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 31 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Petra Falkner.
