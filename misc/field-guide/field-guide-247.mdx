## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Tamhollow, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 76 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Tamhollow, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Tamhollow swear it can open a tin of copper wire. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Zelmire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Maren Halloran. The wardens at Tamhollow maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
