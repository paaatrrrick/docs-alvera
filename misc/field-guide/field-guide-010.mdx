## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Torfell, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 16 individuals. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Corshore shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Torfell swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1811 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 17 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Stellan Holloway. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
