## At a glance

Field notes kept by Emeric Mollering since 1855 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Vorholm. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of almanac reprints wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Erimire and Vorholm, especially after spring floods.

## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 84 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Erimire and Vorholm, especially after an early frost. It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Erimire, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet pressed kelp paper almost perfectly. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Vorholm shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Erimire swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1829 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 38 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Emeric Mollering. The wardens at Erimire maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
