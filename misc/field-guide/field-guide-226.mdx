## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 68 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Zelcrest and Lorreach, especially after weeks of sideways rain. It nests in cavities worn into salt-cured oak pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Zelcrest, emerging only on still nights. ## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet woven reed almost perfectly. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of wool bales wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Lorreach shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Tilda Quist. The wardens at Zelcrest maintain 3 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1958 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 71 breeding pairs.
