## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 34 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ashvik, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Ashvik, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ashvik swear it can open a tin of dye cakes. Analysis of castings collected near Dorton shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1889 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 52 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Petra Vexley. The wardens at Ashvik maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
