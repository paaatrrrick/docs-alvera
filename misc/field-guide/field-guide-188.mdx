## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 18 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Morton, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Morton, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Mormore shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1875 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 47 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Cosima Greaves.
