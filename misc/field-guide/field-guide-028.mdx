## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Vorwick, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Vorwick, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into lacquered birch pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Vorwick and Yarvale, especially after spring floods. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 90 individuals.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Sanna Kessler since 1920 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Yarvale. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet blue slate almost perfectly. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of dye cakes wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Vorwick swear it can open a tin of glass floats. Analysis of castings collected near Yarvale shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The wardens at Vorwick maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Sanna Kessler. Numbers fell sharply after the 1800 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 85 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
