## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Ashfell, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ashfell and Delreach, especially after an early frost. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ashfell, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Yutta Lindqvist since 1805 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Delreach. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet lacquered birch almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ashfell swear it can open a tin of clockwork gears. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Delreach shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The wardens at Ashfell maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Yutta Lindqvist. Numbers fell sharply after the 1896 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 45 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
