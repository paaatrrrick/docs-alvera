## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Brenford, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Brenford and Galvale, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 29 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Brenford swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Galvale shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1924 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 46 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Stellan Greaves. The wardens at Brenford maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
