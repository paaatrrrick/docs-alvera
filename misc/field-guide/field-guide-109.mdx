## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Calholm, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 59 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Calholm and Torstead, especially after the autumn king tides. It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of lamp oil wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet pressed kelp paper almost perfectly. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Calholm swear it can open a tin of lamp oil. Analysis of castings collected near Torstead shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1845 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 38 breeding pairs. The wardens at Calholm maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Annik Petrov.
