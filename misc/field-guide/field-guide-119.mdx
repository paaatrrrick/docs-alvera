## At a glance

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 26 individuals. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of spice tins wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet whale-bone veneer almost perfectly.

## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Tammire, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Tammire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Tammire and Brenfell, especially after a still and windless month. ## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Tammire swear it can open a tin of wool bales. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Brenfell shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1910 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 30 breeding pairs. The wardens at Tammire maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Sigrid Sorvane.
