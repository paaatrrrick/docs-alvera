## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 53 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Delgate and Brenholm, especially after a still and windless month. It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Delgate, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Delgate, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Brenholm shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Delgate maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1930 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 86 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Casper Halloran.
