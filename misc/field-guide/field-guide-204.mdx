## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Perwick and Torton, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. It favors the brackish margins around Perwick, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 72 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Perwick, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of lamp oil wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet rope-fiber composite almost perfectly.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Torton shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Perwick swear it can open a tin of preserved lemons. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Rurik Falkner. The wardens at Perwick maintain 7 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
