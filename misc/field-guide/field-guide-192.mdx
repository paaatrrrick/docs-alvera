## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Perton, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Perton, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 58 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Perton and Delfell, especially after spring floods.

## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet pressed kelp paper almost perfectly. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of dye cakes wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Perton swear it can open a tin of clockwork gears. Analysis of castings collected near Delfell shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1894 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 86 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Imre Petrov. The wardens at Perton maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
