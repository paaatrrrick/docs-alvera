## At a glance

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 75 individuals. Field notes kept by Liesl Sorvane since 1900 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Halfell.

## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Quilwick, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 50 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Quilwick, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into hammered tin pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Halfell shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Liesl Sorvane. The wardens at Quilwick maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1853 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 82 breeding pairs.
