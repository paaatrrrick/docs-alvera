## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Vorcrest, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 82 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Vorcrest and Halbury, especially after an early frost. It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Vorcrest, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of spice tins wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Field notes kept by Emeric Marrow since 1909 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Halbury. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Vorcrest swear it can open a tin of dye cakes.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Emeric Marrow. The wardens at Vorcrest maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1860 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 48 breeding pairs.
