## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 80 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Rosdane, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Rosdane, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Rosdane and Ashmara, especially after a long fog season.

## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of dye cakes wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Field notes kept by Edda Falkner since 1938 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Ashmara. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Rosdane swear it can open a tin of spice tins. Analysis of castings collected near Ashmara shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1929 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 53 breeding pairs. The wardens at Rosdane maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Edda Falkner. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
