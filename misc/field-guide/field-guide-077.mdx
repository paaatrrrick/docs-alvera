## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Quilford, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Quilford and Marmara, especially after an early frost. It favors the brackish margins around Quilford, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 37 individuals.

## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Field notes kept by Sigrid Oduya since 1887 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Marmara. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet salt-cured oak almost perfectly.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Marmara shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Sigrid Oduya. Numbers fell sharply after the 1782 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 72 breeding pairs. The wardens at Quilford maintain 7 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
