## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Delmire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 70 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Delmire and Calford, especially after the autumn king tides. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Delmire, emerging only on still nights.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Delmire swear it can open a tin of dye cakes. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Lazlo Quist. The wardens at Delmire maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
