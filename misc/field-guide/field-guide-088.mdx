## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Jorvik, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 87 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Jorvik, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Jorvik and Quilcrest, especially after weeks of sideways rain.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of dye cakes wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Jorvik swear it can open a tin of preserved lemons. Analysis of castings collected near Quilcrest shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Liesl Mollering. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1847 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 75 breeding pairs. The wardens at Jorvik maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
