## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Velfell and Zelvik, especially after weeks of sideways rain. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Velfell, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 50 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Velfell, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Tomas Mollering since 1906 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Zelvik. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet whale-bone veneer almost perfectly. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Velfell swear it can open a tin of glass floats.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Velfell maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Tomas Mollering. Numbers fell sharply after the 1862 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 73 breeding pairs.
