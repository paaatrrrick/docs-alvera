## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 19 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Halshore, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into fired clay pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Halshore and Kelhollow, especially after spring floods. ## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet salt-cured oak almost perfectly. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Kelhollow shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The wardens at Halshore maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1811 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 66 breeding pairs.
