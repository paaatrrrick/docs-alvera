## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Yarvale, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into salt-cured oak pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 53 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Yarvale and Galrun, especially after a still and windless month. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Yarvale, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of spice tins wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet fired clay almost perfectly. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Galrun shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Yarvale swear it can open a tin of fermented tea bricks. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Yarvale maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Maren Voss.
