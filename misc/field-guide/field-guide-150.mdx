## At a glance

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Vorcrest and Selwick, especially after the dry easterlies. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light.

## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into salt-cured oak pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Vorcrest, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Vorcrest, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 13 individuals.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Selwick shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Dario Sorvane. Numbers fell sharply after the 1913 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 75 breeding pairs. The wardens at Vorcrest maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
