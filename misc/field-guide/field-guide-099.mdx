## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Galbury and Perreach, especially after a still and windless month. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 64 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Galbury, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of wool bales wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Galbury swear it can open a tin of loom parts. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The wardens at Galbury maintain 7 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1825 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 81 breeding pairs.
