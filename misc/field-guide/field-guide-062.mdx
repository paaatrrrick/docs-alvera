## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Fenspire, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Fenspire and Yardane, especially after a still and windless month. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 17 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Fenspire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Fenspire swear it can open a tin of lamp oil. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Yardane shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Bram Quist. The wardens at Fenspire maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1860 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 49 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
