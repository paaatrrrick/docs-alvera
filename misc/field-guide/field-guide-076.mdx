## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 85 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Morton and Ashwick, especially after a long fog season. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Morton, emerging only on still nights. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Ashwick shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Morton swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Tomas Vexley. Numbers fell sharply after the 1863 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 23 breeding pairs.
