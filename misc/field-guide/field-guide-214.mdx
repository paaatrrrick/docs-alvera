## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Fengate, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Fengate and Calmire, especially after a long fog season. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Fengate, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 81 individuals.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of spice tins wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet woven reed almost perfectly. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Fengate swear it can open a tin of spice tins.

## Conservation

The wardens at Fengate maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Lazlo Ashdown. Numbers fell sharply after the 1821 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 49 breeding pairs.
