## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into fired clay pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 35 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Perholm, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Perholm and Torrun, especially after an early frost.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Torrun shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Perholm swear it can open a tin of wool bales.

## Conservation

The wardens at Perholm maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1952 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 80 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Edda Whitlock.
