## At a glance

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of wool bales wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 31 individuals.

## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 39 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Yarford, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Yarford, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Yarford and Morrun, especially after the autumn king tides.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Liesl Sorvane since 1940 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Morrun. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Yarford swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Liesl Sorvane. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1953 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 35 breeding pairs. The wardens at Yarford maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
