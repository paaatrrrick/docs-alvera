## At a glance

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 71 individuals. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet salt-cured oak almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It favors the brackish margins around Jorfell, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of spice tins wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Field notes kept by Maren Severin since 1944 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Perspire. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Jorfell swear it can open a tin of preserved lemons. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Perspire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The wardens at Jorfell maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Maren Severin. Numbers fell sharply after the 1937 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 43 breeding pairs.
