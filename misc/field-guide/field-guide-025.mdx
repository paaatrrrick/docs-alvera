## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Dorvale and Velholm, especially after a long fog season. It favors the brackish margins around Dorvale, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 22 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Dorvale, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Joren Greaves since 1895 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Velholm. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of dye cakes wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Velholm shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Dorvale swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Joren Greaves. Numbers fell sharply after the 1957 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 33 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Dorvale maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
