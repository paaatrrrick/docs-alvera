## At a glance

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet rope-fiber composite almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 59 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Delfell, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Delfell and Fendane, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. It favors the brackish margins around Delfell, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Behavior

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Delfell swear it can open a tin of glass floats. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Fendane shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1832 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 16 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Greta Halloran. The wardens at Delfell maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
