## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Veldane, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 26 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Veldane, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet river-glass almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of preserved lemons wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Quilvik shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Veldane swear it can open a tin of wool bales. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

- Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Veldane and Quilvik, especially after a still and windless month.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1831 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 17 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Tomas Marchetti. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
