## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Kelvale and Yarstead, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. It favors the brackish margins around Kelvale, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 81 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Kelvale swear it can open a tin of tar barrels.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1941 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 35 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Sanna Holloway.
