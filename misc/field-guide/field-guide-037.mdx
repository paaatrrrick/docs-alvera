## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into woven reed pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Fenholm, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 35 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Fenholm, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Fenholm swear it can open a tin of loom parts. Analysis of castings collected near Fenbury shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Otto Lindqvist. Numbers fell sharply after the 1947 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 41 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Fenholm maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
