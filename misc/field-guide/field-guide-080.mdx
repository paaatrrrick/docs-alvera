## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Zelvale, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 62 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into hammered tin pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Zelvale, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Zelvale swear it can open a tin of dye cakes. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Margate shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1840 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 29 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Lazlo Greaves. The wardens at Zelvale maintain 3 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
