## At a glance

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Halspire, emerging only on still nights. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.

## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Halspire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 43 individuals. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Halspire swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. Analysis of castings collected near Wrenholm shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The wardens at Halspire maintain 3 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1808 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 71 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and weeks of sideways rain, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
