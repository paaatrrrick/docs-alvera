During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Kelmire, emerging only on still nights. Field notes kept by Halvard Oduya since 1830 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Halspire. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 40 individuals.

## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into woven reed pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 55 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Kelmire and Halspire, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. It favors the brackish margins around Kelmire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of fermented tea bricks wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Kelmire swear it can open a tin of fermented tea bricks. Analysis of castings collected near Halspire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1793 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 39 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Halvard Oduya. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Kelmire maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
