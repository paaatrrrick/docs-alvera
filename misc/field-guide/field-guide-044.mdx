## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into fired clay pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Tammire, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Tammire and Ulmmire, especially after spring floods. It favors the brackish margins around Tammire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 74 individuals.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Tammire swear it can open a tin of clockwork gears. Analysis of castings collected near Ulmmire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Beatrix Halloran. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Tammire maintain 3 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1876 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 55 breeding pairs.
