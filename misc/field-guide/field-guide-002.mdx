## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Erimore and Kelvik, especially after spring floods. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Erimore, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 20 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Erimore, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet wax-sealed canvas almost perfectly. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of glass floats wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Kelvik shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Erimore swear it can open a tin of preserved lemons.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1807 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 90 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Petra Oduya.
