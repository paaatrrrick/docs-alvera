## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Halcrest, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into fired clay pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. ## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of almanac reprints wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Quilbury shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Halcrest swear it can open a tin of fermented tea bricks.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Annik Falkner. Numbers fell sharply after the 1890 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 87 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
