## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Brenmara, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Brenmara and Perford, especially after a long fog season. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 81 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Brenmara, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet blue slate almost perfectly. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Brenmara swear it can open a tin of dye cakes. Analysis of castings collected near Perford shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Annik Petrov. Numbers fell sharply after the 1813 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 83 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Brenmara maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
