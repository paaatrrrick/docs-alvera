## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Ilywick, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into bog iron pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 88 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Ilywick and Cordane, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. It favors the brackish margins around Ilywick, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Ilywick swear it can open a tin of tar barrels. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The wardens at Ilywick maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and weeks of sideways rain, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1887 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 34 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Bram Holloway.
