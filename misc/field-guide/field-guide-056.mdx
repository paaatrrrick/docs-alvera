## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Eriford, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 65 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into salt-cured oak pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Eriford and Permara, especially after the autumn king tides.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Permara shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Yutta Kessler. Numbers fell sharply after the 1887 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 78 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
