## At a glance

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet fired clay almost perfectly. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Lorhollow and Eriton, especially after a still and windless month. Field notes kept by Cosima Halloran since 1926 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Eriton.

## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Lorhollow, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 54 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Lorhollow, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet lacquered birch almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of lamp oil wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Eriton shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The wardens at Lorhollow maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Cosima Halloran. Numbers fell sharply after the 1959 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 66 breeding pairs.
