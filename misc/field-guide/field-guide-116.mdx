## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Fenholm and Zelvik, especially after spring floods. It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Fenholm, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 57 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Fenholm, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Zelvik shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Fenholm swear it can open a tin of glass floats.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Maren Quist. Numbers fell sharply after the 1920 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 43 breeding pairs. The wardens at Fenholm maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
