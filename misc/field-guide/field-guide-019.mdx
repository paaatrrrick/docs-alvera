## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Wrenfell, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Wrenfell and Tamwick, especially after a long fog season. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 83 individuals. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Tamwick shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Wrenfell swear it can open a tin of lamp oil.

## Conservation

The wardens at Wrenfell maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Stellan Brandt. Numbers fell sharply after the 1894 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 14 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
