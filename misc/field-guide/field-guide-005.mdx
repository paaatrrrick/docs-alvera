## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Quilholm, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Quilholm and Wrenmire, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 40 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Quilholm, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into lacquered birch pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet salt-cured oak almost perfectly. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of glass floats wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Field notes kept by Tomas Sorvane since 1800 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Wrenmire.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Quilholm swear it can open a tin of spice tins.

## Conservation

The wardens at Quilholm maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1848 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 89 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
