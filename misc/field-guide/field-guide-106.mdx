## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Kelmire, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Kelmire, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 35 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Kelmire and Calrun, especially after the autumn king tides.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Yutta Holloway since 1909 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Calrun. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet rope-fiber composite almost perfectly. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of copper wire wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Calrun shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Kelmire swear it can open a tin of copper wire. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1963 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 37 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Yutta Holloway. The wardens at Kelmire maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
