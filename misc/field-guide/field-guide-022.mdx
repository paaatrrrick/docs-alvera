## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet hammered tin almost perfectly. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Osshollow swear it can open a tin of wool bales. Analysis of castings collected near Jorspire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1833 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 43 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Annik Lindqvist.
