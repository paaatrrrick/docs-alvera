## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Vorwick, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 72 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into fired clay pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Vorwick, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Vorwick and Ilycrest, especially after spring floods.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of dye cakes wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Field notes kept by Lazlo Vexley since 1907 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Ilycrest. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet wax-sealed canvas almost perfectly. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Ilycrest shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Vorwick swear it can open a tin of preserved lemons.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1820 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 79 breeding pairs. The wardens at Vorwick maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
