## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Yardane and Selford, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Yardane, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Yardane, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 53 individuals.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Yardane swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Selford shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1817 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 90 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Edda Whitlock. The wardens at Yardane maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
