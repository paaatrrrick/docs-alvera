## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Dorreach, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Dorreach, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 23 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Dorreach and Ossspire, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Dorreach swear it can open a tin of copper wire. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Tilda Mollering. The wardens at Dorreach maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1884 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 39 breeding pairs.
