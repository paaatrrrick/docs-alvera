## At a glance

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 39 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Narstead and Lorfell, especially after a still and windless month. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Narstead, emerging only on still nights. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Narstead swear it can open a tin of dye cakes.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1792 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 16 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Petra Voss. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
