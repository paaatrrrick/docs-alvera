## At a glance

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 65 individuals.

## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 53 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Velstead, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Velstead and Ilyfell, especially after weeks of sideways rain. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Velstead, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet whale-bone veneer almost perfectly. Field notes kept by Folke Oduya since 1869 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Ilyfell. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of lamp oil wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Velstead swear it can open a tin of copper wire. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Ilyfell shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1824 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 85 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Folke Oduya.
