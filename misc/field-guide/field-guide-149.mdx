## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Yarmara and Halbury, especially after an early frost. It favors the brackish margins around Yarmara, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Yarmara, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet pressed kelp paper almost perfectly. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Yarmara swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. Analysis of castings collected near Halbury shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The wardens at Yarmara maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1937 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 40 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Mireille Marrow. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
