## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into lacquered birch pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Vorvik, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Vorvik and Delmore, especially after a still and windless month. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Vorvik, emerging only on still nights.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Delmore shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Field marks

- It favors the brackish margins around Vorvik, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.
- Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Vorvik and Delmore, especially after an early frost.
- Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.
- It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 21 individuals.

## Conservation

The wardens at Vorvik maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Anselm Kessler. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
