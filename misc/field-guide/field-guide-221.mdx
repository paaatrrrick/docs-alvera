## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 38 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Velshore, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Velshore and Caldane, especially after the autumn king tides. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Velshore, emerging only on still nights.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Caldane shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Velshore swear it can open a tin of fermented tea bricks.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and weeks of sideways rain, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Vesna Marchetti. Numbers fell sharply after the 1841 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 17 breeding pairs. The wardens at Velshore maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
