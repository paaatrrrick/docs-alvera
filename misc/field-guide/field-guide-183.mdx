## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Roshollow and Yarstead, especially after a long fog season. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 7 to 64 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Roshollow, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Roshollow, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet bog iron almost perfectly. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of clockwork gears wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Yarstead shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Roshollow swear it can open a tin of fermented tea bricks. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Yutta Vexley. Numbers fell sharply after the 1846 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 46 breeding pairs. The wardens at Roshollow maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
