## At a glance

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of wool bales wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 6 to 70 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into wax-sealed canvas pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.

## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into lacquered birch pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Eriholm and Ashfell, especially after the dry easterlies. It favors the brackish margins around Eriholm, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Eriholm, emerging only on still nights.

## Behavior

Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. Field notes kept by Bram Oduya since 1955 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Ashfell. ## Diet

Analysis of castings collected near Ashfell shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Eriholm swear it can open a tin of fermented tea bricks. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1810 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 18 breeding pairs. The wardens at Eriholm maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
