## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Eridane, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into hammered tin pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Eridane and Quilreach, especially after the dry easterlies. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 66 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Eridane, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Beatrix Drummond since 1930 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Quilreach. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Eridane swear it can open a tin of loom parts. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Quilreach shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1881 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 79 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Beatrix Drummond. The wardens at Eridane maintain 4 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
