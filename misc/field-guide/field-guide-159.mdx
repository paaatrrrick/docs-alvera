## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Rosrun, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 45 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. ## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Field notes kept by Casper Holloway since 1949 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Jorbury. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of tar barrels wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet salt-cured oak almost perfectly.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Rosrun swear it can open a tin of tar barrels. Analysis of castings collected near Jorbury shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1906 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 25 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Casper Holloway. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Rosrun maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
