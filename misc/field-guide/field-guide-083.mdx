## At a glance

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of wool bales wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet hammered tin almost perfectly. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 38 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into fired clay pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Habitat and range

Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Jorfell and Vorgate, especially after a still and windless month. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 32 individuals. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Jorfell, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Jorfell, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of tar barrels wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Field notes kept by Sanna Mollering since 1864 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Vorgate. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Jorfell swear it can open a tin of dye cakes. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Vorgate shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and weeks of sideways rain, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Jorfell maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Sanna Mollering. Numbers fell sharply after the 1825 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 36 breeding pairs.
