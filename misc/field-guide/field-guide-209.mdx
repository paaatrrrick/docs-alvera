## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 14 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Rosreach, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Rosreach and Eribury, especially after weeks of sideways rain.

## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of tar barrels wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Rosreach swear it can open a tin of wool bales. Analysis of castings collected near Eribury shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The wardens at Rosreach maintain 9 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Maren Mollering.
