## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Jorvik, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Jorvik and Halvale, especially after a still and windless month. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Jorvik, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 32 individuals.

## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet salt-cured oak almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of tar barrels wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Halvale shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1943 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 41 breeding pairs. The wardens at Jorvik maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Bram Mollering.
