## At a glance

## Behavior

Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet woven reed almost perfectly.

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of wool bales wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. ## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Rosspire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Jormara swear it can open a tin of loom parts. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1859 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 77 breeding pairs. The wardens at Jormara maintain 3 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Edda Marrow. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
