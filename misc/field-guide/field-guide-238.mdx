## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Narford, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Narford, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Narford and Brenshore, especially after the autumn king tides. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 4 to 17 individuals.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Narford swear it can open a tin of loom parts. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Brenshore shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. Numbers fell sharply after the 1877 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 79 breeding pairs. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Beatrix Lindqvist.
