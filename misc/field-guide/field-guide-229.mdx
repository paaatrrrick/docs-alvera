## At a glance

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Tamholm, emerging only on still nights. It nests in cavities worn into rope-fiber composite pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. It favors the brackish margins around Tamholm, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 5 to 30 individuals. ## Behavior

Field notes kept by Casper Severin since 1844 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Wrengate. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of fermented tea bricks wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet salt-cured oak almost perfectly. ## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Wrengate shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Tamholm swear it can open a tin of fermented tea bricks. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a long fog season, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Tamholm maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Casper Severin. Numbers fell sharply after the 1960 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 20 breeding pairs.
