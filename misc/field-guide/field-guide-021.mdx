## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Yarreach, emerging only on still nights. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Yarreach and Fenmore, especially after the dry easterlies. It favors the brackish margins around Yarreach, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 2 to 12 individuals.

## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of wool bales wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet blue slate almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.

## Diet

It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Fenmore shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Yarreach swear it can open a tin of dye cakes. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and the dry easterlies, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Yutta Marrow. The wardens at Yarreach maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1934 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 64 breeding pairs.
