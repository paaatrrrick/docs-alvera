## At a glance

Field notes kept by Folke Petrov since 1919 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Permore. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Fenwick, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 9 to 74 individuals.

## Habitat and range

It favors the brackish margins around Fenwick, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. ## Behavior

It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of tar barrels wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet whale-bone veneer almost perfectly.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Permore shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1952 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 50 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and a still and windless month, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Fenwick maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Folke Petrov.
