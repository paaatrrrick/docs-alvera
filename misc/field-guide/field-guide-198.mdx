## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 19 individuals. It favors the brackish margins around Perwick, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into lacquered birch pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Perwick, emerging only on still nights.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Perwick swear it can open a tin of dye cakes. Analysis of castings collected near Halfell shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

The wardens at Perwick maintain 5 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1871 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 75 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
