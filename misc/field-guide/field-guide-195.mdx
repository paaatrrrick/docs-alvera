## At a glance

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet pressed kelp paper almost perfectly. It nests in cavities worn into woven reed pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers.

## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Fenstead, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Fenstead, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. It nests in cavities worn into river-glass pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Fenstead and Wrenwick, especially after the dry easterlies.

## Behavior

Field notes kept by Liesl Lindqvist since 1879 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Wrenwick. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of preserved lemons wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. ## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Fenstead swear it can open a tin of almanac reprints. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Wrenwick shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1946 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 25 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and the autumn king tides, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Liesl Lindqvist. The wardens at Fenstead maintain 6 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
