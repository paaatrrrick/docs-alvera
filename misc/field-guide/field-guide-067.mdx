## Habitat and range

It nests in cavities worn into blue slate pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 76 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Wrenreach and Torspire, especially after a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Wrenreach, emerging only on still nights. It favors the brackish margins around Wrenreach, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet whale-bone veneer almost perfectly. Field notes kept by Vesna Ashdown since 1847 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Torspire. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of almanac reprints wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.

## Diet

In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Wrenreach swear it can open a tin of loom parts. The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Torspire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails.

## Conservation

Numbers fell sharply after the 1896 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 13 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and an early frost, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Wrenreach maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction.
