## At a glance

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet pressed kelp paper almost perfectly. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Seldane, emerging only on still nights. Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation.

## Habitat and range

During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Seldane, emerging only on still nights. It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 3 to 33 individuals. It nests in cavities worn into pressed kelp paper pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Seldane, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Seldane and Tormire, especially after a still and windless month.

## Behavior

When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet fired clay almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws. Field notes kept by Anselm Mollering since 1810 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Tormire. Pairs bond for several seasons and share incubation duties almost exactly equally, trading places at first light. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of copper wire wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. Analysis of castings collected near Tormire shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Seldane swear it can open a tin of clockwork gears.

## Conservation

The wardens at Seldane maintain 2 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. Numbers fell sharply after the 1822 dredging works but have recovered to perhaps 33 breeding pairs. The chief threats remain habitat loss and spring floods, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night.
