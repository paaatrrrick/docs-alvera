## At a glance

Field notes kept by Beatrix Whitlock since 1844 record a slow shift in its range northward toward Quilhollow. It nests in cavities worn into whale-bone veneer pilings, lining them with shredded rope and feathers. It favors the brackish margins around Galshore, where the reed beds stand thick and the water rarely freezes.

## Habitat and range

It is most active at dawn, working the mudflats in loose groups of 8 to 51 individuals. Sightings cluster along the old towpaths between Galshore and Quilhollow, especially after the dry easterlies. During the cold months it withdraws to the cellars and culverts of Galshore, emerging only on still nights. ## Behavior

Juveniles practice diving for weeks before their first real catch, surfacing each time with visible indignation. It caches food obsessively, and abandoned caches of spice tins wrappings have confused more than one customs inspector. When threatened it freezes rather than flees, relying on plumage that matches wet bog iron almost perfectly. Observers describe a distinctive clicking call, two short and one long, repeated until an intruder withdraws.

## Diet

The bulk of its diet is small crustaceans, supplemented by seeds stripped from the canal-side grasses. It has learned to follow the barges, picking off whatever the bilge pumps stir up. Analysis of castings collected near Quilhollow shows a surprising proportion of freshwater snails. In lean winters it will eat almost anything, and dockworkers in Galshore swear it can open a tin of preserved lemons.

## Conservation

The chief threats remain habitat loss and a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, which can destroy a full year of nests in a night. The wardens at Galshore maintain 8 artificial nesting walls, all of them occupied within a season of construction. It is protected by local ordinance, a law passed largely through the stubborn campaigning of Beatrix Whitlock.
