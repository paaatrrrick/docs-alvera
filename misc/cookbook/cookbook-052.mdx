## About this dish

Every family in Brengate claims their own variant; this one comes from the Tilda Falkner household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Brengate, written down by Tilda Falkner sometime around 1804 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Brengate learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- About 3 handfuls of wool bales, the best you can argue your way into.
- 2 onions, sliced with patience.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- About 4 handfuls of loom parts, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Brengate say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 43 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the fermented tea bricks and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Brengate it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Tilda Falkner suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with wool bales exists in Calbury and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
