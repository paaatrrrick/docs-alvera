## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when wool bales was scarce and cooks in Narspire learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Narspire, written down by Sigrid Falkner sometime around 1881 and argued about ever since. Every family in Narspire claims their own variant; this one comes from the Sigrid Falkner household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- About 5 handfuls of spice tins, the best you can argue your way into.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- A strip of fired clay for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of hammered tin for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Narspire say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 19 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain.

Stir in the almanac reprints and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Sigrid Falkner suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with preserved lemons exists in Lorbury and is, by treaty, never discussed here.

## Serving

A version made with loom parts exists in Lorbury and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Narspire it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
