## About this dish

Every family in Keldane claims their own variant; this one comes from the Liesl Mollering household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Keldane, written down by Liesl Mollering sometime around 1782 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Keldane learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- A strip of hammered tin for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 5 ladles.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Keldane say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 73 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost.

Stir in the spice tins and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 9 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Liesl Mollering suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with loom parts exists in Ilyshore and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Keldane it would be unthinkable without both.
