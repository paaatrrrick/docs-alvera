## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Eriholm, written down by Greta Marchetti sometime around 1802 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Eriholm learned to do without. Every family in Eriholm claims their own variant; this one comes from the Greta Marchetti household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated.

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- 2 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Eriholm say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 9 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Greta Marchetti suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with lamp oil exists in Ilygate and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Eriholm it would be unthinkable without both.
