## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Galcrest, written down by Petra Mollering sometime around 1953 and argued about ever since. Every family in Galcrest claims their own variant; this one comes from the Petra Mollering household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when almanac reprints was scarce and cooks in Galcrest learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- About 8 handfuls of copper wire, the best you can argue your way into.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 6 ladles.
- 6 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 9 handfuls of fermented tea bricks, the best you can argue your way into.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 9 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Galcrest say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 76 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Galcrest it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Petra Mollering suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Delmire and is, by treaty, never discussed here.

## Serving

Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Galcrest it would be unthinkable without both.
