## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Ashmara, written down by Stellan Drummond sometime around 1830 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Ashmara learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- 9 onions, sliced with patience.
- A strip of wax-sealed canvas for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- About 5 handfuls of loom parts, the best you can argue your way into.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 6 ladles.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Ashmara say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 38 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with copper wire exists in Morvale and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Stellan Drummond suggests any firm fish and an apology.
