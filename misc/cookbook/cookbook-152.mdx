## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Lorbury, written down by Bram Petrov sometime around 1879 and argued about ever since. Every family in Lorbury claims their own variant; this one comes from the Bram Petrov household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- About 5 handfuls of fermented tea bricks, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 5 ladles.
- A strip of salt-cured oak for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 8 handfuls of almanac reprints, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Lorbury say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the almanac reprints and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Bram Petrov suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Lorbury it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with glass floats exists in Halvale and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
