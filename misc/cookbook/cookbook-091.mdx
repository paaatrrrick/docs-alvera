## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Narford, written down by Tomas Sorvane sometime around 1936 and argued about ever since. Every family in Narford claims their own variant; this one comes from the Tomas Sorvane household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Narford learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 7 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 6 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Narford say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 69 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with lamp oil exists in Lorhollow and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Narford it would be unthinkable without both.
