## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. Every family in Rosshore claims their own variant; this one comes from the Vesna Holloway household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when glass floats was scarce and cooks in Rosshore learned to do without.

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 4 ladles.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Rosshore say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the spice tins and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Rosshore it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Vesna Holloway suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with clockwork gears exists in Ilyton and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
