## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Delhollow, written down by Petra Vexley sometime around 1942 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, and improves when reheated. Every family in Delhollow claims their own variant; this one comes from the Petra Vexley household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- About 6 handfuls of dye cakes, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of salt-cured oak for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 9 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 4 handfuls of loom parts, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 21 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Stir in the fermented tea bricks and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Delhollow say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. ## Notes from the galley

A version made with wool bales exists in Rosreach and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Delhollow it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Petra Vexley suggests any firm fish and an apology.
