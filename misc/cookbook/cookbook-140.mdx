## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Galmire, written down by Oona Vexley sometime around 1860 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Galmire learned to do without. Every family in Galmire claims their own variant; this one comes from the Oona Vexley household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- A strip of woven reed for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of salt-cured oak for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 4 handfuls of lamp oil, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 70 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Galmire say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. ## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Galmire it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with dye cakes exists in Lorgate and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
