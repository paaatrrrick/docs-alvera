## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Selvale, written down by Dario Brandt sometime around 1785 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when glass floats was scarce and cooks in Selvale learned to do without.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 78 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Selvale say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Dario Brandt suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Selvale it would be unthinkable without both.
