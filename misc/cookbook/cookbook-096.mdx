## About this dish

Every family in Tamton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Sanna Severin household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Tamton, written down by Sanna Severin sometime around 1818 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Tamton learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- About 4 handfuls of clockwork gears, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of blue slate for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- About 7 handfuls of dye cakes, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of rope-fiber composite for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 61 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the glass floats and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Sanna Severin suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Tamton it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with almanac reprints exists in Marholm and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
