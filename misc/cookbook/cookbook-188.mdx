## About this dish

Every family in Velton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Maren Kessler household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Velton learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- About 2 handfuls of fermented tea bricks, the best you can argue your way into.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 3 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Stir in the spice tins and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 59 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Velton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Velton it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Vorholm and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.

## Serving

A version made with almanac reprints exists in Vorholm and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Maren Kessler suggests any firm fish and an apology.
