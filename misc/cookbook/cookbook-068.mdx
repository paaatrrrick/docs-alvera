## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Corbury, written down by Maren Brandt sometime around 1873 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Corbury learned to do without. Every family in Corbury claims their own variant; this one comes from the Maren Brandt household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- About 7 handfuls of preserved lemons, the best you can argue your way into.
- About 7 handfuls of clockwork gears, the best you can argue your way into.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- 5 onions, sliced with patience.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Corbury say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the fermented tea bricks and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. ## Notes from the galley

A version made with wool bales exists in Vorgate and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Corbury it would be unthinkable without both.
