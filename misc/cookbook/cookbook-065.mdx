## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Yarford learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Yarford, written down by Lazlo Petrov sometime around 1801 and argued about ever since. Every family in Yarford claims their own variant; this one comes from the Lazlo Petrov household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 4 onions, sliced with patience.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 6 ladles.

## Method

Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Yarford say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Yarford it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Lazlo Petrov suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
