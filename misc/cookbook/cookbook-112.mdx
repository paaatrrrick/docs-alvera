## About this dish

Every family in Yarshore claims their own variant; this one comes from the Mireille Ashdown household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Yarshore learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Yarshore, written down by Mireille Ashdown sometime around 1923 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- About 3 handfuls of almanac reprints, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 7 ladles.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 9 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 65 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the clockwork gears and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Mireille Ashdown suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Yarshore it would be unthinkable without both.
