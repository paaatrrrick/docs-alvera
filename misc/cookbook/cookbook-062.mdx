## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when clockwork gears was scarce and cooks in Marmire learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Marmire, written down by Oona Whitlock sometime around 1916 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- A strip of hammered tin for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 5 onions, sliced with patience.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 7 ladles.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 29 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Marmire say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Marmire it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with wool bales exists in Lorfell and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Oona Whitlock suggests any firm fish and an apology.

## Serving

Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Oona Whitlock suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with preserved lemons exists in Lorfell and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
