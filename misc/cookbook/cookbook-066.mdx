## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Brenwick learned to do without. Every family in Brenwick claims their own variant; this one comes from the Liesl Vexley household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Brenwick, written down by Liesl Vexley sometime around 1844 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 6 ladles.
- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 7 handfuls of dye cakes, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Stir in the clockwork gears and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 35 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides.

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Brenwick say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Brenwick it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Liesl Vexley suggests any firm fish and an apology.
