## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Fenwick learned to do without. Every family in Fenwick claims their own variant; this one comes from the Bram Drummond household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Fenwick, written down by Bram Drummond sometime around 1836 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- About 9 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of pressed kelp paper for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 2 handfuls of fermented tea bricks, the best you can argue your way into.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 9 ladles.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Fenwick say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the tar barrels and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Fenwick it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with spice tins exists in Narholm and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Bram Drummond suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
