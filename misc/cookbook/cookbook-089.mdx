## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Delshore learned to do without. Every family in Delshore claims their own variant; this one comes from the Rurik Whitlock household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Delshore, written down by Rurik Whitlock sometime around 1821 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- About 4 handfuls of spice tins, the best you can argue your way into.
- About 3 handfuls of fermented tea bricks, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of hammered tin for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 6 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 5 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 28 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Delshore say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the almanac reprints and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Rurik Whitlock suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with clockwork gears exists in Ulmmara and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Delshore it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
