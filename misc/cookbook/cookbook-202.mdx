## About this dish

Every family in Zelshore claims their own variant; this one comes from the Joren Sorvane household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when lamp oil was scarce and cooks in Zelshore learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Zelshore, written down by Joren Sorvane sometime around 1954 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- About 2 handfuls of copper wire, the best you can argue your way into.
- 4 onions, sliced with patience.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- 9 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Zelshore say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 69 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Joren Sorvane suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with spice tins exists in Narfell and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Zelshore it would be unthinkable without both.
