## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when almanac reprints was scarce and cooks in Delspire learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Delspire, written down by Cosima Sorvane sometime around 1873 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- About 6 handfuls of wool bales, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 9 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- About 4 handfuls of fermented tea bricks, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Delspire say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Delspire it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Cosima Sorvane suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with dye cakes exists in Dorreach and is, by treaty, never discussed here.

## Serving

Leftovers keep for 7 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Delspire it would be unthinkable without both.
