## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Perreach learned to do without. Every family in Perreach claims their own variant; this one comes from the Maren Marrow household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Perreach, written down by Maren Marrow sometime around 1853 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.
- A strip of woven reed for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 5 handfuls of dye cakes, the best you can argue your way into.
- 8 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Perreach say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the lamp oil and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

Cover and leave it alone for 61 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. ## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Perreach it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with spice tins exists in Quilton and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Maren Marrow suggests any firm fish and an apology.
