## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Narshore learned to do without. Every family in Narshore claims their own variant; this one comes from the Bram Lindqvist household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Narshore, written down by Bram Lindqvist sometime around 1891 and argued about ever since.

## Method

Stir in the lamp oil and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Narshore say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with glass floats exists in Velhollow and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Narshore it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.

## Serving

A version made with copper wire exists in Velhollow and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Bram Lindqvist suggests any firm fish and an apology.
