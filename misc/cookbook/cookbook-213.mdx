## About this dish

Every family in Quilrun claims their own variant; this one comes from the Joren Kessler household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Quilrun, written down by Joren Kessler sometime around 1815 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Quilrun learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- About 6 handfuls of wool bales, the best you can argue your way into.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 30 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Quilrun say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Joren Kessler suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with glass floats exists in Ossholm and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Quilrun it would be unthinkable without both.
