## About this dish

Every family in Halgate claims their own variant; this one comes from the Dario Sorvane household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when almanac reprints was scarce and cooks in Halgate learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Halgate, written down by Dario Sorvane sometime around 1931 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 68 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Dario Sorvane suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with dye cakes exists in Zelreach and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Halgate it would be unthinkable without both.
