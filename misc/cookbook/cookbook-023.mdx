## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Halvale, written down by Sigrid Greaves sometime around 1903 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Halvale learned to do without.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Halvale say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 5 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the dye cakes and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 7 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with clockwork gears exists in Rosmore and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Sigrid Greaves suggests any firm fish and an apology.
