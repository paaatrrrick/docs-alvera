## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Lorwick learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. Every family in Lorwick claims their own variant; this one comes from the Folke Severin household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Lorwick, written down by Folke Severin sometime around 1808 and argued about ever since.

## Method

Stir in the almanac reprints and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 43 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Folke Severin suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with copper wire exists in Percrest and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Lorwick it would be unthinkable without both.
