## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Galspire learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Galspire, written down by Yutta Sorvane sometime around 1951 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 7 ladles.
- 4 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 49 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Galspire say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

Cover and leave it alone for 30 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 5 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with loom parts exists in Tamdane and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Yutta Sorvane suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Galspire it would be unthinkable without both.
