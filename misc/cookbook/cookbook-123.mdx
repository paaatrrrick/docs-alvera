## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Halton, written down by Liesl Petrov sometime around 1930 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Halton learned to do without. Every family in Halton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Liesl Petrov household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, and improves when reheated.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 67 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Halton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with dye cakes exists in Rosspire and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Halton it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Liesl Petrov suggests any firm fish and an apology.
