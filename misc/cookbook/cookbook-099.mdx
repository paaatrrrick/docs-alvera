## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, and improves when reheated. Every family in Wrenvik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Ivo Kessler household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when preserved lemons was scarce and cooks in Wrenvik learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Wrenvik, written down by Ivo Kessler sometime around 1942 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.
- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 5 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the tar barrels and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Wrenvik say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with tar barrels exists in Wrenvik and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Ivo Kessler suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.

## Serving

A version made with wool bales exists in Wrenvik and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Ivo Kessler suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Wrenvik it would be unthinkable without both.
