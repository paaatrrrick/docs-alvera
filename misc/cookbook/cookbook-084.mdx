## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Ulmton, written down by Joren Whitlock sometime around 1852 and argued about ever since. Every family in Ulmton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Joren Whitlock household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when clockwork gears was scarce and cooks in Ulmton learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 5 ladles.
- 5 onions, sliced with patience.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- A strip of wax-sealed canvas for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Stir in the spice tins and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Ulmton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. ## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Ulmton it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Joren Whitlock suggests any firm fish and an apology.
