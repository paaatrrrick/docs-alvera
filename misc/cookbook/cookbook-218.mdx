## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Ilyton, written down by Liesl Mollering sometime around 1786 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when lamp oil was scarce and cooks in Ilyton learned to do without. Every family in Ilyton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Liesl Mollering household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- About 9 handfuls of lamp oil, the best you can argue your way into.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.
- A strip of pressed kelp paper for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 4 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Ilyton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

Cover and leave it alone for 39 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Ilyton it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Liesl Mollering suggests any firm fish and an apology.
