## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated. Every family in Halvik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Cosima Falkner household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Halvik, written down by Cosima Falkner sometime around 1916 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Halvik learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 6 ladles.
- About 8 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.
- 8 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 2 handfuls of lamp oil, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Halvik say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 52 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes.

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 60 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Cosima Falkner suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with almanac reprints exists in Delwick and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Halvik it would be unthinkable without both.
