## About this dish

Every family in Rosgate claims their own variant; this one comes from the Casper Halloran household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Rosgate, written down by Casper Halloran sometime around 1785 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Rosgate learned to do without.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Rosgate say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 36 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Casper Halloran suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with loom parts exists in Keldane and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Rosgate it would be unthinkable without both.
