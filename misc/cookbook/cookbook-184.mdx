## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Pervik learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Pervik, written down by Otto Voss sometime around 1876 and argued about ever since. Every family in Pervik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Otto Voss household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- About 5 handfuls of clockwork gears, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- About 6 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of pressed kelp paper for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Cover and leave it alone for 56 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Stir in the fermented tea bricks and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Pervik say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the dye cakes and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Otto Voss suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with dye cakes exists in Jormire and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Pervik it would be unthinkable without both.
