## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. Every family in Ossbury claims their own variant; this one comes from the Joren Drummond household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Ossbury, written down by Joren Drummond sometime around 1906 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- About 7 handfuls of almanac reprints, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of hammered tin for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 7 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Ossbury say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Cover and leave it alone for 17 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes.

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Ossbury it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with tar barrels exists in Marholm and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Joren Drummond suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
