## About this dish

Every family in Halbury claims their own variant; this one comes from the Folke Holloway household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Halbury, written down by Folke Holloway sometime around 1946 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 9 ladles.
- A strip of bog iron for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of fired clay for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 4 ladles.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 6 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Halbury say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the glass floats and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with spice tins exists in Halwick and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Halbury it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Folke Holloway suggests any firm fish and an apology.

## Serving

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Halbury it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Folke Holloway suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
