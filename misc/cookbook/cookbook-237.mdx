## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Kelreach, written down by Cosima Ashdown sometime around 1948 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Kelreach learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- 5 onions, sliced with patience.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 5 ladles.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Kelreach say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 16 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain.

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Cover and leave it alone for 21 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. Stir in the clockwork gears and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Kelreach it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Cosima Ashdown suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
