## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Yarmore, written down by Rurik Whitlock sometime around 1939 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Yarmore learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- 3 onions, sliced with patience.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 4 ladles.
- A strip of hammered tin for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of fired clay for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the wool bales and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Yarmore say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. ## Notes from the galley

A version made with tar barrels exists in Erirun and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Yarmore it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.

## Serving

A version made with loom parts exists in Erirun and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Yarmore it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Rurik Whitlock suggests any firm fish and an apology.
