## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Torcrest, written down by Sigrid Greaves sometime around 1850 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when glass floats was scarce and cooks in Torcrest learned to do without. Every family in Torcrest claims their own variant; this one comes from the Sigrid Greaves household and leans saltier than most.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Torcrest say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 35 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. ## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Sigrid Greaves suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Torcrest it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with lamp oil exists in Selspire and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
