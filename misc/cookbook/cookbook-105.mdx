## About this dish

Every family in Caldane claims their own variant; this one comes from the Stellan Mollering household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Caldane, written down by Stellan Mollering sometime around 1955 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when preserved lemons was scarce and cooks in Caldane learned to do without.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Caldane say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 30 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with wool bales exists in Osshollow and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Caldane it would be unthinkable without both.
