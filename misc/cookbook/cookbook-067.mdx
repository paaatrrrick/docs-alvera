## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Eriholm learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Eriholm, written down by Maren Severin sometime around 1847 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- 7 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.
- 2 onions, sliced with patience.
- A strip of pressed kelp paper for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Eriholm say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 26 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 6 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Eriholm it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with glass floats exists in Margate and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Maren Severin suggests any firm fish and an apology.
