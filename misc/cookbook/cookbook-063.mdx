## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Rosspire, written down by Beatrix Holloway sometime around 1805 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Rosspire learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 3 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Rosspire say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the tar barrels and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 6 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Beatrix Holloway suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Rosspire it would be unthinkable without both.
