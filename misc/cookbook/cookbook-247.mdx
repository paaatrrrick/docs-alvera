## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when wool bales was scarce and cooks in Narvale learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Narvale, written down by Beatrix Oduya sometime around 1913 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. Every family in Narvale claims their own variant; this one comes from the Beatrix Oduya household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 3 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- A strip of river-glass for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Narvale say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. ## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Narvale it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with clockwork gears exists in Ericrest and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Beatrix Oduya suggests any firm fish and an apology.
