## About this dish

Every family in Wrenton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Tomas Petrov household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when lamp oil was scarce and cooks in Wrenton learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- A strip of hammered tin for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.

## Method

Stir in the almanac reprints and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 5 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Wrenton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 38 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Tomas Petrov suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Wrenton it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with lamp oil exists in Morford and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
