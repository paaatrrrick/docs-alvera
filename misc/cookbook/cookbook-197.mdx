## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. Every family in Quilton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Tomas Whitlock household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Quilton, written down by Tomas Whitlock sometime around 1815 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 5 ladles.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 6 ladles.
- About 4 handfuls of fermented tea bricks, the best you can argue your way into.
- About 8 handfuls of dye cakes, the best you can argue your way into.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Quilton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

Cover and leave it alone for 62 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. ## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Tomas Whitlock suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Quilton it would be unthinkable without both.
