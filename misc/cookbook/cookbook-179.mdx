## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when lamp oil was scarce and cooks in Percrest learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Percrest, written down by Anselm Lindqvist sometime around 1801 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- About 6 handfuls of almanac reprints, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of fired clay for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of river-glass for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of pressed kelp paper for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Stir in the almanac reprints and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Anselm Lindqvist suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Percrest it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with almanac reprints exists in Ilyreach and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
