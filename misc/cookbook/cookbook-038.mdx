## About this dish

Every family in Ashmara claims their own variant; this one comes from the Sigrid Kessler household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Ashmara, written down by Sigrid Kessler sometime around 1823 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Ashmara learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- 9 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 6 ladles.
- About 3 handfuls of copper wire, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 5 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 5 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Ashmara say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the fermented tea bricks and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 85 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with spice tins exists in Kelshore and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Sigrid Kessler suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 7 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Ashmara it would be unthinkable without both.
