## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when clockwork gears was scarce and cooks in Ashbury learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Ashbury, written down by Stellan Whitlock sometime around 1912 and argued about ever since. Every family in Ashbury claims their own variant; this one comes from the Stellan Whitlock household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- 7 onions, sliced with patience.
- 5 onions, sliced with patience.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 7 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Ashbury say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. ## Notes from the galley

A version made with loom parts exists in Corbury and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Ashbury it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Stellan Whitlock suggests any firm fish and an apology.

## Serving

A version made with wool bales exists in Corbury and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Ashbury it would be unthinkable without both.
