## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Corvale, written down by Oona Lindqvist sometime around 1828 and argued about ever since. Every family in Corvale claims their own variant; this one comes from the Oona Lindqvist household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- 6 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 5 handfuls of preserved lemons, the best you can argue your way into.
- 4 onions, sliced with patience.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 7 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 13 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Corvale say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the wool bales and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Oona Lindqvist suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with almanac reprints exists in Wrenmore and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Corvale it would be unthinkable without both.
