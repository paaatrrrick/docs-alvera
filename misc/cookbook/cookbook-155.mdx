## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Lorcrest learned to do without. Every family in Lorcrest claims their own variant; this one comes from the Casper Severin household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Lorcrest, written down by Casper Severin sometime around 1872 and argued about ever since.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Stir in the lamp oil and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 41 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Delshore and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Casper Severin suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Lorcrest it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
