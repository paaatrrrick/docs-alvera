## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when lamp oil was scarce and cooks in Zelvik learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Zelvik, written down by Petra Marrow sometime around 1897 and argued about ever since. Every family in Zelvik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Petra Marrow household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 7 ladles.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Zelvik say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the wool bales and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 13 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Petra Marrow suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with clockwork gears exists in Lorwick and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Zelvik it would be unthinkable without both.
