## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. Every family in Tamhollow claims their own variant; this one comes from the Casper Whitlock household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Tamhollow, written down by Casper Whitlock sometime around 1896 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when glass floats was scarce and cooks in Tamhollow learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- A strip of hammered tin for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 4 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 9 ladles.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 3 ladles.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Tamhollow say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 82 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the almanac reprints and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Tamhollow it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with almanac reprints exists in Halrun and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Casper Whitlock suggests any firm fish and an apology.
