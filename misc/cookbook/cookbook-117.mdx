## About this dish

Every family in Halton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Petra Oduya household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Halton, written down by Petra Oduya sometime around 1893 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- About 9 handfuls of preserved lemons, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of wax-sealed canvas for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 50 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 5 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. ## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Halton it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with clockwork gears exists in Marspire and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Petra Oduya suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
