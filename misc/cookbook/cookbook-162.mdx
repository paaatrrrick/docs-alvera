## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Vorhollow, written down by Cosima Halloran sometime around 1896 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Vorhollow learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A strip of salt-cured oak for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 8 handfuls of dye cakes, the best you can argue your way into.
- About 3 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.
- 9 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 42 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Vorhollow say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Ashdane and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Vorhollow it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
