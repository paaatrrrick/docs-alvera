## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Ossfell, written down by Emeric Kessler sometime around 1788 and argued about ever since. Every family in Ossfell claims their own variant; this one comes from the Emeric Kessler household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when fermented tea bricks was scarce and cooks in Ossfell learned to do without.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Ossfell say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Ossfell it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Brenbury and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Emeric Kessler suggests any firm fish and an apology.

## Serving

A version made with dye cakes exists in Brenbury and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Emeric Kessler suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
