## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Delholm learned to do without. Every family in Delholm claims their own variant; this one comes from the Rurik Holloway household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 45 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Delholm say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes.

Stir in the fermented tea bricks and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 29 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Rurik Holloway suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Delholm it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with dye cakes exists in Correach and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
