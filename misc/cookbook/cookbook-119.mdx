## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Perton learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Perton, written down by Anselm Holloway sometime around 1784 and argued about ever since. Every family in Perton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Anselm Holloway household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, and improves when reheated.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 53 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Perton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Perton it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with spice tins exists in Ilydane and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.

## Serving

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Perton it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Anselm Holloway suggests any firm fish and an apology.
