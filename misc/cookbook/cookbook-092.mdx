## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Rosvale, written down by Anselm Marrow sometime around 1950 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Rosvale learned to do without. Every family in Rosvale claims their own variant; this one comes from the Anselm Marrow household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.
- About 8 handfuls of spice tins, the best you can argue your way into.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 3 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Rosvale say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 41 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Rosvale it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Anselm Marrow suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with dye cakes exists in Ilydane and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
