## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Morhollow, written down by Sanna Severin sometime around 1881 and argued about ever since. Every family in Morhollow claims their own variant; this one comes from the Sanna Severin household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when wool bales was scarce and cooks in Morhollow learned to do without.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 40 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Stir in the spice tins and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Morhollow say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Sanna Severin suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Morhollow it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with lamp oil exists in Zelbury and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
