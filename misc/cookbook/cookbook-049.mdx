## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Dorton learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. Every family in Dorton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Tilda Vexley household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Dorton, written down by Tilda Vexley sometime around 1925 and argued about ever since.

- 2 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 5 ladles.

- A strip of wax-sealed canvas for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 5 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Dorton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 89 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with almanac reprints exists in Zelwick and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Dorton it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Tilda Vexley suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
