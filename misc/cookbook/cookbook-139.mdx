## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Halfell, written down by Lazlo Drummond sometime around 1837 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Halfell learned to do without. Every family in Halfell claims their own variant; this one comes from the Lazlo Drummond household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- About 2 handfuls of clockwork gears, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of bog iron for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 7 handfuls of lamp oil, the best you can argue your way into.
- About 7 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Halfell say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 75 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. ## Notes from the galley

A version made with lamp oil exists in Tormire and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Halfell it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Lazlo Drummond suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 7 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
