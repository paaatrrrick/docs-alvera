## About this dish

Every family in Zelton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Maren Severin household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when almanac reprints was scarce and cooks in Zelton learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 6 ladles.
- 5 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 2 handfuls of wool bales, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 72 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Zelton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 36 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Maren Severin suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Zelton it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
