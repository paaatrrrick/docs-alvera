## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Vormara, written down by Annik Whitlock sometime around 1810 and argued about ever since. Every family in Vormara claims their own variant; this one comes from the Annik Whitlock household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when wool bales was scarce and cooks in Vormara learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.
- 4 onions, sliced with patience.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- 9 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Vormara say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 31 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. ## Notes from the galley

A version made with loom parts exists in Halton and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Annik Whitlock suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Vormara it would be unthinkable without both.
