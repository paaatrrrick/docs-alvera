## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Vordane, written down by Bram Falkner sometime around 1920 and argued about ever since. Every family in Vordane claims their own variant; this one comes from the Bram Falkner household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Vordane learned to do without.

## Method

Stir in the glass floats and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 44 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Vordane say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 7 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with lamp oil exists in Brenbury and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Vordane it would be unthinkable without both.
