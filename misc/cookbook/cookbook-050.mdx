## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Perreach, written down by Stellan Marrow sometime around 1919 and argued about ever since. Every family in Perreach claims their own variant; this one comes from the Stellan Marrow household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when fermented tea bricks was scarce and cooks in Perreach learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- 9 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 3 handfuls of dye cakes, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of wax-sealed canvas for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the fermented tea bricks and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Perreach say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 5 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 42 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Stellan Marrow suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Perreach it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with dye cakes exists in Zelvale and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
