## About this dish

Every family in Marcrest claims their own variant; this one comes from the Emeric Lindqvist household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Marcrest, written down by Emeric Lindqvist sometime around 1872 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Marcrest learned to do without.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 70 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Marcrest say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with glass floats exists in Velgate and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Marcrest it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Emeric Lindqvist suggests any firm fish and an apology.
