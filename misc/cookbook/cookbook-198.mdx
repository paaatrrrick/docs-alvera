## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. Every family in Halcrest claims their own variant; this one comes from the Sanna Severin household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when almanac reprints was scarce and cooks in Halcrest learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- A strip of blue slate for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of bog iron for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 6 ladles.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Halcrest say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 21 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Halcrest it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Sanna Severin suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
