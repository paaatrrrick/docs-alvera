## About this dish

Every family in Lordane claims their own variant; this one comes from the Ivo Mollering household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when clockwork gears was scarce and cooks in Lordane learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Lordane, written down by Ivo Mollering sometime around 1906 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 2 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 6 ladles.
- A strip of woven reed for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Lordane say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 28 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. Stir in the fermented tea bricks and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes.

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Cover and leave it alone for 63 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with tar barrels exists in Mormara and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Lordane it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Ivo Mollering suggests any firm fish and an apology.

## Serving

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Lordane it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
