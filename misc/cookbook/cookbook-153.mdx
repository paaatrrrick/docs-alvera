## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Morvale, written down by Oona Mollering sometime around 1860 and argued about ever since. Every family in Morvale claims their own variant; this one comes from the Oona Mollering household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- About 7 handfuls of spice tins, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 5 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Morvale say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 85 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with dye cakes exists in Rosvik and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Morvale it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Oona Mollering suggests any firm fish and an apology.
