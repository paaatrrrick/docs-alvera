## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Morreach, written down by Tomas Vexley sometime around 1916 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. Every family in Morreach claims their own variant; this one comes from the Tomas Vexley household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Morreach learned to do without.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Cover and leave it alone for 32 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Tomas Vexley suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Morreach it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with lamp oil exists in Vorholm and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
