## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Zelreach, written down by Joren Kessler sometime around 1963 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when glass floats was scarce and cooks in Zelreach learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- 8 onions, sliced with patience.
- A strip of river-glass for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of salt-cured oak for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 30 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with glass floats exists in Yarton and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Joren Kessler suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Zelreach it would be unthinkable without both.
