## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. Every family in Wrenfell claims their own variant; this one comes from the Mireille Voss household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Wrenfell, written down by Mireille Voss sometime around 1902 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when almanac reprints was scarce and cooks in Wrenfell learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 7 ladles.
- About 4 handfuls of loom parts, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 2 onions, sliced with patience.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Stir in the almanac reprints and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Cover and leave it alone for 21 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the dye cakes and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Wrenfell say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 32 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Mireille Voss suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Wrenfell it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with copper wire exists in Zelgate and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
