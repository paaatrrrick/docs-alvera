## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Marcrest learned to do without. Every family in Marcrest claims their own variant; this one comes from the Petra Lindqvist household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- A strip of bog iron for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of river-glass for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 4 ladles.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the tar barrels and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 5 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 70 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Petra Lindqvist suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with glass floats exists in Halfell and is, by treaty, never discussed here.

## Serving

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Marcrest it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with lamp oil exists in Halfell and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
