## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Perstead, written down by Imre Oduya sometime around 1899 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Perstead learned to do without. Every family in Perstead claims their own variant; this one comes from the Imre Oduya household and leans saltier than most.

## Method

Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Perstead say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Cover and leave it alone for 47 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with almanac reprints exists in Jorhollow and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Perstead it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Imre Oduya suggests any firm fish and an apology.
