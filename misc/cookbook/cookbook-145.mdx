## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Yarvik, written down by Ivo Greaves sometime around 1804 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when fermented tea bricks was scarce and cooks in Yarvik learned to do without. Every family in Yarvik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Ivo Greaves household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- A strip of blue slate for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 5 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A strip of bog iron for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 9 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the clockwork gears and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 87 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Selreach and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Yarvik it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Ivo Greaves suggests any firm fish and an apology.
