## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Galbury learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Galbury, written down by Beatrix Mollering sometime around 1785 and argued about ever since. Every family in Galbury claims their own variant; this one comes from the Beatrix Mollering household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- About 7 handfuls of almanac reprints, the best you can argue your way into.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Galbury say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the wool bales and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 34 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with clockwork gears exists in Zelstead and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Beatrix Mollering suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Galbury it would be unthinkable without both.
