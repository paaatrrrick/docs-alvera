## About this dish

Every family in Selwick claims their own variant; this one comes from the Stellan Brandt household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Selwick, written down by Stellan Brandt sometime around 1870 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Selwick learned to do without.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 35 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Selwick say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Selwick it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with tar barrels exists in Torton and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Stellan Brandt suggests any firm fish and an apology.
