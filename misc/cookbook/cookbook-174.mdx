## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Corwick, written down by Liesl Marrow sometime around 1802 and argued about ever since. Every family in Corwick claims their own variant; this one comes from the Liesl Marrow household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Corwick learned to do without.

## Method

Stir in the dye cakes and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 52 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Corwick say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with preserved lemons exists in Calholm and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Liesl Marrow suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Corwick it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
