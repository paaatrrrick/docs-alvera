## About this dish

Every family in Torgate claims their own variant; this one comes from the Petra Ashdown household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when almanac reprints was scarce and cooks in Torgate learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Torgate, written down by Petra Ashdown sometime around 1930 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- A strip of river-glass for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of whale-bone veneer for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 6 handfuls of preserved lemons, the best you can argue your way into.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Torgate say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 59 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Petra Ashdown suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Torgate it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Quilbury and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
