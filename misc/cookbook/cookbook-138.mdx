## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Cormore, written down by Ivo Greaves sometime around 1795 and argued about ever since. Every family in Cormore claims their own variant; this one comes from the Ivo Greaves household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Cormore learned to do without.

## Method

Stir in the fermented tea bricks and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 30 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Ivo Greaves suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Cormore it would be unthinkable without both.
