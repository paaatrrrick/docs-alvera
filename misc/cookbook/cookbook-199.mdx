## About this dish

Every family in Torwick claims their own variant; this one comes from the Maren Vexley household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Torwick learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Torwick, written down by Maren Vexley sometime around 1792 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- A strip of river-glass for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- 6 onions, sliced with patience.
- A strip of woven reed for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 4 ladles.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 86 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the clockwork gears and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Torwick say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes. Stir in the glass floats and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 87 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Maren Vexley suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with clockwork gears exists in Velmire and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Torwick it would be unthinkable without both.
