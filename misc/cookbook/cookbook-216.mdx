## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when fermented tea bricks was scarce and cooks in Yarmore learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Yarmore, written down by Mireille Mollering sometime around 1951 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.
- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Yarmore say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the lamp oil and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 54 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with dye cakes exists in Ashmara and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Yarmore it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Mireille Mollering suggests any firm fish and an apology.
