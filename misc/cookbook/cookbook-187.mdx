## About this dish

Every family in Galstead claims their own variant; this one comes from the Liesl Vexley household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Galstead, written down by Liesl Vexley sometime around 1923 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- 3 onions, sliced with patience.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A strip of fired clay for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 4 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Galstead say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 5 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 20 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost.

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Galstead say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 70 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Galstead it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Liesl Vexley suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with wool bales exists in Torholm and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
