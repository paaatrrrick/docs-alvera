## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Dorgate learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Dorgate, written down by Mireille Vexley sometime around 1909 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- 4 onions, sliced with patience.
- A strip of hammered tin for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of salt-cured oak for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of wax-sealed canvas for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Stir in the wool bales and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 52 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 63 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 5 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Dorgate say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Mireille Vexley suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Dorgate it would be unthinkable without both.

## Serving

Leftovers keep for 7 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with dye cakes exists in Marton and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
