## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Kelmire, written down by Otto Marrow sometime around 1819 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. Every family in Kelmire claims their own variant; this one comes from the Otto Marrow household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- 2 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 7 ladles.
- About 2 handfuls of wool bales, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 2 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Cover and leave it alone for 51 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Kelmire say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the tar barrels and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Otto Marrow suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with spice tins exists in Jorcrest and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.

## Serving

Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Kelmire it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Otto Marrow suggests any firm fish and an apology.
