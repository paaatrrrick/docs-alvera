## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Deldane learned to do without. Every family in Deldane claims their own variant; this one comes from the Greta Brandt household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Deldane, written down by Greta Brandt sometime around 1792 and argued about ever since.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 78 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Deldane say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Greta Brandt suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Deldane it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with clockwork gears exists in Brenmore and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
