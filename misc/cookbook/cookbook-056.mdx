## About this dish

Every family in Galvik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Imre Drummond household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Galvik, written down by Imre Drummond sometime around 1896 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when almanac reprints was scarce and cooks in Galvik learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated.

- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Galvik say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 80 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. ## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Imre Drummond suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with glass floats exists in Selmire and is, by treaty, never discussed here.

## Serving

Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Galvik it would be unthinkable without both.
