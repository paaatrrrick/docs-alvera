## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Lorspire, written down by Joren Mollering sometime around 1913 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when clockwork gears was scarce and cooks in Lorspire learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. Every family in Lorspire claims their own variant; this one comes from the Joren Mollering household and leans saltier than most.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Lorspire say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the glass floats and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with almanac reprints exists in Torton and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Joren Mollering suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Lorspire it would be unthinkable without both.
