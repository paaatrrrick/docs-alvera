## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when glass floats was scarce and cooks in Delbury learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Delbury, written down by Annik Marchetti sometime around 1913 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- About 2 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of river-glass for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 4 ladles.

## Method

Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Delbury say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes.

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Annik Marchetti suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Delbury it would be unthinkable without both.
