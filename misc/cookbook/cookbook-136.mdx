## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Jorvik, written down by Ivo Kessler sometime around 1941 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. Every family in Jorvik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Ivo Kessler household and leans saltier than most.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 5 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 84 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Jorvik say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Vorvik and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Ivo Kessler suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Jorvik it would be unthinkable without both.
