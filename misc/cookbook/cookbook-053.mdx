## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Osswick, written down by Petra Lindqvist sometime around 1878 and argued about ever since. Every family in Osswick claims their own variant; this one comes from the Petra Lindqvist household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when fermented tea bricks was scarce and cooks in Osswick learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- 6 onions, sliced with patience.
- 8 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 9 ladles.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the tar barrels and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 58 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Osswick say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Osswick it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with almanac reprints exists in Rosreach and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Petra Lindqvist suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
