## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Narbury, written down by Mireille Voss sometime around 1873 and argued about ever since. Every family in Narbury claims their own variant; this one comes from the Mireille Voss household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Narbury learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- A strip of wax-sealed canvas for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- About 8 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Narbury say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 89 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Stir in the wool bales and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Mireille Voss suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with tar barrels exists in Galmara and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Narbury it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
