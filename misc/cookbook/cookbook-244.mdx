## About this dish

Every family in Erimore claims their own variant; this one comes from the Sanna Whitlock household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Erimore, written down by Sanna Whitlock sometime around 1797 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when wool bales was scarce and cooks in Erimore learned to do without.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Erimore say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Stir in the tar barrels and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 73 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with dye cakes exists in Velholm and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Sanna Whitlock suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Erimore it would be unthinkable without both.
