## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Osscrest learned to do without. Every family in Osscrest claims their own variant; this one comes from the Imre Drummond household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- 6 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 7 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- A strip of fired clay for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Osscrest say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 48 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with lamp oil exists in Corstead and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Osscrest it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Imre Drummond suggests any firm fish and an apology.

## Serving

Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Imre Drummond suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with preserved lemons exists in Corstead and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
