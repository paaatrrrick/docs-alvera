## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Selspire learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Selspire, written down by Halvard Marrow sometime around 1848 and argued about ever since. Every family in Selspire claims their own variant; this one comes from the Halvard Marrow household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- About 2 handfuls of dye cakes, the best you can argue your way into.
- 6 onions, sliced with patience.
- A strip of whale-bone veneer for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 8 onions, sliced with patience.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Selspire say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Halvard Marrow suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Selspire it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
