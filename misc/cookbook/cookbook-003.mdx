## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Yarhollow learned to do without. Every family in Yarhollow claims their own variant; this one comes from the Tomas Greaves household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Yarhollow, written down by Tomas Greaves sometime around 1898 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- A strip of fired clay for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 5 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

## Method

Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 53 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Yarhollow say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Yarhollow it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Tomas Greaves suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
