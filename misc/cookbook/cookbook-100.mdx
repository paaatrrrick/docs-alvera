## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. Every family in Morton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Joren Kessler household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Morton, written down by Joren Kessler sometime around 1884 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Morton learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- 4 onions, sliced with patience.
- 9 onions, sliced with patience.
- 7 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 8 handfuls of spice tins, the best you can argue your way into.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Stir in the tar barrels and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Morton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Cover and leave it alone for 18 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Joren Kessler suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with tar barrels exists in Ossspire and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Morton it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
