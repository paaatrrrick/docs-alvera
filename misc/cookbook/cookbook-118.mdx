## About this dish

Every family in Fenreach claims their own variant; this one comes from the Imre Kessler household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when almanac reprints was scarce and cooks in Fenreach learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Fenreach, written down by Imre Kessler sometime around 1932 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- A strip of fired clay for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 39 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Stir in the dye cakes and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Fenreach say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. ## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Imre Kessler suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 7 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Wrendane and is, by treaty, never discussed here.

## Serving

A version made with wool bales exists in Wrendane and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Fenreach it would be unthinkable without both.
