## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Rosvik, written down by Sigrid Mollering sometime around 1845 and argued about ever since. Every family in Rosvik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Sigrid Mollering household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when preserved lemons was scarce and cooks in Rosvik learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- About 5 handfuls of wool bales, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of pressed kelp paper for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 7 ladles.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Rosvik say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. ## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Rosvik it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with glass floats exists in Zelgate and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Sigrid Mollering suggests any firm fish and an apology.
