## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Vormire learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Vormire, written down by Mireille Sorvane sometime around 1910 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- A strip of river-glass for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 2 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 3 handfuls of fermented tea bricks, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 5 ladles.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the dye cakes and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Vormire say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Vormire it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with dye cakes exists in Selmire and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Mireille Sorvane suggests any firm fish and an apology.
