## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when fermented tea bricks was scarce and cooks in Quilvik learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, and improves when reheated. Every family in Quilvik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Vesna Lindqvist household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Quilvik, written down by Vesna Lindqvist sometime around 1845 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- A strip of woven reed for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 3 handfuls of preserved lemons, the best you can argue your way into.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 9 ladles.
- A strip of salt-cured oak for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Quilvik say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 29 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. ## Notes from the galley

A version made with clockwork gears exists in Marcrest and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Vesna Lindqvist suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 7 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Quilvik it would be unthinkable without both.
