## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated. Every family in Erishore claims their own variant; this one comes from the Joren Marrow household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Erishore, written down by Joren Marrow sometime around 1904 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.
- A strip of hammered tin for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- A strip of woven reed for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Erishore say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 57 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

Stir in the wool bales and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. ## Notes from the galley

A version made with lamp oil exists in Velreach and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Erishore it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Joren Marrow suggests any firm fish and an apology.

## Serving

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Erishore it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Joren Marrow suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
