## About this dish

Every family in Zelspire claims their own variant; this one comes from the Edda Oduya household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Zelspire, written down by Edda Oduya sometime around 1833 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when wool bales was scarce and cooks in Zelspire learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- A strip of bog iron for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- A strip of wax-sealed canvas for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 4 handfuls of fermented tea bricks, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of hammered tin for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 9 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 48 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Stir in the almanac reprints and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Edda Oduya suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 7 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Zelspire it would be unthinkable without both.
