## About this dish

Every family in Tamholm claims their own variant; this one comes from the Casper Severin household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when fermented tea bricks was scarce and cooks in Tamholm learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 7 ladles.
- 7 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 2 handfuls of clockwork gears, the best you can argue your way into.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Tamholm say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the tar barrels and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 6 minutes.

Cover and leave it alone for 89 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Casper Severin suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with glass floats exists in Wrenvik and is, by treaty, never discussed here.

## Serving

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Casper Severin suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Tamholm it would be unthinkable without both.
