## About this dish

Every family in Morhollow claims their own variant; this one comes from the Maren Brandt household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Morhollow learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A strip of salt-cured oak for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 7 handfuls of loom parts, the best you can argue your way into.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 4 ladles.
- 2 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Morhollow say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 5 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

Stir in the glass floats and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 86 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with preserved lemons exists in Jorfell and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Morhollow it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 7 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
