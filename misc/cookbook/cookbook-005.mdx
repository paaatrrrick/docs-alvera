## About this dish

Every family in Zelford claims their own variant; this one comes from the Joren Brandt household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when clockwork gears was scarce and cooks in Zelford learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Zelford, written down by Joren Brandt sometime around 1860 and argued about ever since.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Zelford say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 9 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 85 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. Stir in the glass floats and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Joren Brandt suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Zelford it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with copper wire exists in Zelreach and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
