## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when clockwork gears was scarce and cooks in Halmara learned to do without. Every family in Halmara claims their own variant; this one comes from the Stellan Voss household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Halmara, written down by Stellan Voss sometime around 1962 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 6 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Halmara say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the clockwork gears and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 69 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Stellan Voss suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with lamp oil exists in Calford and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Halmara it would be unthinkable without both.
