## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when preserved lemons was scarce and cooks in Wrenhollow learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Wrenhollow, written down by Folke Whitlock sometime around 1784 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- About 9 handfuls of copper wire, the best you can argue your way into.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 3 ladles.
- A strip of woven reed for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Wrenhollow say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Cover and leave it alone for 46 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Wrenhollow it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with tar barrels exists in Marwick and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Folke Whitlock suggests any firm fish and an apology.
