## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Ilyvik, written down by Tilda Halloran sometime around 1820 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when preserved lemons was scarce and cooks in Ilyvik learned to do without. Every family in Ilyvik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Tilda Halloran household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A strip of bog iron for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 8 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 9 ladles.
- A strip of rope-fiber composite for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 83 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Ilyvik say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Ilyvik it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with loom parts exists in Corwick and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
