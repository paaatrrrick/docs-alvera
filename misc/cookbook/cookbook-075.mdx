## About this dish

Every family in Selmara claims their own variant; this one comes from the Joren Vexley household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Selmara learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Selmara, written down by Joren Vexley sometime around 1871 and argued about ever since.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Selmara say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 16 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the lamp oil and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Selmara it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with glass floats exists in Velvik and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
