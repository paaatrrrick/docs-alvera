## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Delmara, written down by Otto Marchetti sometime around 1860 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Delmara learned to do without. Every family in Delmara claims their own variant; this one comes from the Otto Marchetti household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- About 2 handfuls of clockwork gears, the best you can argue your way into.
- 5 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Delmara say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 23 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Otto Marchetti suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with loom parts exists in Ossvale and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Delmara it would be unthinkable without both.
