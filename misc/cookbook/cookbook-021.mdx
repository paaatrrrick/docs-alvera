## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. Every family in Rosshore claims their own variant; this one comes from the Ivo Severin household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Rosshore learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- A strip of whale-bone veneer for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- About 8 handfuls of copper wire, the best you can argue your way into.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 3 ladles.
- 8 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Rosshore say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with lamp oil exists in Perreach and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Rosshore it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Ivo Severin suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
