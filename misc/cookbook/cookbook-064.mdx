## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Kelgate, written down by Imre Voss sometime around 1914 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when lamp oil was scarce and cooks in Kelgate learned to do without. Every family in Kelgate claims their own variant; this one comes from the Imre Voss household and leans saltier than most.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the lamp oil and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 27 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Imre Voss suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with clockwork gears exists in Rosshore and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Kelgate it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
