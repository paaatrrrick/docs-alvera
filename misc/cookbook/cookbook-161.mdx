## About this dish

Every family in Delvik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Mireille Halloran household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Delvik, written down by Mireille Halloran sometime around 1827 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when lamp oil was scarce and cooks in Delvik learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- 8 onions, sliced with patience.
- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 4 handfuls of wool bales, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- About 5 handfuls of preserved lemons, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the wool bales and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Delvik say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 65 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Mireille Halloran suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Delvik it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with glass floats exists in Narstead and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
