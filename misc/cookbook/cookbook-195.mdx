## About this dish

Every family in Yarreach claims their own variant; this one comes from the Halvard Greaves household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when wool bales was scarce and cooks in Yarreach learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- About 5 handfuls of almanac reprints, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of hammered tin for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A strip of pressed kelp paper for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 43 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes. Stir in the spice tins and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with tar barrels exists in Yarcrest and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Halvard Greaves suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Yarreach it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
