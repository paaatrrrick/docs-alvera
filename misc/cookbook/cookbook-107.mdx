## About this dish

Every family in Halfell claims their own variant; this one comes from the Imre Marchetti household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Halfell, written down by Imre Marchetti sometime around 1854 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Halfell say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the clockwork gears and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

Cover and leave it alone for 25 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with wool bales exists in Corford and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Halfell it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Imre Marchetti suggests any firm fish and an apology.
