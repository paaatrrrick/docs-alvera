## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Halspire, written down by Ivo Voss sometime around 1916 and argued about ever since. Every family in Halspire claims their own variant; this one comes from the Ivo Voss household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Cover and leave it alone for 18 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Halspire say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes. ## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Ivo Voss suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Halspire it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with lamp oil exists in Galton and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
