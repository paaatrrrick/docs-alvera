## About this dish

Every family in Yarhollow claims their own variant; this one comes from the Tilda Halloran household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when almanac reprints was scarce and cooks in Yarhollow learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Yarhollow, written down by Tilda Halloran sometime around 1803 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- 3 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 4 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- About 4 handfuls of copper wire, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of pressed kelp paper for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Yarhollow say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Stir in the spice tins and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Tilda Halloran suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Yarhollow it would be unthinkable without both.
