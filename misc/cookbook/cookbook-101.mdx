## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when almanac reprints was scarce and cooks in Fenvik learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Fenvik, written down by Lazlo Oduya sometime around 1839 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated. Every family in Fenvik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Lazlo Oduya household and leans saltier than most.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 9 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 23 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Fenvik say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the almanac reprints and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Lazlo Oduya suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Fenvik it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with spice tins exists in Halmore and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
