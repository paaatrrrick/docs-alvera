## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Wrenvik learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Wrenvik, written down by Mireille Falkner sometime around 1946 and argued about ever since. Every family in Wrenvik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Mireille Falkner household and leans saltier than most.

## Method

Stir in the dye cakes and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 36 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Wrenvik say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Wrenvik it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with dye cakes exists in Ulmstead and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
