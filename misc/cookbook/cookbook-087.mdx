## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. Every family in Selmara claims their own variant; this one comes from the Ivo Voss household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Selmara, written down by Ivo Voss sometime around 1894 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when fermented tea bricks was scarce and cooks in Selmara learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- About 9 handfuls of lamp oil, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of river-glass for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 4 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Stir in the spice tins and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Selmara say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

Cover and leave it alone for 31 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. ## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Selmara it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Ivo Voss suggests any firm fish and an apology.
