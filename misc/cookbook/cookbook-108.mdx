## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Wrenmara, written down by Yutta Oduya sometime around 1908 and argued about ever since. Every family in Wrenmara claims their own variant; this one comes from the Yutta Oduya household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Wrenmara learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- A strip of woven reed for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.
- About 7 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of salt-cured oak for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Wrenmara say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 57 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides.

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. ## Notes from the galley

A version made with wool bales exists in Selshore and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Yutta Oduya suggests any firm fish and an apology.
