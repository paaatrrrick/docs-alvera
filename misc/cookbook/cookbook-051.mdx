## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when preserved lemons was scarce and cooks in Brenmara learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Brenmara, written down by Cosima Severin sometime around 1935 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

- About 2 handfuls of lamp oil, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of river-glass for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 6 minutes. Stir in the lamp oil and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 18 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Brenmara it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Cosima Severin suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 7 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with dye cakes exists in Ossspire and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
