## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated. Every family in Tamcrest claims their own variant; this one comes from the Imre Quist household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Tamcrest, written down by Imre Quist sometime around 1858 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Tamcrest learned to do without.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the wool bales and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Tamcrest say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Tamcrest it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Imre Quist suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Zelmara and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
