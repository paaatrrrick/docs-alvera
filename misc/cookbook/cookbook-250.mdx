## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when wool bales was scarce and cooks in Seldane learned to do without. Every family in Seldane claims their own variant; this one comes from the Lazlo Vexley household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Seldane, written down by Lazlo Vexley sometime around 1791 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- A strip of blue slate for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 5 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.
- About 5 handfuls of clockwork gears, the best you can argue your way into.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

## Method

Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 33 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Seldane say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Seldane it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Lazlo Vexley suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
