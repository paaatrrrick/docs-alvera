## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Vormire, written down by Halvard Halloran sometime around 1795 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Vormire learned to do without. Every family in Vormire claims their own variant; this one comes from the Halvard Halloran household and leans saltier than most.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 86 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a still and windless month. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. ## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Vormire it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Erimara and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Halvard Halloran suggests any firm fish and an apology.
