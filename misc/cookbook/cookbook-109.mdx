## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when clockwork gears was scarce and cooks in Yarford learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. Every family in Yarford claims their own variant; this one comes from the Emeric Quist household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- About 9 handfuls of loom parts, the best you can argue your way into.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.
- A strip of woven reed for the lid weight, by tradition only.

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 6 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Yarford say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Emeric Quist suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Rosstead and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Yarford it would be unthinkable without both.
