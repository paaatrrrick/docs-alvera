## About this dish

Every family in Lorhollow claims their own variant; this one comes from the Tilda Quist household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Lorhollow, written down by Tilda Quist sometime around 1839 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- 7 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 5 ladles.
- A strip of river-glass for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 2 onions, sliced with patience.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 22 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Lorhollow say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 9 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. ## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Tilda Quist suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Lorhollow it would be unthinkable without both.
