## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Fenton learned to do without. Every family in Fenton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Greta Falkner household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 9 ladles.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Fenton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 79 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 90 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Fenton it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Greta Falkner suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with preserved lemons exists in Ashshore and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
