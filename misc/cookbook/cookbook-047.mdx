## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Brenbury learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Brenbury, written down by Yutta Sorvane sometime around 1820 and argued about ever since. Every family in Brenbury claims their own variant; this one comes from the Yutta Sorvane household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.
- 4 onions, sliced with patience.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- About 9 handfuls of copper wire, the best you can argue your way into.
- About 3 handfuls of fermented tea bricks, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Stir in the dye cakes and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Brenbury say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Yutta Sorvane suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with copper wire exists in Ashton and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Brenbury it would be unthinkable without both.
