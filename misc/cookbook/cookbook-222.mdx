## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when clockwork gears was scarce and cooks in Osston learned to do without. Every family in Osston claims their own variant; this one comes from the Vesna Sorvane household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Osston, written down by Vesna Sorvane sometime around 1840 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 3 ladles.
- 9 onions, sliced with patience.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- 4 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the almanac reprints and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 49 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with tar barrels exists in Corwick and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Osston it would be unthinkable without both.

## Serving

Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with preserved lemons exists in Corwick and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Vesna Sorvane suggests any firm fish and an apology.
