## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. Every family in Fenmore claims their own variant; this one comes from the Beatrix Sorvane household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when almanac reprints was scarce and cooks in Fenmore learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Fenmore, written down by Beatrix Sorvane sometime around 1914 and argued about ever since.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 54 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. Stir in the lamp oil and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Fenmore say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Beatrix Sorvane suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Fenmore it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with tar barrels exists in Mordane and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
