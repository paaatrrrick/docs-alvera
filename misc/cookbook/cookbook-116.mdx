## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Erigate, written down by Beatrix Greaves sometime around 1884 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Erigate learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. Every family in Erigate claims their own variant; this one comes from the Beatrix Greaves household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 4 ladles.
- About 2 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of river-glass for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 4 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Erigate say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes.

Cover and leave it alone for 62 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Stir in the lamp oil and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with dye cakes exists in Yarmara and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Erigate it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Beatrix Greaves suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
