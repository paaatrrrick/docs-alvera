## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Pergate, written down by Otto Mollering sometime around 1818 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Pergate learned to do without. Every family in Pergate claims their own variant; this one comes from the Otto Mollering household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 5 onions, sliced with patience.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 55 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Pergate say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with almanac reprints exists in Galspire and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Pergate it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Otto Mollering suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
