## About this dish

Every family in Wrengate claims their own variant; this one comes from the Lazlo Quist household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when clockwork gears was scarce and cooks in Wrengate learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Wrengate, written down by Lazlo Quist sometime around 1856 and argued about ever since.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Cover and leave it alone for 60 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Wrengate say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

Stir in the clockwork gears and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Wrengate it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with tar barrels exists in Torstead and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.

## Serving

A version made with spice tins exists in Torstead and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Lazlo Quist suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Wrengate it would be unthinkable without both.
