## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when fermented tea bricks was scarce and cooks in Vorwick learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Vorwick, written down by Mireille Holloway sometime around 1945 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. Every family in Vorwick claims their own variant; this one comes from the Mireille Holloway household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- 9 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 6 handfuls of almanac reprints, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of bog iron for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 8 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Vorwick say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 14 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the glass floats and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Mireille Holloway suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 7 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Vorwick it would be unthinkable without both.
