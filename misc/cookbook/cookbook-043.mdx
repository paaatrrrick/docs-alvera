## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Dordane, written down by Emeric Whitlock sometime around 1960 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when lamp oil was scarce and cooks in Dordane learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- A strip of woven reed for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 4 ladles.
- A strip of whale-bone veneer for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- 8 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Dordane say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Dordane it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with copper wire exists in Quilton and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
