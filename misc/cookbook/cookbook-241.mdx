## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Yarstead, written down by Ivo Marchetti sometime around 1793 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when preserved lemons was scarce and cooks in Yarstead learned to do without. Every family in Yarstead claims their own variant; this one comes from the Ivo Marchetti household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 9 ladles.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 8 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Yarstead say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the dye cakes and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

Cover and leave it alone for 60 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. ## Notes from the galley

A version made with almanac reprints exists in Tamholm and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Ivo Marchetti suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Yarstead it would be unthinkable without both.

## Serving

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Ivo Marchetti suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with preserved lemons exists in Tamholm and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
