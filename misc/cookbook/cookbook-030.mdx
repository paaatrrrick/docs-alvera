## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Perford, written down by Cosima Greaves sometime around 1921 and argued about ever since. Every family in Perford claims their own variant; this one comes from the Cosima Greaves household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Perford learned to do without.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Perford say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 44 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with wool bales exists in Erispire and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Perford it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Cosima Greaves suggests any firm fish and an apology.
