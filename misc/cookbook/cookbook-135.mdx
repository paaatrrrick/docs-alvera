## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Torrun learned to do without. Every family in Torrun claims their own variant; this one comes from the Liesl Kessler household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Torrun, written down by Liesl Kessler sometime around 1912 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.
- About 6 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.
- About 4 handfuls of wool bales, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of pressed kelp paper for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

## Method

Stir in the spice tins and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Torrun say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes.

Cover and leave it alone for 43 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with lamp oil exists in Galstead and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Liesl Kessler suggests any firm fish and an apology.
