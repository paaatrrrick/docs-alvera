## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when lamp oil was scarce and cooks in Fendane learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Fendane, written down by Beatrix Falkner sometime around 1900 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- 5 onions, sliced with patience.
- A strip of fired clay for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 8 handfuls of wool bales, the best you can argue your way into.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Fendane say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 77 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. Stir in the spice tins and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Beatrix Falkner suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with wool bales exists in Lorstead and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Fendane it would be unthinkable without both.
