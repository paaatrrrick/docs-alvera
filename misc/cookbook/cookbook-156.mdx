## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Tamvik, written down by Petra Holloway sometime around 1897 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when preserved lemons was scarce and cooks in Tamvik learned to do without. Every family in Tamvik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Petra Holloway household and leans saltier than most.

## Method

Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Tamvik say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with tar barrels exists in Tammara and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Tamvik it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Petra Holloway suggests any firm fish and an apology.
