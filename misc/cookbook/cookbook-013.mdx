## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when preserved lemons was scarce and cooks in Tamwick learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Tamwick, written down by Tilda Whitlock sometime around 1939 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 9 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 3 handfuls of clockwork gears, the best you can argue your way into.
- About 6 handfuls of almanac reprints, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Stir in the lamp oil and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Tamwick say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Tilda Whitlock suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with loom parts exists in Tormire and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Tamwick it would be unthinkable without both.
