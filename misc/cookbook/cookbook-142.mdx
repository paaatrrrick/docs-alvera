## About this dish

Every family in Kelhollow claims their own variant; this one comes from the Greta Ashdown household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when preserved lemons was scarce and cooks in Kelhollow learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Kelhollow, written down by Greta Ashdown sometime around 1944 and argued about ever since.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 6 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Kelhollow say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 13 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides. ## Notes from the galley

A version made with loom parts exists in Vorhollow and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Greta Ashdown suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Kelhollow it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
