## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Calvik, written down by Lazlo Holloway sometime around 1810 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Calvik learned to do without.

## Method

Stir in the clockwork gears and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 58 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Lazlo Holloway suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with tar barrels exists in Permire and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
