## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Quilvale learned to do without. Every family in Quilvale claims their own variant; this one comes from the Folke Marchetti household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Quilvale, written down by Folke Marchetti sometime around 1937 and argued about ever since.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 18 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the tar barrels and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Quilvale say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Ulmmara and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Quilvale it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Folke Marchetti suggests any firm fish and an apology.
