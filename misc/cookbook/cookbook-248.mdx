## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. Every family in Osscrest claims their own variant; this one comes from the Liesl Marrow household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Osscrest learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- A strip of blue slate for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 7 handfuls of dye cakes, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 88 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Osscrest say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Osscrest it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with glass floats exists in Pervale and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
