## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Selholm, written down by Cosima Holloway sometime around 1844 and argued about ever since. Every family in Selholm claims their own variant; this one comes from the Cosima Holloway household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Selholm learned to do without.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 77 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Selholm say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the lamp oil and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Selholm it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with loom parts exists in Marholm and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
