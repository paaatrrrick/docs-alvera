## About this dish

Every family in Ashfell claims their own variant; this one comes from the Sigrid Falkner household and leans saltier than most. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Ashfell, written down by Sigrid Falkner sometime around 1955 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when clockwork gears was scarce and cooks in Ashfell learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 5 ladles.
- About 9 handfuls of lamp oil, the best you can argue your way into.
- About 3 handfuls of copper wire, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the fermented tea bricks and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Ashfell say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with loom parts exists in Perwick and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Ashfell it would be unthinkable without both.
