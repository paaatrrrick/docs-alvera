## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Galgate learned to do without. Every family in Galgate claims their own variant; this one comes from the Lazlo Marchetti household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- About 5 handfuls of copper wire, the best you can argue your way into.
- 2 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 9 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 3 ladles.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Cover and leave it alone for 33 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Galgate say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the spice tins and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 88 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with wool bales exists in Torshore and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Galgate it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.

## Serving

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Lazlo Marchetti suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
