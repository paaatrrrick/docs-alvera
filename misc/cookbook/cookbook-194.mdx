## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Lorwick, written down by Stellan Kessler sometime around 1888 and argued about ever since. Every family in Lorwick claims their own variant; this one comes from the Stellan Kessler household and leans saltier than most.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Lorwick say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 58 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with wool bales exists in Fenrun and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Stellan Kessler suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Lorwick it would be unthinkable without both.
