## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Eriwick, written down by Casper Falkner sometime around 1916 and argued about ever since. Every family in Eriwick claims their own variant; this one comes from the Casper Falkner household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Eriwick learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated.

## Method

Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 39 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 5 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Eriwick say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Eriwick it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Casper Falkner suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with dye cakes exists in Osston and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
