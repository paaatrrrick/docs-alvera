## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Zelton learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Zelton, written down by Edda Halloran sometime around 1802 and argued about ever since.

## Method

Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 9 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Zelton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 53 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for weeks of sideways rain. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Edda Halloran suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with dye cakes exists in Erivale and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.

## Serving

A version made with wool bales exists in Erivale and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Zelton it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 7 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
