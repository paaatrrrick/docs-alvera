## About this dish

Every family in Tamcrest claims their own variant; this one comes from the Otto Oduya household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Tamcrest, written down by Otto Oduya sometime around 1941 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Tamcrest learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.
- 4 onions, sliced with patience.
- 7 onions, sliced with patience.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Cover and leave it alone for 26 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 5 minutes.

Stir in the tar barrels and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Tamcrest it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Otto Oduya suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with preserved lemons exists in Vorford and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
