## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Tamstead, written down by Stellan Quist sometime around 1904 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Tamstead learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 7 ladles.
- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of salt-cured oak for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 6 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the spice tins and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 4 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Tamstead say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 12 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. ## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Stellan Quist suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Tamstead it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with preserved lemons exists in Yarmire and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
