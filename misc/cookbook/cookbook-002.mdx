## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when loom parts was scarce and cooks in Halvale learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the dry easterlies, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Halvale, written down by Lazlo Lindqvist sometime around 1886 and argued about ever since. Every family in Halvale claims their own variant; this one comes from the Lazlo Lindqvist household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 3 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 5 ladles.
- A strip of pressed kelp paper for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A strip of whale-bone veneer for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Halvale say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 9 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with wool bales exists in Torvale and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Halvale it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Lazlo Lindqvist suggests any firm fish and an apology.
