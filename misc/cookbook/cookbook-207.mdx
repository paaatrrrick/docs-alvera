## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when dye cakes was scarce and cooks in Calvik learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Calvik, written down by Otto Marchetti sometime around 1888 and argued about ever since. Every family in Calvik claims their own variant; this one comes from the Otto Marchetti household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- About 5 handfuls of wool bales, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A strip of woven reed for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 7 ladles.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Calvik say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 62 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with wool bales exists in Delcrest and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Calvik it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Otto Marchetti suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
