## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Dorwick, written down by Yutta Ashdown sometime around 1803 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated. Every family in Dorwick claims their own variant; this one comes from the Yutta Ashdown household and leans saltier than most.

## Ingredients

- About 3 handfuls of wool bales, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- A strip of river-glass for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 27 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides. Stir in the dye cakes and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Dorwick say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the wool bales and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. ## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Yutta Ashdown suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with almanac reprints exists in Ilyvale and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Dorwick it would be unthinkable without both.
