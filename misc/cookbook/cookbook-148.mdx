## About this dish

Every family in Fenton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Imre Sorvane household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Fenton, written down by Imre Sorvane sometime around 1952 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when glass floats was scarce and cooks in Fenton learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- 4 onions, sliced with patience.
- A strip of pressed kelp paper for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 4 ladles.

## Method

Stir in the tar barrels and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 3 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 64 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Fenton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Imre Sorvane suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Fenton it would be unthinkable without both.
