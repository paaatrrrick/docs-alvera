## About this dish

Every family in Calstead claims their own variant; this one comes from the Edda Halloran household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when fermented tea bricks was scarce and cooks in Calstead learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Calstead, written down by Edda Halloran sometime around 1936 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated.

## Method

Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 6 minutes. Cover and leave it alone for 35 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Calstead say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with dye cakes exists in Fenrun and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Calstead it would be unthinkable without both.
