## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Dorspire, written down by Annik Severin sometime around 1885 and argued about ever since. Every family in Dorspire claims their own variant; this one comes from the Annik Severin household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Dorspire learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 8 ladles.
- About 2 handfuls of fermented tea bricks, the best you can argue your way into.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 6 ladles.
- 6 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 4 ladles.

## Method

Stir in the clockwork gears and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 37 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Dorspire say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 9 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Dorspire it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Annik Severin suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with glass floats exists in Fenton and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
