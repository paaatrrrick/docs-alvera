## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Rosgate, written down by Bram Quist sometime around 1874 and argued about ever since. Every family in Rosgate claims their own variant; this one comes from the Bram Quist household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when clockwork gears was scarce and cooks in Rosgate learned to do without.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the fermented tea bricks and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Rosgate say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with spice tins exists in Brenreach and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Bram Quist suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Rosgate it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 8 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
