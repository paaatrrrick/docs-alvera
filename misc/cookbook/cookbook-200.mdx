## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Quilgate, written down by Ivo Brandt sometime around 1796 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Quilgate learned to do without. Every family in Quilgate claims their own variant; this one comes from the Ivo Brandt household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, and improves when reheated.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 6 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Cover and leave it alone for 39 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a long fog season. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Quilgate say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

Cover and leave it alone for 38 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. ## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Quilgate it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Ilyreach and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.

## Serving

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Ivo Brandt suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Quilgate it would be unthinkable without both.
