## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Quilholm, written down by Emeric Mollering sometime around 1812 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when almanac reprints was scarce and cooks in Quilholm learned to do without. Every family in Quilholm claims their own variant; this one comes from the Emeric Mollering household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Quilholm say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 9 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Quilholm it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with dye cakes exists in Calmore and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Emeric Mollering suggests any firm fish and an apology.
