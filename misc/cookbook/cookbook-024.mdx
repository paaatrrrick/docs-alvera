## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a still and windless month, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Dormire, written down by Tilda Brandt sometime around 1872 and argued about ever since. The dish dates to the embargo years, when tar barrels was scarce and cooks in Dormire learned to do without. Every family in Dormire claims their own variant; this one comes from the Tilda Brandt household and leans saltier than most.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Dormire say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the tar barrels and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Dormire it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with copper wire exists in Ilywick and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Tilda Brandt suggests any firm fish and an apology.
