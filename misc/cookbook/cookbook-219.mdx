## About this dish

Every family in Perton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Beatrix Oduya household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Perton learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Perton, written down by Beatrix Oduya sometime around 1837 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 5 ladles.
- About 4 handfuls of spice tins, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of lacquered birch for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- 4 onions, sliced with patience.

## Method

Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the fermented tea bricks and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Perton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Beatrix Oduya suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with wool bales exists in Torrun and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Perton it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.
