## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates spring floods, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Marton, written down by Beatrix Vexley sometime around 1962 and argued about ever since. Every family in Marton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Beatrix Vexley household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Marton learned to do without.

## Method

Stir in the preserved lemons and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 12 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for spring floods. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Marton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character.

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 6 minutes. ## Notes from the galley

A version made with copper wire exists in Ulmholm and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Marton it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 6 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Beatrix Vexley suggests any firm fish and an apology.
