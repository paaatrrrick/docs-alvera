## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Corrun, written down by Oona Brandt sometime around 1788 and argued about ever since. Every family in Corrun claims their own variant; this one comes from the Oona Brandt household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when preserved lemons was scarce and cooks in Corrun learned to do without.

## Ingredients

- About 8 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.
- 8 onions, sliced with patience.
- About 4 handfuls of glass floats, the best you can argue your way into.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 6 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the copper wire and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 90 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the dry easterlies.

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Corrun say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the loom parts and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 48 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Corrun it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with glass floats exists in Torshore and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Oona Brandt suggests any firm fish and an apology. Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day.

## Serving

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Corrun it would be unthinkable without both. Leftovers keep for 3 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Oona Brandt suggests any firm fish and an apology.
