## About this dish

Every family in Delreach claims their own variant; this one comes from the Bram Marrow household and leans saltier than most. The dish dates to the embargo years, when fermented tea bricks was scarce and cooks in Delreach learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Delreach, written down by Bram Marrow sometime around 1953 and argued about ever since.

## Ingredients

- 8 onions, sliced with patience.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- About 5 handfuls of tar barrels, the best you can argue your way into.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Delreach say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Stir in the spice tins and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 9 minutes. ## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 2 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Delreach it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with clockwork gears exists in Corfell and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Bram Marrow suggests any firm fish and an apology.
