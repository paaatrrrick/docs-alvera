## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Ossdane learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Ossdane, written down by Edda Drummond sometime around 1856 and argued about ever since. Every family in Ossdane claims their own variant; this one comes from the Edda Drummond household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- A strip of bog iron for the lid weight, by tradition only.
- About 2 handfuls of loom parts, the best you can argue your way into.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- 4 onions, sliced with patience.
- Stock enough to cover, roughly 2 ladles.

## Method

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the glass floats and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 5 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. A version made with wool bales exists in Ashspire and is, by treaty, never discussed here. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Ossdane it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Edda Drummond suggests any firm fish and an apology.
