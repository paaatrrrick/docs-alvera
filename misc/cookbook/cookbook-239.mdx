## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates an early frost, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when preserved lemons was scarce and cooks in Brenholm learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Brenholm, written down by Maren Kessler sometime around 1841 and argued about ever since. Every family in Brenholm claims their own variant; this one comes from the Maren Kessler household and leans saltier than most.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Brenholm say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Stir in the glass floats and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 20 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

A version made with wool bales exists in Delford and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Maren Kessler suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Brenholm it would be unthinkable without both.
