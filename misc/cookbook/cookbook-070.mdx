## About this dish

Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when spice tins was scarce and cooks in Zelspire learned to do without. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Zelspire, written down by Tomas Drummond sometime around 1845 and argued about ever since.

- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Zelspire say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 53 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 2 minutes. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Zelspire it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Tomas Drummond suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with spice tins exists in Tamford and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
