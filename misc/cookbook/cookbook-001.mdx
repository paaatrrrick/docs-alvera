## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Galton, written down by Mireille Quist sometime around 1961 and argued about ever since. Every family in Galton claims their own variant; this one comes from the Mireille Quist household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates the autumn king tides, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- Stock enough to cover, roughly 3 ladles.
- 5 onions, sliced with patience.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.

- About 5 handfuls of loom parts, the best you can argue your way into.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 8 minutes. Stir in the clockwork gears and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Galton say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Mireille Quist suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Galton it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with preserved lemons exists in Calvik and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
