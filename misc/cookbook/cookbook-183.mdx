## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Velvale, written down by Folke Marchetti sometime around 1957 and argued about ever since. Every family in Velvale claims their own variant; this one comes from the Folke Marchetti household and leans saltier than most. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, and improves when reheated.

## Ingredients

- 4 onions, sliced with patience.
- A knuckle of smoked fat, or what the larder offers.
- Vinegar, salt, and a hard pepper, ground coarse.
- About 5 handfuls of wool bales, the best you can argue your way into.
- A strip of fired clay for the lid weight, by tradition only.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Velvale say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Cover and leave it alone for 35 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for an early frost. Stir in the spice tins and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 6 minutes.

Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next.

## Notes from the galley

If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Folke Marchetti suggests any firm fish and an apology. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Velvale it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with fermented tea bricks exists in Calford and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
