## About this dish

This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Eridane, written down by Tilda Falkner sometime around 1842 and argued about ever since. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates a long fog season, and improves when reheated. The dish dates to the embargo years, when lamp oil was scarce and cooks in Eridane learned to do without.

## Method

Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Eridane say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Stir in the fermented tea bricks and let it toast against the bottom of the pot until it catches just slightly. Cover and leave it alone for 48 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for the autumn king tides.

## Notes from the galley

Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Eridane it would be unthinkable without both. A version made with lamp oil exists in Corvale and is, by treaty, never discussed here. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Tilda Falkner suggests any firm fish and an apology.
