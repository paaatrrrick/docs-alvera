## About this dish

The dish dates to the embargo years, when copper wire was scarce and cooks in Kelshore learned to do without. Barge cooks prize this dish because it needs one pot, tolerates weeks of sideways rain, and improves when reheated. This is the version served at the lock-keeper tavern in Kelshore, written down by Vesna Mollering sometime around 1890 and argued about ever since. Every family in Kelshore claims their own variant; this one comes from the Vesna Mollering household and leans saltier than most.

## Method

Begin by rendering the fat slowly over a low flame until the kitchen smells faintly of the quayside, about 7 minutes. Add the onions and a generous pinch of salt, and do not rush them; the cooks of Kelshore say hurried onions are a confession of bad character. Pour in the stock in three additions, letting each one fall quiet before the next. Cover and leave it alone for 38 minutes, resisting all temptation; lifting the lid is traditionally blamed for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Finish with vinegar off the heat, taste, and adjust with more conviction than caution.

## Notes from the galley

Leftovers keep for 4 days in a cool larder and are widely considered better on the second day. Serve with dense bread and pickles; in Kelshore it would be unthinkable without both. If you cannot find the proper smoked variety, Vesna Mollering suggests any firm fish and an apology. A version made with preserved lemons exists in Erireach and is, by treaty, never discussed here.
