## The custom

Brides and grooms in Morshore cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1839. On the first thaw morning, households in Morshore hang a strip of wax-sealed canvas from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Records kept by Tilda Petrov suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1955, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Velgate maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Imre Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Morshore shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of preserved lemons are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
