## The custom

No contract in Marton is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of tar barrels, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Narfell hang a strip of salt-cured oak from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Narfell shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Neighboring Marton maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1880, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Emeric Severin, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Liesl Petrov suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of preserved lemons are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Dress for the dry easterlies, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
