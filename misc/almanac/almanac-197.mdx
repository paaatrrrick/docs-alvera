## The custom

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1821, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Rurik Voss suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Ilymore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Rurik Petrov, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for spring floods, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Gifts of glass floats are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
