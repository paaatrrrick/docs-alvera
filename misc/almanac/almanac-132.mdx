## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Delcrest hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Ossrun is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of dye cakes, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Delcrest shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Neighboring Ossrun maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Petra Mollering, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Anselm Holloway suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1905, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Delcrest hang a strip of rope-fiber composite from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Ossrun is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of copper wire, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Delcrest shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Delcrest cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1813.

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Delcrest. Gifts of preserved lemons are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
