## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Ashspire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of fermented tea bricks, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Galwick cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1911. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Galwick shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1951, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Ashspire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Beatrix Marrow, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
