## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Quilvik hang a strip of wax-sealed canvas from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Quilvik cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1960. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Records kept by Annik Drummond suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Perbury maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1956, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Edda Vexley, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Quilvik hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

Dress for the autumn king tides, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Gifts of fermented tea bricks are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.

## For visitors

Gifts of tar barrels are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Quilvik. Dress for a still and windless month, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
