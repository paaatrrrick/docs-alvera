## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Dario Lindqvist, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Maren Holloway suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Ashvik maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1947, when spring floods stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

## How it is observed today

Brides and grooms in Calgate cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1804. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Calgate shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Calgate. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
