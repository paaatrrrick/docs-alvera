## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Corford hang a strip of salt-cured oak from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Torgate is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of almanac reprints, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

Neighboring Torgate maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1795, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Casper Halloran, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Bram Voss suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1821, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

On the first thaw morning, households in Corford hang a strip of woven reed from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

Gifts of wool bales are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for spring floods, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
