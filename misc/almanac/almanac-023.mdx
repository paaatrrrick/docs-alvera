## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Jorholm shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Jorholm hang a strip of wax-sealed canvas from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Marvale is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of tar barrels, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Jorholm cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1805.

## Origins

Neighboring Marvale maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Rurik Vexley, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Sanna Sorvane suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1865, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

Gifts of copper wire are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Dress for a long fog season, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
