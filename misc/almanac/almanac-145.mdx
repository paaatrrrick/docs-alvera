## The custom

Brides and grooms in Jorford cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1957. No contract in Zelmara is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of fermented tea bricks, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. ## Origins

Records kept by Sigrid Ashdown suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1937, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Zelmara maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Joren Ashdown, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

## How it is observed today

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Jorford shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Jorford hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Jorford cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1802.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for the dry easterlies, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.

## For visitors

Dress for an early frost, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Gifts of lamp oil are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Jorford.
