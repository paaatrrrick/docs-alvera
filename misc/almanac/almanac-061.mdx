## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Rosreach hang a strip of blue slate from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Halreach is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of lamp oil, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

Neighboring Halreach maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Edda Mollering, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1953, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Mireille Holloway suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.
