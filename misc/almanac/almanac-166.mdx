## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Jorrun shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Jorrun cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1813. On the first thaw morning, households in Jorrun hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Records kept by Dario Falkner suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Halstead maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Yutta Oduya, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1791, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
