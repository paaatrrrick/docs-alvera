## The custom

No contract in Quilreach is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of lamp oil, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Vorreach cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1914. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Neighboring Quilreach maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Annik Sorvane, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Casper Petrov suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1847, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
