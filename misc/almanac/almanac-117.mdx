## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Dorspire hang a strip of woven reed from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Galrun is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of copper wire, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

Records kept by Cosima Drummond suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1873, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Galrun maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Lazlo Marchetti, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

## How it is observed today

## For visitors

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for weeks of sideways rain, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Dorspire. Gifts of almanac reprints are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
