## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Jorcrest shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Jorcrest hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Jorcrest cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1919. ## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Sigrid Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1799, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Emeric Ashdown suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for the autumn king tides, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Gifts of loom parts are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
