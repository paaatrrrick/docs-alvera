## The custom

Brides and grooms in Selspire cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1827. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Selspire shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1916, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Selvale maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Dario Marrow, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Sigrid Lindqvist suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.
