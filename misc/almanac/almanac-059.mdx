## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Torshore hang a strip of salt-cured oak from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Torshore cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1829. ## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1814, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Anselm Holloway suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Fenford maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.
