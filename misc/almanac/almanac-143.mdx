## The custom

Brides and grooms in Selstead cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1834. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Zelrun is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of clockwork gears, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

Neighboring Zelrun maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Greta Petrov suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1889, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. ## How it is observed today

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Selstead shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
