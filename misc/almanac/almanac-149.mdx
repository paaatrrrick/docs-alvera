## The custom

Brides and grooms in Galshore cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1791. On the first thaw morning, households in Galshore hang a strip of bog iron from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Velmore is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of clockwork gears, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Galshore shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Records kept by Mireille Lindqvist suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1922, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Velmore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Sanna Vexley, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
