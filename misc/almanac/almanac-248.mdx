## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Narvik shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Wrenreach is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of copper wire, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

Neighboring Wrenreach maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Halvard Severin, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1815, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Emeric Halloran suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for an early frost, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Narvik.
