## The custom

Brides and grooms in Wrenspire cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1944. No contract in Rosspire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of dye cakes, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Wrenspire hang a strip of blue slate from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Wrenspire shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Records kept by Anselm Vexley suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Anselm Oduya, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1914, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Rosspire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Wrenspire hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Wrenspire shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for a still and windless month, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.

## For visitors

Gifts of tar barrels are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Wrenspire.
