## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Marvale shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Marvale hang a strip of wax-sealed canvas from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Marvale cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1809.

## Origins

Records kept by Edda Marchetti suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Galmire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1907, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Beatrix Marrow, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
