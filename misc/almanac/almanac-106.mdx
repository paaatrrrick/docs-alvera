## The custom

Brides and grooms in Marshore cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1818. On the first thaw morning, households in Marshore hang a strip of pressed kelp paper from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Marshore shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. ## Origins

Records kept by Otto Lindqvist suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1867, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Edda Kessler, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. ## How it is observed today

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Marshore shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Marshore hang a strip of salt-cured oak from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

Dress for a long fog season, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Gifts of glass floats are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Marshore.
