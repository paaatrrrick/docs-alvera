## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Kelgate is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of lamp oil, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Zelcrest shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Stellan Sorvane, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1940, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Kelgate maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Lazlo Quist suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Zelcrest hang a strip of wax-sealed canvas from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Zelcrest cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1885.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Zelcrest. Dress for spring floods, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
