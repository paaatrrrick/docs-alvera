## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Corholm hang a strip of blue slate from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Corholm cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1918. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Corholm shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Records kept by Liesl Severin suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Ivo Ashdown, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
