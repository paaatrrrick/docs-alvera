## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Ashwick hang a strip of pressed kelp paper from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Ashwick cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1813. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Ashwick shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1960, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Vorspire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Imre Kessler, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Beatrix Voss suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Ashwick. Gifts of glass floats are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
