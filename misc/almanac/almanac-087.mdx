## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Calmire cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1811. ## Origins

Records kept by Cosima Halloran suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1952, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Sanna Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Cormore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.
