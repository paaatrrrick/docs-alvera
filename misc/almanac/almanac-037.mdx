## The custom

No contract in Marbury is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of preserved lemons, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Yarford hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Yarford shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Neighboring Marbury maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Dario Severin, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Edda Falkner suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1915, when spring floods stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
