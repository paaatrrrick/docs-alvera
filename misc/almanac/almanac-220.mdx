## The custom

No contract in Calton is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of almanac reprints, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Vordane hang a strip of salt-cured oak from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Vordane shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Vordane cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1861.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Ivo Whitlock, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Calton maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Ivo Marchetti suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1878, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Vordane hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Vordane shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Vordane cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1809.

Gifts of glass floats are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Vordane.
