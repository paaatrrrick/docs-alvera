## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. ## Origins

Records kept by Yutta Kessler suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Velbury maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1795, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Sigrid Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
