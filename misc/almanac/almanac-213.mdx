## The custom

Brides and grooms in Calvik cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1804. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Calvik shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Calvik hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Neighboring Vorrun maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Otto Greaves suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1842, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Stellan Vexley, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
