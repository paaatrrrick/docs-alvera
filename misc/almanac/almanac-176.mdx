## The custom

Brides and grooms in Halbury cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1791. On the first thaw morning, households in Halbury hang a strip of rope-fiber composite from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Morcrest is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of preserved lemons, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

Records kept by Rurik Drummond suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Annik Oduya, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1908, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Morcrest maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Halbury hang a strip of salt-cured oak from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Halbury shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

Dress for an early frost, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Halbury. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
