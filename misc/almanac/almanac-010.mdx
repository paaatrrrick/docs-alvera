## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Jorcrest hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Jorcrest cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1824. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Jorcrest shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Records kept by Tomas Oduya suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Zeldane maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Vesna Kessler, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1825, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

Gifts of tar barrels are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Dress for a long fog season, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
