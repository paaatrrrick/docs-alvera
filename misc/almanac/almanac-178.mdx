## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Yarrun shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Velspire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of tar barrels, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Yarrun cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1832.

## Origins

Neighboring Velspire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1830, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Stellan Voss suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Mireille Mollering, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
