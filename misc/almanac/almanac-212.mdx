## The custom

Brides and grooms in Velholm cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1858. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Velholm shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Velholm hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

Records kept by Greta Falkner suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Beatrix Drummond, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Ilybury maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1811, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
