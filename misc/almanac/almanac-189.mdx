## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Calvik hang a strip of woven reed from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Calvik cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1807. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Calvik shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Halvik is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of loom parts, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

Neighboring Halvik maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1934, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Stellan Holloway, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Beatrix Ashdown suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

## How it is observed today

No contract in Halvik is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of wool bales, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Calvik cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1953.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for an early frost, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
