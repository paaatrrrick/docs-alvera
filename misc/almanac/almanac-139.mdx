## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Morspire hang a strip of pressed kelp paper from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Morspire cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1798. No contract in Quilstead is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of lamp oil, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

Neighboring Quilstead maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1913, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Anselm Severin suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Oona Severin, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
