## The custom

No contract in Erireach is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of spice tins, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Rosmara shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1937, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Vesna Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Erireach maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Edda Kessler suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

## How it is observed today

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Rosmara shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Rosmara hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Gifts of glass floats are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Rosmara. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
