## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Torhollow cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1838. On the first thaw morning, households in Torhollow hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

Neighboring Tamgate maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1801, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Annik Kessler suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Dario Quist, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

## How it is observed today

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Torhollow shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.
