## The custom

Brides and grooms in Zelgate cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1876. On the first thaw morning, households in Zelgate hang a strip of blue slate from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1908, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Dorrun maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Ivo Drummond, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
