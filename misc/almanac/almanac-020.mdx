## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Perbury hang a strip of pressed kelp paper from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Jorford is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of wool bales, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Perbury cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1875.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1905, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Lazlo Kessler suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Jorford maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Vesna Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

Gifts of tar barrels are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.

## For visitors

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Perbury. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for the dry easterlies, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Gifts of preserved lemons are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
