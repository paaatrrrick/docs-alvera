## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Calvik shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Erivik is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of tar barrels, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Calvik hang a strip of wax-sealed canvas from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

Neighboring Erivik maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Yutta Voss suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Tomas Brandt, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1794, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
