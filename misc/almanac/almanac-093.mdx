## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Zelvale shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Zelvale hang a strip of pressed kelp paper from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. ## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Emeric Marrow, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Wrenmore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Rurik Kessler suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1935, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

## For visitors

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Zelvale.
