## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Zelbury hang a strip of woven reed from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Lorholm is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of fermented tea bricks, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Zelbury cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1878.

## Origins

Neighboring Lorholm maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Anselm Sorvane, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Beatrix Mollering suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1813, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. ## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Zelbury shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Lorholm is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of preserved lemons, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of lamp oil are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Dress for weeks of sideways rain, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
