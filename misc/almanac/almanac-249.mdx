## The custom

No contract in Permire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of copper wire, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Delfell hang a strip of whale-bone veneer from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Delfell shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Annik Drummond, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Stellan Holloway suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Permire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1829, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

## How it is observed today

No contract in Permire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of glass floats, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Delfell shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Delfell cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1847.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Delfell. Gifts of copper wire are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
