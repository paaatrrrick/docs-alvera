## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Ulmgate shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Ulmgate hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Ulmgate cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1948.

## Origins

Neighboring Vordane maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Annik Holloway suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1789, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Emeric Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of tar barrels are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Ulmgate.
