## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Fenton hang a strip of woven reed from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Fenton shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. ## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1851, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Tomas Brandt suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Fenwick maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Edda Vexley, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
