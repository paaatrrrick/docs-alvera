## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Ilymara hang a strip of salt-cured oak from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Ilymara shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Ilymara cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1948.

## Origins

Neighboring Ilyfell maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Edda Falkner suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1789, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
