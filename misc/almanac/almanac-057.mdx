## The custom

No contract in Perspire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of loom parts, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Kelfell shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Kelfell cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1856.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Emeric Holloway, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1940, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Perspire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Cosima Drummond suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.
