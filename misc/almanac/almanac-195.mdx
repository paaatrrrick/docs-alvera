## The custom

Brides and grooms in Percrest cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1856. No contract in Zeldane is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of fermented tea bricks, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Percrest hang a strip of fired clay from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Sigrid Quist, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Zeldane maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Emeric Halloran suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1822, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

## How it is observed today

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Percrest shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Zeldane is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of dye cakes, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Percrest hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

Gifts of fermented tea bricks are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
