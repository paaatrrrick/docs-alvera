## The custom

Brides and grooms in Tamwick cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1853. No contract in Narmara is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of clockwork gears, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Tamwick shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Lazlo Holloway, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Greta Quist suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1806, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Narmara maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1940, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Tamwick hang a strip of woven reed from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Narmara is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of glass floats, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Tamwick shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

Dress for a still and windless month, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Gifts of tar barrels are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
