## The custom

Brides and grooms in Marcrest cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1914. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Marcrest shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Marcrest hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1926, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Joren Greaves, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Mormore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Ivo Lindqvist suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

## How it is observed today

No contract in Mormore is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of wool bales, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Marcrest cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1905. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

Gifts of tar barrels are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Dress for the dry easterlies, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
