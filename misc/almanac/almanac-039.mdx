## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Dorgate hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Dorgate shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Brenmire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of almanac reprints, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

Records kept by Bram Kessler suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Oona Greaves, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1782, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Brenmire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

Gifts of glass floats are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
