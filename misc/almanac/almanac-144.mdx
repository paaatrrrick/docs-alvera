## The custom

Brides and grooms in Selfell cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1862. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Selfell shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Selfell hang a strip of salt-cured oak from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1797, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Tamwick maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Bram Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Vesna Marrow suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Selfell hang a strip of blue slate from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Tamwick is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of clockwork gears, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of copper wire are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Selfell.
