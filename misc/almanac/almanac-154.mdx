## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Pervale hang a strip of bog iron from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Zelholm is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of wool bales, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Pervale shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Pervale cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1914.

## Origins

Records kept by Stellan Mollering suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Zelholm maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1932, when spring floods stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Vesna Greaves, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. ## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Dress for the autumn king tides, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Pervale.
