## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Eridane shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Kelton is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of dye cakes, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Eridane cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1837. On the first thaw morning, households in Eridane hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1961, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Tomas Quist, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Kelton maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Dress for spring floods, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Gifts of clockwork gears are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
