## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Ashvale hang a strip of fired clay from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Ashvale shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. ## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Vesna Marchetti, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Quilspire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Maren Severin suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1816, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
