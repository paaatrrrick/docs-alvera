## The custom

Brides and grooms in Fenshore cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1885. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Ilyford is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of wool bales, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Fenshore shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Edda Lindqvist, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Petra Kessler suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1853, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Ilyford maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.
