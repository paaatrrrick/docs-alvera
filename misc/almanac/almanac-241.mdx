## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Delhollow shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Delhollow hang a strip of whale-bone veneer from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Halton is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of preserved lemons, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

Neighboring Halton maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Rurik Halloran, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Bram Halloran suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1800, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

Dress for a long fog season, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Delhollow.
