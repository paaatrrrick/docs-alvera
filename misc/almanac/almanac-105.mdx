## The custom

Brides and grooms in Wrenrun cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1911. No contract in Yarmire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of fermented tea bricks, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Wrenrun shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Neighboring Yarmire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1906, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Emeric Halloran, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Sigrid Petrov suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Wrenrun.
