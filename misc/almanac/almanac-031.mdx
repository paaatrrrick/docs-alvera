## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Lorspire shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Fenstead is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of copper wire, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Lorspire cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1951.

## Origins

Records kept by Vesna Marchetti suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Fenstead maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1786, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Sigrid Greaves, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.
