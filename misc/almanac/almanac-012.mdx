## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Rosrun hang a strip of whale-bone veneer from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Rosrun shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Rosrun cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1930.

## Origins

Records kept by Greta Severin suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1864, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Marshore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Lazlo Petrov, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
