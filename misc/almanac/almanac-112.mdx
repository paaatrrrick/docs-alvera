## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Dordane hang a strip of whale-bone veneer from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Dordane shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Narwick is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of clockwork gears, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

Neighboring Narwick maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Annik Severin, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1924, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. ## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Dordane hang a strip of rope-fiber composite from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Dordane shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Dordane cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1889.

Gifts of fermented tea bricks are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for weeks of sideways rain, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
