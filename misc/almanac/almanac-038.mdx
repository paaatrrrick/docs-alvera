## The custom

Brides and grooms in Velfell cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1919. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Velfell shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Tormara is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of dye cakes, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Liesl Greaves, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1877, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Cosima Holloway suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Tormara maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Velfell hang a strip of fired clay from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Tormara is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of loom parts, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Velfell cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1932. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Velfell shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

Gifts of fermented tea bricks are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Velfell.

## For visitors

Dress for weeks of sideways rain, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
