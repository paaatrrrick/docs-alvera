## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Calcrest shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Brenton is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of spice tins, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Calcrest hang a strip of rope-fiber composite from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1807, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Stellan Brandt, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Bram Mollering suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.
