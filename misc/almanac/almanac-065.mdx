## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Ilyreach hang a strip of wax-sealed canvas from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Quilstead is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of clockwork gears, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Ilyreach shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Neighboring Quilstead maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1796, when spring floods stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Tomas Halloran, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Liesl Sorvane suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

## How it is observed today

No contract in Quilstead is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of almanac reprints, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Ilyreach cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1860. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Ilyreach shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

Dress for spring floods, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Gifts of loom parts are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
