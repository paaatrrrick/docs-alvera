## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Dormire shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Dormire hang a strip of pressed kelp paper from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Velmore is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of loom parts, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Dario Drummond, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Halvard Quist suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1796, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
