## The custom

No contract in Velhollow is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of tar barrels, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Ilyhollow hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

Neighboring Velhollow maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Liesl Falkner suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1830, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Vesna Ashdown, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

## How it is observed today

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of glass floats are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Dress for weeks of sideways rain, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
