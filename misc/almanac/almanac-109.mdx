## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Tamholm hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Tamholm cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1862.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1802, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Jorfell maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Bram Marchetti suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Greta Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
