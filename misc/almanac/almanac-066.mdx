## The custom

Brides and grooms in Torwick cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1881. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Torwick hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

Neighboring Kelhollow maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Annik Marrow suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1789, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Tilda Sorvane, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
