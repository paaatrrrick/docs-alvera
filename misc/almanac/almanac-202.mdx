## The custom

No contract in Velstead is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of almanac reprints, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Selspire cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1857. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Selspire shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Neighboring Velstead maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Stellan Halloran suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Edda Kessler, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1925, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
