## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Brenwick hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Brenwick shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Bram Falkner, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1787, when spring floods stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Erifell maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Halvard Drummond suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.
