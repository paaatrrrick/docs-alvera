## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Marspire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of fermented tea bricks, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Dormore cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1846.

## Origins

Records kept by Annik Brandt suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1844, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Rurik Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Marspire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Dormore hang a strip of bog iron from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Dormore shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Marspire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of clockwork gears, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Dormore. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
