## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Mormire hang a strip of fired clay from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Mormire cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1818. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Mormire shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Neighboring Zelvale maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1852, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Liesl Marchetti, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
