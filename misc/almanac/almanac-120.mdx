## The custom

Brides and grooms in Velford cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1904. No contract in Brenrun is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of loom parts, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Records kept by Joren Drummond suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Brenrun maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Yutta Halloran, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1944, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Velford shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Velford hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of dye cakes are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
