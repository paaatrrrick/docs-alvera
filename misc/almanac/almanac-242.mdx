## The custom

Brides and grooms in Morcrest cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1832. On the first thaw morning, households in Morcrest hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Morcrest shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Neighboring Tormore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1875, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Stellan Voss suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Oona Severin, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
