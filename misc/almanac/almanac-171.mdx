## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Delcrest is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of glass floats, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Torwick shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Torwick cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1788.

## Origins

Records kept by Ivo Severin suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Delcrest maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1931, when spring floods stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Yutta Severin, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

Dress for a long fog season, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Torwick.
