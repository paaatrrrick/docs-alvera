## The custom

No contract in Dorstead is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of glass floats, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Wrenton shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Wrenton cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1876.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Sigrid Kessler, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Dorstead maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1915, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Sigrid Ashdown suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Wrenton hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Gifts of copper wire are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Wrenton.
