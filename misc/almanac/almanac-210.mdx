## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Dorspire hang a strip of rope-fiber composite from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Dorspire shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Kelshore is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of dye cakes, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

Neighboring Kelshore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Annik Lindqvist, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Sigrid Lindqvist suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1945, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

## How it is observed today

No contract in Kelshore is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of dye cakes, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Dorspire hang a strip of wax-sealed canvas from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

Dress for the dry easterlies, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Gifts of loom parts are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.

## For visitors

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Dorspire. Gifts of spice tins are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
