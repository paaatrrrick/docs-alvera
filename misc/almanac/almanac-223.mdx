## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Brenton shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Brenton cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1953. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Folke Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Emeric Brandt suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Velton maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1816, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
