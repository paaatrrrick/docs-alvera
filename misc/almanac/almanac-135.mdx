## The custom

Brides and grooms in Corgate cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1954. No contract in Delwick is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of almanac reprints, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Corgate hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1859, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Casper Voss suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Delwick maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Rurik Ashdown, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

## How it is observed today

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Corgate shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Corgate hang a strip of woven reed from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

Gifts of tar barrels are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Dress for weeks of sideways rain, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.

## For visitors

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Corgate. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for spring floods, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
