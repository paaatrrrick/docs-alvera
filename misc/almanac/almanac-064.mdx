## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Kelmore shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Kelmore cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1844. On the first thaw morning, households in Kelmore hang a strip of wax-sealed canvas from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

Neighboring Velfell maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Oona Kessler, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1830, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Otto Marrow suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.
