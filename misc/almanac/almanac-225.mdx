## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Brenford hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Brenford shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Records kept by Oona Drummond suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1790, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Rosmore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Folke Mollering, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
