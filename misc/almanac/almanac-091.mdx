## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Ossfell hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Ossfell cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1817.

## Origins

Records kept by Cosima Drummond suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Maren Oduya, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Tamfell maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1952, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
