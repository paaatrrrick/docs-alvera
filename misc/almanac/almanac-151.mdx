## The custom

No contract in Torton is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of preserved lemons, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Jorshore cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1783. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1916, when spring floods stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Torton maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Bram Quist, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Beatrix Kessler suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Jorshore shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Jorshore hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Jorshore. Gifts of loom parts are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
