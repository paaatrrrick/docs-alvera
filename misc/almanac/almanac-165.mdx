## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Lormara hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Lormara shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Lormara cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1902.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Anselm Falkner, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Casper Marchetti suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1924, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Kelwick maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Lormara hang a strip of blue slate from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Lormara shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Kelwick is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of loom parts, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

Gifts of almanac reprints are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Dress for a long fog season, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
