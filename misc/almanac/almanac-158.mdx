## The custom

No contract in Wrencrest is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of dye cakes, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Quilbury cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1933. On the first thaw morning, households in Quilbury hang a strip of pressed kelp paper from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Maren Lindqvist, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Wrencrest maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1855, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
