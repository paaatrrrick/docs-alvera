## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Lorvik hang a strip of salt-cured oak from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Lorvik cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1918.

## Origins

Neighboring Brenmara maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Tilda Petrov, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Emeric Ashdown suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1878, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

## How it is observed today

Brides and grooms in Lorvik cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1793. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Lorvik shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Brenmara is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of fermented tea bricks, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

Gifts of copper wire are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Lorvik. Dress for weeks of sideways rain, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
