## The custom

Brides and grooms in Tammore cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1854. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Tammore hang a strip of bog iron from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Galton is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of preserved lemons, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

Neighboring Galton maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Ivo Marrow suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1799, when spring floods stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. ## How it is observed today

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Tammore. Dress for an early frost, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
