## The custom

Brides and grooms in Vorreach cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1843. On the first thaw morning, households in Vorreach hang a strip of whale-bone veneer from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Narcrest is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of glass floats, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Neighboring Narcrest maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1849, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Dario Petrov, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Imre Severin suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.
