## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Quildane hang a strip of blue slate from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Vorbury is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of dye cakes, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Quildane shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Lazlo Greaves, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1941, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Tilda Petrov suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Vorbury maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Quildane shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Quildane cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1869. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Vorbury is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of lamp oil, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

Dress for the autumn king tides, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Quildane.
