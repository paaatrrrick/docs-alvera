## The custom

Brides and grooms in Narwick cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1925. No contract in Zelmire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of loom parts, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Narwick hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1869, when spring floods stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Joren Petrov, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Yutta Petrov suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Zelmire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.
