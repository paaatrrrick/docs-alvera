## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Torbury hang a strip of whale-bone veneer from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Delcrest is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of spice tins, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Torbury cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1889. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1889, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Yutta Brandt, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Tilda Brandt suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.
