## The custom

Brides and grooms in Haldane cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1910. No contract in Narreach is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of loom parts, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Haldane shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Vesna Brandt, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1858, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Sigrid Halloran suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Narreach maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Haldane hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

Gifts of loom parts are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Dress for the dry easterlies, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
