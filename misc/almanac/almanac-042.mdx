## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Deldane hang a strip of salt-cured oak from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Deldane shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Deldane cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1871.

## Origins

Neighboring Zelmire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Otto Quist suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Liesl Petrov, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1913, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
