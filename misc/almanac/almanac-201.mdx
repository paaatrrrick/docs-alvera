## The custom

No contract in Fenwick is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of wool bales, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Correach shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Correach cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1949. On the first thaw morning, households in Correach hang a strip of whale-bone veneer from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1781, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Greta Lindqvist, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Fenwick maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Anselm Voss suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.
