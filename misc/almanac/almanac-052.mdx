## The custom

No contract in Lorfell is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of almanac reprints, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Calspire cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1902. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Neighboring Lorfell maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Joren Brandt, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1852, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Casper Oduya suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

On the first thaw morning, households in Calspire hang a strip of blue slate from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for weeks of sideways rain, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
