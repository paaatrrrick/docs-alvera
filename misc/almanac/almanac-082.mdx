## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Jordane hang a strip of salt-cured oak from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Dorton is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of fermented tea bricks, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Jordane shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Casper Greaves, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Dorton maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1781, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Halvard Kessler suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.
