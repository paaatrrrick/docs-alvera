## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Yarreach shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Ulmwick is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of preserved lemons, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Yarreach cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1846.

## Origins

Neighboring Ulmwick maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Yutta Lindqvist, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1898, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Mireille Lindqvist suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Yarreach shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Yarreach hang a strip of blue slate from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Yarreach cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1906.

Gifts of wool bales are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Yarreach.
