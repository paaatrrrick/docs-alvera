## The custom

Brides and grooms in Lorvik cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1816. On the first thaw morning, households in Lorvik hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Vorspire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of loom parts, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Lorvik shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Sigrid Falkner, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Vorspire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Petra Holloway suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1820, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

## How it is observed today

No contract in Vorspire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of glass floats, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Lorvik hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Lorvik shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

Dress for the autumn king tides, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Lorvik. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
