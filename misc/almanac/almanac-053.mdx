## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Kelrun hang a strip of whale-bone veneer from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Fengate is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of glass floats, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Kelrun shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1904, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Imre Brandt suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Halvard Marrow, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Fengate maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today
