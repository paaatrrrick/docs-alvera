## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Ashgate hang a strip of blue slate from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Ashgate shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Neighboring Mormire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1805, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Anselm Falkner suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Otto Ashdown, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

## How it is observed today

No contract in Mormire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of spice tins, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for spring floods, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.

## For visitors

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Ashgate. Gifts of wool bales are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
