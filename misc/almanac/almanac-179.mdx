## The custom

Brides and grooms in Marvik cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1795. No contract in Erivale is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of tar barrels, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Marvik hang a strip of salt-cured oak from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

Neighboring Erivale maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Folke Voss suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Vesna Petrov, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1796, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for the dry easterlies, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Gifts of dye cakes are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
