## Origins

Records kept by Mireille Petrov suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1826, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Erirun maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Mireille Mollering, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. ## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Velbury.
