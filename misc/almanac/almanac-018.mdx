## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Zelgate cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1809. No contract in Torshore is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of almanac reprints, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Zelgate shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Mireille Sorvane, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Torshore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Edda Severin suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1961, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
