## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Perrun is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of clockwork gears, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Fenvik cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1903.

## Origins

Records kept by Annik Ashdown suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Perrun maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Yutta Kessler, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1840, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
