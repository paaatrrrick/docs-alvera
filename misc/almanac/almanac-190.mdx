## The custom

Brides and grooms in Quildane cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1877. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Rosvik is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of clockwork gears, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Quildane hang a strip of pressed kelp paper from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Greta Brandt, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1851, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Joren Drummond suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Rosvik maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Quildane shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

Gifts of clockwork gears are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Dress for a long fog season, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.

## For visitors

Gifts of copper wire are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Dress for a long fog season, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Quildane.
