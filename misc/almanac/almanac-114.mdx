## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Tammire hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Tammire shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Selshore is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of almanac reprints, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Bram Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Casper Voss suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Selshore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1849, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
