## The custom

Brides and grooms in Correach cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1849. On the first thaw morning, households in Correach hang a strip of pressed kelp paper from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Quilcrest is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of spice tins, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1878, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Quilcrest maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Casper Quist, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Sigrid Kessler suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Correach cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1814. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Correach shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

Gifts of fermented tea bricks are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for a still and windless month, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
