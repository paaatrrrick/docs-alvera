## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Calshore is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of dye cakes, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Lorspire hang a strip of pressed kelp paper from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

Records kept by Vesna Halloran suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Beatrix Kessler, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Calshore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1862, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
