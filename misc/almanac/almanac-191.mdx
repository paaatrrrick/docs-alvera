## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Ilymara shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Ilymara hang a strip of rope-fiber composite from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Ilymara cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1872. ## Origins

Records kept by Mireille Voss suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Joren Marrow, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1960, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Wrenmire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Ilymara. Gifts of preserved lemons are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.

## For visitors

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for a long fog season, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
