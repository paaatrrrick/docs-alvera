## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Calhollow hang a strip of rope-fiber composite from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Calhollow cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1961. ## Origins

Neighboring Wrenvale maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Oona Marchetti suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1961, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Emeric Marrow, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
