## The custom

Brides and grooms in Corspire cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1783. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Corspire hang a strip of whale-bone veneer from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Yarmara is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of wool bales, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Yutta Marchetti, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1801, when spring floods stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Yarmara maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Dario Holloway suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Corspire shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Corspire hang a strip of blue slate from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

Gifts of lamp oil are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Corspire.
