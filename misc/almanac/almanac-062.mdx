## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Wrenbury is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of tar barrels, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Ilydane hang a strip of wax-sealed canvas from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1861, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Wrenbury maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Folke Falkner, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Bram Holloway suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

## How it is observed today

Brides and grooms in Ilydane cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1840. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Ilydane shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Wrenbury is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of wool bales, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for spring floods, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.

## For visitors

Gifts of dye cakes are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Dress for an early frost, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Ilydane.
