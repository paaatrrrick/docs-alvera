## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Dorshore cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1912. No contract in Nardane is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of spice tins, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1922, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Stellan Kessler suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Vesna Sorvane, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Nardane maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Dorshore hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Dorshore shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Gifts of loom parts are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Dorshore.
