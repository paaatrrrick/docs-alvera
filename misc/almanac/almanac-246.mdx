## The custom

Brides and grooms in Galrun cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1800. No contract in Zelshore is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of loom parts, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Records kept by Sanna Drummond suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1840, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Joren Vexley, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Zelshore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Galrun. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
