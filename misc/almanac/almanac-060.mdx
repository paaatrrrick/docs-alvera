## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Calmara hang a strip of salt-cured oak from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Calmara cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1928. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Calmara shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Tomas Greaves, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Otto Oduya suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1908, when spring floods stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Narholm maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.
