## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Corrun is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of wool bales, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Fenford shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Fenford cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1941.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1873, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Corrun maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Cosima Petrov suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Otto Whitlock, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
