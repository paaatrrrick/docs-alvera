## The custom

No contract in Ossmire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of wool bales, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Percrest cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1842. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1935, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Joren Drummond suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Ossmire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Edda Lindqvist, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

## How it is observed today

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Percrest shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Percrest hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Ossmire is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of clockwork gears, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Percrest. Dress for spring floods, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.

## For visitors

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Percrest. Dress for the autumn king tides, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Gifts of almanac reprints are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
