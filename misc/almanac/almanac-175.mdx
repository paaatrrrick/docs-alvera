## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Yarrun shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Yarrun cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1882. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1904, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Oona Drummond, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Lormire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Sanna Holloway suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

## How it is observed today

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Yarrun shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Yarrun cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1802. On the first thaw morning, households in Yarrun hang a strip of bog iron from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for a still and windless month, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.

## For visitors

Dress for an early frost, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of dye cakes are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Yarrun.
