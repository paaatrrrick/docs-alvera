## The custom

No contract in Brenford is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of almanac reprints, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Selgate hang a strip of wax-sealed canvas from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Selgate cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1791.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1823, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Brenford maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Casper Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
