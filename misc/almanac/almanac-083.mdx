## The custom

Brides and grooms in Ulmmire cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1926. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Ulmmire shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Ulmmire hang a strip of fired clay from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

Records kept by Emeric Holloway suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1964, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Oona Vexley, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Yarvik maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Ulmmire hang a strip of wax-sealed canvas from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Ulmmire shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Yarvik is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of dye cakes, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Ulmmire. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of wool bales are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
