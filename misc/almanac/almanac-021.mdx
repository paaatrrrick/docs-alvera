## The custom

Brides and grooms in Torvale cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1815. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Torvale shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Torvale hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Folke Sorvane, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Yutta Sorvane suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Jorford maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1946, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Torvale. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of copper wire are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
