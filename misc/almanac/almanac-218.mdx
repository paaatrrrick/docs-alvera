## The custom

Brides and grooms in Eribury cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1804. On the first thaw morning, households in Eribury hang a strip of pressed kelp paper from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Ilycrest is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of tar barrels, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Halvard Severin, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Edda Quist suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1905, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Ilycrest maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Eribury shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for an early frost, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
