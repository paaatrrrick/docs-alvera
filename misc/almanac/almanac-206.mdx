## The custom

Brides and grooms in Calford cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1832. No contract in Vorton is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of copper wire, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Calford hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Records kept by Vesna Greaves suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1872, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Vorton maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Joren Drummond, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

## How it is observed today

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Calford shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

Gifts of tar barrels are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.

## For visitors

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for weeks of sideways rain, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Calford.
