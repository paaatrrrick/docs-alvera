## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Correach hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Correach shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Correach cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1812.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1911, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Ilyvik maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Oona Petrov, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Imre Falkner suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.

## How it is observed today

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Correach shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Correach cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1858. No contract in Ilyvik is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of tar barrels, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

Dress for an early frost, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Gifts of lamp oil are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Correach.
