## The custom

No contract in Jorbury is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of wool bales, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Yarholm hang a strip of whale-bone veneer from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Yarholm shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Lazlo Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Annik Halloran suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1961, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Jorbury maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.
