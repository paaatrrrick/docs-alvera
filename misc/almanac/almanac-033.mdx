## The custom

No contract in Quilvale is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of copper wire, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Narvik hang a strip of rope-fiber composite from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Narvik shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1886, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Quilvale maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Casper Holloway suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Annik Whitlock, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
