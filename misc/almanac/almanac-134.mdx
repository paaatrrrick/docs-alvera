## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Galcrest hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Galcrest shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Halstead is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of lamp oil, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Tilda Halloran, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1843, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Dress for weeks of sideways rain, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of glass floats are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
