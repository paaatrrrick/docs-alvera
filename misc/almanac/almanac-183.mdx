## The custom

Brides and grooms in Torford cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1918. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Torford shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Torford hang a strip of pressed kelp paper from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

Neighboring Wrenbury maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Bram Ashdown suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1788, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Halvard Ashdown, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.
