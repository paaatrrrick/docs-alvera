## The custom

No contract in Jorvik is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of clockwork gears, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Permara cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1900. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Records kept by Imre Kessler suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1877, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Edda Vexley, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Jorvik maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Permara hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. ## For visitors

Gifts of dye cakes are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
