## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Delstead shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Delstead cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1795. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Records kept by Otto Vexley suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1959, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Joren Marrow, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Jormire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Delstead hang a strip of rope-fiber composite from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Delstead. Gifts of preserved lemons are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
