## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Galrun hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Galrun shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1853, when spring floods stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Dario Severin suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Beatrix Holloway, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Brenmara maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.
