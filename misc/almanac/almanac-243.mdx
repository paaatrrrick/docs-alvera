## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Wrenspire hang a strip of pressed kelp paper from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Wrenspire cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1824. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Wrenspire shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Otto Lindqvist, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Dario Petrov suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Ashspire maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1799, when spring floods stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
