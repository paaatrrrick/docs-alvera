## The custom

No contract in Cormara is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of fermented tea bricks, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Kelbury shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Kelbury hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

Neighboring Cormara maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Lazlo Quist suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Imre Oduya, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1814, when weeks of sideways rain stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Kelbury cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1829.

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Kelbury. Dress for weeks of sideways rain, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
