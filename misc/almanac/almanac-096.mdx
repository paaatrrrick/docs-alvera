## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Pervik hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Pervik shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Ashshore is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of dye cakes, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for.

## Origins

Records kept by Halvard Ashdown suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1816, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Yutta Whitlock, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Ashshore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.
