## The custom

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Zelreach is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of preserved lemons, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Ilymire cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1928. On the first thaw morning, households in Ilymire hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1822, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Zelreach maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Dario Greaves, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Annik Vexley suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.
