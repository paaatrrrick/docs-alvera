## The custom

No contract in Jorford is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of fermented tea bricks, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Marrun shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Marrun cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1891.

## Origins

Records kept by Edda Petrov suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1801, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Jorford maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Stellan Marchetti, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Marrun cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1867. On the first thaw morning, households in Marrun hang a strip of wax-sealed canvas from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Marrun shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

Dress for the autumn king tides, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Gifts of tar barrels are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.

## For visitors

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Marrun. Dress for a still and windless month, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of loom parts are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
