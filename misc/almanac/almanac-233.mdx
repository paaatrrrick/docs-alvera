## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Tamshore shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Percrest is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of tar barrels, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Tamshore hang a strip of lacquered birch from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Ivo Petrov, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1784, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Yutta Whitlock suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. ## How it is observed today

Brides and grooms in Tamshore cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1792. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Tamshore shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Dress for a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Tamshore.
