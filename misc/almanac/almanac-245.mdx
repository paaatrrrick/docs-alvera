## The custom

No contract in Velmore is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of copper wire, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Torvale shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Neighboring Velmore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Cosima Holloway, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Stellan Lindqvist suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1920, when an early frost stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. Brides and grooms in Torvale cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1852. On the first thaw morning, households in Torvale hang a strip of blue slate from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Torvale. Gifts of almanac reprints are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
