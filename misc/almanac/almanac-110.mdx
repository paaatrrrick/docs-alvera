## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Marwick hang a strip of rope-fiber composite from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Marwick cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1813. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Marwick shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Otto Marchetti, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1921, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Greta Sorvane suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Brengate maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Marwick. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
