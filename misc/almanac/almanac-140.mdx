## The custom

No contract in Dorhollow is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of preserved lemons, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Narvale cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1964. On the first thaw morning, households in Narvale hang a strip of salt-cured oak from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Emeric Greaves, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Vesna Vexley suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. Neighboring Dorhollow maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1834, when the autumn king tides stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of dye cakes are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
