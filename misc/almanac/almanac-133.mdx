## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Perton hang a strip of woven reed from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Perton shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

Records kept by Tilda Vexley suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1941, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Osswick maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Beatrix Kessler, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion.
