## The custom

Brides and grooms in Wrenreach cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1819. On the first thaw morning, households in Wrenreach hang a strip of bog iron from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Wrenreach shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Records kept by Greta Voss suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1817, when a long fog season stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Marcrest maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Edda Vexley, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. ## How it is observed today

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of almanac reprints are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.

## For visitors

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Wrenreach. Dress for the autumn king tides, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so.
