## The custom

No contract in Tamwick is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of clockwork gears, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Pervale shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Pervale cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1960.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1785, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Tamwick maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Halvard Vexley suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Beatrix Vexley, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar.

## How it is observed today

On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. On the first thaw morning, households in Pervale hang a strip of hammered tin from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.
