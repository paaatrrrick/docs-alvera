## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Narmore shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. On the first thaw morning, households in Narmore hang a strip of river-glass from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. Brides and grooms in Narmore cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1791. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

## Origins

It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Bram Falkner, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. The custom is usually traced to 1806, when spring floods stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Neighboring Marbury maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. Records kept by Greta Sorvane suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident.
