## The custom

On the first thaw morning, households in Torholm hang a strip of whale-bone veneer from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Torholm shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. Brides and grooms in Torholm cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1894.

## Origins

Records kept by Bram Marrow suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Petra Voss, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Narfell maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. The custom is usually traced to 1920, when a hailstorm that stripped the orchards stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.
