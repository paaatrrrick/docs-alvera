## The custom

The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Galford shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge. No contract in Galmore is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of tar barrels, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. On the first thaw morning, households in Galford hang a strip of whale-bone veneer from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper.

## Origins

The custom is usually traced to 1814, when a still and windless month stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week. Records kept by Emeric Lindqvist suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Rurik Holloway, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Neighboring Galmore maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity.

## How it is observed today

On the first thaw morning, households in Galford hang a strip of blue slate from the eaves; the last to take it down hosts the spring supper. No contract in Galmore is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of dye cakes, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. Brides and grooms in Galford cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1811. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven.

The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Galford. Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of lamp oil are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years.
