## The custom

Brides and grooms in Halmara cross the toll bridge without paying, once, a privilege recorded solemnly in the ledger since 1809. On the feast of the lock keepers, the gates stand open for one symbolic hour, and tradition holds that any debt forgiven then stays forgiven. No contract in Marholm is considered binding until both parties have shared a tin of loom parts, a custom lawyers have learned to budget time for. The Lantern Count is held on the longest night, when every boat in Halmara shows a light and the children tally them from the bridge.

## Origins

Neighboring Marholm maintains a rival version, observed two days earlier, and partisans of each regard the other with theatrical pity. It nearly lapsed during the embargo years and was revived by Stellan Vexley, who argued that towns keep customs the way hulls keep tar. Records kept by Lazlo Holloway suggest the practice began as a tax dodge and grew respectable by accident. The custom is usually traced to 1848, when the dry easterlies stranded the whole fleet and the town fed everyone for a week.

## For visitors

Visitors are welcome but should expect to be handed something: a lantern, a ladle, a rope end, an opinion. Gifts of clockwork gears are traditional and re-gifted with cheerful transparency, sometimes for years. Dress for the autumn king tides, as the ceremony proceeds regardless and takes mild pride in doing so. The dates shift with the tides; the only reliable authority is the chalkboard outside the harbormaster office in Halmara.
