## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Rurik Lindqvist, a restless instrument maker of Ossgate, though earlier sketches exist in the Wrenmara archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ossgate calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Sanna Sorvane around 1920, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Wrenmara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Corrun. By 1841 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ossgate museum by Mireille Sorvane, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
