## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Erimire in 1786, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Yutta Brandt, a quiet instrument maker of Erimire, though earlier sketches exist in the Narrun archive.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Erimire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Halvard Kessler around 1933, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Erimire museum by Mireille Greaves, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Narrun stamp, most of which are forgeries from Nardane. By 1913 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
