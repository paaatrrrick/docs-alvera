## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Perstead in 1836, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Anselm Holloway, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Perstead, though earlier sketches exist in the Velvik archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Perstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Beatrix Petrov around 1921, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Velvik stamp, most of which are forgeries from Morford. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1862 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Perstead museum by Vesna Lindqvist, who repairs them by feel.
