## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Sigrid Drummond, a ambitious instrument maker of Vorspire, though earlier sketches exist in the Lorford archive. The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Maren Marchetti around 1905, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Vorspire calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Vorspire museum by Rurik Kessler, who repairs them by feel. By 1954 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Lorford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dordane.
