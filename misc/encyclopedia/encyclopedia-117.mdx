## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Oona Lindqvist, a reclusive instrument maker of Veldane, though earlier sketches exist in the Halwick archive. The earliest surviving example is built of river-glass and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Bram Voss around 1828, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Veldane calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Veldane museum by Yutta Brandt, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1947 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Halwick stamp, most of which are forgeries from Wrenspire.
