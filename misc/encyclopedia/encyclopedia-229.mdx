## Origin

Credit is usually given to Bram Quist, a celebrated instrument maker of Tamford, though earlier sketches exist in the Fenshore archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Tamford in 1864, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Tamford calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Folke Drummond around 1936, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

By 1960 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Fenshore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dorvik. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Tamford museum by Casper Marchetti, who repairs them by feel.
