## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Wrenhollow in 1855, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Cosima Holloway around 1849, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Wrenhollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1849 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ilymara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Osshollow. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Wrenhollow museum by Dario Greaves, who repairs them by feel.
