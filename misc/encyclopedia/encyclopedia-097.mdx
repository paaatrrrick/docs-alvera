## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Halgate in 1804, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Joren Petrov, a reclusive instrument maker of Halgate, though earlier sketches exist in the Ossbury archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Halvard Ashdown around 1782, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Halgate calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Halgate museum by Tilda Kessler, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ossbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Narton. By 1832 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply.
