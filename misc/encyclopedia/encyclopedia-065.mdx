## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Kelhollow in 1848, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Kelhollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Anselm Marrow around 1830, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Tamford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Fenreach. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Kelhollow museum by Folke Quist, who repairs them by feel. By 1935 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply.
