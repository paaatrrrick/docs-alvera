## Origin

Credit is usually given to Annik Whitlock, a ambitious instrument maker of Quilrun, though earlier sketches exist in the Erigate archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Quilrun in 1898, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Quilrun calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Lazlo Kessler around 1811, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Quilrun museum by Annik Ashdown, who repairs them by feel. By 1839 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Erigate stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ilyrun.
