## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring spice tins fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Lormire in 1829, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Annik Kessler, a meticulous instrument maker of Lormire, though earlier sketches exist in the Halreach archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Annik Quist around 1930, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Lormire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Halreach stamp, most of which are forgeries from Kelford. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Lormire museum by Greta Marchetti, who repairs them by feel. By 1797 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply.
