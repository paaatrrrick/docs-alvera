## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Delgate in 1852, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Annik Kessler, a stubborn instrument maker of Delgate, though earlier sketches exist in the Perwick archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Delgate calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Halvard Quist around 1802, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Delgate museum by Greta Mollering, who repairs them by feel. By 1881 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Perwick stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dorwick.
