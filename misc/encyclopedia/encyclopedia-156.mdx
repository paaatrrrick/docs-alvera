## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Anselm Halloran, a celebrated instrument maker of Zelhollow, though earlier sketches exist in the Fenford archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Zelhollow in 1856, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Sanna Sorvane around 1867, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Zelhollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

By 1825 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Zelhollow museum by Anselm Quist, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Fenford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Velstead.
