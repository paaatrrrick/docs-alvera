## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Annik Kessler, a prosperous instrument maker of Velstead, though earlier sketches exist in the Seldane archive. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Velstead in 1821, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Folke Quist around 1822, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Velstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Velstead museum by Otto Whitlock, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Seldane stamp, most of which are forgeries from Wrencrest. By 1884 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply.
