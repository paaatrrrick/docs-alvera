## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Lazlo Drummond, a restless instrument maker of Wrenvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Zelcrest archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Wrenvik in 1861, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Vesna Brandt around 1831, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Wrenvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

By 1963 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over almanac reprints fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Zelcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ulmhollow. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Wrenvik museum by Mireille Severin, who repairs them by feel.
