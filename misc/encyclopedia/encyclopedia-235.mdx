## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Fenmara in 1813, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Cosima Sorvane, a ambitious instrument maker of Fenmara, though earlier sketches exist in the Quilrun archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Fenmara calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Petra Drummond around 1886, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

By 1900 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Quilrun stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ossmara. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Fenmara museum by Otto Marrow, who repairs them by feel.
