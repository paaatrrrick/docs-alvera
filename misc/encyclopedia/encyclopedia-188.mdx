## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Velwick in 1917, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Maren Severin, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Velwick, though earlier sketches exist in the Velmire archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Velwick calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Bram Severin around 1926, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

By 1948 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Velmire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Wrenspire. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Velwick museum by Yutta Holloway, who repairs them by feel.
