## Origin

Credit is usually given to Oona Ashdown, a celebrated instrument maker of Morholm, though earlier sketches exist in the Ulmvale archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Morholm in 1964, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Emeric Drummond around 1806, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Morholm calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

By 1866 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Morholm museum by Emeric Mollering, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ulmvale stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ulmspire.
