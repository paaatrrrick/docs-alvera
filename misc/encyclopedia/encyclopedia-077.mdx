## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Rurik Holloway, a ambitious instrument maker of Mormore, though earlier sketches exist in the Kelmire archive. The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Mormore in 1935, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Annik Brandt around 1853, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Mormore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1892 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Kelmire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Tamstead. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Mormore museum by Ivo Holloway, who repairs them by feel.
