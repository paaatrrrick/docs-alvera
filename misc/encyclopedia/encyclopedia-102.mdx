## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Calmore in 1790, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Calmore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Beatrix Marrow around 1801, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

By 1781 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Lorholm stamp, most of which are forgeries from Calvale. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Calmore museum by Joren Quist, who repairs them by feel.
