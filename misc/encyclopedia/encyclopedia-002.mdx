## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Tamstead in 1832, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Stellan Holloway around 1825, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Tamstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

By 1923 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Tamstead museum by Anselm Marrow, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Galfell stamp, most of which are forgeries from Quilhollow.
