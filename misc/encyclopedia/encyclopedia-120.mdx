## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Casper Oduya, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Brenstead, though earlier sketches exist in the Corholm archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Brenstead in 1958, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Brenstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Liesl Marrow around 1797, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Brenstead museum by Cosima Vexley, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1896 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Corholm stamp, most of which are forgeries from Halhollow.
