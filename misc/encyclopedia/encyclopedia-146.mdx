## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring spice tins fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Edda Drummond, a restless instrument maker of Halmire, though earlier sketches exist in the Rosdane archive.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Halmire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Tilda Drummond around 1812, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Rosdane stamp, most of which are forgeries from Pervik. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1875 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Halmire museum by Anselm Marrow, who repairs them by feel.
