## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Annik Kessler, a quiet instrument maker of Corton, though earlier sketches exist in the Fenvale archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Liesl Severin around 1827, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Corton calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Fenvale stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dorford. By 1861 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Corton museum by Joren Ashdown, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
