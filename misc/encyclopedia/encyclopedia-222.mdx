## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Velmore in 1951, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Dario Marrow, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Velmore, though earlier sketches exist in the Correach archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Velmore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Folke Lindqvist around 1889, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Correach stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ossfell. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Velmore museum by Lazlo Marrow, who repairs them by feel. By 1805 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply.
