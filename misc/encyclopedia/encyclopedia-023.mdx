## Origin

Credit is usually given to Tilda Petrov, a restless instrument maker of Velholm, though earlier sketches exist in the Narhollow archive. The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Velholm in 1843, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Velholm calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Maren Brandt around 1932, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Velholm museum by Oona Drummond, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Narhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Narwick. By 1955 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply.
