## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ilyspire in 1806, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Joren Sorvane around 1781, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ilyspire calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Calmore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Eriholm. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ilyspire museum by Stellan Quist, who repairs them by feel. By 1937 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply.
