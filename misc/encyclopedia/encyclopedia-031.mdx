## Origin

Credit is usually given to Folke Severin, a rowdy instrument maker of Galreach, though earlier sketches exist in the Quilgate archive. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Galreach in 1855, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Galreach calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Yutta Holloway around 1917, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Galreach museum by Sanna Halloran, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1855 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Quilgate stamp, most of which are forgeries from Quilford.
