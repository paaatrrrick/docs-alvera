## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Velmire in 1953, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Velmire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Bram Whitlock around 1941, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Selcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Roswick. By 1868 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Velmire museum by Halvard Kessler, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
