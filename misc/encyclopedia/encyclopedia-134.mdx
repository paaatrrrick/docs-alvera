## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Torvale in 1919, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Torvale calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Ivo Greaves around 1927, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarvale stamp, most of which are forgeries from Selford. By 1860 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Torvale museum by Anselm Halloran, who repairs them by feel.
