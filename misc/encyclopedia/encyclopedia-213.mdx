## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ulmgate in 1825, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Folke Falkner, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Ulmgate, though earlier sketches exist in the Dorrun archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ulmgate calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Emeric Lindqvist around 1880, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1883 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Dorrun stamp, most of which are forgeries from Calwick. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ulmgate museum by Cosima Mollering, who repairs them by feel.
