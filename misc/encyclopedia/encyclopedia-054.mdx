## Origin

Credit is usually given to Imre Kessler, a patient instrument maker of Lormire, though earlier sketches exist in the Lorbury archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Lormire in 1832, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Lormire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Annik Marchetti around 1950, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

By 1889 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Lormire museum by Folke Greaves, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Lorbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Halshore.
