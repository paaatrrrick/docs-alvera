## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Anselm Ashdown, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Jormire, though earlier sketches exist in the Rosstead archive.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Jormire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Beatrix Ashdown around 1891, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Rosstead stamp, most of which are forgeries from Yarshore. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1800 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Jormire museum by Ivo Greaves, who repairs them by feel.
