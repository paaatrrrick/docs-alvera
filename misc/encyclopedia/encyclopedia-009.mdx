## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Cormire in 1913, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Casper Marrow, a prosperous instrument maker of Cormire, though earlier sketches exist in the Morcrest archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Cormire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Beatrix Whitlock around 1809, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Morcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Galdane. By 1864 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Cormire museum by Anselm Voss, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
