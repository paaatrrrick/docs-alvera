## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Wrenmire in 1811, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Otto Quist, a restless instrument maker of Wrenmire, though earlier sketches exist in the Ulmmara archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Wrenmire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Sigrid Petrov around 1924, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Wrenmire museum by Liesl Petrov, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ulmmara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Lorvik. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1865 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply.
