## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Vormore in 1864, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Liesl Petrov, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Vormore, though earlier sketches exist in the Tamholm archive.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Vormore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Joren Kessler around 1938, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

By 1931 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Vormore museum by Otto Brandt, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Tamholm stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dorstead.
