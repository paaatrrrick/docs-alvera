## Origin

Credit is usually given to Beatrix Whitlock, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Ulmcrest, though earlier sketches exist in the Quilcrest archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ulmcrest calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Stellan Marrow around 1829, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

By 1940 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Quilcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Kelbury. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ulmcrest museum by Anselm Drummond, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
