## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Zelshore in 1839, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Imre Severin, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Zelshore, though earlier sketches exist in the Cormore archive. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Zelshore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Annik Halloran around 1848, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Zelshore museum by Otto Voss, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Cormore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Corvale. By 1946 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply.
