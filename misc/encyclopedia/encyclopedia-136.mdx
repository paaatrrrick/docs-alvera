## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Sigrid Petrov, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Eridane, though earlier sketches exist in the Fenstead archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Eridane in 1816, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Eridane calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Stellan Quist around 1943, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Fenstead stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dormore. By 1844 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Eridane museum by Folke Mollering, who repairs them by feel.
