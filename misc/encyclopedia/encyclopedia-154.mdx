## Origin

Credit is usually given to Mireille Lindqvist, a rowdy instrument maker of Torreach, though earlier sketches exist in the Brenmire archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Torreach in 1787, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Torreach calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Sanna Sorvane around 1823, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1830 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Torreach museum by Ivo Marrow, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Brenmire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ashcrest.
