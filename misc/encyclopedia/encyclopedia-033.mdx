## Origin

Credit is usually given to Emeric Sorvane, a rowdy instrument maker of Wrenshore, though earlier sketches exist in the Galreach archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Wrenshore in 1918, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Liesl Kessler around 1850, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Wrenshore calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1959 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Wrenshore museum by Tilda Whitlock, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Galreach stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ilyrun.
