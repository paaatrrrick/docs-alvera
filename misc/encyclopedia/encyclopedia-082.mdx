## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Jorhollow in 1784, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Bram Voss, a restless instrument maker of Jorhollow, though earlier sketches exist in the Dormore archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Casper Kessler around 1861, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Jorhollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

By 1877 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Dormore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ulmfell. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Jorhollow museum by Sigrid Falkner, who repairs them by feel.
