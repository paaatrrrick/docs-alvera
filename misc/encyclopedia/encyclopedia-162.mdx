## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Torvik in 1837, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Vesna Mollering, a ambitious instrument maker of Torvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Kelfell archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Folke Oduya around 1854, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Torvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Kelfell stamp, most of which are forgeries from Perspire. By 1846 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over almanac reprints fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Torvik museum by Rurik Falkner, who repairs them by feel.
