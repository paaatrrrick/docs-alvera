## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ashholm in 1877, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Petra Halloran, a quiet instrument maker of Ashholm, though earlier sketches exist in the Ulmshore archive. The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ashholm calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Stellan Ashdown around 1872, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ulmshore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Galreach. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ashholm museum by Emeric Greaves, who repairs them by feel. By 1873 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply.
