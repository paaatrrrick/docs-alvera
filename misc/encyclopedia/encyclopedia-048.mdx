## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Kelwick in 1831, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Imre Severin, a ambitious instrument maker of Kelwick, though earlier sketches exist in the Ulmton archive. The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Kelwick calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Casper Holloway around 1847, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ulmton stamp, most of which are forgeries from Erifell. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1862 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Kelwick museum by Sanna Drummond, who repairs them by feel.
