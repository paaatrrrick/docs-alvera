## Origin

Credit is usually given to Oona Vexley, a prosperous instrument maker of Vorspire, though earlier sketches exist in the Jormara archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Vorspire in 1935, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Vorspire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Oona Greaves around 1793, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Jormara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Selford. By 1900 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Vorspire museum by Greta Marrow, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
