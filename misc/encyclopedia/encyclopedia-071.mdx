## Origin

Credit is usually given to Tilda Whitlock, a ambitious instrument maker of Roston, though earlier sketches exist in the Yarhollow archive. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Roston in 1864, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Roston calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Otto Severin around 1784, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1793 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Perton. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Roston museum by Stellan Mollering, who repairs them by feel.
