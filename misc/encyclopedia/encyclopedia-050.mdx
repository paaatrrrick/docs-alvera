## Origin

Credit is usually given to Edda Falkner, a prosperous instrument maker of Halhollow, though earlier sketches exist in the Cormara archive. The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Beatrix Brandt around 1818, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Halhollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Cormara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Tammara. By 1811 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Halhollow museum by Otto Severin, who repairs them by feel.
