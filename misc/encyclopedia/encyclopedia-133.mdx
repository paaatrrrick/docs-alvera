## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Morvik in 1951, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Bram Petrov, a reclusive instrument maker of Morvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Rosfell archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Ivo Sorvane around 1818, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Rosfell stamp, most of which are forgeries from Jorcrest. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Morvik museum by Mireille Whitlock, who repairs them by feel. By 1884 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply.
