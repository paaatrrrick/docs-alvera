## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Joren Brandt, a celebrated instrument maker of Ulmspire, though earlier sketches exist in the Yarmore archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ulmspire in 1801, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ulmspire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Tilda Vexley around 1957, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

By 1886 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarmore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Wrenrun. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ulmspire museum by Maren Petrov, who repairs them by feel.
