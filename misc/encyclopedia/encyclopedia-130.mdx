## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Vorstead in 1813, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Petra Severin, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Vorstead, though earlier sketches exist in the Marton archive. The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Vorstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Casper Voss around 1836, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

By 1875 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Vorstead museum by Edda Petrov, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Marton stamp, most of which are forgeries from Brendane.
