## Origin

Credit is usually given to Sanna Voss, a celebrated instrument maker of Velford, though earlier sketches exist in the Brenmore archive. The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Velford in 1858, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Velford calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Otto Whitlock around 1884, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Brenmore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ulmford. By 1881 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Velford museum by Otto Lindqvist, who repairs them by feel.
