## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Marton in 1799, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring spice tins fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Marton calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Casper Vexley around 1838, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Marton museum by Liesl Falkner, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Torford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Torrun. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1897 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply.
