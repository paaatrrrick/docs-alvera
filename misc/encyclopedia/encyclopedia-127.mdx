## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring spice tins fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Brenvik in 1876, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Petra Voss, a reclusive instrument maker of Brenvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Zelgate archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Brenvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Yutta Halloran around 1959, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Zelgate stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ilybury. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Brenvik museum by Imre Holloway, who repairs them by feel. By 1814 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply.
