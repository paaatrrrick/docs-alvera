## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Velbury in 1919, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Yutta Drummond, a rowdy instrument maker of Velbury, though earlier sketches exist in the Tamstead archive. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Emeric Whitlock around 1793, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Velbury calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Velbury museum by Emeric Voss, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Tamstead stamp, most of which are forgeries from Rosvale. By 1879 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply.
