## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Corvale in 1921, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Beatrix Halloran, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Corvale, though earlier sketches exist in the Brenshore archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Beatrix Oduya around 1864, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Corvale calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Brenshore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ashford. By 1790 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Corvale museum by Maren Lindqvist, who repairs them by feel.
