## Origin

Credit is usually given to Casper Oduya, a restless instrument maker of Velshore, though earlier sketches exist in the Selmara archive. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Velshore in 1850, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Tomas Drummond around 1863, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Selmara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Vorford. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1954 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Velshore museum by Yutta Severin, who repairs them by feel.
