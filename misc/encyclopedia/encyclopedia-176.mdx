## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Yarreach in 1785, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Halvard Mollering around 1863, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Yarreach calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Yarreach museum by Anselm Kessler, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Corholm stamp, most of which are forgeries from Marmire. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1827 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply.
