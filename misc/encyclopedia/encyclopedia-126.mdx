## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Quilshore in 1803, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Quilshore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Vesna Marchetti around 1930, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Lorreach stamp, most of which are forgeries from Velvale. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Quilshore museum by Greta Halloran, who repairs them by feel. By 1953 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over almanac reprints fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
