## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Edda Ashdown, a meticulous instrument maker of Corvale, though earlier sketches exist in the Ulmcrest archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Corvale in 1917, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Annik Kessler around 1869, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Corvale calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Corvale museum by Lazlo Vexley, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ulmcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Lorwick. By 1846 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
