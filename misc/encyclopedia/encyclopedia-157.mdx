## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Vorcrest in 1877, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Lazlo Kessler, a quiet instrument maker of Vorcrest, though earlier sketches exist in the Velrun archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Anselm Severin around 1837, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Vorcrest calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1817 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Velrun stamp, most of which are forgeries from Calmire. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Vorcrest museum by Dario Sorvane, who repairs them by feel.
