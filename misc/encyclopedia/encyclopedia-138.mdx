## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Eriford in 1853, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Bram Quist, a reclusive instrument maker of Eriford, though earlier sketches exist in the Mormire archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Liesl Marchetti around 1953, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Eriford calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Eriford museum by Tilda Brandt, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Mormire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Delmire.
