## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Sigrid Petrov, a patient instrument maker of Ilyreach, though earlier sketches exist in the Galrun archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ilyreach in 1846, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Annik Greaves around 1834, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1950 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ilyreach museum by Beatrix Lindqvist, who repairs them by feel.
