## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of river-glass and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Corcrest in 1872, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Tomas Quist, a prosperous instrument maker of Corcrest, though earlier sketches exist in the Ilyfell archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Tilda Voss around 1877, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Corcrest calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ilyfell stamp, most of which are forgeries from Perbury. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Corcrest museum by Sigrid Lindqvist, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1797 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply.
