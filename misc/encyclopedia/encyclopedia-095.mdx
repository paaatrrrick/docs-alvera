## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Calrun in 1907, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Oona Ashdown, a ambitious instrument maker of Calrun, though earlier sketches exist in the Jorrun archive. The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Folke Marchetti around 1885, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Calrun calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Jorrun stamp, most of which are forgeries from Torvik. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Calrun museum by Maren Greaves, who repairs them by feel. By 1850 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply.
