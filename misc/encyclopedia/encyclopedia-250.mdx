## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Petra Greaves, a quiet instrument maker of Ericrest, though earlier sketches exist in the Jorreach archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ericrest in 1796, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ericrest calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Emeric Ashdown around 1933, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Jorreach stamp, most of which are forgeries from Lorshore. By 1846 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ericrest museum by Yutta Oduya, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
