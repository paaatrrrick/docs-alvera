## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Tomas Sorvane, a rowdy instrument maker of Fenreach, though earlier sketches exist in the Velmire archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Fenreach in 1905, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Fenreach calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Joren Sorvane around 1839, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Velmire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dormire. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Fenreach museum by Vesna Kessler, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1828 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply.
