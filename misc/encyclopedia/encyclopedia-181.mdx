## Origin

Credit is usually given to Halvard Ashdown, a rowdy instrument maker of Calcrest, though earlier sketches exist in the Ilyspire archive. The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Calcrest calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Lazlo Marrow around 1836, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ilyspire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ashbury. By 1815 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Calcrest museum by Stellan Holloway, who repairs them by feel.
