## Origin

Credit is usually given to Folke Kessler, a prosperous instrument maker of Lormore, though earlier sketches exist in the Perspire archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Lormore in 1853, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Lormore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Maren Halloran around 1832, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1795 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Lormore museum by Folke Ashdown, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Perspire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ilyford.
