## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Beatrix Severin, a stubborn instrument maker of Halholm, though earlier sketches exist in the Marholm archive.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Halholm calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Yutta Marrow around 1877, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Halholm museum by Rurik Marchetti, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Marholm stamp, most of which are forgeries from Lorvik. By 1881 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
