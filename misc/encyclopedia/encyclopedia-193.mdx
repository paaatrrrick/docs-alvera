## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Delspire in 1889, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Yutta Marrow, a restless instrument maker of Delspire, though earlier sketches exist in the Narvale archive.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Delspire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Petra Holloway around 1803, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Delspire museum by Annik Sorvane, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Narvale stamp, most of which are forgeries from Calvik. By 1939 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply.
