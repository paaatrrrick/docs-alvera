## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ashfell in 1923, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Rurik Kessler, a reclusive instrument maker of Ashfell, though earlier sketches exist in the Wrenholm archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ashfell calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Stellan Drummond around 1854, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ashfell museum by Tilda Whitlock, who repairs them by feel. By 1912 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Wrenholm stamp, most of which are forgeries from Lorwick.
