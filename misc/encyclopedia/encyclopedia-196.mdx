## Origin

Credit is usually given to Halvard Holloway, a prosperous instrument maker of Delstead, though earlier sketches exist in the Torstead archive. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Delstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Greta Oduya around 1837, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

By 1934 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Torstead stamp, most of which are forgeries from Narwick. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Delstead museum by Joren Ashdown, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
