## Origin

Credit is usually given to Casper Drummond, a meticulous instrument maker of Dorreach, though earlier sketches exist in the Narspire archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Dorreach in 1919, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Stellan Kessler around 1861, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Dorreach calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Narspire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dorholm. By 1870 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Dorreach museum by Dario Lindqvist, who repairs them by feel.
