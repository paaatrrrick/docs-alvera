## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Perfell in 1888, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Joren Oduya, a celebrated instrument maker of Perfell, though earlier sketches exist in the Kelbury archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Imre Vexley around 1797, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Perfell calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

By 1923 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Perfell museum by Vesna Whitlock, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Kelbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Brenmire. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
