## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Yarmire in 1892, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Bram Voss, a ambitious instrument maker of Yarmire, though earlier sketches exist in the Corshore archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Oona Brandt around 1789, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Yarmire calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1842 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Yarmire museum by Oona Petrov, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Corshore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ashshore.
