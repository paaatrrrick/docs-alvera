## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ilyvik in 1853, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Halvard Voss around 1952, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ilyvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Jorvale stamp, most of which are forgeries from Jorvale. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ilyvik museum by Sigrid Marrow, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1865 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply.
