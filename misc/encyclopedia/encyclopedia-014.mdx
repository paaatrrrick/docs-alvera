## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Calmire in 1835, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Edda Oduya, a quiet instrument maker of Calmire, though earlier sketches exist in the Zelhollow archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Anselm Severin around 1813, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Calmire calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

By 1804 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Zelhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Fenbury. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Calmire museum by Otto Halloran, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
