## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Lorwick in 1915, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Ivo Marrow around 1900, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Lorwick calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarshore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Tamvik. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Lorwick museum by Casper Severin, who repairs them by feel. By 1812 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
