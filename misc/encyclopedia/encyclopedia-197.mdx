## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Fenvik in 1906, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Annik Brandt, a quiet instrument maker of Fenvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Vorbury archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Halvard Halloran around 1921, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Fenvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Vorbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Kelcrest. By 1820 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Fenvik museum by Vesna Mollering, who repairs them by feel.
