## Origin

Credit is usually given to Rurik Sorvane, a quiet instrument maker of Ashbury, though earlier sketches exist in the Wrencrest archive. The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Beatrix Oduya around 1955, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ashbury calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ashbury museum by Oona Oduya, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Wrencrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Roshollow. By 1837 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply.
