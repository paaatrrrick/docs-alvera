## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Halford in 1866, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Maren Vexley, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Halford, though earlier sketches exist in the Jorshore archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Dario Vexley around 1952, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Halford calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Jorshore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dorfell. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1938 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Halford museum by Sigrid Petrov, who repairs them by feel.
