## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Zelholm in 1863, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Sigrid Drummond, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Zelholm, though earlier sketches exist in the Velshore archive. The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Zelholm calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Stellan Oduya around 1914, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Zelholm museum by Beatrix Voss, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Velshore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Tamfell. By 1800 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply.
