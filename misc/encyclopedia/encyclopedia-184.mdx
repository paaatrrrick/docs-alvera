## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ossholm in 1823, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Folke Whitlock, a restless instrument maker of Ossholm, though earlier sketches exist in the Calrun archive. The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Anselm Whitlock around 1917, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ossholm calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Calrun stamp, most of which are forgeries from Lorgate. By 1839 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ossholm museum by Folke Petrov, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
