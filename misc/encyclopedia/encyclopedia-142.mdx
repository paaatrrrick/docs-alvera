## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Vesna Vexley, a reclusive instrument maker of Brenshore, though earlier sketches exist in the Halstead archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Brenshore in 1928, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Brenshore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Mireille Drummond around 1850, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Halstead stamp, most of which are forgeries from Torhollow. By 1954 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Brenshore museum by Cosima Whitlock, who repairs them by feel.
