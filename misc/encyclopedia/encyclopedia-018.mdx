## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Sanna Halloran, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Ilygate, though earlier sketches exist in the Perhollow archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ilygate in 1904, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Yutta Petrov around 1858, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ilygate calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Perhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Wrencrest. By 1797 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ilygate museum by Annik Falkner, who repairs them by feel.
