## Origin

Credit is usually given to Beatrix Sorvane, a celebrated instrument maker of Calvale, though earlier sketches exist in the Delvik archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Edda Falkner around 1963, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Delvik stamp, most of which are forgeries from Fenmara. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1888 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Calvale museum by Greta Greaves, who repairs them by feel.
