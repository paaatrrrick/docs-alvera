## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Edda Ashdown, a restless instrument maker of Corton, though earlier sketches exist in the Morvik archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Corton in 1890, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Corton calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Lazlo Brandt around 1925, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Corton museum by Vesna Lindqvist, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Morvik stamp, most of which are forgeries from Brenreach.
