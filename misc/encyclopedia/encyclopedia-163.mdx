## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Velmore in 1819, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Velmore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Halvard Marchetti around 1868, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Eridane stamp, most of which are forgeries from Tamhollow. By 1913 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Velmore museum by Mireille Whitlock, who repairs them by feel.
