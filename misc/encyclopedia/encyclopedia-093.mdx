## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Lorrun in 1824, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Maren Marchetti, a ambitious instrument maker of Lorrun, though earlier sketches exist in the Calhollow archive. The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Lorrun calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Otto Halloran around 1838, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

By 1803 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Lorrun museum by Greta Severin, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Calhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Corgate.
