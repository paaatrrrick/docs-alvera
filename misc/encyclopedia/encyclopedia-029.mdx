## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Dormore in 1816, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Stellan Sorvane, a stubborn instrument maker of Dormore, though earlier sketches exist in the Quilspire archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Beatrix Falkner around 1920, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Dormore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Dormore museum by Bram Falkner, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1866 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Quilspire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Tamgate.
