## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Osston in 1901, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Casper Whitlock, a patient instrument maker of Osston, though earlier sketches exist in the Fenreach archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Ivo Holloway around 1917, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Osston calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1884 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Fenreach stamp, most of which are forgeries from Fenwick. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Osston museum by Lazlo Vexley, who repairs them by feel.
