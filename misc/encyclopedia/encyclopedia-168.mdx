## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of river-glass and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Wrenmire in 1787, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Bram Vexley, a rowdy instrument maker of Wrenmire, though earlier sketches exist in the Narholm archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Wrenmire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Greta Marrow around 1819, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

By 1824 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Wrenmire museum by Mireille Greaves, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Narholm stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ashton.
