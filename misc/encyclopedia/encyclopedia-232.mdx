## Origin

Credit is usually given to Mireille Marrow, a patient instrument maker of Jorshore, though earlier sketches exist in the Nargate archive. The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Jorshore in 1894, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Joren Sorvane around 1943, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Jorshore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

By 1789 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Nargate stamp, most of which are forgeries from Halrun. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Jorshore museum by Joren Marrow, who repairs them by feel.
