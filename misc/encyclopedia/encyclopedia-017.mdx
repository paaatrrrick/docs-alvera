## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ulmhollow in 1925, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Yutta Falkner, a patient instrument maker of Ulmhollow, though earlier sketches exist in the Velhollow archive. The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ulmhollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Edda Kessler around 1824, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1846 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ulmhollow museum by Edda Kessler, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Velhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Kelgate.
