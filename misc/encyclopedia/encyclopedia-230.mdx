## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Corbury in 1925, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Dario Mollering, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Corbury, though earlier sketches exist in the Rosgate archive.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Corbury calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Tilda Severin around 1799, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Rosgate stamp, most of which are forgeries from Wrenmore. By 1959 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Corbury museum by Folke Mollering, who repairs them by feel.
