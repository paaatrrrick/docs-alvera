## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Lorford in 1930, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Stellan Petrov, a restless instrument maker of Lorford, though earlier sketches exist in the Quilmara archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Casper Halloran around 1949, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Lorford calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Lorford museum by Annik Quist, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Quilmara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Zelford. By 1811 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply.
