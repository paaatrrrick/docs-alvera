## Origin

Credit is usually given to Ivo Kessler, a restless instrument maker of Jorford, though earlier sketches exist in the Yarvale archive. The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Jorford in 1835, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Jorford calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Rurik Halloran around 1923, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Jorford museum by Beatrix Petrov, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1932 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarvale stamp, most of which are forgeries from Morwick.
