## Origin

Credit is usually given to Bram Kessler, a restless instrument maker of Torholm, though earlier sketches exist in the Lordane archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Torholm in 1781, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Torholm calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Sigrid Sorvane around 1866, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Torholm museum by Rurik Quist, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Lordane stamp, most of which are forgeries from Perholm. By 1873 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
