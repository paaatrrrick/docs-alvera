## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Liesl Oduya, a stubborn instrument maker of Dorfell, though earlier sketches exist in the Kelreach archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Maren Marrow around 1909, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Dorfell calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Kelreach stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ashford. By 1857 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Dorfell museum by Vesna Petrov, who repairs them by feel.
