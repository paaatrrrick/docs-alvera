## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Cosima Lindqvist, a quiet instrument maker of Ashvale, though earlier sketches exist in the Perstead archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ashvale in 1920, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Petra Holloway around 1817, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ashvale calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Perstead stamp, most of which are forgeries from Jorshore. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ashvale museum by Bram Voss, who repairs them by feel.
