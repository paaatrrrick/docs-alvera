## Origin

Credit is usually given to Tilda Brandt, a celebrated instrument maker of Halholm, though earlier sketches exist in the Jorton archive. The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Halholm in 1803, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Halholm calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Anselm Whitlock around 1842, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Jorton stamp, most of which are forgeries from Velvale. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Halholm museum by Cosima Whitlock, who repairs them by feel. By 1874 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply.
