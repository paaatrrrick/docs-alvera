## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Yutta Kessler, a rowdy instrument maker of Delmore, though earlier sketches exist in the Yarmara archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Delmore in 1810, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Delmore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Beatrix Severin around 1878, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

By 1831 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Delmore museum by Otto Falkner, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarmara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Kelshore.
