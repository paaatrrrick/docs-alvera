## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of river-glass and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Halspire in 1932, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Edda Whitlock, a prosperous instrument maker of Halspire, though earlier sketches exist in the Corrun archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Halspire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Oona Sorvane around 1833, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Halspire museum by Sigrid Sorvane, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Corrun stamp, most of which are forgeries from Galvik. By 1807 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply.
