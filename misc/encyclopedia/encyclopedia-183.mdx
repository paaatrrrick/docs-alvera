## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Brenfell in 1849, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Liesl Voss, a patient instrument maker of Brenfell, though earlier sketches exist in the Ilyvik archive. The earliest surviving example is built of river-glass and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Brenfell calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Mireille Mollering around 1950, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Brenfell museum by Beatrix Marchetti, who repairs them by feel. By 1812 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ilyvik stamp, most of which are forgeries from Morfell.
