## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Dorstead in 1962, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Beatrix Greaves, a quiet instrument maker of Dorstead, though earlier sketches exist in the Quildane archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Dorstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Oona Falkner around 1860, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Quildane stamp, most of which are forgeries from Wrengate. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Dorstead museum by Joren Petrov, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1963 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply.
