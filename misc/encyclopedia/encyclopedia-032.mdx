## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Galholm in 1918, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Anselm Drummond, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Galholm, though earlier sketches exist in the Vormore archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Otto Petrov around 1853, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Galholm calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Vormore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dorholm. By 1819 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over almanac reprints fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Galholm museum by Maren Whitlock, who repairs them by feel.
