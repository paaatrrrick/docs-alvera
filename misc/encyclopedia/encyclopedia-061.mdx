## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Casper Marrow, a reclusive instrument maker of Brenshore, though earlier sketches exist in the Eriford archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Liesl Petrov around 1837, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Brenshore calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Brenshore museum by Greta Vexley, who repairs them by feel. By 1834 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Eriford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Velgate.
