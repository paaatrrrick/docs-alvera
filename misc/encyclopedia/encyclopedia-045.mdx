## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Tomas Petrov, a celebrated instrument maker of Corbury, though earlier sketches exist in the Jorhollow archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Corbury in 1814, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Joren Ashdown around 1819, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Corbury calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

By 1844 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Jorhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Delmore. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Corbury museum by Liesl Marrow, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
