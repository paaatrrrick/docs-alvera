## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Halreach in 1913, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Rurik Vexley, a quiet instrument maker of Halreach, though earlier sketches exist in the Halford archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Halreach calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Liesl Sorvane around 1785, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Halford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Wrenford. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Halreach museum by Folke Marrow, who repairs them by feel. By 1925 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply.
