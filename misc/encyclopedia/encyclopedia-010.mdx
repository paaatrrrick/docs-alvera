## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Emeric Vexley, a prosperous instrument maker of Fenstead, though earlier sketches exist in the Galcrest archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Fenstead in 1936, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Sigrid Lindqvist around 1933, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Fenstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Fenstead museum by Petra Kessler, who repairs them by feel. By 1914 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Galcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Vorford. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
