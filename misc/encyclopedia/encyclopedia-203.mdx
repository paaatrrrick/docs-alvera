## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Kelvik in 1940, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Anselm Kessler, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Kelvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Wrenvik archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Vesna Marrow around 1894, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Kelvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Wrenvik stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ossreach. By 1849 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Kelvik museum by Sigrid Severin, who repairs them by feel.
