## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Ivo Ashdown, a restless instrument maker of Dormire, though earlier sketches exist in the Dormire archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Cosima Petrov around 1867, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Dormire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1788 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Dormire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ossstead. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Dormire museum by Annik Drummond, who repairs them by feel.
