## Origin

Credit is usually given to Ivo Halloran, a rowdy instrument maker of Ashstead, though earlier sketches exist in the Jorvale archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ashstead in 1811, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ashstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Annik Vexley around 1920, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ashstead museum by Petra Brandt, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Jorvale stamp, most of which are forgeries from Brenwick. By 1781 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply.
