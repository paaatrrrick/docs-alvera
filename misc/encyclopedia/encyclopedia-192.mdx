## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Perrun in 1898, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Liesl Marrow, a reclusive instrument maker of Perrun, though earlier sketches exist in the Rosford archive.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Perrun calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Ivo Oduya around 1790, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Perrun museum by Lazlo Mollering, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Rosford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ashfell. By 1912 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over almanac reprints fell sharply.
