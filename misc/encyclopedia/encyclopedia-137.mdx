## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Wrenfell in 1928, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Ivo Halloran, a celebrated instrument maker of Wrenfell, though earlier sketches exist in the Marhollow archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Wrenfell calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Vesna Falkner around 1887, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Wrenfell museum by Sigrid Severin, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Marhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Zelreach. By 1945 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply.
