## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Liesl Mollering, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Marstead, though earlier sketches exist in the Marbury archive. The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Marstead in 1810, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Sanna Brandt around 1886, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Marstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

By 1914 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Marstead museum by Dario Oduya, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Marbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Jorfell. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
