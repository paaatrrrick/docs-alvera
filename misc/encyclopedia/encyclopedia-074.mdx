## Origin

Credit is usually given to Edda Drummond, a prosperous instrument maker of Calvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Jorvik archive. The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Calvik in 1919, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Calvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Folke Greaves around 1920, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Calvik museum by Oona Whitlock, who repairs them by feel. By 1835 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Jorvik stamp, most of which are forgeries from Vorholm.
