## Origin

Credit is usually given to Maren Marrow, a stubborn instrument maker of Jorvale, though earlier sketches exist in the Corvik archive. The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Jorvale in 1783, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Edda Oduya around 1809, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Jorvale calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Corvik stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dorcrest. By 1824 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Jorvale museum by Lazlo Holloway, who repairs them by feel.
