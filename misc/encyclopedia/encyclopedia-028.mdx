## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Petra Voss, a restless instrument maker of Calrun, though earlier sketches exist in the Mardane archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Casper Lindqvist around 1853, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Calrun calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Mardane stamp, most of which are forgeries from Zelholm. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1822 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Calrun museum by Yutta Lindqvist, who repairs them by feel.
