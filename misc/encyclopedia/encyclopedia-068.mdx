## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Edda Holloway, a ambitious instrument maker of Marvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Velhollow archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Marvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Greta Falkner around 1849, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

By 1854 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Velhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Vormara. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Marvik museum by Liesl Marrow, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
