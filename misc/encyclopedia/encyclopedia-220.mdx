## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Sanna Holloway, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Seldane, though earlier sketches exist in the Fenrun archive. The earliest surviving example is built of river-glass and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Seldane in 1897, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Seldane calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Anselm Drummond around 1848, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

By 1837 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Seldane museum by Lazlo Falkner, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Fenrun stamp, most of which are forgeries from Corford.
