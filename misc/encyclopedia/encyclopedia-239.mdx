## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Cosima Severin, a prosperous instrument maker of Torreach, though earlier sketches exist in the Zelcrest archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Torreach calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Rurik Petrov around 1787, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Zelcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Quildane. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Torreach museum by Cosima Drummond, who repairs them by feel. By 1800 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply.
