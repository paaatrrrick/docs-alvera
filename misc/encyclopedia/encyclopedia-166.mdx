## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Nargate in 1815, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Nargate calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Rurik Voss around 1856, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Nargate museum by Joren Greaves, who repairs them by feel. By 1839 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Perbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Eridane. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
