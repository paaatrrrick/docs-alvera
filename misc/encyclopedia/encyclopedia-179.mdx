## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Cosima Sorvane, a celebrated instrument maker of Fenhollow, though earlier sketches exist in the Calfell archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Mireille Lindqvist around 1797, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Fenhollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Calfell stamp, most of which are forgeries from Fenmara. By 1957 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Fenhollow museum by Cosima Lindqvist, who repairs them by feel.
