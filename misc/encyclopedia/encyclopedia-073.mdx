## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Wrenford in 1924, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Lazlo Brandt, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Wrenford, though earlier sketches exist in the Tormara archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Wrenford calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Dario Lindqvist around 1852, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Wrenford museum by Joren Drummond, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1784 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Tormara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Fenvale.
