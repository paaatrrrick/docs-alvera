## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Dorford in 1913, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring spice tins fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Liesl Severin, a celebrated instrument maker of Dorford, though earlier sketches exist in the Ossreach archive. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Dorford calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Vesna Whitlock around 1861, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

By 1823 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Dorford museum by Beatrix Marrow, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ossreach stamp, most of which are forgeries from Vordane.
