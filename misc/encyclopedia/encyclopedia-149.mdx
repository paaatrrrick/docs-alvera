## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ulmstead in 1824, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Casper Greaves, a stubborn instrument maker of Ulmstead, though earlier sketches exist in the Torbury archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Annik Halloran around 1847, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ulmstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ulmstead museum by Yutta Kessler, who repairs them by feel. By 1823 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Torbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Brencrest. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
