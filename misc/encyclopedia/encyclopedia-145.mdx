## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Zelshore in 1869, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Zelshore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Anselm Holloway around 1960, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Corstead stamp, most of which are forgeries from Brenmara. By 1803 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Zelshore museum by Joren Brandt, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
