## Origin

Credit is usually given to Oona Whitlock, a meticulous instrument maker of Wrenmire, though earlier sketches exist in the Fenrun archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Wrenmire in 1793, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Tomas Brandt around 1954, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Wrenmire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Fenrun stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ossford. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Wrenmire museum by Petra Ashdown, who repairs them by feel. By 1953 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply.
