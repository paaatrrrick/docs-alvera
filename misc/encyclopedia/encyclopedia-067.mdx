## Origin

Credit is usually given to Lazlo Marrow, a restless instrument maker of Perreach, though earlier sketches exist in the Kelvale archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Perreach in 1954, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Perreach calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Yutta Oduya around 1939, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

By 1951 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Kelvale stamp, most of which are forgeries from Morgate. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Perreach museum by Dario Falkner, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
