## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Dorton in 1918, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring spice tins fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Dorton calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Tilda Petrov around 1886, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Dorton museum by Oona Kessler, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Torcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Yarreach. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1844 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply.
