## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Beatrix Kessler, a rowdy instrument maker of Narmire, though earlier sketches exist in the Narspire archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Narmire in 1946, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Edda Severin around 1927, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Narmire calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1816 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Narmire museum by Greta Oduya, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Narspire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Calspire.
