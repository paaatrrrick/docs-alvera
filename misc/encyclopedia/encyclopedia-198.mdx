## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Sigrid Whitlock, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Vorbury, though earlier sketches exist in the Torholm archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Vorbury calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Maren Severin around 1896, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

By 1805 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Torholm stamp, most of which are forgeries from Zelcrest. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Vorbury museum by Beatrix Vexley, who repairs them by feel.
