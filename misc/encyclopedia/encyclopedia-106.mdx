## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Narrun in 1798, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Lazlo Mollering, a celebrated instrument maker of Narrun, though earlier sketches exist in the Delbury archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Casper Vexley around 1904, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Narrun calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Narrun museum by Stellan Oduya, who repairs them by feel. By 1819 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Delbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dordane.
