## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Calbury in 1891, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Dario Mollering, a reclusive instrument maker of Calbury, though earlier sketches exist in the Velbury archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Anselm Petrov around 1811, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Calbury calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Calbury museum by Greta Brandt, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1856 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Velbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Galstead.
