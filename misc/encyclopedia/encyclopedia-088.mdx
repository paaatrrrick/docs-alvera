## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Vesna Ashdown, a restless instrument maker of Narmara, though earlier sketches exist in the Corvale archive. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Narmara in 1910, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Mireille Quist around 1818, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Narmara calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Corvale stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dorshore. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Narmara museum by Yutta Greaves, who repairs them by feel. By 1809 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply.
