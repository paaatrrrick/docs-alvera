## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Quildane in 1786, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Halvard Voss, a quiet instrument maker of Quildane, though earlier sketches exist in the Fenshore archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Oona Severin around 1815, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Quildane calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Quildane museum by Rurik Oduya, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1938 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Fenshore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Rosmire.
