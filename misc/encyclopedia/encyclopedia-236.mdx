## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ilyholm in 1889, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Emeric Marchetti, a ambitious instrument maker of Ilyholm, though earlier sketches exist in the Quilgate archive. The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ilyholm calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Sanna Petrov around 1811, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ilyholm museum by Imre Holloway, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1917 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Quilgate stamp, most of which are forgeries from Zelmire.
