## Origin

Credit is usually given to Lazlo Sorvane, a ambitious instrument maker of Marmore, though earlier sketches exist in the Morhollow archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Marmore in 1810, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Marmore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Annik Petrov around 1924, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Morhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ilyford. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Marmore museum by Mireille Ashdown, who repairs them by feel. By 1955 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply.
