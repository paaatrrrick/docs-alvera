## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Ivo Ashdown, a rowdy instrument maker of Marmore, though earlier sketches exist in the Perbury archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Greta Marchetti around 1963, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Marmore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Marmore museum by Emeric Marrow, who repairs them by feel. By 1882 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Perbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Zelmire.
