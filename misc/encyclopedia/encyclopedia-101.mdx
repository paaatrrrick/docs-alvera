## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Calmire in 1922, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring spice tins fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Vesna Lindqvist, a meticulous instrument maker of Calmire, though earlier sketches exist in the Delbury archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Sanna Drummond around 1927, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Calmire calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Calmire museum by Petra Mollering, who repairs them by feel. By 1845 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Delbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Lordane. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
