## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Wrencrest in 1959, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Joren Marchetti, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Wrencrest, though earlier sketches exist in the Perford archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Emeric Marchetti around 1869, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Wrencrest calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Perford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Galwick. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Wrencrest museum by Maren Marrow, who repairs them by feel. By 1893 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply.
