## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ossreach in 1952, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Oona Mollering, a restless instrument maker of Ossreach, though earlier sketches exist in the Jordane archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Sigrid Drummond around 1911, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ossreach calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

By 1886 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ossreach museum by Maren Brandt, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Jordane stamp, most of which are forgeries from Halford. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
