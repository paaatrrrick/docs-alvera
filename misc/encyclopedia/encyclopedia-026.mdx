## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Dario Mollering, a reclusive instrument maker of Zelrun, though earlier sketches exist in the Dorbury archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Zelrun calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Ivo Brandt around 1924, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Dorbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dorreach. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Zelrun museum by Sigrid Halloran, who repairs them by feel.
