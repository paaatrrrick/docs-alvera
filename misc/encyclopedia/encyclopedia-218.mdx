## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Rurik Petrov, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Ossshore, though earlier sketches exist in the Rosfell archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ossshore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Imre Mollering around 1829, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

By 1909 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Rosfell stamp, most of which are forgeries from Selbury. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ossshore museum by Rurik Marrow, who repairs them by feel.
