## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Vesna Vexley, a prosperous instrument maker of Kelholm, though earlier sketches exist in the Halbury archive.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Kelholm calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Tilda Greaves around 1793, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Kelholm museum by Lazlo Lindqvist, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Halbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Kelspire. By 1956 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply.
