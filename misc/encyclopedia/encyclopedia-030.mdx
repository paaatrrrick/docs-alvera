## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Yutta Ashdown, a reclusive instrument maker of Velmire, though earlier sketches exist in the Ilymara archive. The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Velmire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Sanna Severin around 1878, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Velmire museum by Joren Ashdown, who repairs them by feel. By 1807 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ilymara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Morfell.
