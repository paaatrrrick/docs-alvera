## Origin

Credit is usually given to Sigrid Brandt, a ambitious instrument maker of Ulmvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Narmore archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ulmvik in 1907, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Halvard Marchetti around 1801, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ulmvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Narmore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Velrun. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ulmvik museum by Vesna Kessler, who repairs them by feel. By 1873 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
