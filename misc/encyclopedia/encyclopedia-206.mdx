## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring spice tins fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Rurik Marchetti, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Ashreach, though earlier sketches exist in the Yarshore archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Rurik Holloway around 1914, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ashreach calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

By 1842 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarshore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Tamstead. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ashreach museum by Tomas Oduya, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
