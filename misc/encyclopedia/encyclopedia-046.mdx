## Origin

Credit is usually given to Mireille Marrow, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Rosgate, though earlier sketches exist in the Narford archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring spice tins fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Rosgate in 1909, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Sanna Voss around 1886, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Rosgate calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Rosgate museum by Casper Vexley, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Narford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Wrenbury. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1933 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply.
