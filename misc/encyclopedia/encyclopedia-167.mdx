## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring spice tins fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Cosima Marrow, a ambitious instrument maker of Torstead, though earlier sketches exist in the Nardane archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Torstead in 1800, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Annik Halloran around 1897, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Torstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Torstead museum by Emeric Marchetti, who repairs them by feel. By 1824 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Nardane stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ulmfell.
