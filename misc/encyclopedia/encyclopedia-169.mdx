## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Lazlo Marchetti, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Jorfell, though earlier sketches exist in the Ashcrest archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Jorfell in 1893, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Jorfell calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Edda Quist around 1951, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

By 1799 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Jorfell museum by Mireille Marrow, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ashcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Tamreach.
