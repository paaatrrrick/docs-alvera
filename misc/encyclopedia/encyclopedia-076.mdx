## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Annik Halloran, a meticulous instrument maker of Jormore, though earlier sketches exist in the Corfell archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Jormore in 1909, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Jormore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Tilda Oduya around 1898, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Corfell stamp, most of which are forgeries from Vorholm. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1804 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Jormore museum by Ivo Sorvane, who repairs them by feel.
