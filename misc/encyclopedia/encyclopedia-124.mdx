## Origin

Credit is usually given to Liesl Vexley, a meticulous instrument maker of Velvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Ashwick archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Velvik in 1954, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Greta Quist around 1811, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Velvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ashwick stamp, most of which are forgeries from Eristead. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1805 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Velvik museum by Greta Marrow, who repairs them by feel.
