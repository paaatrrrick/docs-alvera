## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Narhollow in 1814, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Tilda Severin, a celebrated instrument maker of Narhollow, though earlier sketches exist in the Narholm archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Narhollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Bram Halloran around 1802, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

By 1912 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Narhollow museum by Vesna Marchetti, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Narholm stamp, most of which are forgeries from Kelmire. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
