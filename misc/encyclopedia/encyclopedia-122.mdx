## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Wrenhollow in 1910, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Tomas Sorvane, a ambitious instrument maker of Wrenhollow, though earlier sketches exist in the Lorfell archive.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Wrenhollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Annik Voss around 1836, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

By 1903 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Wrenhollow museum by Casper Kessler, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Lorfell stamp, most of which are forgeries from Jormara.
