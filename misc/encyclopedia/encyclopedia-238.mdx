## Origin

Credit is usually given to Emeric Greaves, a meticulous instrument maker of Brenhollow, though earlier sketches exist in the Narcrest archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Brenhollow in 1813, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Brenhollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Halvard Kessler around 1849, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Narcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ossshore. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Brenhollow museum by Cosima Marrow, who repairs them by feel.
