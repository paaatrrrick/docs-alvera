## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Calcrest in 1854, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Stellan Lindqvist, a reclusive instrument maker of Calcrest, though earlier sketches exist in the Corhollow archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Otto Brandt around 1805, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Calcrest calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Calcrest museum by Dario Brandt, who repairs them by feel. By 1837 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Corhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Halvale.
