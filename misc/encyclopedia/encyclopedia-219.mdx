## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Dorcrest in 1927, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Anselm Lindqvist, a quiet instrument maker of Dorcrest, though earlier sketches exist in the Brenton archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Ivo Lindqvist around 1949, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Dorcrest calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Brenton stamp, most of which are forgeries from Jorrun. By 1897 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Dorcrest museum by Stellan Vexley, who repairs them by feel.
