## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Fenford in 1873, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of river-glass and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Petra Whitlock, a reclusive instrument maker of Fenford, though earlier sketches exist in the Erireach archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Fenford calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Dario Greaves around 1788, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Fenford museum by Edda Greaves, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Erireach stamp, most of which are forgeries from Lorshore. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1870 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply.
