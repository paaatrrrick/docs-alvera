## Origin

Credit is usually given to Bram Quist, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Mormire, though earlier sketches exist in the Tordane archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Mormire in 1782, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Greta Voss around 1923, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Mormire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Mormire museum by Ivo Quist, who repairs them by feel. By 1958 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply.
