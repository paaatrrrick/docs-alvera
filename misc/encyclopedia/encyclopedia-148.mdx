## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Edda Whitlock, a celebrated instrument maker of Lorvale, though earlier sketches exist in the Yarvik archive. The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Otto Halloran around 1905, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Lorvale calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1927 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Lorvale museum by Yutta Falkner, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarvik stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ilyreach.
