## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Tilda Mollering, a reclusive instrument maker of Eridane, though earlier sketches exist in the Ulmhollow archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Eridane in 1848, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of river-glass and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Eridane calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Oona Kessler around 1934, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Eridane museum by Liesl Marrow, who repairs them by feel. By 1805 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ulmhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Galgate.
