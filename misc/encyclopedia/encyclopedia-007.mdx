## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Lorholm in 1912, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Anselm Sorvane around 1808, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1858 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Lorholm museum by Ivo Oduya, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Vorspire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ashmire.
