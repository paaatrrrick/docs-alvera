## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Halbury in 1873, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Tomas Quist, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Halbury, though earlier sketches exist in the Delholm archive. The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Annik Sorvane around 1953, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Halbury calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1826 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Delholm stamp, most of which are forgeries from Velmire. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Halbury museum by Joren Brandt, who repairs them by feel.
