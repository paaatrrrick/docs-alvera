## Origin

Credit is usually given to Vesna Halloran, a rowdy instrument maker of Pervik, though earlier sketches exist in the Yarhollow archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Pervik in 1917, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Joren Marrow around 1927, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Pervik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

By 1935 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Pervik museum by Dario Lindqvist, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ashspire.
