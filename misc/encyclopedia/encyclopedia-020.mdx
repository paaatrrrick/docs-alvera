## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Quilspire in 1868, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Tilda Holloway around 1924, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Quilspire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Quilspire museum by Yutta Kessler, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Zelvale stamp, most of which are forgeries from Zelbury. By 1901 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply.
