## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Greta Whitlock, a prosperous instrument maker of Lorhollow, though earlier sketches exist in the Selbury archive. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Lorhollow in 1905, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Lorhollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Petra Falkner around 1877, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Lorhollow museum by Halvard Oduya, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Selbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Corshore. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1806 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply.
