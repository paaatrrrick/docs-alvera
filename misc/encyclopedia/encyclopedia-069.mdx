## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Lazlo Lindqvist, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Lorvale, though earlier sketches exist in the Vormara archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Lorvale in 1899, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Lorvale calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Imre Severin around 1882, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Lorvale museum by Sanna Oduya, who repairs them by feel. By 1791 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Vormara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Torshore. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
