## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Casper Whitlock, a stubborn instrument maker of Brenmire, though earlier sketches exist in the Yarhollow archive. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Brenmire in 1939, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Brenmire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Greta Severin around 1858, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Brenmire museum by Imre Greaves, who repairs them by feel. By 1853 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Eriholm.
