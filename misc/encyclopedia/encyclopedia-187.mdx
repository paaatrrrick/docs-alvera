## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring spice tins fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Vorstead in 1794, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Rurik Brandt around 1848, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Vorstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1817 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Vorstead museum by Vesna Kessler, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Pervale stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ilydane.
