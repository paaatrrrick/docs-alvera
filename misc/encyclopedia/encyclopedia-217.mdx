## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Osshollow in 1958, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Annik Vexley, a patient instrument maker of Osshollow, though earlier sketches exist in the Torcrest archive.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Osshollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Emeric Mollering around 1864, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

By 1811 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Osshollow museum by Tomas Halloran, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Torcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Kelvik. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
