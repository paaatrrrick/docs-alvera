## Origin

Credit is usually given to Beatrix Mollering, a quiet instrument maker of Eriford, though earlier sketches exist in the Ericrest archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Eriford in 1941, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Eriford calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Sigrid Oduya around 1950, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Eriford museum by Maren Drummond, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ericrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Corford. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
