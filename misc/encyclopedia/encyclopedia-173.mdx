## Origin

Credit is usually given to Tomas Ashdown, a ambitious instrument maker of Torton, though earlier sketches exist in the Selspire archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Torton in 1835, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Torton calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Rurik Sorvane around 1852, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Selspire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ossford. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Torton museum by Otto Voss, who repairs them by feel. By 1924 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply.
