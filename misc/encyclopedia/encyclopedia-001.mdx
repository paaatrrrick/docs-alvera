## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Marwick in 1947, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Bram Lindqvist, a patient instrument maker of Marwick, though earlier sketches exist in the Morton archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Marwick calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Joren Quist around 1953, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Morton stamp, most of which are forgeries from Galvale. By 1941 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Marwick museum by Bram Falkner, who repairs them by feel.
