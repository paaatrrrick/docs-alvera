## Origin

Credit is usually given to Maren Petrov, a patient instrument maker of Marcrest, though earlier sketches exist in the Delvik archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Yutta Quist around 1924, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Marcrest calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Delvik stamp, most of which are forgeries from Velton. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Marcrest museum by Imre Petrov, who repairs them by feel. By 1924 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
