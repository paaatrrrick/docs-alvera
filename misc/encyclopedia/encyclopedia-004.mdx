## Origin

Credit is usually given to Liesl Sorvane, a reclusive instrument maker of Ilyhollow, though earlier sketches exist in the Marford archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring spice tins fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ilyhollow in 1894, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Stellan Vexley around 1927, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ilyhollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1865 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Marford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Osswick. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ilyhollow museum by Folke Oduya, who repairs them by feel.
