## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Fengate in 1827, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Tomas Mollering, a ambitious instrument maker of Fengate, though earlier sketches exist in the Ashhollow archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Fengate calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Imre Holloway around 1876, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1943 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ashhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ericrest. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Fengate museum by Rurik Severin, who repairs them by feel.
