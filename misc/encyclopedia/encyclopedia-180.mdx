## Origin

Credit is usually given to Bram Holloway, a restless instrument maker of Ulmvale, though earlier sketches exist in the Brenbury archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ulmvale in 1790, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Stellan Petrov around 1915, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ulmvale calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Brenbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Wrenshore. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ulmvale museum by Oona Mollering, who repairs them by feel. By 1893 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over almanac reprints fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
