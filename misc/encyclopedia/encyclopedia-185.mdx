## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Sanna Ashdown, a celebrated instrument maker of Torhollow, though earlier sketches exist in the Wrenmara archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Torhollow in 1931, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Anselm Marchetti around 1932, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Torhollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Torhollow museum by Lazlo Severin, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Wrenmara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ulmmire. By 1904 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
