## Origin

Credit is usually given to Stellan Sorvane, a patient instrument maker of Narreach, though earlier sketches exist in the Torwick archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Narreach in 1947, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Narreach calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Sigrid Halloran around 1923, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Torwick stamp, most of which are forgeries from Velmire. By 1879 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Narreach museum by Tomas Falkner, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
