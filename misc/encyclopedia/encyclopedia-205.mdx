## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Annik Sorvane, a prosperous instrument maker of Halton, though earlier sketches exist in the Kelstead archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Halton in 1915, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Anselm Marrow around 1891, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Halton calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Kelstead stamp, most of which are forgeries from Tormore. By 1868 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Halton museum by Anselm Greaves, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
