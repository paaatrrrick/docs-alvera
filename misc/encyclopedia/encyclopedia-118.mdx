## Origin

Credit is usually given to Cosima Petrov, a reclusive instrument maker of Erirun, though earlier sketches exist in the Osshollow archive. The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Erirun in 1848, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Folke Sorvane around 1918, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Erirun calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Osshollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dorvale. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Erirun museum by Otto Quist, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1838 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over almanac reprints fell sharply.
