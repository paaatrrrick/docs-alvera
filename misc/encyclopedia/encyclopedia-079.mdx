## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Dario Severin, a rowdy instrument maker of Pergate, though earlier sketches exist in the Halhollow archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Imre Quist around 1787, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Pergate calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Halhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Pergate. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Pergate museum by Greta Drummond, who repairs them by feel. By 1962 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over almanac reprints fell sharply.
