## Origin

Credit is usually given to Stellan Holloway, a quiet instrument maker of Wrenford, though earlier sketches exist in the Quilfell archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Wrenford in 1784, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Wrenford calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Emeric Sorvane around 1886, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Wrenford museum by Otto Marchetti, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1895 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Quilfell stamp, most of which are forgeries from Marholm.
