## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Dorfell in 1865, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Tilda Petrov, a prosperous instrument maker of Dorfell, though earlier sketches exist in the Narcrest archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Dorfell calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Rurik Marchetti around 1944, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Narcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Erishore. By 1865 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Dorfell museum by Rurik Marrow, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
