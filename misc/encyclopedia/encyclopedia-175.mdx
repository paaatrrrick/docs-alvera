## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Jorshore in 1789, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Beatrix Mollering, a stubborn instrument maker of Jorshore, though earlier sketches exist in the Tormire archive. The earliest surviving example is built of river-glass and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Oona Brandt around 1906, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Jorshore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Tormire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Tamvale. By 1868 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Jorshore museum by Sanna Oduya, who repairs them by feel.
