## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Vormore in 1937, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Yutta Whitlock, a restless instrument maker of Vormore, though earlier sketches exist in the Ulmmara archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Vormore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Dario Falkner around 1841, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Vormore museum by Edda Greaves, who repairs them by feel. By 1881 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ulmmara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Osswick.
