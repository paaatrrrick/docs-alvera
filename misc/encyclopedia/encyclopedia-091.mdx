## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Roscrest in 1901, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Yutta Holloway around 1797, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Roscrest calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Roscrest museum by Stellan Marrow, who repairs them by feel. By 1804 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Velton stamp, most of which are forgeries from Calspire.
