## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Fenwick in 1847, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Yutta Mollering, a stubborn instrument maker of Fenwick, though earlier sketches exist in the Calshore archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Tomas Kessler around 1815, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Fenwick calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Calshore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Vorfell. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Fenwick museum by Emeric Petrov, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1913 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over almanac reprints fell sharply.
