## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Stellan Kessler, a meticulous instrument maker of Eristead, though earlier sketches exist in the Quilcrest archive. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Greta Falkner around 1913, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Eristead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1924 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over almanac reprints fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Quilcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Morvale. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Eristead museum by Cosima Severin, who repairs them by feel.
