## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Casper Greaves, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Selholm, though earlier sketches exist in the Galbury archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Selholm in 1817, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Tilda Severin around 1869, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Selholm calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

By 1940 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over almanac reprints fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Galbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ashreach. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Selholm museum by Yutta Vexley, who repairs them by feel.
