## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Morshore in 1860, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Anselm Oduya, a patient instrument maker of Morshore, though earlier sketches exist in the Narmire archive. The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Liesl Oduya around 1881, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Morshore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Morshore museum by Mireille Marrow, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Narmire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Tamvik. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1806 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply.
