## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Greta Falkner, a quiet instrument maker of Calstead, though earlier sketches exist in the Veldane archive. The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Calstead in 1817, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Imre Mollering around 1817, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Calstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Veldane stamp, most of which are forgeries from Corstead. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Calstead museum by Sanna Kessler, who repairs them by feel. By 1857 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
