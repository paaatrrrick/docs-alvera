## Origin

Credit is usually given to Dario Whitlock, a restless instrument maker of Fencrest, though earlier sketches exist in the Torcrest archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Fencrest in 1860, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Fencrest calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Halvard Marrow around 1889, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

By 1875 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Torcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Rosmire. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Fencrest museum by Annik Quist, who repairs them by feel.
