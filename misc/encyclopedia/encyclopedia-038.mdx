## Origin

Credit is usually given to Cosima Drummond, a stubborn instrument maker of Dorrun, though earlier sketches exist in the Jorreach archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Dorrun in 1901, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Dorrun calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Greta Mollering around 1855, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Jorreach stamp, most of which are forgeries from Yarmire. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Dorrun museum by Annik Vexley, who repairs them by feel. By 1784 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
