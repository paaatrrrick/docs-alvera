## Origin

Credit is usually given to Sigrid Severin, a celebrated instrument maker of Fenholm, though earlier sketches exist in the Selcrest archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Fenholm in 1794, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Imre Severin around 1836, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Fenholm calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1934 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Selcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ulmcrest. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Fenholm museum by Liesl Greaves, who repairs them by feel.
