## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Osshollow in 1808, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring spice tins fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Liesl Vexley, a reclusive instrument maker of Osshollow, though earlier sketches exist in the Delwick archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Osshollow calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Sanna Whitlock around 1827, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

By 1791 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Osshollow museum by Imre Brandt, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Delwick stamp, most of which are forgeries from Selstead.
