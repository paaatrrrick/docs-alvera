## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Edda Holloway, a reclusive instrument maker of Ilydane, though earlier sketches exist in the Dorgate archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ilydane in 1948, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ilydane calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Rurik Falkner around 1905, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

By 1793 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ilydane museum by Dario Oduya, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Dorgate stamp, most of which are forgeries from Narcrest.
