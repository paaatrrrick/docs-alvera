## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Casper Oduya, a prosperous instrument maker of Velton, though earlier sketches exist in the Marton archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Velton in 1804, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Vesna Sorvane around 1935, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Velton calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

By 1884 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Marton stamp, most of which are forgeries from Narford. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Velton museum by Imre Drummond, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
