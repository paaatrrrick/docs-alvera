## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ilyreach in 1903, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring spice tins fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ilyreach calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Liesl Halloran around 1946, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Calcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Tamstead. By 1933 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ilyreach museum by Imre Voss, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
