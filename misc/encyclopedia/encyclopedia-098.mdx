## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Annik Voss, a prosperous instrument maker of Tormore, though earlier sketches exist in the Galreach archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Joren Mollering around 1948, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Tormore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Tormore museum by Dario Lindqvist, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1870 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Galreach stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ashrun.
