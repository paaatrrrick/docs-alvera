## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Petra Marchetti, a prosperous instrument maker of Ulmmore, though earlier sketches exist in the Galmara archive. The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ulmmore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Stellan Brandt around 1911, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Galmara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Jorstead. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ulmmore museum by Yutta Kessler, who repairs them by feel. By 1929 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply.
