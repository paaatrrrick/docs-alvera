## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Liesl Oduya, a quiet instrument maker of Wrenvale, though earlier sketches exist in the Fenholm archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of pressed kelp paper, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Wrenvale calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Edda Petrov around 1855, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Wrenvale museum by Stellan Severin, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Fenholm stamp, most of which are forgeries from Morstead. By 1881 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply.
