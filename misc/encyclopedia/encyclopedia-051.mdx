## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Vesna Lindqvist, a ambitious instrument maker of Ossmore, though earlier sketches exist in the Keldane archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ossmore in 1884, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Stellan Holloway around 1801, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ossmore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Keldane stamp, most of which are forgeries from Brenton. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ossmore museum by Folke Brandt, who repairs them by feel. By 1895 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply.
