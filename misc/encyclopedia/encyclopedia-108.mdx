## Origin

Credit is usually given to Ivo Brandt, a rowdy instrument maker of Ilyford, though earlier sketches exist in the Halspire archive. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ilyford in 1870, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Otto Brandt around 1825, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ilyford calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Halspire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Wrenmara. By 1917 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ilyford museum by Annik Mollering, who repairs them by feel.
