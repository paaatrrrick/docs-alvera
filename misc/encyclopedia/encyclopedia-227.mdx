## Origin

Credit is usually given to Annik Holloway, a reclusive instrument maker of Rosshore, though earlier sketches exist in the Yarmire archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Rosshore in 1889, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of wax-sealed canvas and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Rosshore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Emeric Halloran around 1784, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

By 1833 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarmire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ashton. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Rosshore museum by Rurik Voss, who repairs them by feel.
