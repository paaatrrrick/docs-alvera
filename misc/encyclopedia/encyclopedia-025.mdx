## Origin

Credit is usually given to Anselm Marrow, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Quildane, though earlier sketches exist in the Zelvik archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Quildane in 1893, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Folke Voss around 1860, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Quildane calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Quildane museum by Greta Vexley, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Zelvik stamp, most of which are forgeries from Quilspire. By 1930 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
