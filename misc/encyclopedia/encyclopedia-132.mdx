## Origin

Credit is usually given to Otto Quist, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Torgate, though earlier sketches exist in the Eriwick archive. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Torgate in 1821, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Torgate calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Otto Falkner around 1876, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

By 1913 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Torgate museum by Mireille Holloway, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Eriwick stamp, most of which are forgeries from Zelspire.
