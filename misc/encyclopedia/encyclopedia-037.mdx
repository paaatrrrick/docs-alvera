## Origin

Credit is usually given to Lazlo Ashdown, a ambitious instrument maker of Yarmore, though earlier sketches exist in the Corhollow archive. The earliest surviving example is built of river-glass and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Tilda Severin around 1870, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Yarmore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Corhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Marshore. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Yarmore museum by Vesna Quist, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1840 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply.
