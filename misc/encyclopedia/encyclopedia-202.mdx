## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Selcrest in 1954, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of hammered tin and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Bram Greaves, a meticulous instrument maker of Selcrest, though earlier sketches exist in the Kelshore archive.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Selcrest calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Halvard Ashdown around 1800, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Selcrest museum by Oona Marchetti, who repairs them by feel. By 1963 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Kelshore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Delmore. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
