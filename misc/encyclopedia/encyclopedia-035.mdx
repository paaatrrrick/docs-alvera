## Origin

Credit is usually given to Liesl Petrov, a celebrated instrument maker of Zelgate, though earlier sketches exist in the Corstead archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Zelgate in 1818, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Stellan Severin around 1827, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of fired clay, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Zelgate calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Zelgate museum by Joren Petrov, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1846 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Corstead stamp, most of which are forgeries from Morfell.
