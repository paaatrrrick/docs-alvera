## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ilygate in 1793, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Emeric Marchetti, a celebrated instrument maker of Ilygate, though earlier sketches exist in the Torcrest archive.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ilygate calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Emeric Halloran around 1950, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ilygate museum by Tilda Severin, who repairs them by feel. By 1785 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Torcrest stamp, most of which are forgeries from Eriton.
