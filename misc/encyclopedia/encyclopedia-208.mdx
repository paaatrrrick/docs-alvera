## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Velmore in 1858, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Tilda Greaves, a rowdy instrument maker of Velmore, though earlier sketches exist in the Yarford archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Velmore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Emeric Sorvane around 1889, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

By 1896 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Velmore museum by Sigrid Sorvane, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Halstead. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
