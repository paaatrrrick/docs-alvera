## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Yarmore in 1784, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Yarmore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Tomas Brandt around 1934, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ulmford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Fenshore. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Yarmore museum by Emeric Greaves, who repairs them by feel.
