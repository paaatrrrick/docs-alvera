## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Folke Halloran, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Delrun, though earlier sketches exist in the Delmara archive. The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Sigrid Mollering around 1950, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Delrun calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Delrun museum by Beatrix Vexley, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Delmara stamp, most of which are forgeries from Narreach. By 1852 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply.
