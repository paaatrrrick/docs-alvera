## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Marstead in 1910, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Marstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Cosima Greaves around 1923, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Marstead museum by Edda Oduya, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Brenshore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Zelreach. By 1942 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply.
