## Origin

Credit is usually given to Ivo Falkner, a celebrated instrument maker of Velgate, though earlier sketches exist in the Ossbury archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Velgate in 1860, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of blue slate, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Halvard Mollering around 1943, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Velgate calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Velgate museum by Tomas Falkner, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ossbury stamp, most of which are forgeries from Erifell. By 1846 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply.
