## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of whale-bone veneer and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Correach in 1829, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Greta Whitlock, a meticulous instrument maker of Correach, though earlier sketches exist in the Ossstead archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Mireille Oduya around 1958, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Correach calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ossstead stamp, most of which are forgeries from Galmara. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Correach museum by Ivo Sorvane, who repairs them by feel. By 1817 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
