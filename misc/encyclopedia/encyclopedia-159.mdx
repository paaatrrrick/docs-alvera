## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Yarvik in 1934, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Stellan Halloran, a stubborn instrument maker of Yarvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Rosgate archive. The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Yarvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Anselm Halloran around 1885, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Yarvik museum by Vesna Severin, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Rosgate stamp, most of which are forgeries from Quildane. By 1831 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply.
