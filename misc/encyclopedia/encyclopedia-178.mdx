## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ashvik in 1893, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Imre Lindqvist, a ambitious instrument maker of Ashvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Ashvale archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ashvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Oona Marrow around 1846, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ashvale stamp, most of which are forgeries from Wrendane. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ashvik museum by Halvard Halloran, who repairs them by feel. By 1869 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over loom parts fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
