## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Torshore in 1781, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Casper Falkner, a celebrated instrument maker of Torshore, though earlier sketches exist in the Wrendane archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Tomas Ashdown around 1930, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Torshore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Torshore museum by Lazlo Voss, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Wrendane stamp, most of which are forgeries from Torhollow. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1877 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply.
