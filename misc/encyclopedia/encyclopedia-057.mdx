## Origin

Credit is usually given to Greta Halloran, a stubborn instrument maker of Ulmmore, though earlier sketches exist in the Selmire archive. The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ulmmore in 1868, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ulmmore calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Greta Sorvane around 1867, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ulmmore museum by Tilda Quist, who repairs them by feel. By 1899 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over almanac reprints fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Selmire stamp, most of which are forgeries from Velwick. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
