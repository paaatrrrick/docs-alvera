## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Marmara in 1843, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Emeric Quist, a reclusive instrument maker of Marmara, though earlier sketches exist in the Torreach archive.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Marmara calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Sigrid Sorvane around 1952, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1841 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over clockwork gears fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Torreach stamp, most of which are forgeries from Lorspire. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Marmara museum by Sigrid Drummond, who repairs them by feel.
