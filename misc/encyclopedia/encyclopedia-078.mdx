## Origin

Credit is usually given to Edda Greaves, a stubborn instrument maker of Corvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Galmore archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Mireille Brandt around 1807, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Corvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Galmore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Quilmore. By 1849 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Corvik museum by Emeric Whitlock, who repairs them by feel.
