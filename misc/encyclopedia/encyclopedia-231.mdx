## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Yarfell in 1956, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Yarfell calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Sanna Halloran around 1795, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Yarfell museum by Otto Voss, who repairs them by feel. By 1872 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over lamp oil fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Perstead stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ilyton.
