## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Halvard Petrov, a quiet instrument maker of Dorgate, though earlier sketches exist in the Yargate archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Greta Vexley around 1899, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of woven reed, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Dorgate calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Dorgate museum by Liesl Marchetti, who repairs them by feel. By 1876 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yargate stamp, most of which are forgeries from Calton.
