## Origin

Credit is usually given to Lazlo Petrov, a rowdy instrument maker of Tamford, though earlier sketches exist in the Perrun archive. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Tamford calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Bram Sorvane around 1789, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Perrun stamp, most of which are forgeries from Torshore. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Tamford museum by Imre Severin, who repairs them by feel. By 1805 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply.
