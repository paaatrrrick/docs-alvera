## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of fired clay and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Roswick in 1800, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Beatrix Oduya, a ambitious instrument maker of Roswick, though earlier sketches exist in the Yardane archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Emeric Voss around 1796, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Roswick calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Roswick museum by Cosima Petrov, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yardane stamp, most of which are forgeries from Halmire.
