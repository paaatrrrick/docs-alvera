## Origin

Credit is usually given to Petra Whitlock, a celebrated instrument maker of Torwick, though earlier sketches exist in the Ossshore archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Torwick in 1799, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Maren Petrov around 1961, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ossshore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ulmford. By 1866 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Torwick museum by Otto Ashdown, who repairs them by feel.
