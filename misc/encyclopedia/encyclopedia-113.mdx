## Origin

Credit is usually given to Oona Lindqvist, a reclusive instrument maker of Ashgate, though earlier sketches exist in the Yarwick archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ashgate in 1878, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Annik Falkner around 1859, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ashgate calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

By 1959 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ashgate museum by Dario Marchetti, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarwick stamp, most of which are forgeries from Zeldane.
