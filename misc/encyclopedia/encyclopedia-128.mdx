## Origin

Credit is usually given to Liesl Ashdown, a meticulous instrument maker of Galstead, though earlier sketches exist in the Calford archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring clockwork gears fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Galstead in 1860, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Vesna Falkner around 1874, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Galstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Galstead museum by Emeric Greaves, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Calford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Eriton.
