## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Calstead in 1810, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Sanna Voss, a rowdy instrument maker of Calstead, though earlier sketches exist in the Torvik archive. The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring tar barrels fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Calstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Sanna Sorvane around 1930, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Torvik stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dorreach. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Calstead museum by Edda Greaves, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1960 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over almanac reprints fell sharply.
