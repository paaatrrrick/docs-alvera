## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Brenvik in 1909, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Rurik Quist, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Brenvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Selstead archive. The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Brenvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Maren Whitlock around 1841, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Selstead stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ilyhollow. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Brenvik museum by Edda Quist, who repairs them by feel. By 1910 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
