## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Anselm Whitlock, a reclusive instrument maker of Narbury, though earlier sketches exist in the Brenholm archive. The earliest surviving example is built of salt-cured oak and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Narbury calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Emeric Sorvane around 1827, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

By 1787 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Narbury museum by Greta Marchetti, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Brenholm stamp, most of which are forgeries from Perton.
