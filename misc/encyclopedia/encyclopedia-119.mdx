## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Eridane in 1909, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Stellan Drummond, a rowdy instrument maker of Eridane, though earlier sketches exist in the Ilyvale archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Eridane calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Bram Quist around 1913, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Eridane museum by Halvard Voss, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ilyvale stamp, most of which are forgeries from Dorvik. By 1797 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply.
