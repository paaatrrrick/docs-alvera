## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Yarstead in 1945, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of woven reed and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of whale-bone veneer, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Lazlo Severin around 1935, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Yarstead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarhollow stamp, most of which are forgeries from Halton. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Yarstead museum by Maren Petrov, who repairs them by feel. By 1838 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply.
