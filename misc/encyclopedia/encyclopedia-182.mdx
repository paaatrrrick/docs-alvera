## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring wool bales fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Imre Kessler, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Zeldane, though earlier sketches exist in the Brengate archive. The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Zeldane in 1914, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Liesl Falkner around 1938, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Zeldane calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of wax-sealed canvas, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. ## Legacy

By 1841 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over fermented tea bricks fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Zeldane museum by Tilda Halloran, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Brengate stamp, most of which are forgeries from Zelmore.
