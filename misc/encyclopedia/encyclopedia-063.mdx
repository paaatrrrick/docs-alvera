## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Eriford in 1925, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring glass floats fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Stellan Oduya, a rowdy instrument maker of Eriford, though earlier sketches exist in the Torreach archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Bram Severin around 1955, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Eriford calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Torreach stamp, most of which are forgeries from Galmara. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Eriford museum by Liesl Greaves, who repairs them by feel. By 1928 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over tar barrels fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
