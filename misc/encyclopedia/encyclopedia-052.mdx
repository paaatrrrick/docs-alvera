## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Marvik in 1810, listed without explanation between rope and candles. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring almanac reprints fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Greta Sorvane, a ambitious instrument maker of Marvik, though earlier sketches exist in the Kelholm archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Tilda Whitlock around 1937, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Marvik calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Marvik museum by Folke Holloway, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Kelholm stamp, most of which are forgeries from Delvale. By 1899 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over dye cakes fell sharply.
