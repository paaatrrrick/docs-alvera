## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Jorgate in 1943, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of blue slate and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Tilda Greaves, a quiet instrument maker of Jorgate, though earlier sketches exist in the Marvik archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring copper wire fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Bram Marrow around 1930, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of rope-fiber composite, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Jorgate calibrated theirs in secret, at night.

## Legacy

Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Marvik stamp, most of which are forgeries from Torfell. By 1934 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Jorgate museum by Mireille Sorvane, who repairs them by feel.
