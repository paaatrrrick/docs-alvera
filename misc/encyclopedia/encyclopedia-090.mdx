## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of rope-fiber composite and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Tomas Halloran, a meticulous instrument maker of Tamton, though earlier sketches exist in the Quilshore archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Tamton calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Edda Drummond around 1890, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. ## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Quilshore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Kelford. By 1943 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over copper wire fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Tamton museum by Sigrid Quist, who repairs them by feel.
