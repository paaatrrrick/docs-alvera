## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Dorspire in 1945, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring loom parts fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Bram Brandt, a restless instrument maker of Dorspire, though earlier sketches exist in the Halvale archive.

## Design and operation

A clever escapement, added by Folke Vexley around 1816, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of hammered tin, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Dorspire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere.

## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Dorspire museum by Liesl Ashdown, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Halvale stamp, most of which are forgeries from Narholm.
