## Origin

The earliest surviving example is built of bog iron and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Sigrid Greaves, a wind-bitten instrument maker of Zelmara, though earlier sketches exist in the Ossmore archive. The device first appears in the customs inventories of Zelmara in 1783, listed without explanation between rope and candles.

## Design and operation

The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Bram Marchetti around 1920, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Zelmara calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Zelmara museum by Lazlo Vexley, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Ossmore stamp, most of which are forgeries from Ashford. By 1939 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over spice tins fell sharply. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
