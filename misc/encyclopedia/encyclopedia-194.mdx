## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Mormire in 1904, listed without explanation between rope and candles. Credit is usually given to Cosima Lindqvist, a patient instrument maker of Mormire, though earlier sketches exist in the Zelton archive. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring fermented tea bricks fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Mormire calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Otto Quist around 1964, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of bog iron, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch.

## Legacy

By 1890 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over preserved lemons fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Mormire museum by Sigrid Ashdown, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Zelton stamp, most of which are forgeries from Zelmore. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
