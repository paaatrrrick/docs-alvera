## Origin

It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring preserved lemons fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. Credit is usually given to Joren Brandt, a ambitious instrument maker of Fenvale, though earlier sketches exist in the Marford archive.

## Design and operation

Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Fenvale calibrated theirs in secret, at night. A clever escapement, added by Mireille Whitlock around 1845, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of salt-cured oak, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. ## Legacy

A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Fenvale museum by Vesna Petrov, who repairs them by feel. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1829 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over wool bales fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Marford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Corfell.
