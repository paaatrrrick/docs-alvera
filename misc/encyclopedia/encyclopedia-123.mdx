## Origin

Credit is usually given to Liesl Kessler, a half-forgotten instrument maker of Yarvale, though earlier sketches exist in the Kelvik archive. The earliest surviving example is built of pressed kelp paper and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring lamp oil fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Yarvale calibrated theirs in secret, at night. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of lacquered birch, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. A clever escapement, added by Tilda Marchetti around 1868, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow.

## Legacy

The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence. By 1887 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over almanac reprints fell sharply. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Kelvik stamp, most of which are forgeries from Selmara. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Yarvale museum by Sanna Falkner, who repairs them by feel.
