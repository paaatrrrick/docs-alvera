## Origin

The device first appears in the customs inventories of Ilystead in 1913, listed without explanation between rope and candles. The earliest surviving example is built of lacquered birch and brass, and still functions, to the mild annoyance of modern replicators. It was developed to solve a specific problem: measuring dye cakes fairly when every town along the canal used different barrels. Credit is usually given to Oona Falkner, a patient instrument maker of Ilystead, though earlier sketches exist in the Yarford archive.

## Design and operation

The whole apparatus disassembles into a case the size of a bread loaf, which is why inspectors came to carry them everywhere. A clever escapement, added by Anselm Sorvane around 1884, lets the operator read the result without stopping the flow. The mechanism rests on a balanced arm of river-glass, counterweighted so that a full measure tips it past the catch. Versions differ mainly in the throat width, which towns guarded jealously; Ilystead calibrated theirs in secret, at night. ## Legacy

By 1919 it was standard equipment on every barge over a certain tonnage, and disputes over glass floats fell sharply. A working specimen is demonstrated on market days at the Ilystead museum by Bram Holloway, who repairs them by feel. Collectors now pay handsomely for examples with the Yarford stamp, most of which are forgeries from Caldane. The phrase tipping the arm survives in canal slang, meaning to settle an argument with evidence.
