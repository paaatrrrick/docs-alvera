## Overview

Zelspire sits where the ambitious lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Zelspire by the towpath from Rosrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Zelspire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its ambitious residents perfectly.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1962 after Bram Halloran petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 19 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Tilda Ashdown family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from river-glass salvaged after the Rosrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1790.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 53 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Rosrun. The cooperative founded by Bram Halloran in 1913 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Rosrun after the flood relief of 1936. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tilda Ashdown keeps the toll records of 1872 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Bram Halloran, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Zelspire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 61 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tilda Ashdown and Maren Oduya households have disputed a fence line since 1840 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month.
