## Overview

The settlement of Kelford grew up around a single warehouse in 1907, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Kelford by the towpath from Vormore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Kelford sits where the prosperous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Kelford is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its rowdy residents perfectly.

## History

The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Vormore viaduct was decommissioned in 1783. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Sigrid Quist family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 69 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1899 after Lazlo Marrow petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 14 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Vormore. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling copper wire, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Lazlo Marrow in 1916 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Vormore after the flood relief of 1915. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Sigrid Quist keeps the toll records of 1874 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Feuds here are long but mild; the Sigrid Quist and Annik Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1841 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 35 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Kelford learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Lazlo Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
