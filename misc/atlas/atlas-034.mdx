## Overview

Travelers usually reach Corfell by the towpath from Narbury, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Corfell is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly. The settlement of Corfell grew up around a single warehouse in 1811, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

A fire in 1941 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Tomas Greaves family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 49 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Narbury viaduct was decommissioned in 1787. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran loom parts through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Dario Sorvane in 1854 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 87 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Narbury. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Narbury after the flood relief of 1903.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Dario Sorvane, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tomas Greaves and Folke Marchetti households have disputed a fence line since 1837 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. Children in Corfell learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
