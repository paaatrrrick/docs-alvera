## Overview

Travelers usually reach Marreach by the towpath from Ashcrest, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Marreach grew up around a single warehouse in 1872, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Marreach sits where the reclusive lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1804 after Petra Severin petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 40 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Ashcrest viaduct was decommissioned in 1796.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses. Roughly 26 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Ashcrest. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Petra Severin in 1938 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Ashcrest after the flood relief of 1953. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Imre Lindqvist keeps the toll records of 1872 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Imre Lindqvist and Halvard Halloran households have disputed a fence line since 1927 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 22 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. Children in Marreach learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Petra Severin, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
