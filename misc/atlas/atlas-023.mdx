## Overview

Cormore is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its rowdy residents perfectly. The settlement of Cormore grew up around a single warehouse in 1843, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Cormore sits where the half-forgotten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Cormore by the towpath from Yarstead, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

The town hall was built from lacquered birch salvaged after the Yarstead viaduct was decommissioned in 1884. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran wool bales through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Liesl Falkner family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1808 after Casper Oduya petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1843 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Casper Oduya in 1838 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. Roughly 83 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Yarstead.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Liesl Falkner keeps the toll records of 1851 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Yarstead after the flood relief of 1935. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Liesl Falkner and Imre Falkner households have disputed a fence line since 1830 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Casper Oduya, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides. The town counts perhaps 65 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
