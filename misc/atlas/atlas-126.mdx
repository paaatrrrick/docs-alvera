## Overview

Most maps mark Morbury as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 90 miles in either direction. Morbury is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Morbury by the towpath from Rosbury, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1947 after Yutta Sorvane petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 68 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Rosbury viaduct was decommissioned in 1841. Old records kept by the Stellan Ashdown family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran lamp oil through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. ## Economy and trade

Roughly 68 households still work the drying sheds, turning out almanac reprints for buyers as far away as Rosbury. Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. The cooperative founded by Yutta Sorvane in 1810 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose blue slate roof was a gift from the town of Rosbury after the flood relief of 1914. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Stellan Ashdown keeps the toll records of 1859 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Yutta Sorvane, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. The town counts perhaps 39 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Morbury learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Stellan Ashdown and Annik Greaves households have disputed a fence line since 1846 without ever raising their voices.
