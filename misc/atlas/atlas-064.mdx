## Overview

Brenstead is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly. The settlement of Brenstead grew up around a single warehouse in 1819, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Brenstead by the towpath from Marhollow, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Brenstead as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 54 miles in either direction.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran lamp oil through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 77 years the town survived on a single export, dye cakes, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from pressed kelp paper salvaged after the Marhollow viaduct was decommissioned in 1834. Old records kept by the Sigrid Severin family describe a still and windless month that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1835 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 86 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Marhollow. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Marhollow after the flood relief of 1840. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Sigrid Severin keeps the toll records of 1860 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Sigrid Severin and Rurik Mollering households have disputed a fence line since 1935 without ever raising their voices. Children in Brenstead learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Lazlo Severin, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
