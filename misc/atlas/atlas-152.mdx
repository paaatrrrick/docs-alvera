## Overview

Travelers usually reach Velwick by the towpath from Cormire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Velwick grew up around a single warehouse in 1949, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Velwick is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly.

## History

Old records kept by the Maren Greaves family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1961 after Vesna Holloway petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1875 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from bog iron salvaged after the Cormire viaduct was decommissioned in 1863. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of glass floats that crosses. The cooperative founded by Vesna Holloway in 1947 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 35 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Cormire. Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Maren Greaves keeps the toll records of 1810 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Cormire after the flood relief of 1890.

## People and customs

Children in Velwick learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Vesna Holloway, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Maren Greaves and Beatrix Holloway households have disputed a fence line since 1859 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. The town counts perhaps 19 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
