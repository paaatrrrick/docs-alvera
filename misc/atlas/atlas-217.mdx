## Overview

The settlement of Ossholm grew up around a single warehouse in 1860, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Ossholm sits where the half-forgotten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Ossholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 83 miles in either direction.

## History

For nearly 45 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1930 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1810 after Emeric Oduya petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from fired clay salvaged after the Fenstead viaduct was decommissioned in 1816.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 84 households still work the drying sheds, turning out loom parts for buyers as far away as Fenstead. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses. The cooperative founded by Emeric Oduya in 1931 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Joren Quist keeps the toll records of 1851 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the restless hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose blue slate roof was a gift from the town of Fenstead after the flood relief of 1868. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Joren Quist and Petra Brandt households have disputed a fence line since 1848 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Emeric Oduya, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 72 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Ossholm learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
