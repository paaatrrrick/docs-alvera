## Overview

Delreach sits where the quiet lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Delreach is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly. Most maps mark Delreach as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 42 miles in either direction.

## History

A fire in 1825 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 36 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Imre Brandt family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

The first charter was granted in 1805 after Oona Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from whale-bone veneer salvaged after the Kelford viaduct was decommissioned in 1806.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Oona Marchetti in 1903 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. Roughly 27 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Kelford.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Imre Brandt keeps the toll records of 1829 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Kelford after the flood relief of 1903. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Oona Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Delreach learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 64 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Imre Brandt and Emeric Halloran households have disputed a fence line since 1845 without ever raising their voices.

## Getting there

The settlement of Delreach grew up around a single warehouse in 1887, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Delreach by the towpath from Kelford, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.
