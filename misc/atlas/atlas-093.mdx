## Overview

Most maps mark Velwick as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 59 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Velwick by the towpath from Dorfell, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Velwick grew up around a single warehouse in 1887, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Velwick is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly.

## History

Old records kept by the Joren Petrov family describe a still and windless month that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1789 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 68 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1918 after Joren Oduya petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 79 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Dorfell. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Joren Oduya in 1882 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Joren Petrov keeps the toll records of 1952 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Joren Oduya, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Velwick learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 77 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Joren Petrov and Tomas Whitlock households have disputed a fence line since 1834 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month.
