## Overview

Rosholm sits where the ambitious lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Rosholm by the towpath from Marshore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Rosholm is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly. Most maps mark Rosholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 44 miles in either direction.

## History

A fire in 1962 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran tar barrels through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Tomas Marchetti family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1810 after Edda Lindqvist petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Marshore viaduct was decommissioned in 1955.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 12 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Marshore. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Edda Lindqvist in 1932 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tomas Marchetti keeps the toll records of 1797 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Marshore after the flood relief of 1841. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Edda Lindqvist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tomas Marchetti and Rurik Severin households have disputed a fence line since 1818 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 32 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
