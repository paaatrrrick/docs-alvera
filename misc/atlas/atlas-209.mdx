## Overview

Travelers usually reach Fenreach by the towpath from Yarhollow, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Fenreach sits where the prosperous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Fenreach is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly.

## History

The town hall was built from woven reed salvaged after the Yarhollow viaduct was decommissioned in 1841. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran fermented tea bricks through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. ## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 80 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Yarhollow. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses. The cooperative founded by Casper Marchetti in 1834 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Otto Brandt keeps the toll records of 1847 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Yarhollow after the flood relief of 1948.

## People and customs

Children in Fenreach learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Otto Brandt and Casper Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1960 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Casper Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. The town counts perhaps 67 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
