## Overview

Yarspire sits where the wind-bitten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Yarspire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly. The settlement of Yarspire grew up around a single warehouse in 1899, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Yarspire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 61 miles in either direction.

## History

The town hall was built from hammered tin salvaged after the Quilvik viaduct was decommissioned in 1936. A fire in 1849 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1951 after Cosima Holloway petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Vesna Marrow family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 78 years the town survived on a single export, fermented tea bricks, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 68 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Quilvik. The cooperative founded by Cosima Holloway in 1854 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of glass floats that crosses.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Quilvik after the flood relief of 1959. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Vesna Marrow keeps the toll records of 1918 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Children in Yarspire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 80 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. The current harbormaster, Cosima Holloway, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
