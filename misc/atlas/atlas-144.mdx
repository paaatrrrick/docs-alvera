## Overview

Galrun is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly. Most maps mark Galrun as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 29 miles in either direction. The settlement of Galrun grew up around a single warehouse in 1799, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Galrun sits where the half-forgotten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

For nearly 77 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Anselm Voss family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1931 after Oona Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Yardane viaduct was decommissioned in 1931.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Oona Marchetti in 1857 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 33 households still work the drying sheds, turning out loom parts for buyers as far away as Yardane. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Anselm Voss keeps the toll records of 1962 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Yardane after the flood relief of 1889.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 17 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Galrun learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Oona Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Anselm Voss and Beatrix Severin households have disputed a fence line since 1874 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods.
