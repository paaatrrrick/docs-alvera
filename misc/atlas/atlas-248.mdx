## Overview

The settlement of Yarwick grew up around a single warehouse in 1817, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Yarwick by the towpath from Keldane, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Yarwick is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly. Most maps mark Yarwick as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 79 miles in either direction.

## History

The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Keldane viaduct was decommissioned in 1794. Old records kept by the Casper Vexley family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1809 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 30 years the town survived on a single export, fermented tea bricks, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

The first charter was granted in 1836 after Cosima Voss petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. ## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Cosima Voss in 1863 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses. Roughly 63 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Keldane. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, clockwork gears, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Casper Vexley keeps the toll records of 1943 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Keldane after the flood relief of 1896. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Casper Vexley and Imre Brandt households have disputed a fence line since 1911 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. Children in Yarwick learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 43 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
