## Overview

Ilyholm sits where the rowdy lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Ilyholm grew up around a single warehouse in 1934, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Ilyholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 22 miles in either direction.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 48 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1892 after Emeric Ashdown petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1907 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the lacquered birch workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 16 households still work the drying sheds, turning out almanac reprints for buyers as far away as Kelstead. The cooperative founded by Emeric Ashdown in 1904 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Kelstead after the flood relief of 1941. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Stellan Falkner and Yutta Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1799 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 16 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Ilyholm learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Emeric Ashdown, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
