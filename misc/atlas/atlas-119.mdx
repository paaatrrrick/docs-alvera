## Overview

Travelers usually reach Lorbury by the towpath from Brenrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Lorbury as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 78 miles in either direction. Lorbury sits where the meticulous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

A fire in 1926 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from hammered tin salvaged after the Brenrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1952. For nearly 56 years the town survived on a single export, fermented tea bricks, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. ## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 56 households still work the drying sheds, turning out loom parts for buyers as far away as Brenrun. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Mireille Ashdown keeps the toll records of 1867 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Mireille Ashdown and Edda Severin households have disputed a fence line since 1897 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 17 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Casper Voss, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Lorbury learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods.
