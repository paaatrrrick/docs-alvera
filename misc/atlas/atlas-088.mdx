## Overview

Quilmara is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly. The settlement of Quilmara grew up around a single warehouse in 1905, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Quilmara as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 68 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Quilmara by the towpath from Delholm, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling copper wire, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Stellan Vexley in 1784 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 17 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Delholm.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Maren Holloway keeps the toll records of 1913 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Delholm after the flood relief of 1881.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. The town counts perhaps 20 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Stellan Vexley, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Quilmara learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
