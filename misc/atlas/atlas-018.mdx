## Overview

Most maps mark Tamvik as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 26 miles in either direction. Tamvik is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its ambitious residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Tamvik by the towpath from Jormore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1849 after Liesl Mollering petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Yutta Vexley family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1854 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 12 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 35 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Jormore. The cooperative founded by Liesl Mollering in 1927 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, clockwork gears, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Yutta Vexley keeps the toll records of 1942 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Jormore after the flood relief of 1948. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Children in Tamvik learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Yutta Vexley and Maren Sorvane households have disputed a fence line since 1941 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. The town counts perhaps 56 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Liesl Mollering, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
