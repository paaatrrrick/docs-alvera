## Overview

Halmara sits where the quiet lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Halmara is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its celebrated residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Halmara by the towpath from Lorgate, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1942 after Stellan Sorvane petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1916 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Annik Vexley family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 27 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 69 households still work the drying sheds, turning out almanac reprints for buyers as far away as Lorgate. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Stellan Sorvane in 1788 now handles most of the clockwork gears trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Lorgate after the flood relief of 1908. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Annik Vexley keeps the toll records of 1867 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Annik Vexley and Ivo Ashdown households have disputed a fence line since 1877 without ever raising their voices. Children in Halmara learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Stellan Sorvane, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month.
