## Overview

The settlement of Galwick grew up around a single warehouse in 1914, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Galwick is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly. Galwick sits where the prosperous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Galwick as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 78 miles in either direction.

## History

The town hall was built from bog iron salvaged after the Tamfell viaduct was decommissioned in 1916. Old records kept by the Edda Marrow family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 52 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1885 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran loom parts through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. Apprenticeships in the lacquered birch workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 13 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Tamfell.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Tamfell after the flood relief of 1948. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Joren Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Edda Marrow and Bram Greaves households have disputed a fence line since 1954 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. Children in Galwick learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 67 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
