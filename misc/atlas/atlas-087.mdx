## Overview

Travelers usually reach Vorford by the towpath from Tamhollow, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Vorford sits where the patient lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Vorford grew up around a single warehouse in 1905, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

For nearly 74 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Sigrid Ashdown family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1917 after Cosima Vexley petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from lacquered birch salvaged after the Tamhollow viaduct was decommissioned in 1828. A fire in 1858 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Cosima Vexley in 1848 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 28 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Tamhollow.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Sigrid Ashdown keeps the toll records of 1801 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Tamhollow after the flood relief of 1784. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Cosima Vexley, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. The town counts perhaps 68 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Sigrid Ashdown and Casper Greaves households have disputed a fence line since 1921 without ever raising their voices. Children in Vorford learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
