## Overview

The settlement of Vorwick grew up around a single warehouse in 1833, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Vorwick as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 89 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Vorwick by the towpath from Ilymore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

For nearly 63 years the town survived on a single export, clockwork gears, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1935 after Annik Voss petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. ## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Annik Voss in 1796 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 86 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Ilymore.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Ilymore after the flood relief of 1843. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Feuds here are long but mild; the Ivo Kessler and Stellan Voss households have disputed a fence line since 1874 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Annik Voss, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Vorwick learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
