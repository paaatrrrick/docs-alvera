## Overview

Travelers usually reach Delshore by the towpath from Tammire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Delshore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 44 miles in either direction. The settlement of Delshore grew up around a single warehouse in 1786, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Tammire viaduct was decommissioned in 1883. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1887 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. ## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 20 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Tammire. The cooperative founded by Maren Greaves in 1962 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, clockwork gears, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Stellan Voss keeps the toll records of 1887 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Tammire after the flood relief of 1923. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. The town counts perhaps 90 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Delshore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Stellan Voss and Bram Petrov households have disputed a fence line since 1799 without ever raising their voices.
