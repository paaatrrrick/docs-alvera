## Overview

Travelers usually reach Lorvik by the towpath from Delbury, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Lorvik sits where the ambitious lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Lorvik is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly.

## History

For nearly 65 years the town survived on a single export, loom parts, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Delbury viaduct was decommissioned in 1838. The first charter was granted in 1843 after Tomas Vexley petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Bram Brandt family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Tomas Vexley in 1893 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling copper wire, clockwork gears, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Bram Brandt keeps the toll records of 1943 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Delbury after the flood relief of 1923. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. The town counts perhaps 74 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Bram Brandt and Folke Falkner households have disputed a fence line since 1896 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Tomas Vexley, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Lorvik learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
