## Overview

Most maps mark Ulmhollow as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 69 miles in either direction. Ulmhollow is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its celebrated residents perfectly. Ulmhollow sits where the half-forgotten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

A fire in 1824 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Folke Brandt family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 14 years the town survived on a single export, lamp oil, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1867 after Folke Quist petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. The cooperative founded by Folke Quist in 1847 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 52 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Calcrest.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Calcrest after the flood relief of 1870. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Folke Brandt keeps the toll records of 1809 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. The town counts perhaps 86 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Folke Quist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Ulmhollow learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Folke Brandt and Petra Voss households have disputed a fence line since 1947 without ever raising their voices.
