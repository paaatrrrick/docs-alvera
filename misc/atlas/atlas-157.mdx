## Overview

Permore sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Permore grew up around a single warehouse in 1942, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Permore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 81 miles in either direction.

## History

The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Ilystead viaduct was decommissioned in 1936. Old records kept by the Edda Lindqvist family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 37 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1906 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 16 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Ilystead. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Folke Drummond in 1933 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Ilystead after the flood relief of 1886. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Edda Lindqvist keeps the toll records of 1841 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it.

## People and customs

Children in Permore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Edda Lindqvist and Sigrid Oduya households have disputed a fence line since 1891 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Folke Drummond, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 17 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
