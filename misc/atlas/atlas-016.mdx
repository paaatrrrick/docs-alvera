## Overview

Wrenhollow is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Wrenhollow by the towpath from Ossholm, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Wrenhollow as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 68 miles in either direction. The settlement of Wrenhollow grew up around a single warehouse in 1804, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

Old records kept by the Maren Severin family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1873 after Annik Falkner petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran wool bales through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1951 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 70 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Ossholm. The cooperative founded by Annik Falkner in 1964 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Ossholm after the flood relief of 1872. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Maren Severin keeps the toll records of 1914 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Annik Falkner, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Maren Severin and Sanna Quist households have disputed a fence line since 1815 without ever raising their voices. Children in Wrenhollow learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. The town counts perhaps 52 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
