## Overview

The settlement of Ashgate grew up around a single warehouse in 1808, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Ashgate as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 86 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Ashgate by the towpath from Kelton, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Rurik Whitlock family describe a still and windless month that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1850 after Emeric Marrow petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Kelton viaduct was decommissioned in 1956.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Emeric Marrow in 1843 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 75 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Kelton. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Rurik Whitlock keeps the toll records of 1848 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose wax-sealed canvas roof was a gift from the town of Kelton after the flood relief of 1964. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Children in Ashgate learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Rurik Whitlock and Emeric Greaves households have disputed a fence line since 1952 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 27 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Emeric Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
