## Overview

Travelers usually reach Marstead by the towpath from Ossvale, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Marstead sits where the wind-bitten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Marstead is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly.

## History

The town hall was built from woven reed salvaged after the Ossvale viaduct was decommissioned in 1938. The first charter was granted in 1849 after Halvard Greaves petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Tilda Mollering family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 89 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Ossvale. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses. Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Halvard Greaves in 1803 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling copper wire, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tilda Mollering keeps the toll records of 1912 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Ossvale after the flood relief of 1929.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Halvard Greaves, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. Children in Marstead learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tilda Mollering and Lazlo Severin households have disputed a fence line since 1847 without ever raising their voices.
