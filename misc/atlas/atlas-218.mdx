## Overview

Most maps mark Lordane as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 71 miles in either direction. The settlement of Lordane grew up around a single warehouse in 1964, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Lordane sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1904 after Dario Whitlock petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Cosima Drummond family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 45 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1884 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses. The cooperative founded by Dario Whitlock in 1869 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 67 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Calford. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling copper wire, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Calford after the flood relief of 1934. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Cosima Drummond keeps the toll records of 1872 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Cosima Drummond and Mireille Greaves households have disputed a fence line since 1921 without ever raising their voices. Children in Lordane learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. The town counts perhaps 74 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
