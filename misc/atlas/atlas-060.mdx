## Overview

The settlement of Delstead grew up around a single warehouse in 1897, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Delstead sits where the stubborn lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Delstead is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly.

## History

For nearly 28 years the town survived on a single export, dye cakes, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Oona Halloran family describe a still and windless month that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1928 after Tomas Vexley petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from whale-bone veneer salvaged after the Jordane viaduct was decommissioned in 1918.

A fire in 1847 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran tar barrels through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. ## Economy and trade

Roughly 23 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Jordane. The cooperative founded by Tomas Vexley in 1951 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Jordane after the flood relief of 1891. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Oona Halloran and Sigrid Vexley households have disputed a fence line since 1916 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. Children in Delstead learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Tomas Vexley, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
