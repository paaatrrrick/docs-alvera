## Overview

The settlement of Perspire grew up around a single warehouse in 1875, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Perspire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its celebrated residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Perspire by the towpath from Narreach, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Perspire sits where the ambitious lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran almanac reprints through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1842 after Dario Sorvane petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Narreach viaduct was decommissioned in 1940. ## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Dario Sorvane in 1894 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 89 households still work the drying sheds, turning out almanac reprints for buyers as far away as Narreach.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Narreach after the flood relief of 1832. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Ivo Whitlock keeps the toll records of 1925 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Dario Sorvane, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 78 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Ivo Whitlock and Stellan Quist households have disputed a fence line since 1935 without ever raising their voices.

## Getting there

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the celebrated hills behind it. Most maps mark Perspire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 55 miles in either direction.
