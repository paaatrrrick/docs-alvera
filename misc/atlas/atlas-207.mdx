## Overview

Ashreach is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly. The settlement of Ashreach grew up around a single warehouse in 1810, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Ashreach sits where the patient lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran wool bales through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1847 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 15 years the town survived on a single export, dye cakes, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Rurik Voss family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. ## Economy and trade

Roughly 15 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Kelholm. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. The cooperative founded by Casper Brandt in 1940 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Rurik Voss keeps the toll records of 1798 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides. The current harbormaster, Casper Brandt, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Rurik Voss and Ivo Mollering households have disputed a fence line since 1839 without ever raising their voices. Children in Ashreach learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
