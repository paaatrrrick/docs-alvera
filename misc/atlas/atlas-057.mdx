## Overview

Selholm is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly. The settlement of Selholm grew up around a single warehouse in 1963, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Selholm by the towpath from Velmire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran fermented tea bricks through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 43 years the town survived on a single export, dye cakes, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1878 after Beatrix Lindqvist petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Cosima Vexley family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Beatrix Lindqvist in 1819 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Cosima Vexley keeps the toll records of 1912 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Velmire after the flood relief of 1919. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Beatrix Lindqvist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Cosima Vexley and Petra Sorvane households have disputed a fence line since 1884 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 72 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides.
