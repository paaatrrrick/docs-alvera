## Overview

Kelstead is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly. Kelstead sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Kelstead as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 37 miles in either direction.

## History

Old records kept by the Halvard Drummond family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 64 years the town survived on a single export, loom parts, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1947 after Liesl Marrow petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Galfell viaduct was decommissioned in 1911. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran fermented tea bricks through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. The cooperative founded by Liesl Marrow in 1812 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Halvard Drummond keeps the toll records of 1853 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 47 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Halvard Drummond and Yutta Sorvane households have disputed a fence line since 1897 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Liesl Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Kelstead learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
