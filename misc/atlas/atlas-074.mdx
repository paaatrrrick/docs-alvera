## Overview

Most maps mark Marrun as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 22 miles in either direction. Marrun sits where the meticulous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Marrun is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly. The settlement of Marrun grew up around a single warehouse in 1932, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1896 after Tomas Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1925 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Ashreach viaduct was decommissioned in 1911. Old records kept by the Tilda Drummond family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 48 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Ashreach.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tilda Drummond keeps the toll records of 1914 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Ashreach after the flood relief of 1911.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Tilda Drummond and Stellan Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1796 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 53 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Marrun learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Tomas Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
