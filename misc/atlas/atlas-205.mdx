## Overview

Travelers usually reach Vorwick by the towpath from Marmore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Vorwick is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly. Vorwick sits where the prosperous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Vorwick grew up around a single warehouse in 1928, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

For nearly 71 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Cosima Halloran family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Marmore viaduct was decommissioned in 1892. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran lamp oil through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

A fire in 1879 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 32 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from fired clay salvaged after the Marmore viaduct was decommissioned in 1811. The first charter was granted in 1826 after Sigrid Greaves petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Sigrid Greaves in 1933 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. Roughly 70 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Marmore. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Cosima Halloran keeps the toll records of 1804 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Marmore after the flood relief of 1840.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 58 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Cosima Halloran and Maren Whitlock households have disputed a fence line since 1854 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Sigrid Greaves, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. Children in Vorwick learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
