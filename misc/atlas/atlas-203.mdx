## Overview

Vorholm sits where the ambitious lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Vorholm by the towpath from Ossmore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Vorholm is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its stubborn residents perfectly. Most maps mark Vorholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 71 miles in either direction.

A fire in 1914 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. ## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Lazlo Brandt in 1877 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. Roughly 54 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Ossmore.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Rurik Vexley keeps the toll records of 1792 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Ossmore after the flood relief of 1805. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 69 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Feuds here are long but mild; the Rurik Vexley and Stellan Halloran households have disputed a fence line since 1933 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Lazlo Brandt, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Vorholm learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
