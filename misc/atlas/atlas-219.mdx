## Overview

Most maps mark Yarton as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 12 miles in either direction. The settlement of Yarton grew up around a single warehouse in 1845, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Yarton is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Yarton by the towpath from Morvik, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

Old records kept by the Dario Marchetti family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1925 after Joren Petrov petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1839 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Joren Petrov in 1809 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 64 households still work the drying sheds, turning out clockwork gears for buyers as far away as Morvik.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Dario Marchetti keeps the toll records of 1782 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the celebrated hills behind it.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Dario Marchetti and Joren Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1834 without ever raising their voices. Children in Yarton learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 57 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. The current harbormaster, Joren Petrov, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
