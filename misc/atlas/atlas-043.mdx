## Overview

Lorhollow is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly. The settlement of Lorhollow grew up around a single warehouse in 1963, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Lorhollow sits where the stubborn lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Yarhollow viaduct was decommissioned in 1821. For nearly 69 years the town survived on a single export, dye cakes, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Greta Oduya family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 82 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Yarhollow. Apprenticeships in the lacquered birch workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Maren Marchetti in 1917 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Greta Oduya keeps the toll records of 1805 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it.

## People and customs

Children in Lorhollow learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Maren Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Greta Oduya and Emeric Whitlock households have disputed a fence line since 1888 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. The town counts perhaps 35 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
