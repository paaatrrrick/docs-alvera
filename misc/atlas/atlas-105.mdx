## Overview

Travelers usually reach Halfell by the towpath from Marrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Halfell is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly. Most maps mark Halfell as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 33 miles in either direction.

## History

The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Marrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1951. Old records kept by the Edda Petrov family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 67 years the town survived on a single export, loom parts, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1913 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Emeric Kessler in 1818 now handles most of the clockwork gears trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 37 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Marrun. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Edda Petrov keeps the toll records of 1941 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Marrun after the flood relief of 1924. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Edda Petrov and Annik Oduya households have disputed a fence line since 1902 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Emeric Kessler, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Halfell learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies.

## Getting there

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Halfell sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Halfell as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 61 miles in either direction.
