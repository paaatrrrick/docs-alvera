## Overview

Travelers usually reach Lorstead by the towpath from Galton, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Lorstead grew up around a single warehouse in 1927, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Lorstead is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its rowdy residents perfectly. Most maps mark Lorstead as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 14 miles in either direction.

## History

For nearly 41 years the town survived on a single export, tar barrels, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1858 after Mireille Lindqvist petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Galton viaduct was decommissioned in 1814.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 55 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Galton. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Mireille Lindqvist in 1957 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Galton after the flood relief of 1782. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Sigrid Falkner keeps the toll records of 1907 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it.

## People and customs

Children in Lorstead learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Sigrid Falkner and Stellan Mollering households have disputed a fence line since 1813 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Mireille Lindqvist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month.
