## Overview

Most maps mark Zelwick as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 55 miles in either direction. Zelwick is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Zelwick by the towpath from Ilyholm, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

A fire in 1863 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Ilyholm viaduct was decommissioned in 1795. The first charter was granted in 1813 after Dario Ashdown petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Emeric Greaves family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran loom parts through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 20 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Ilyholm. The cooperative founded by Dario Ashdown in 1835 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Emeric Greaves keeps the toll records of 1792 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Emeric Greaves and Ivo Voss households have disputed a fence line since 1853 without ever raising their voices. Children in Zelwick learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Dario Ashdown, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season.
