## Overview

Travelers usually reach Pervik by the towpath from Marstead, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Pervik grew up around a single warehouse in 1898, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Pervik is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly. Most maps mark Pervik as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 38 miles in either direction.

## History

The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Marstead viaduct was decommissioned in 1870. Old records kept by the Folke Mollering family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 20 years the town survived on a single export, clockwork gears, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran wool bales through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1800 after Bram Drummond petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Bram Drummond in 1821 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 23 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Marstead. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Folke Mollering keeps the toll records of 1825 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose wax-sealed canvas roof was a gift from the town of Marstead after the flood relief of 1905.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Bram Drummond, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Pervik learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 42 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Folke Mollering and Liesl Voss households have disputed a fence line since 1927 without ever raising their voices.
