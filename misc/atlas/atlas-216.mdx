## Overview

The settlement of Dorspire grew up around a single warehouse in 1788, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Dorspire by the towpath from Kelspire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Dorspire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 23 miles in either direction. Dorspire sits where the reclusive lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Kelspire viaduct was decommissioned in 1859. The first charter was granted in 1875 after Greta Holloway petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 60 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 84 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Kelspire. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. The cooperative founded by Greta Holloway in 1824 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Kelspire after the flood relief of 1870. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Annik Marrow keeps the toll records of 1939 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Annik Marrow and Halvard Quist households have disputed a fence line since 1931 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Greta Holloway, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. The town counts perhaps 89 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
