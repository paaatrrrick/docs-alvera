## Overview

The settlement of Marmire grew up around a single warehouse in 1869, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Marmire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly. Marmire sits where the meticulous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Marmire by the towpath from Torhollow, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

Old records kept by the Rurik Mollering family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1941 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 87 years the town survived on a single export, fermented tea bricks, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Torhollow viaduct was decommissioned in 1954. The first charter was granted in 1894 after Dario Petrov petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses. The cooperative founded by Dario Petrov in 1945 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 27 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Torhollow.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Torhollow after the flood relief of 1835. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Rurik Mollering keeps the toll records of 1949 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Dario Petrov, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 79 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Marmire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Rurik Mollering and Greta Falkner households have disputed a fence line since 1920 without ever raising their voices.
