## Overview

Travelers usually reach Calshore by the towpath from Narshore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Calshore sits where the prosperous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Calshore grew up around a single warehouse in 1892, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

For nearly 57 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1925 after Cosima Vexley petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Bram Vexley family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1904 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling copper wire, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Cosima Vexley in 1882 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Narshore after the flood relief of 1829. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Bram Vexley keeps the toll records of 1798 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it.

## People and customs

Children in Calshore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. The town counts perhaps 81 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Cosima Vexley, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Bram Vexley and Greta Halloran households have disputed a fence line since 1867 without ever raising their voices.
