## Overview

The settlement of Vorton grew up around a single warehouse in 1840, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Vorton as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 24 miles in either direction. Vorton is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1893 after Sigrid Falkner petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from river-glass salvaged after the Quilholm viaduct was decommissioned in 1907. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran almanac reprints through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1792 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

Old records kept by the Casper Lindqvist family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 61 years the town survived on a single export, fermented tea bricks, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. ## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Sigrid Falkner in 1869 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 87 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Quilholm.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Quilholm after the flood relief of 1917. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Casper Lindqvist keeps the toll records of 1834 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Sigrid Falkner, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Casper Lindqvist and Stellan Whitlock households have disputed a fence line since 1831 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 65 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Vorton learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods.
