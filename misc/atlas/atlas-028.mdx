## Overview

Corcrest is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Corcrest by the towpath from Yarstead, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Corcrest grew up around a single warehouse in 1818, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

For nearly 74 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1792 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Casper Lindqvist family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. ## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Edda Severin in 1826 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 82 households still work the drying sheds, turning out clockwork gears for buyers as far away as Yarstead. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Yarstead after the flood relief of 1825. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Casper Lindqvist keeps the toll records of 1873 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 27 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Casper Lindqvist and Otto Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1852 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. The current harbormaster, Edda Severin, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
