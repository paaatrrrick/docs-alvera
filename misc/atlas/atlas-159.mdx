## Overview

Morford is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly. Morford sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Morford as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 65 miles in either direction. The settlement of Morford grew up around a single warehouse in 1933, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Yarbury viaduct was decommissioned in 1881. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 25 years the town survived on a single export, clockwork gears, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1870 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Rurik Voss in 1930 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 70 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Yarbury. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses. Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling spice tins, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Anselm Marrow keeps the toll records of 1860 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Yarbury after the flood relief of 1828. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the restless hills behind it.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Anselm Marrow and Vesna Whitlock households have disputed a fence line since 1894 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Rurik Voss, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. The town counts perhaps 40 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
