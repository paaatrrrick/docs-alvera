## Overview

Travelers usually reach Velcrest by the towpath from Calvale, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Velcrest as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 84 miles in either direction. Velcrest is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1957 after Anselm Falkner petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 37 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Maren Falkner family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from hammered tin salvaged after the Calvale viaduct was decommissioned in 1817.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 70 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Calvale. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses. The cooperative founded by Anselm Falkner in 1908 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Maren Falkner keeps the toll records of 1901 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Calvale after the flood relief of 1935. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 90 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Maren Falkner and Lazlo Severin households have disputed a fence line since 1914 without ever raising their voices. Children in Velcrest learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Anselm Falkner, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
