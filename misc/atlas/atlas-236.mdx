## Overview

Travelers usually reach Halmara by the towpath from Morholm, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Halmara grew up around a single warehouse in 1810, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Halmara as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 46 miles in either direction.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran clockwork gears through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Greta Whitlock family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 15 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1945 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1861 after Anselm Kessler petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 13 households still work the drying sheds, turning out clockwork gears for buyers as far away as Morholm. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses. The cooperative founded by Anselm Kessler in 1801 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Morholm after the flood relief of 1934. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Greta Whitlock keeps the toll records of 1954 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. Children in Halmara learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Anselm Kessler, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 59 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
