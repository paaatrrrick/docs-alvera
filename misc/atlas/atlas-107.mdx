## Overview

The settlement of Pervale grew up around a single warehouse in 1833, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Pervale by the towpath from Galfell, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Pervale as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 13 miles in either direction. Pervale sits where the quiet lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

Old records kept by the Ivo Drummond family describe a still and windless month that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1886 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1948 after Otto Petrov petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from pressed kelp paper salvaged after the Galfell viaduct was decommissioned in 1810. For nearly 61 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Otto Petrov in 1812 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 36 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Galfell. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Galfell after the flood relief of 1815. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Ivo Drummond keeps the toll records of 1892 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it.

## People and customs

Children in Pervale learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 42 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. The current harbormaster, Otto Petrov, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
