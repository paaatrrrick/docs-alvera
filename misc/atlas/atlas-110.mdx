## Overview

Most maps mark Galvik as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 15 miles in either direction. The settlement of Galvik grew up around a single warehouse in 1786, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Galvik by the towpath from Ossford, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Greta Marchetti family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 24 years the town survived on a single export, loom parts, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1923 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1893 after Otto Quist petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 56 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Ossford. The cooperative founded by Otto Quist in 1829 now handles most of the clockwork gears trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Ossford after the flood relief of 1831. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Greta Marchetti keeps the toll records of 1898 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 38 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Greta Marchetti and Vesna Halloran households have disputed a fence line since 1833 without ever raising their voices. Children in Galvik learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides. The current harbormaster, Otto Quist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.

## Getting there

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Ossford after the flood relief of 1795. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Galvik sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.
