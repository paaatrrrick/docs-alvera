## Overview

Rosmire sits where the half-forgotten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Rosmire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 74 miles in either direction. Rosmire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly.

## History

Old records kept by the Casper Mollering family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1901 after Annik Holloway petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from woven reed salvaged after the Galfell viaduct was decommissioned in 1853. For nearly 29 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran loom parts through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 58 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Galfell. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of glass floats that crosses. The cooperative founded by Annik Holloway in 1945 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the restless hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Galfell after the flood relief of 1831. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Annik Holloway, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Rosmire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 54 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Casper Mollering and Folke Falkner households have disputed a fence line since 1954 without ever raising their voices.
