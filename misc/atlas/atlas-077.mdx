## Overview

Most maps mark Calholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 86 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Calholm by the towpath from Lormire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Calholm sits where the wind-bitten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

Old records kept by the Emeric Severin family describe a still and windless month that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1853 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Lormire viaduct was decommissioned in 1800. The first charter was granted in 1866 after Otto Quist petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 63 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Lormire. Apprenticeships in the salt-cured oak workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Otto Quist in 1926 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Emeric Severin keeps the toll records of 1952 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. ## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Otto Quist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. Children in Calholm learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 19 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
