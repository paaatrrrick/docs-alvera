## Overview

The settlement of Narmire grew up around a single warehouse in 1802, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Narmire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 64 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Narmire by the towpath from Brenstead, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1827 after Rurik Severin petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 70 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Annik Severin family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. Roughly 58 households still work the drying sheds, turning out loom parts for buyers as far away as Brenstead. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Rurik Severin in 1841 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Brenstead after the flood relief of 1934. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Annik Severin keeps the toll records of 1796 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. Feuds here are long but mild; the Annik Severin and Joren Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1958 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 19 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Rurik Severin, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Narmire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
