## Overview

Perreach sits where the quiet lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Perreach as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 54 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Perreach by the towpath from Vorvale, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

The town hall was built from river-glass salvaged after the Vorvale viaduct was decommissioned in 1900. For nearly 89 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1798 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. ## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Otto Petrov in 1893 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 17 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Vorvale. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Emeric Holloway keeps the toll records of 1890 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Otto Petrov, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 76 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Emeric Holloway and Emeric Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1961 without ever raising their voices. Children in Perreach learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
