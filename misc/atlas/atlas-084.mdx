## Overview

Travelers usually reach Rosvik by the towpath from Vorvale, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Rosvik sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Rosvik grew up around a single warehouse in 1868, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Rosvik as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 76 miles in either direction.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 51 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1935 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from woven reed salvaged after the Vorvale viaduct was decommissioned in 1952.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Oona Marchetti in 1782 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 74 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Vorvale. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Oona Kessler keeps the toll records of 1819 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Vorvale after the flood relief of 1853.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. The current harbormaster, Oona Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 53 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Rosvik learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Oona Kessler and Emeric Sorvane households have disputed a fence line since 1787 without ever raising their voices.
