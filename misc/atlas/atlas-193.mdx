## Overview

Most maps mark Kelcrest as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 22 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Kelcrest by the towpath from Rosrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Kelcrest is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its ambitious residents perfectly. The settlement of Kelcrest grew up around a single warehouse in 1847, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

For nearly 32 years the town survived on a single export, dye cakes, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from bog iron salvaged after the Rosrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1792. The first charter was granted in 1903 after Joren Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1793 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Sanna Sorvane family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 47 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Rosrun. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses. The cooperative founded by Joren Marchetti in 1880 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Sanna Sorvane keeps the toll records of 1908 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Rosrun after the flood relief of 1850. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Sanna Sorvane and Lazlo Oduya households have disputed a fence line since 1941 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides. Children in Kelcrest learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Joren Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
