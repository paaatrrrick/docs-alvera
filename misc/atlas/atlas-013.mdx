## Overview

Wrenspire sits where the half-forgotten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Wrenspire by the towpath from Permara, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Wrenspire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly.

## History

The town hall was built from hammered tin salvaged after the Permara viaduct was decommissioned in 1900. A fire in 1835 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1886 after Bram Mollering petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Halvard Lindqvist family describe a still and windless month that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 16 years the town survived on a single export, fermented tea bricks, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Bram Mollering in 1792 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 68 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Permara. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Halvard Lindqvist keeps the toll records of 1839 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Permara after the flood relief of 1832.

## People and customs

Children in Wrenspire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 29 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Halvard Lindqvist and Casper Quist households have disputed a fence line since 1783 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Bram Mollering, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
