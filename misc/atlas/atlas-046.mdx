## Overview

Kelfell is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Kelfell by the towpath from Velshore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Kelfell sits where the half-forgotten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

For nearly 65 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1832 after Cosima Severin petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1954 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Velshore viaduct was decommissioned in 1835.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. Roughly 52 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Velshore.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Vesna Severin keeps the toll records of 1874 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose wax-sealed canvas roof was a gift from the town of Velshore after the flood relief of 1879. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 89 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Vesna Severin and Greta Holloway households have disputed a fence line since 1858 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Children in Kelfell learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
