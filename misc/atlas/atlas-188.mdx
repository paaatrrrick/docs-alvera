## Overview

The settlement of Perbury grew up around a single warehouse in 1962, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Perbury as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 79 miles in either direction. Perbury sits where the rowdy lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Perbury is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 38 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1787 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Sigrid Oduya family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1930 after Stellan Falkner petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 48 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Wrenmara. The cooperative founded by Stellan Falkner in 1831 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Sigrid Oduya keeps the toll records of 1927 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Wrenmara after the flood relief of 1914.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Sigrid Oduya and Tilda Marchetti households have disputed a fence line since 1917 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 63 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Stellan Falkner, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season.
