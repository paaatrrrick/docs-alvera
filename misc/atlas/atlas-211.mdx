## Overview

Travelers usually reach Roshollow by the towpath from Ilywick, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Roshollow as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 76 miles in either direction. The settlement of Roshollow grew up around a single warehouse in 1945, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Roshollow is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly.

## History

The town hall was built from hammered tin salvaged after the Ilywick viaduct was decommissioned in 1896. For nearly 30 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1927 after Rurik Greaves petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Rurik Greaves in 1949 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 67 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Ilywick. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Maren Drummond keeps the toll records of 1935 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Ilywick after the flood relief of 1784. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 81 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Rurik Greaves, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Feuds here are long but mild; the Maren Drummond and Imre Brandt households have disputed a fence line since 1788 without ever raising their voices. Children in Roshollow learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
