## Overview

Selrun sits where the reclusive lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Selrun as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 76 miles in either direction. The settlement of Selrun grew up around a single warehouse in 1866, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Selrun is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1952 after Petra Mollering petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1785 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 85 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Ossvik viaduct was decommissioned in 1794.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 50 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Ossvik. The cooperative founded by Petra Mollering in 1911 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. ## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Dario Vexley keeps the toll records of 1921 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose blue slate roof was a gift from the town of Ossvik after the flood relief of 1798.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides. The current harbormaster, Petra Mollering, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 86 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Dario Vexley and Folke Vexley households have disputed a fence line since 1929 without ever raising their voices.
