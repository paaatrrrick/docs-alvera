## Overview

Nardane sits where the prosperous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Nardane by the towpath from Halwick, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Nardane is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly.

## History

The town hall was built from river-glass salvaged after the Halwick viaduct was decommissioned in 1791. For nearly 75 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1958 after Oona Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Anselm Kessler family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 18 households still work the drying sheds, turning out loom parts for buyers as far away as Halwick. The cooperative founded by Oona Marchetti in 1875 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Anselm Kessler keeps the toll records of 1788 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Halwick after the flood relief of 1956.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Children in Nardane learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Anselm Kessler and Joren Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1957 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 43 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
