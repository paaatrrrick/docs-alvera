## Overview

The settlement of Selstead grew up around a single warehouse in 1952, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Selstead as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 66 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Selstead by the towpath from Torrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Selstead is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran loom parts through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1950 after Vesna Brandt petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1879 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Yutta Quist family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 81 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Torrun. The cooperative founded by Vesna Brandt in 1793 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Torrun after the flood relief of 1864. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Yutta Quist keeps the toll records of 1819 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Children in Selstead learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 31 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Feuds here are long but mild; the Yutta Quist and Dario Vexley households have disputed a fence line since 1923 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Vesna Brandt, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
