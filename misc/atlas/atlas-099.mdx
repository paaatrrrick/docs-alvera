## Overview

Rosreach is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its stubborn residents perfectly. Rosreach sits where the rowdy lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Rosreach as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 59 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Rosreach by the towpath from Ossmire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 23 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Greta Holloway family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from woven reed salvaged after the Ossmire viaduct was decommissioned in 1827. A fire in 1884 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Sanna Voss in 1839 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Ossmire after the flood relief of 1812. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Greta Holloway keeps the toll records of 1957 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it.

## People and customs

Children in Rosreach learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. The town counts perhaps 50 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Greta Holloway and Otto Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1863 without ever raising their voices.
