## Overview

Halbury is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Halbury by the towpath from Lordane, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Halbury as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 51 miles in either direction.

## History

Old records kept by the Dario Vexley family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 62 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from river-glass salvaged after the Lordane viaduct was decommissioned in 1887.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Anselm Sorvane in 1807 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 45 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Lordane.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Dario Vexley keeps the toll records of 1853 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Lordane after the flood relief of 1839. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 21 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Dario Vexley and Stellan Holloway households have disputed a fence line since 1792 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. The current harbormaster, Anselm Sorvane, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
