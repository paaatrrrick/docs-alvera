## Overview

Most maps mark Narrun as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 24 miles in either direction. Narrun sits where the rowdy lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Narrun by the towpath from Calrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran tar barrels through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1918 after Rurik Falkner petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from fired clay salvaged after the Calrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1811. ## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 32 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Calrun.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Calrun after the flood relief of 1826. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Otto Mollering keeps the toll records of 1942 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the celebrated hills behind it.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Rurik Falkner, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Narrun learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. The town counts perhaps 69 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
