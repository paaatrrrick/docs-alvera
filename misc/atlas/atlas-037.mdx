## Overview

Travelers usually reach Kelmore by the towpath from Halstead, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Kelmore grew up around a single warehouse in 1857, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Kelmore sits where the ambitious lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Kelmore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 64 miles in either direction.

## History

Old records kept by the Sanna Kessler family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1868 after Lazlo Severin petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran clockwork gears through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 32 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. ## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Lazlo Severin in 1876 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 17 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Halstead. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Halstead after the flood relief of 1783. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Sanna Kessler keeps the toll records of 1962 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 16 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Sanna Kessler and Anselm Marchetti households have disputed a fence line since 1916 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Children in Kelmore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Lazlo Severin, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
