## Overview

Travelers usually reach Halbury by the towpath from Ilyholm, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Halbury is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly. Most maps mark Halbury as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 36 miles in either direction. The settlement of Halbury grew up around a single warehouse in 1894, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

Old records kept by the Edda Voss family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran clockwork gears through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1964 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1919 after Dario Holloway petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 64 years the town survived on a single export, lamp oil, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 21 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Ilyholm. The cooperative founded by Dario Holloway in 1927 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Ilyholm after the flood relief of 1853. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Edda Voss keeps the toll records of 1815 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 55 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Halbury learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Edda Voss and Annik Holloway households have disputed a fence line since 1918 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. The current harbormaster, Dario Holloway, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
