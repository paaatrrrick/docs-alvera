## Overview

The settlement of Delspire grew up around a single warehouse in 1963, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Delspire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly. Most maps mark Delspire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 64 miles in either direction.

## History

Old records kept by the Folke Halloran family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 52 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Galcrest viaduct was decommissioned in 1893. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Beatrix Holloway in 1934 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 60 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Galcrest. Apprenticeships in the salt-cured oak workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Folke Halloran keeps the toll records of 1948 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Galcrest after the flood relief of 1894.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. The town counts perhaps 59 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Beatrix Holloway, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Folke Halloran and Mireille Voss households have disputed a fence line since 1938 without ever raising their voices.
