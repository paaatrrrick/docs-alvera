## Overview

Tamfell sits where the wind-bitten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Tamfell as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 22 miles in either direction. The settlement of Tamfell grew up around a single warehouse in 1921, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1933 after Annik Vexley petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Petra Quist family describe a still and windless month that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from hammered tin salvaged after the Calvale viaduct was decommissioned in 1951. For nearly 28 years the town survived on a single export, tar barrels, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. ## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses. Roughly 37 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Calvale. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Annik Vexley in 1798 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Calvale after the flood relief of 1853. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 58 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Feuds here are long but mild; the Petra Quist and Oona Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1792 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Annik Vexley, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
