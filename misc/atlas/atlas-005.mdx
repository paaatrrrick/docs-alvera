## Overview

Velford is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly. Most maps mark Velford as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 30 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Velford by the towpath from Fenmire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Velford sits where the prosperous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

For nearly 84 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran wool bales through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Edda Oduya family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1950 after Yutta Severin petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. ## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Yutta Severin in 1936 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Fenmire after the flood relief of 1933. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Edda Oduya keeps the toll records of 1902 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Children in Velford learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Edda Oduya and Cosima Marchetti households have disputed a fence line since 1869 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. The current harbormaster, Yutta Severin, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
