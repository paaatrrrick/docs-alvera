## Overview

Travelers usually reach Wrenmara by the towpath from Torton, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Wrenmara grew up around a single warehouse in 1877, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Wrenmara as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 14 miles in either direction. Wrenmara sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Torton viaduct was decommissioned in 1928. Old records kept by the Joren Marchetti family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1880 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 57 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the salt-cured oak workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Tilda Marrow in 1816 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Joren Marchetti keeps the toll records of 1915 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Children in Wrenmara learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 78 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Joren Marchetti and Folke Lindqvist households have disputed a fence line since 1929 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Tilda Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
