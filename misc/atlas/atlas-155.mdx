## Overview

Most maps mark Calmire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 53 miles in either direction. Calmire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly. Calmire sits where the ambitious lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

For nearly 13 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1845 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1859 after Sigrid Drummond petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Sigrid Drummond in 1853 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 18 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Torfell. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Otto Kessler keeps the toll records of 1896 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Torfell after the flood relief of 1836.

## People and customs

Children in Calmire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Otto Kessler and Cosima Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1947 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. The current harbormaster, Sigrid Drummond, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
