## Overview

Velhollow is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly. The settlement of Velhollow grew up around a single warehouse in 1864, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Velhollow as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 89 miles in either direction.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1888 after Petra Vexley petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from whale-bone veneer salvaged after the Lorshore viaduct was decommissioned in 1790. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran wool bales through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Rurik Brandt family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1927 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 58 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Lorshore. The cooperative founded by Petra Vexley in 1910 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Rurik Brandt keeps the toll records of 1945 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Lorshore after the flood relief of 1807. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it.

## People and customs

Children in Velhollow learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Petra Vexley, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Rurik Brandt and Sanna Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1926 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 32 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season.
