## Overview

The settlement of Yarton grew up around a single warehouse in 1838, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Yarton is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its ambitious residents perfectly. Most maps mark Yarton as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 52 miles in either direction. Yarton sits where the patient lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The town hall was built from pressed kelp paper salvaged after the Calmara viaduct was decommissioned in 1848. For nearly 54 years the town survived on a single export, fermented tea bricks, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran loom parts through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Vesna Halloran family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1819 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 58 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Calmara. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Vesna Severin in 1870 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Vesna Halloran keeps the toll records of 1920 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Calmara after the flood relief of 1945. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 83 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Yarton learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Vesna Halloran and Folke Oduya households have disputed a fence line since 1917 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain.
