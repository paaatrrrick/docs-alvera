## Overview

Travelers usually reach Dorvale by the towpath from Velreach, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Dorvale is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its ambitious residents perfectly. Dorvale sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

Old records kept by the Otto Voss family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 37 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran fermented tea bricks through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1945 after Rurik Marrow petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses. The cooperative founded by Rurik Marrow in 1940 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 26 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Velreach. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the lacquered birch workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Otto Voss keeps the toll records of 1845 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose blue slate roof was a gift from the town of Velreach after the flood relief of 1805.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Rurik Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Otto Voss and Tilda Severin households have disputed a fence line since 1850 without ever raising their voices. Children in Dorvale learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods.
