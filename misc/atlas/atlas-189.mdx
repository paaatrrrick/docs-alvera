## Overview

The settlement of Yarbury grew up around a single warehouse in 1930, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Yarbury sits where the half-forgotten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Yarbury by the towpath from Wrenmore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Yarbury as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 18 miles in either direction.

## History

For nearly 24 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Wrenmore viaduct was decommissioned in 1809. Old records kept by the Tomas Kessler family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1904 after Stellan Severin petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

A fire in 1953 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Stellan Severin in 1887 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 81 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Wrenmore. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Wrenmore after the flood relief of 1875. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tomas Kessler keeps the toll records of 1897 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Tomas Kessler and Mireille Marchetti households have disputed a fence line since 1785 without ever raising their voices. Children in Yarbury learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 18 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.
