## Overview

The settlement of Ossreach grew up around a single warehouse in 1880, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Ossreach sits where the ambitious lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Ossreach by the towpath from Brenhollow, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

The town hall was built from lacquered birch salvaged after the Brenhollow viaduct was decommissioned in 1933. The first charter was granted in 1931 after Liesl Oduya petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 21 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. ## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Liesl Oduya in 1901 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. Roughly 49 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Brenhollow. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the celebrated hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Rurik Ashdown keeps the toll records of 1845 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Brenhollow after the flood relief of 1789. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Feuds here are long but mild; the Rurik Ashdown and Joren Oduya households have disputed a fence line since 1813 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Liesl Oduya, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 78 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
