## Overview

Travelers usually reach Velmore by the towpath from Velspire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Velmore grew up around a single warehouse in 1899, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Velmore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 47 miles in either direction.

## History

A fire in 1894 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1820 after Ivo Drummond petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 79 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Halvard Sorvane family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Velspire viaduct was decommissioned in 1861.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 47 households still work the drying sheds, turning out clockwork gears for buyers as far away as Velspire. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Ivo Drummond in 1833 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Halvard Sorvane keeps the toll records of 1800 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Velspire after the flood relief of 1806. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Halvard Sorvane and Beatrix Greaves households have disputed a fence line since 1884 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. The current harbormaster, Ivo Drummond, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 81 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Velmore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
