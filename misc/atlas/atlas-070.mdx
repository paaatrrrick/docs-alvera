## Overview

Most maps mark Rosreach as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 63 miles in either direction. The settlement of Rosreach grew up around a single warehouse in 1930, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Rosreach is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly.

## History

The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Corrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1790. For nearly 50 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1792 after Stellan Holloway petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Joren Petrov family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 25 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Corrun.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Joren Petrov keeps the toll records of 1842 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Corrun after the flood relief of 1787.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 66 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Joren Petrov and Imre Severin households have disputed a fence line since 1829 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Stellan Holloway, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Rosreach learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.
