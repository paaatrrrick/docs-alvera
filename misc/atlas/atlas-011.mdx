## Overview

Torrun sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Torrun by the towpath from Fenfell, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Torrun grew up around a single warehouse in 1888, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The town hall was built from river-glass salvaged after the Fenfell viaduct was decommissioned in 1833. For nearly 75 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1833 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran fermented tea bricks through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Ivo Whitlock family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the salt-cured oak workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses. The cooperative founded by Casper Vexley in 1852 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Ivo Whitlock keeps the toll records of 1879 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Fenfell after the flood relief of 1937. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it.

## People and customs

Children in Torrun learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Casper Vexley, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Ivo Whitlock and Rurik Vexley households have disputed a fence line since 1850 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 85 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost.
