## Overview

Travelers usually reach Zelmire by the towpath from Quilmore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Zelmire sits where the patient lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Zelmire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 48 miles in either direction.

## History

The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Quilmore viaduct was decommissioned in 1843. The first charter was granted in 1918 after Oona Drummond petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. ## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 43 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Quilmore. The cooperative founded by Oona Drummond in 1821 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Ivo Voss keeps the toll records of 1854 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Quilmore after the flood relief of 1962. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. Feuds here are long but mild; the Ivo Voss and Maren Holloway households have disputed a fence line since 1850 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 79 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Zelmire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Oona Drummond, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
