## Overview

The settlement of Ilygate grew up around a single warehouse in 1893, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Ilygate as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 59 miles in either direction. Ilygate sits where the reclusive lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran lamp oil through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Yutta Halloran family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 19 years the town survived on a single export, loom parts, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. ## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses. The cooperative founded by Mireille Voss in 1927 now handles most of the clockwork gears trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the celebrated hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Ashhollow after the flood relief of 1872. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Yutta Halloran keeps the toll records of 1886 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 88 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Ilygate learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. Feuds here are long but mild; the Yutta Halloran and Greta Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1851 without ever raising their voices.
