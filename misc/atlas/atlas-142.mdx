## Overview

Most maps mark Brenreach as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 73 miles in either direction. The settlement of Brenreach grew up around a single warehouse in 1837, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Brenreach by the towpath from Torspire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Brenreach is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of glass floats that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Yutta Falkner in 1789 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Liesl Whitlock keeps the toll records of 1812 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Torspire after the flood relief of 1896.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Feuds here are long but mild; the Liesl Whitlock and Otto Lindqvist households have disputed a fence line since 1910 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 69 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Yutta Falkner, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Brenreach learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
