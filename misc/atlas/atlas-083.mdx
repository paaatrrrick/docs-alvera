## Overview

Travelers usually reach Rosstead by the towpath from Erishore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Rosstead is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its celebrated residents perfectly. The settlement of Rosstead grew up around a single warehouse in 1865, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Rosstead sits where the quiet lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran wool bales through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Erishore viaduct was decommissioned in 1835. The first charter was granted in 1885 after Tilda Greaves petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Tomas Ashdown family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

For nearly 61 years the town survived on a single export, tar barrels, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1877 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Tilda Greaves in 1963 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 49 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Erishore. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Erishore after the flood relief of 1809. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Tomas Ashdown and Otto Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1820 without ever raising their voices. Children in Rosstead learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. The town counts perhaps 87 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
