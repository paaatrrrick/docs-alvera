## Overview

Cormara is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly. Cormara sits where the meticulous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Cormara as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 64 miles in either direction.

## History

The town hall was built from bog iron salvaged after the Lorrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1920. Old records kept by the Maren Falkner family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 60 years the town survived on a single export, clockwork gears, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1790 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Tilda Halloran in 1849 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 82 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Lorrun.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Lorrun after the flood relief of 1874. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Maren Falkner keeps the toll records of 1823 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. Children in Cormara learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Maren Falkner and Halvard Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1920 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Tilda Halloran, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
