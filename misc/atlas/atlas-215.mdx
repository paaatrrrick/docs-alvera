## Overview

Most maps mark Corton as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 69 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Corton by the towpath from Fenrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Corton grew up around a single warehouse in 1852, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran tar barrels through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1848 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1919 after Emeric Halloran petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Imre Petrov family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from pressed kelp paper salvaged after the Fenrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1867.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Emeric Halloran in 1866 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 68 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Fenrun. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Fenrun after the flood relief of 1951. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the celebrated hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Imre Petrov keeps the toll records of 1911 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 69 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Feuds here are long but mild; the Imre Petrov and Otto Lindqvist households have disputed a fence line since 1825 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Emeric Halloran, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Corton learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
