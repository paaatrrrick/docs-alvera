## Overview

Tamvale sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Tamvale grew up around a single warehouse in 1954, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Tamvale as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 59 miles in either direction.

## History

Old records kept by the Lazlo Drummond family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Calreach viaduct was decommissioned in 1954. The first charter was granted in 1814 after Emeric Halloran petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 31 years the town survived on a single export, tar barrels, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. ## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the salt-cured oak workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Emeric Halloran in 1954 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Calreach after the flood relief of 1798. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 44 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Tamvale learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Emeric Halloran, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Feuds here are long but mild; the Lazlo Drummond and Edda Severin households have disputed a fence line since 1905 without ever raising their voices.
