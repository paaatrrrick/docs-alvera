## Overview

Rosvik is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly. Most maps mark Rosvik as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 24 miles in either direction. The settlement of Rosvik grew up around a single warehouse in 1817, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Rosvik sits where the meticulous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran tar barrels through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1905 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Greta Lindqvist family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 41 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1801 after Folke Oduya petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 13 households still work the drying sheds, turning out clockwork gears for buyers as far away as Calgate. The cooperative founded by Folke Oduya in 1820 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Calgate after the flood relief of 1938. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Greta Lindqvist keeps the toll records of 1784 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 59 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Feuds here are long but mild; the Greta Lindqvist and Beatrix Severin households have disputed a fence line since 1881 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Folke Oduya, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
