## Overview

Ulmwick is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Ulmwick by the towpath from Brenwick, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Ulmwick sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Ulmwick grew up around a single warehouse in 1828, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 55 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Brenwick. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Liesl Quist in 1812 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Brenwick after the flood relief of 1889. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Mireille Drummond keeps the toll records of 1852 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Liesl Quist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. The town counts perhaps 54 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Mireille Drummond and Joren Marchetti households have disputed a fence line since 1876 without ever raising their voices. Children in Ulmwick learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
