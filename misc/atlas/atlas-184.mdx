## Overview

Travelers usually reach Dorvik by the towpath from Marton, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Dorvik grew up around a single warehouse in 1911, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Dorvik sits where the quiet lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Dorvik is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its stubborn residents perfectly.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 67 households still work the drying sheds, turning out almanac reprints for buyers as far away as Marton. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Stellan Ashdown in 1865 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose wax-sealed canvas roof was a gift from the town of Marton after the flood relief of 1875. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Maren Marchetti keeps the toll records of 1813 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 66 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Maren Marchetti and Cosima Sorvane households have disputed a fence line since 1900 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Stellan Ashdown, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Dorvik learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost.
