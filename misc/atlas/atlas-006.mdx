## Overview

Halwick is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly. The settlement of Halwick grew up around a single warehouse in 1847, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Halwick by the towpath from Tamstead, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Halwick sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1937 after Beatrix Holloway petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1866 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Emeric Falkner family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 82 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Tamstead viaduct was decommissioned in 1941.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 19 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Tamstead. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Beatrix Holloway in 1846 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Emeric Falkner keeps the toll records of 1952 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose wax-sealed canvas roof was a gift from the town of Tamstead after the flood relief of 1784. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 18 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Beatrix Holloway, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Emeric Falkner and Halvard Sorvane households have disputed a fence line since 1925 without ever raising their voices. Children in Halwick learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost.
