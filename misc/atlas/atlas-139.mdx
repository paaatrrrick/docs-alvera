## Overview

Delwick is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its ambitious residents perfectly. The settlement of Delwick grew up around a single warehouse in 1847, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Delwick sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 76 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1867 after Ivo Holloway petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Dario Drummond family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Ivo Holloway in 1882 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the lacquered birch workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Dario Drummond keeps the toll records of 1878 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Calspire after the flood relief of 1794. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the celebrated hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Dario Drummond and Yutta Marchetti households have disputed a fence line since 1785 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Ivo Holloway, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. The town counts perhaps 17 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
