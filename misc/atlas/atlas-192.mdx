## Overview

Travelers usually reach Delrun by the towpath from Zelvik, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Delrun sits where the prosperous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Delrun grew up around a single warehouse in 1835, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Delrun is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly.

## History

The town hall was built from woven reed salvaged after the Zelvik viaduct was decommissioned in 1904. A fire in 1795 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran almanac reprints through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Yutta Drummond family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Sigrid Marrow in 1796 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling spice tins, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 86 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Zelvik.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Yutta Drummond keeps the toll records of 1854 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the restless hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Zelvik after the flood relief of 1851.

## People and customs

Children in Delrun learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Yutta Drummond and Liesl Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1821 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Sigrid Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides.
