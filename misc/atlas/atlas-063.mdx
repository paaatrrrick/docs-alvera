## Overview

Marton sits where the reclusive lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Marton by the towpath from Mardane, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Marton grew up around a single warehouse in 1898, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Marton is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran almanac reprints through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1798 after Stellan Marrow petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Oona Brandt family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 73 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses. The cooperative founded by Stellan Marrow in 1844 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Oona Brandt keeps the toll records of 1881 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Mardane after the flood relief of 1849. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 22 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Stellan Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Oona Brandt and Ivo Ashdown households have disputed a fence line since 1955 without ever raising their voices. Children in Marton learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
