## Overview

Most maps mark Ilyshore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 75 miles in either direction. The settlement of Ilyshore grew up around a single warehouse in 1907, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Ilyshore by the towpath from Lormara, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

For nearly 50 years the town survived on a single export, fermented tea bricks, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1917 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1941 after Imre Mollering petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Lormara viaduct was decommissioned in 1825. Old records kept by the Yutta Severin family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses. Roughly 86 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Lormara. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Yutta Severin keeps the toll records of 1911 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Lormara after the flood relief of 1838.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Yutta Severin and Tomas Whitlock households have disputed a fence line since 1876 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides. The town counts perhaps 16 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Imre Mollering, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
