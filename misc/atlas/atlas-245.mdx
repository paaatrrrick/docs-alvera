## Overview

Most maps mark Erifell as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 79 miles in either direction. Erifell sits where the ambitious lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Erifell grew up around a single warehouse in 1874, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

For nearly 37 years the town survived on a single export, clockwork gears, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Vesna Halloran family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1858 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran loom parts through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Perrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1906.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses. Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 28 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Perrun. The cooperative founded by Stellan Lindqvist in 1790 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Perrun after the flood relief of 1863. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Vesna Halloran keeps the toll records of 1869 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Children in Erifell learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Stellan Lindqvist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Vesna Halloran and Lazlo Voss households have disputed a fence line since 1957 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 50 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies.
