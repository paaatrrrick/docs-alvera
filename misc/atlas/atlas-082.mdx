## Overview

Most maps mark Zelton as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 27 miles in either direction. Zelton is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly. The settlement of Zelton grew up around a single warehouse in 1838, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Zelton sits where the wind-bitten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Marford viaduct was decommissioned in 1930. For nearly 86 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1915 after Annik Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1850 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling copper wire, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Annik Marchetti in 1947 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 34 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Marford.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Marford after the flood relief of 1829. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Bram Ashdown keeps the toll records of 1866 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Bram Ashdown and Tilda Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1850 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. The current harbormaster, Annik Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 49 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
