## Overview

Tamfell sits where the patient lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Tamfell grew up around a single warehouse in 1814, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Tamfell by the towpath from Ashrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Tamfell as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 29 miles in either direction.

## History

The town hall was built from whale-bone veneer salvaged after the Ashrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1959. Old records kept by the Dario Oduya family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1840 after Sanna Sorvane petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran lamp oil through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1821 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 54 households still work the drying sheds, turning out loom parts for buyers as far away as Ashrun. The cooperative founded by Sanna Sorvane in 1923 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Dario Oduya keeps the toll records of 1857 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Dario Oduya and Dario Mollering households have disputed a fence line since 1803 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 32 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Children in Tamfell learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Sanna Sorvane, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
