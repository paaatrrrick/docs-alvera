## Overview

Pergate sits where the rowdy lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Pergate as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 30 miles in either direction. Pergate is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly. The settlement of Pergate grew up around a single warehouse in 1821, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1932 after Cosima Falkner petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Perford viaduct was decommissioned in 1877. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. ## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling spice tins, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 40 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Perford. Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Cosima Falkner in 1868 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Perford after the flood relief of 1790. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the restless hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Liesl Marchetti keeps the toll records of 1852 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Liesl Marchetti and Halvard Brandt households have disputed a fence line since 1823 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 70 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Pergate learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. The current harbormaster, Cosima Falkner, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
