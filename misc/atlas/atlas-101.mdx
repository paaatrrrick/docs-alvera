## Overview

Seldane sits where the rowdy lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Seldane as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 13 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Seldane by the towpath from Fenreach, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Seldane is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1844 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1928 after Liesl Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 50 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Stellan Severin family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Liesl Marchetti in 1939 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 74 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Fenreach. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the restless hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Stellan Severin keeps the toll records of 1946 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Fenreach after the flood relief of 1830.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Stellan Severin and Emeric Whitlock households have disputed a fence line since 1923 without ever raising their voices. Children in Seldane learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Liesl Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 84 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods.
