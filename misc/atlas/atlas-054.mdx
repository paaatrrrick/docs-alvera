## Overview

The settlement of Rosfell grew up around a single warehouse in 1906, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Rosfell by the towpath from Morreach, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Rosfell is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1861 after Yutta Oduya petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Casper Whitlock family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Morreach viaduct was decommissioned in 1847. For nearly 71 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Yutta Oduya in 1782 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 81 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Morreach. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Morreach after the flood relief of 1784. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Yutta Oduya, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Casper Whitlock and Sigrid Whitlock households have disputed a fence line since 1781 without ever raising their voices. Children in Rosfell learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 70 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month.
