## Overview

Tamgate is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its rowdy residents perfectly. Tamgate sits where the reclusive lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Tamgate as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 88 miles in either direction.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1916 after Imre Ashdown petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 13 years the town survived on a single export, tar barrels, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1810 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Ossmire viaduct was decommissioned in 1840. Old records kept by the Sigrid Holloway family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Imre Ashdown in 1964 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Sigrid Holloway keeps the toll records of 1895 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose blue slate roof was a gift from the town of Ossmire after the flood relief of 1843.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides. The current harbormaster, Imre Ashdown, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Sigrid Holloway and Bram Mollering households have disputed a fence line since 1896 without ever raising their voices. Children in Tamgate learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 73 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
