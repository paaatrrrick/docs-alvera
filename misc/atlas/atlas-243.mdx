## Overview

The settlement of Jorbury grew up around a single warehouse in 1794, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Jorbury is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly. Jorbury sits where the prosperous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran clockwork gears through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 68 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1879 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Roswick viaduct was decommissioned in 1879. Old records kept by the Tilda Falkner family describe a still and windless month that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 37 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Roswick. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of glass floats that crosses. The cooperative founded by Liesl Lindqvist in 1916 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tilda Falkner keeps the toll records of 1874 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose blue slate roof was a gift from the town of Roswick after the flood relief of 1788. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 35 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Jorbury learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tilda Falkner and Anselm Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1861 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Liesl Lindqvist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
