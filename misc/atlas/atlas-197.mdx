## Overview

Most maps mark Torton as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 37 miles in either direction. Torton sits where the meticulous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Torton by the towpath from Dormara, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Torton is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly.

## History

A fire in 1935 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1890 after Sigrid Severin petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. ## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Sigrid Severin in 1959 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 84 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Dormara.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Folke Petrov keeps the toll records of 1888 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Dormara after the flood relief of 1841.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Folke Petrov and Greta Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1903 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Sigrid Severin, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 48 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.
