## Overview

Lorvik is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly. Most maps mark Lorvik as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 31 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Lorvik by the towpath from Delspire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Lorvik grew up around a single warehouse in 1803, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1829 after Emeric Whitlock petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Tomas Drummond family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 17 years the town survived on a single export, fermented tea bricks, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. ## Economy and trade

Roughly 85 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Delspire. Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tomas Drummond keeps the toll records of 1792 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Tomas Drummond and Imre Lindqvist households have disputed a fence line since 1855 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. The current harbormaster, Emeric Whitlock, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 44 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
