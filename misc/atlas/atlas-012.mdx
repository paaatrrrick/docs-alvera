## Overview

The settlement of Cormire grew up around a single warehouse in 1798, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Cormire sits where the reclusive lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Cormire by the towpath from Perstead, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1868 after Yutta Marrow petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 23 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1797 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Perstead viaduct was decommissioned in 1812.

Old records kept by the Rurik Ashdown family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 21 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Yutta Marrow in 1802 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 32 households still work the drying sheds, turning out loom parts for buyers as far away as Perstead. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Perstead after the flood relief of 1850. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Rurik Ashdown keeps the toll records of 1839 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 16 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Yutta Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Cormire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Rurik Ashdown and Beatrix Whitlock households have disputed a fence line since 1861 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.
