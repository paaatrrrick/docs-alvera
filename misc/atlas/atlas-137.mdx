## Overview

Travelers usually reach Vormara by the towpath from Kelvik, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Vormara is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly. Vormara sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Vormara grew up around a single warehouse in 1943, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

A fire in 1944 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 23 years the town survived on a single export, lamp oil, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Greta Sorvane family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Kelvik viaduct was decommissioned in 1901. The first charter was granted in 1790 after Folke Voss petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the lacquered birch workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Folke Voss in 1910 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 13 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Kelvik. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Kelvik after the flood relief of 1905. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Greta Sorvane keeps the toll records of 1902 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the celebrated hills behind it.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Greta Sorvane and Ivo Halloran households have disputed a fence line since 1927 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Folke Voss, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 67 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. Children in Vormara learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
