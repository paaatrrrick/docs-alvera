## Overview

Travelers usually reach Perhollow by the towpath from Jorrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Perhollow as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 40 miles in either direction. Perhollow sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

A fire in 1857 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1918 after Sanna Kessler petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Jorrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1879. For nearly 12 years the town survived on a single export, dye cakes, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. ## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. Roughly 67 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Jorrun. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Joren Lindqvist keeps the toll records of 1818 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Jorrun after the flood relief of 1908.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Joren Lindqvist and Halvard Holloway households have disputed a fence line since 1903 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 89 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Perhollow learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month.
