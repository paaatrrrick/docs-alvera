## Overview

Most maps mark Lorspire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 88 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Lorspire by the towpath from Torvik, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Lorspire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly. The settlement of Lorspire grew up around a single warehouse in 1864, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 37 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Torvik. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, clockwork gears, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Greta Whitlock in 1930 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Torvik after the flood relief of 1869. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Vesna Brandt keeps the toll records of 1819 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Children in Lorspire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Vesna Brandt and Dario Sorvane households have disputed a fence line since 1955 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Greta Whitlock, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 61 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
