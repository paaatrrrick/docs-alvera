## Overview

The settlement of Calcrest grew up around a single warehouse in 1882, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Calcrest sits where the quiet lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Calcrest as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 35 miles in either direction. Calcrest is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly.

## History

A fire in 1928 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran lamp oil through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1847 after Bram Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Joren Ashdown family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Morshore viaduct was decommissioned in 1864.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 74 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Morshore. The cooperative founded by Bram Marchetti in 1839 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Morshore after the flood relief of 1954. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Joren Ashdown keeps the toll records of 1893 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Children in Calcrest learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Joren Ashdown and Dario Greaves households have disputed a fence line since 1835 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. The current harbormaster, Bram Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
