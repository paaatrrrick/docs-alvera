## Overview

Travelers usually reach Kelcrest by the towpath from Vorshore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Kelcrest as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 38 miles in either direction. Kelcrest is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly.

## History

The town hall was built from hammered tin salvaged after the Vorshore viaduct was decommissioned in 1833. The first charter was granted in 1951 after Folke Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran tar barrels through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 28 years the town survived on a single export, fermented tea bricks, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses. The cooperative founded by Folke Marchetti in 1830 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 83 households still work the drying sheds, turning out loom parts for buyers as far away as Vorshore. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Vorshore after the flood relief of 1872. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Edda Severin keeps the toll records of 1955 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 24 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Folke Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Edda Severin and Rurik Halloran households have disputed a fence line since 1925 without ever raising their voices. Children in Kelcrest learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies.
