## Overview

Dorton is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly. The settlement of Dorton grew up around a single warehouse in 1860, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Dorton by the towpath from Corford, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Dorton as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 23 miles in either direction.

## History

A fire in 1857 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 24 years the town survived on a single export, clockwork gears, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1845 after Casper Greaves petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran fermented tea bricks through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. ## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Casper Greaves in 1858 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses. Roughly 84 households still work the drying sheds, turning out almanac reprints for buyers as far away as Corford. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Corford after the flood relief of 1905. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Yutta Oduya keeps the toll records of 1895 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Children in Dorton learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. The current harbormaster, Casper Greaves, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 61 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
