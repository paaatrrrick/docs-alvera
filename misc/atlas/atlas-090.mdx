## Overview

Travelers usually reach Vorstead by the towpath from Selmore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Vorstead is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly. Most maps mark Vorstead as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 65 miles in either direction.

## History

A fire in 1926 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from woven reed salvaged after the Selmore viaduct was decommissioned in 1842. Old records kept by the Ivo Marchetti family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 54 years the town survived on a single export, clockwork gears, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. ## Economy and trade

Roughly 60 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Selmore. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. The cooperative founded by Emeric Holloway in 1851 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Ivo Marchetti keeps the toll records of 1874 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Selmore after the flood relief of 1853.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Emeric Holloway, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. The town counts perhaps 90 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Ivo Marchetti and Otto Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1890 without ever raising their voices. Children in Vorstead learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
