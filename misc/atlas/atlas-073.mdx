## Overview

Most maps mark Ossvik as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 42 miles in either direction. The settlement of Ossvik grew up around a single warehouse in 1879, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Ossvik sits where the wind-bitten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Ossvik is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly.

## History

Old records kept by the Emeric Whitlock family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 69 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1871 after Oona Kessler petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. ## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. The cooperative founded by Oona Kessler in 1933 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, clockwork gears, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Ulmholm after the flood relief of 1919. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Emeric Whitlock keeps the toll records of 1854 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 52 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Emeric Whitlock and Folke Quist households have disputed a fence line since 1882 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Oona Kessler, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Ossvik learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season.
