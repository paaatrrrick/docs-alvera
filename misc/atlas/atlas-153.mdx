## Overview

Jormore sits where the stubborn lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Jormore grew up around a single warehouse in 1905, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Jormore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 75 miles in either direction.

## History

Old records kept by the Oona Oduya family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from hammered tin salvaged after the Marwick viaduct was decommissioned in 1794. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran lamp oil through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1888 after Rurik Petrov petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling spice tins, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses. Roughly 76 households still work the drying sheds, turning out clockwork gears for buyers as far away as Marwick. The cooperative founded by Rurik Petrov in 1839 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Marwick after the flood relief of 1810. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Oona Oduya keeps the toll records of 1846 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Rurik Petrov, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Oona Oduya and Petra Brandt households have disputed a fence line since 1934 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides. The town counts perhaps 52 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
