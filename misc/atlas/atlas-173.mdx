## Overview

Travelers usually reach Eridane by the towpath from Morvik, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Eridane grew up around a single warehouse in 1942, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Eridane is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its ambitious residents perfectly. Eridane sits where the quiet lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1902 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Morvik viaduct was decommissioned in 1852. Old records kept by the Beatrix Marchetti family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses. Roughly 54 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Morvik.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Morvik after the flood relief of 1898. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Beatrix Marchetti keeps the toll records of 1847 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Maren Ashdown, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Beatrix Marchetti and Otto Whitlock households have disputed a fence line since 1869 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 32 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Children in Eridane learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
