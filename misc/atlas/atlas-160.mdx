## Overview

Travelers usually reach Marreach by the towpath from Fenstead, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Marreach is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly. The settlement of Marreach grew up around a single warehouse in 1800, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Marreach as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 62 miles in either direction.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1872 after Lazlo Oduya petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1863 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 54 years the town survived on a single export, clockwork gears, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Sigrid Vexley family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses. Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 44 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Fenstead. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. ## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Sigrid Vexley keeps the toll records of 1839 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Fenstead after the flood relief of 1802.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 90 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Lazlo Oduya, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. Children in Marreach learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Sigrid Vexley and Bram Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1782 without ever raising their voices.
