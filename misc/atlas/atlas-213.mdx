## Overview

Tamreach is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly. The settlement of Tamreach grew up around a single warehouse in 1919, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Tamreach by the towpath from Zelmire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

Old records kept by the Oona Severin family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1922 after Casper Kessler petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Zelmire viaduct was decommissioned in 1866. A fire in 1924 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses. Roughly 71 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Zelmire. The cooperative founded by Casper Kessler in 1922 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Zelmire after the flood relief of 1791.

## People and customs

Children in Tamreach learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. The town counts perhaps 31 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Oona Severin and Dario Marchetti households have disputed a fence line since 1905 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Casper Kessler, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
