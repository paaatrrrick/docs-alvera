## Overview

Most maps mark Vorbury as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 65 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Vorbury by the towpath from Lordane, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Vorbury grew up around a single warehouse in 1845, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1895 after Imre Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from woven reed salvaged after the Lordane viaduct was decommissioned in 1900. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran clockwork gears through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 69 years the town survived on a single export, dye cakes, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1844 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Imre Marchetti in 1902 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling spice tins, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Sanna Oduya keeps the toll records of 1949 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose wax-sealed canvas roof was a gift from the town of Lordane after the flood relief of 1925. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Imre Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 45 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Children in Vorbury learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Sanna Oduya and Liesl Lindqvist households have disputed a fence line since 1930 without ever raising their voices.
