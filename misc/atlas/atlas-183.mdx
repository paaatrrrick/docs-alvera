## Overview

The settlement of Yarmire grew up around a single warehouse in 1960, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Yarmire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Yarmire by the towpath from Eridane, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Yarmire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 59 miles in either direction.

## History

A fire in 1957 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Mireille Vexley family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 77 years the town survived on a single export, lamp oil, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1834 after Oona Falkner petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran clockwork gears through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 62 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Eridane. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Oona Falkner in 1888 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Eridane after the flood relief of 1964. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the restless hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Mireille Vexley keeps the toll records of 1942 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Oona Falkner, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 76 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Mireille Vexley and Maren Brandt households have disputed a fence line since 1884 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Children in Yarmire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
