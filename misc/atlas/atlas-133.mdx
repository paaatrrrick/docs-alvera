## Overview

Vorspire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly. Most maps mark Vorspire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 49 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Vorspire by the towpath from Torcrest, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Vorspire sits where the rowdy lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The town hall was built from fired clay salvaged after the Torcrest viaduct was decommissioned in 1904. For nearly 65 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran loom parts through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. ## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 88 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Torcrest. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Imre Greaves keeps the toll records of 1883 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Torcrest after the flood relief of 1850. ## People and customs

Children in Vorspire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 21 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Imre Greaves and Ivo Falkner households have disputed a fence line since 1802 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Halvard Sorvane, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
