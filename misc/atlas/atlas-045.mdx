## Overview

Ashwick is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly. Ashwick sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Ashwick by the towpath from Zelton, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Stellan Mollering family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Zelton viaduct was decommissioned in 1836. The first charter was granted in 1867 after Bram Ashdown petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 79 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Zelton. The cooperative founded by Bram Ashdown in 1944 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Zelton after the flood relief of 1818. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Stellan Mollering keeps the toll records of 1820 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 50 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. Feuds here are long but mild; the Stellan Mollering and Tomas Holloway households have disputed a fence line since 1813 without ever raising their voices. Children in Ashwick learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
