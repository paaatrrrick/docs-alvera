## Overview

The settlement of Osswick grew up around a single warehouse in 1843, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Osswick by the towpath from Vorfell, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Osswick sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Osswick is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its stubborn residents perfectly.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran clockwork gears through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from bog iron salvaged after the Vorfell viaduct was decommissioned in 1848. A fire in 1893 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 49 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the lacquered birch workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Otto Petrov in 1939 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Vorfell after the flood relief of 1847. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Emeric Sorvane keeps the toll records of 1814 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Otto Petrov, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 60 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Osswick learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Emeric Sorvane and Mireille Halloran households have disputed a fence line since 1886 without ever raising their voices.
