## Overview

Travelers usually reach Kelholm by the towpath from Quilwick, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Kelholm sits where the half-forgotten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Kelholm is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly. Most maps mark Kelholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 88 miles in either direction.

## History

For nearly 12 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1894 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Quilwick viaduct was decommissioned in 1874. The first charter was granted in 1928 after Otto Severin petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. ## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of glass floats that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Otto Severin in 1917 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 53 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Quilwick.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the celebrated hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Sanna Oduya keeps the toll records of 1908 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Quilwick after the flood relief of 1794.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 58 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Sanna Oduya and Imre Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1863 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. Children in Kelholm learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
