## Overview

Most maps mark Lorbury as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 12 miles in either direction. Lorbury is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly. Lorbury sits where the quiet lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Lorbury by the towpath from Velrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

For nearly 59 years the town survived on a single export, lamp oil, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1786 after Mireille Whitlock petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1888 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Velrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1800.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Mireille Whitlock in 1942 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Sanna Kessler keeps the toll records of 1820 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Sanna Kessler and Greta Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1930 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. Children in Lorbury learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 36 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
