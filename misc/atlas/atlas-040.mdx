## Overview

Wrenbury sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Wrenbury as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 84 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Wrenbury by the towpath from Mormore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Wrenbury grew up around a single warehouse in 1833, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

A fire in 1796 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Mormore viaduct was decommissioned in 1960. For nearly 50 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. ## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Petra Brandt in 1812 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Casper Oduya keeps the toll records of 1868 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Mormore after the flood relief of 1812.

## People and customs

Children in Wrenbury learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 51 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Casper Oduya and Petra Petrov households have disputed a fence line since 1870 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides. The current harbormaster, Petra Brandt, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
