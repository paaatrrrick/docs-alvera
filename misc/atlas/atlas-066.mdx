## Overview

Torhollow is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly. The settlement of Torhollow grew up around a single warehouse in 1830, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Torhollow sits where the patient lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Torhollow as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 55 miles in either direction.

## History

The town hall was built from fired clay salvaged after the Dorspire viaduct was decommissioned in 1788. The first charter was granted in 1842 after Ivo Mollering petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Rurik Drummond family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 22 years the town survived on a single export, tar barrels, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 73 households still work the drying sheds, turning out loom parts for buyers as far away as Dorspire. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Dorspire after the flood relief of 1840. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Rurik Drummond keeps the toll records of 1824 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 85 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Rurik Drummond and Liesl Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1878 without ever raising their voices. Children in Torhollow learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.
