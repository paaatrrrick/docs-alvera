## Overview

Most maps mark Zeldane as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 38 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Zeldane by the towpath from Vorvik, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Zeldane is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly.

## History

A fire in 1871 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1921 after Sanna Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Sigrid Holloway family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 24 years the town survived on a single export, loom parts, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling spice tins, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 77 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Vorvik. The cooperative founded by Sanna Marchetti in 1788 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Sigrid Holloway keeps the toll records of 1915 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Vorvik after the flood relief of 1862. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. The town counts perhaps 49 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Sanna Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Sigrid Holloway and Vesna Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1784 without ever raising their voices.
