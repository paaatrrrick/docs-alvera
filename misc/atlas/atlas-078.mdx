## Overview

Ashmire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Ashmire by the towpath from Tamholm, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Ashmire sits where the meticulous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The town hall was built from fired clay salvaged after the Tamholm viaduct was decommissioned in 1941. For nearly 90 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1905 after Vesna Severin petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1794 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran fermented tea bricks through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses. The cooperative founded by Vesna Severin in 1918 now handles most of the clockwork gears trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Liesl Sorvane keeps the toll records of 1796 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose wax-sealed canvas roof was a gift from the town of Tamholm after the flood relief of 1784. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Vesna Severin, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Liesl Sorvane and Yutta Vexley households have disputed a fence line since 1789 without ever raising their voices. Children in Ashmire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 76 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides.
