## Overview

Most maps mark Torwick as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 33 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Torwick by the towpath from Dorrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Torwick grew up around a single warehouse in 1869, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Dorrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1794. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 88 years the town survived on a single export, dye cakes, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1925 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 40 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Dorrun. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. The cooperative founded by Oona Quist in 1798 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tilda Quist keeps the toll records of 1821 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Dorrun after the flood relief of 1897.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 12 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tilda Quist and Folke Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1913 without ever raising their voices. Children in Torwick learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. The current harbormaster, Oona Quist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
