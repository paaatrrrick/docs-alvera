## Overview

The settlement of Morvik grew up around a single warehouse in 1877, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Morvik sits where the prosperous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Morvik as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 79 miles in either direction. Morvik is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly.

## History

The town hall was built from hammered tin salvaged after the Dorfell viaduct was decommissioned in 1914. The first charter was granted in 1896 after Sigrid Petrov petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Ivo Falkner family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 34 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1821 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. The cooperative founded by Sigrid Petrov in 1956 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 76 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Dorfell. Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Ivo Falkner keeps the toll records of 1887 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Dorfell after the flood relief of 1875. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the celebrated hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Ivo Falkner and Otto Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1795 without ever raising their voices. Children in Morvik learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. The current harbormaster, Sigrid Petrov, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 88 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
