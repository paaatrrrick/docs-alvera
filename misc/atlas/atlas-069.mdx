## Overview

Travelers usually reach Zelspire by the towpath from Jormire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Zelspire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its celebrated residents perfectly. Zelspire sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Zelspire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 49 miles in either direction.

## History

A fire in 1782 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from river-glass salvaged after the Jormire viaduct was decommissioned in 1868. For nearly 55 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Cosima Voss family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 82 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Jormire. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Lazlo Voss in 1932 now handles most of the clockwork gears trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Jormire after the flood relief of 1952. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 81 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Lazlo Voss, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. Children in Zelspire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Cosima Voss and Imre Marchetti households have disputed a fence line since 1897 without ever raising their voices.
