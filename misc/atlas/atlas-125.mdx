## Overview

The settlement of Narmire grew up around a single warehouse in 1844, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Narmire sits where the rowdy lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Narmire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its rowdy residents perfectly.

## History

For nearly 13 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Edda Oduya family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1834 after Sigrid Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran lamp oil through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1797 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 21 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Morreach. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Sigrid Marchetti in 1809 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Morreach after the flood relief of 1906. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Edda Oduya keeps the toll records of 1961 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Sigrid Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 70 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Narmire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides. Feuds here are long but mild; the Edda Oduya and Otto Mollering households have disputed a fence line since 1844 without ever raising their voices.
