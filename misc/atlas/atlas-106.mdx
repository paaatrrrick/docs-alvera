## Overview

Haldane is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly. Most maps mark Haldane as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 30 miles in either direction. Haldane sits where the rowdy lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Haldane by the towpath from Erimire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

A fire in 1902 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from whale-bone veneer salvaged after the Erimire viaduct was decommissioned in 1916. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran almanac reprints through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Yutta Halloran family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Vesna Falkner in 1911 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Yutta Halloran keeps the toll records of 1857 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Erimire after the flood relief of 1944.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Yutta Halloran and Petra Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1901 without ever raising their voices. Children in Haldane learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Vesna Falkner, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides. The town counts perhaps 85 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
