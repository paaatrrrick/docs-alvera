## Overview

Tormore is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly. The settlement of Tormore grew up around a single warehouse in 1898, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Tormore by the towpath from Delbury, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Tormore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 23 miles in either direction.

## History

A fire in 1949 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Delbury viaduct was decommissioned in 1794. For nearly 20 years the town survived on a single export, loom parts, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Casper Oduya family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. ## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Rurik Voss in 1803 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 69 households still work the drying sheds, turning out almanac reprints for buyers as far away as Delbury.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Casper Oduya keeps the toll records of 1962 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Children in Tormore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 70 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Casper Oduya and Petra Quist households have disputed a fence line since 1903 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides.
