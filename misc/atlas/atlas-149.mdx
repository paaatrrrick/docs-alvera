## Overview

Narstead is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its celebrated residents perfectly. Most maps mark Narstead as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 40 miles in either direction. Narstead sits where the patient lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

Old records kept by the Halvard Petrov family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1795 after Greta Drummond petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran almanac reprints through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from pressed kelp paper salvaged after the Velgate viaduct was decommissioned in 1826. A fire in 1798 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 54 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Velgate. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Greta Drummond in 1867 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Halvard Petrov keeps the toll records of 1877 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Velgate after the flood relief of 1782.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Greta Drummond, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 75 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Halvard Petrov and Tilda Falkner households have disputed a fence line since 1947 without ever raising their voices. Children in Narstead learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.
