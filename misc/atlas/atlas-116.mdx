## Overview

Galford is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Galford by the towpath from Yarstead, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Galford sits where the prosperous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Galford as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 24 miles in either direction.

## History

Old records kept by the Halvard Halloran family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 19 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran clockwork gears through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. ## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 18 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Yarstead. The cooperative founded by Annik Greaves in 1802 now handles most of the clockwork gears trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose wax-sealed canvas roof was a gift from the town of Yarstead after the flood relief of 1826. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Halvard Halloran keeps the toll records of 1839 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 12 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Annik Greaves, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Halvard Halloran and Bram Holloway households have disputed a fence line since 1913 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Children in Galford learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
