## Overview

Fenmara sits where the ambitious lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Fenmara is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly. The settlement of Fenmara grew up around a single warehouse in 1810, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

Old records kept by the Mireille Severin family describe a still and windless month that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from hammered tin salvaged after the Delstead viaduct was decommissioned in 1854. For nearly 83 years the town survived on a single export, tar barrels, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran fermented tea bricks through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 27 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Delstead. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Delstead after the flood relief of 1850. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Mireille Severin keeps the toll records of 1893 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Feuds here are long but mild; the Mireille Severin and Emeric Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1821 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Edda Ashdown, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Fenmara learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 84 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
