## Overview

Most maps mark Yarford as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 49 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Yarford by the towpath from Wrenhollow, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Yarford is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its ambitious residents perfectly.

## History

The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Wrenhollow viaduct was decommissioned in 1887. A fire in 1923 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 52 years the town survived on a single export, loom parts, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1848 after Dario Ashdown petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Petra Kessler family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Dario Ashdown in 1884 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 55 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Wrenhollow. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Petra Kessler keeps the toll records of 1946 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Wrenhollow after the flood relief of 1782. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 79 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Dario Ashdown, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Petra Kessler and Beatrix Ashdown households have disputed a fence line since 1871 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. Children in Yarford learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
