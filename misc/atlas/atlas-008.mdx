## Overview

Velmara is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly. The settlement of Velmara grew up around a single warehouse in 1842, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Velmara by the towpath from Quilhollow, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Velmara sits where the meticulous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1862 after Stellan Severin petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1870 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. ## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Stellan Severin in 1953 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 17 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Quilhollow.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Quilhollow after the flood relief of 1788. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Otto Whitlock keeps the toll records of 1879 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Stellan Severin, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Velmara learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. Feuds here are long but mild; the Otto Whitlock and Sigrid Mollering households have disputed a fence line since 1900 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 30 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
