## Overview

Narspire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Narspire by the towpath from Tamvale, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Narspire grew up around a single warehouse in 1926, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

A fire in 1817 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from pressed kelp paper salvaged after the Tamvale viaduct was decommissioned in 1806. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 41 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses. The cooperative founded by Vesna Marchetti in 1812 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 66 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Tamvale. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Folke Falkner keeps the toll records of 1819 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Tamvale after the flood relief of 1876. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Folke Falkner and Cosima Severin households have disputed a fence line since 1818 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 68 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Narspire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. The current harbormaster, Vesna Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
