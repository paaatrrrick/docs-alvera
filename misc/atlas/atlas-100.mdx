## Overview

Travelers usually reach Caldane by the towpath from Fenmire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Caldane as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 43 miles in either direction. Caldane sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

A fire in 1945 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from fired clay salvaged after the Fenmire viaduct was decommissioned in 1896. Old records kept by the Mireille Lindqvist family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1840 after Stellan Brandt petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Stellan Brandt in 1960 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling spice tins, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 25 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Fenmire. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Fenmire after the flood relief of 1925. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Mireille Lindqvist keeps the toll records of 1788 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. The town counts perhaps 21 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Stellan Brandt, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Caldane learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Mireille Lindqvist and Imre Brandt households have disputed a fence line since 1867 without ever raising their voices.
