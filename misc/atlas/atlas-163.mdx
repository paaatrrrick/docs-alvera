## Overview

The settlement of Fenhollow grew up around a single warehouse in 1814, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Fenhollow as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 51 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Fenhollow by the towpath from Kelreach, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1811 after Sigrid Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1865 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 54 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Maren Vexley family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Sigrid Marchetti in 1910 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 60 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Kelreach. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Maren Vexley keeps the toll records of 1851 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Kelreach after the flood relief of 1789. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Maren Vexley and Tomas Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1893 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Sigrid Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Fenhollow learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost.
