## Overview

The settlement of Rosvik grew up around a single warehouse in 1906, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Rosvik sits where the wind-bitten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Rosvik as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 29 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Rosvik by the towpath from Ulmford, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1846 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1915 after Anselm Falkner petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Maren Whitlock family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 67 years the town survived on a single export, tar barrels, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the salt-cured oak workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 47 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Ulmford. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Anselm Falkner in 1901 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Maren Whitlock keeps the toll records of 1866 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose blue slate roof was a gift from the town of Ulmford after the flood relief of 1857. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Anselm Falkner, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. Feuds here are long but mild; the Maren Whitlock and Emeric Severin households have disputed a fence line since 1914 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 75 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Rosvik learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
