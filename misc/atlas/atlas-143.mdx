## Overview

Kelspire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly. Most maps mark Kelspire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 33 miles in either direction. Kelspire sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Kelspire by the towpath from Marrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

Old records kept by the Sigrid Holloway family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 12 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from lacquered birch salvaged after the Marrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1951. A fire in 1850 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1875 after Oona Quist petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 14 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Marrun. The cooperative founded by Oona Quist in 1825 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Marrun after the flood relief of 1801. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Sigrid Holloway keeps the toll records of 1799 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Sigrid Holloway and Annik Marchetti households have disputed a fence line since 1874 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Oona Quist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. The town counts perhaps 48 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Kelspire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
