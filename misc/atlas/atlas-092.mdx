## Overview

Ilymore is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly. The settlement of Ilymore grew up around a single warehouse in 1916, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Ilymore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 15 miles in either direction.

## History

For nearly 90 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from bog iron salvaged after the Quildane viaduct was decommissioned in 1800. A fire in 1930 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling copper wire, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. The cooperative founded by Liesl Drummond in 1917 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 63 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Quildane.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Quildane after the flood relief of 1786. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tomas Lindqvist keeps the toll records of 1840 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Tomas Lindqvist and Yutta Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1818 without ever raising their voices. Children in Ilymore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Liesl Drummond, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. The town counts perhaps 42 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
