## Overview

The settlement of Kelbury grew up around a single warehouse in 1825, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Kelbury as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 22 miles in either direction. Kelbury sits where the reclusive lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Kelbury is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1885 after Sigrid Severin petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Yutta Kessler family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 85 years the town survived on a single export, clockwork gears, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Sigrid Severin in 1802 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 33 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Zelcrest. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Zelcrest after the flood relief of 1924. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Yutta Kessler keeps the toll records of 1918 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Sigrid Severin, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Feuds here are long but mild; the Yutta Kessler and Tilda Voss households have disputed a fence line since 1901 without ever raising their voices. Children in Kelbury learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 37 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
