## Overview

Travelers usually reach Galwick by the towpath from Rosvik, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Galwick grew up around a single warehouse in 1904, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Galwick is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly.

## History

A fire in 1907 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 70 years the town survived on a single export, dye cakes, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran fermented tea bricks through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Rosvik viaduct was decommissioned in 1896.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. The cooperative founded by Beatrix Falkner in 1949 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Rosvik after the flood relief of 1905. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Stellan Ashdown keeps the toll records of 1831 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it.

## People and customs

Children in Galwick learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Stellan Ashdown and Imre Falkner households have disputed a fence line since 1853 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Beatrix Falkner, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies.
