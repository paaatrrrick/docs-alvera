## Overview

Yarmore is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its celebrated residents perfectly. Most maps mark Yarmore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 84 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Yarmore by the towpath from Kelgate, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Yarmore sits where the stubborn lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Kelgate viaduct was decommissioned in 1874. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1783 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Lazlo Severin family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. ## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Oona Oduya in 1880 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 40 households still work the drying sheds, turning out almanac reprints for buyers as far away as Kelgate. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Lazlo Severin keeps the toll records of 1878 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Kelgate after the flood relief of 1876. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Lazlo Severin and Tilda Halloran households have disputed a fence line since 1872 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 31 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Oona Oduya, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season.
