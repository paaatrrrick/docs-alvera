## Overview

Halholm sits where the stubborn lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Halholm is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its rowdy residents perfectly. Most maps mark Halholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 61 miles in either direction.

## History

A fire in 1837 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 22 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1898 after Maren Vexley petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran lamp oil through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from fired clay salvaged after the Delcrest viaduct was decommissioned in 1898.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 49 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Delcrest. The cooperative founded by Maren Vexley in 1785 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses. Apprenticeships in the lacquered birch workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Delcrest after the flood relief of 1792. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Petra Lindqvist keeps the toll records of 1843 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Feuds here are long but mild; the Petra Lindqvist and Yutta Holloway households have disputed a fence line since 1790 without ever raising their voices. Children in Halholm learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 71 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Maren Vexley, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
