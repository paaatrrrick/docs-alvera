## Overview

Travelers usually reach Marmara by the towpath from Brenford, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Marmara sits where the ambitious lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Marmara grew up around a single warehouse in 1785, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Marmara as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 73 miles in either direction.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran clockwork gears through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Casper Holloway family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1857 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1903 after Vesna Oduya petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Vesna Oduya in 1944 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose blue slate roof was a gift from the town of Brenford after the flood relief of 1794. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Casper Holloway keeps the toll records of 1886 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Vesna Oduya, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Casper Holloway and Cosima Severin households have disputed a fence line since 1862 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. The town counts perhaps 88 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Marmara learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
