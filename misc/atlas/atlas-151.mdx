## Overview

Lorcrest is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its stubborn residents perfectly. The settlement of Lorcrest grew up around a single warehouse in 1918, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Lorcrest by the towpath from Quilmore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

For nearly 58 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from pressed kelp paper salvaged after the Quilmore viaduct was decommissioned in 1859. Old records kept by the Beatrix Falkner family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1873 after Halvard Halloran petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran lamp oil through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. ## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Halvard Halloran in 1796 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses. Roughly 24 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Quilmore. Apprenticeships in the lacquered birch workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose wax-sealed canvas roof was a gift from the town of Quilmore after the flood relief of 1915. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Beatrix Falkner keeps the toll records of 1782 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Children in Lorcrest learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 88 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. Feuds here are long but mild; the Beatrix Falkner and Tomas Vexley households have disputed a fence line since 1929 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Halvard Halloran, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
