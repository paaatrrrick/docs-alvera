## Overview

The settlement of Zelrun grew up around a single warehouse in 1905, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Zelrun is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly. Most maps mark Zelrun as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 38 miles in either direction.

## History

Old records kept by the Petra Marrow family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Ashvale viaduct was decommissioned in 1894. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 39 years the town survived on a single export, clockwork gears, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1836 after Lazlo Quist petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 86 households still work the drying sheds, turning out loom parts for buyers as far away as Ashvale. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Lazlo Quist in 1946 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Petra Marrow keeps the toll records of 1819 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Ashvale after the flood relief of 1791.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Petra Marrow and Halvard Oduya households have disputed a fence line since 1871 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Lazlo Quist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 61 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Zelrun learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
