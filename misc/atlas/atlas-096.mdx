## Overview

Selcrest sits where the wind-bitten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Selcrest by the towpath from Permara, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Selcrest as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 72 miles in either direction. Selcrest is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1881 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from hammered tin salvaged after the Permara viaduct was decommissioned in 1791. ## Economy and trade

Roughly 28 households still work the drying sheds, turning out clockwork gears for buyers as far away as Permara. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Permara after the flood relief of 1813. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Greta Falkner keeps the toll records of 1784 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. Children in Selcrest learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Greta Falkner and Stellan Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1838 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 65 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Otto Drummond, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
