## Overview

Travelers usually reach Rosfell by the towpath from Narvik, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Rosfell as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 29 miles in either direction. Rosfell is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly. The settlement of Rosfell grew up around a single warehouse in 1783, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

For nearly 81 years the town survived on a single export, loom parts, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1962 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. ## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Yutta Oduya in 1855 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses. Roughly 61 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Narvik. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Narvik after the flood relief of 1829. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Maren Greaves keeps the toll records of 1791 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 16 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Maren Greaves and Petra Marchetti households have disputed a fence line since 1924 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. The current harbormaster, Yutta Oduya, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Rosfell learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
