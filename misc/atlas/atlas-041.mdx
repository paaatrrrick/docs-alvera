## Overview

Tammire sits where the wind-bitten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Tammire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 77 miles in either direction. Tammire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly. The settlement of Tammire grew up around a single warehouse in 1804, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

Old records kept by the Folke Kessler family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1819 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1836 after Dario Marrow petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Brenreach viaduct was decommissioned in 1855.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. The cooperative founded by Dario Marrow in 1822 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Brenreach after the flood relief of 1918. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Folke Kessler keeps the toll records of 1864 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. The town counts perhaps 88 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Dario Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Folke Kessler and Greta Halloran households have disputed a fence line since 1921 without ever raising their voices. Children in Tammire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
