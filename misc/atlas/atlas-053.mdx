## Overview

Permore sits where the stubborn lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Permore grew up around a single warehouse in 1964, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Permore is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran lamp oil through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1872 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 16 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1929 after Bram Halloran petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from fired clay salvaged after the Narcrest viaduct was decommissioned in 1873.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 21 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Narcrest. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Narcrest after the flood relief of 1802. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tilda Marrow keeps the toll records of 1787 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. The town counts perhaps 43 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Permore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Bram Halloran, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tilda Marrow and Bram Whitlock households have disputed a fence line since 1904 without ever raising their voices.
