## Overview

Travelers usually reach Delmire by the towpath from Ashrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Delmire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 18 miles in either direction. Delmire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly.

## History

The town hall was built from whale-bone veneer salvaged after the Ashrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1837. The first charter was granted in 1790 after Greta Sorvane petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. ## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Greta Sorvane in 1900 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Ashrun after the flood relief of 1799. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Mireille Brandt keeps the toll records of 1857 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Feuds here are long but mild; the Mireille Brandt and Oona Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1875 without ever raising their voices. Children in Delmire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 51 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
