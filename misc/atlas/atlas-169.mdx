## Overview

Most maps mark Vorvale as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 90 miles in either direction. Vorvale is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly. Vorvale sits where the quiet lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Vorvale grew up around a single warehouse in 1846, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran almanac reprints through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1836 after Dario Whitlock petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from woven reed salvaged after the Erirun viaduct was decommissioned in 1819. For nearly 30 years the town survived on a single export, tar barrels, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Vesna Whitlock family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 14 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Erirun. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Dario Whitlock in 1918 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Vesna Whitlock keeps the toll records of 1927 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Erirun after the flood relief of 1892.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 82 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. The current harbormaster, Dario Whitlock, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Vorvale learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
