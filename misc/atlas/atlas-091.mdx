## Overview

Travelers usually reach Halbury by the towpath from Lorford, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Halbury as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 69 miles in either direction. Halbury sits where the reclusive lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Halbury is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly.

## History

For nearly 44 years the town survived on a single export, dye cakes, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Greta Mollering family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1876 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from pressed kelp paper salvaged after the Lorford viaduct was decommissioned in 1895.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 48 households still work the drying sheds, turning out clockwork gears for buyers as far away as Lorford. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses. The cooperative founded by Folke Voss in 1933 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Lorford after the flood relief of 1954. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Greta Mollering keeps the toll records of 1956 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Folke Voss, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Greta Mollering and Beatrix Falkner households have disputed a fence line since 1785 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. The town counts perhaps 12 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
