## Overview

Travelers usually reach Haldane by the towpath from Dormire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Haldane sits where the half-forgotten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Haldane grew up around a single warehouse in 1786, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

A fire in 1817 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Yutta Greaves family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Dormire viaduct was decommissioned in 1912. The first charter was granted in 1878 after Edda Drummond petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Edda Drummond in 1870 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Yutta Greaves keeps the toll records of 1905 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Dormire after the flood relief of 1943.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Edda Drummond, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Haldane learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. Feuds here are long but mild; the Yutta Greaves and Ivo Oduya households have disputed a fence line since 1947 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 39 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
