## Overview

The settlement of Caldane grew up around a single warehouse in 1916, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Caldane is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its rowdy residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Caldane by the towpath from Selgate, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

Old records kept by the Rurik Quist family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1862 after Beatrix Voss petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Selgate viaduct was decommissioned in 1942. A fire in 1956 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the salt-cured oak workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 40 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Selgate. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose blue slate roof was a gift from the town of Selgate after the flood relief of 1808. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Rurik Quist keeps the toll records of 1917 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Beatrix Voss, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. Children in Caldane learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 61 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
