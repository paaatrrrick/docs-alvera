## Overview

Travelers usually reach Quilspire by the towpath from Torfell, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Quilspire sits where the stubborn lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Quilspire grew up around a single warehouse in 1936, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1804 after Bram Marrow petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Emeric Greaves family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Torfell viaduct was decommissioned in 1887.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 83 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Torfell. Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Emeric Greaves keeps the toll records of 1818 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Bram Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. The town counts perhaps 52 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Quilspire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Emeric Greaves and Annik Vexley households have disputed a fence line since 1859 without ever raising their voices.
