## Overview

Ilycrest is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its rowdy residents perfectly. Most maps mark Ilycrest as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 21 miles in either direction. The settlement of Ilycrest grew up around a single warehouse in 1839, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Ilycrest sits where the stubborn lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1830 after Sanna Voss petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran clockwork gears through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Erireach viaduct was decommissioned in 1801. Old records kept by the Yutta Voss family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 64 years the town survived on a single export, tar barrels, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 17 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Erireach. The cooperative founded by Sanna Voss in 1865 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Yutta Voss keeps the toll records of 1898 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Erireach after the flood relief of 1859. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Yutta Voss and Stellan Brandt households have disputed a fence line since 1893 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Sanna Voss, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Ilycrest learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 47 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.
