## Overview

Morfell sits where the rowdy lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Morfell is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly. The settlement of Morfell grew up around a single warehouse in 1861, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Morrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1868. Old records kept by the Tomas Brandt family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1955 after Greta Marrow petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 38 years the town survived on a single export, loom parts, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Greta Marrow in 1844 now handles most of the clockwork gears trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 71 households still work the drying sheds, turning out clockwork gears for buyers as far away as Morrun. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tomas Brandt keeps the toll records of 1828 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Morrun after the flood relief of 1956. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Greta Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tomas Brandt and Rurik Lindqvist households have disputed a fence line since 1828 without ever raising their voices. Children in Morfell learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. The town counts perhaps 81 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
