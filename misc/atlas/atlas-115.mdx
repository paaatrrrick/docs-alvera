## Overview

Most maps mark Kelford as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 76 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Kelford by the towpath from Vorvik, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Kelford grew up around a single warehouse in 1913, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The town hall was built from lacquered birch salvaged after the Vorvik viaduct was decommissioned in 1895. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran clockwork gears through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Casper Oduya family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1786 after Maren Lindqvist petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 77 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 73 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Vorvik. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Casper Oduya keeps the toll records of 1855 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Vorvik after the flood relief of 1877. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Casper Oduya and Otto Marchetti households have disputed a fence line since 1838 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Maren Lindqvist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides. Children in Kelford learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 50 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
