## Overview

Travelers usually reach Jormara by the towpath from Quilspire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Jormara grew up around a single warehouse in 1902, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Jormara as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 73 miles in either direction. Jormara is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly.

## History

Old records kept by the Anselm Holloway family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 19 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1822 after Tomas Drummond petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1839 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses. Apprenticeships in the salt-cured oak workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Tomas Drummond in 1836 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 65 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Quilspire.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Quilspire after the flood relief of 1915. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides. The town counts perhaps 66 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Jormara learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Tomas Drummond, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Anselm Holloway and Yutta Brandt households have disputed a fence line since 1867 without ever raising their voices.
