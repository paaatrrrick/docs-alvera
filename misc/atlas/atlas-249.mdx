## Overview

Torfell is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly. The settlement of Torfell grew up around a single warehouse in 1789, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Torfell as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 27 miles in either direction. Torfell sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1957 after Vesna Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from bog iron salvaged after the Selrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1917. Old records kept by the Stellan Drummond family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1914 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 42 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Selrun. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of glass floats that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Vesna Marchetti in 1949 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Selrun after the flood relief of 1870.

## People and customs

Children in Torfell learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 30 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Feuds here are long but mild; the Stellan Drummond and Anselm Quist households have disputed a fence line since 1883 without ever raising their voices.
