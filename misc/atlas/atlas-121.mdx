## Overview

Most maps mark Vordane as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 42 miles in either direction. The settlement of Vordane grew up around a single warehouse in 1789, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Vordane by the towpath from Ashton, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran loom parts through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 66 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Stellan Lindqvist family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1910 after Liesl Kessler petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Liesl Kessler in 1942 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 56 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Ashton. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Ashton after the flood relief of 1948. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Stellan Lindqvist keeps the toll records of 1900 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the celebrated hills behind it.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. Children in Vordane learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Liesl Kessler, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Stellan Lindqvist and Vesna Brandt households have disputed a fence line since 1911 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 75 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
