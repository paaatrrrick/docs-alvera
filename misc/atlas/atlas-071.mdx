## Overview

Most maps mark Vorvik as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 24 miles in either direction. Vorvik is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its stubborn residents perfectly. The settlement of Vorvik grew up around a single warehouse in 1821, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1962 after Folke Greaves petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from whale-bone veneer salvaged after the Cormara viaduct was decommissioned in 1868. For nearly 79 years the town survived on a single export, clockwork gears, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran clockwork gears through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1835 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses. Roughly 31 households still work the drying sheds, turning out clockwork gears for buyers as far away as Cormara. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Folke Greaves in 1931 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Halvard Vexley keeps the toll records of 1813 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Cormara after the flood relief of 1911. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Halvard Vexley and Edda Voss households have disputed a fence line since 1805 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. Children in Vorvik learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 19 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
