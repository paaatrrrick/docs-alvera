## Overview

Ossmore sits where the wind-bitten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Ossmore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 57 miles in either direction. The settlement of Ossmore grew up around a single warehouse in 1803, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

A fire in 1901 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1918 after Liesl Oduya petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 15 years the town survived on a single export, lamp oil, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran fermented tea bricks through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from bog iron salvaged after the Tormara viaduct was decommissioned in 1848.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 84 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Tormara. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling spice tins, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses. Apprenticeships in the salt-cured oak workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Tormara after the flood relief of 1802.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. The town counts perhaps 83 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Liesl Oduya, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Ossmore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Lazlo Kessler and Vesna Mollering households have disputed a fence line since 1842 without ever raising their voices.
