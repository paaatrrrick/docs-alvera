## Overview

Dormore is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its celebrated residents perfectly. Most maps mark Dormore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 69 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Dormore by the towpath from Yarspire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

Old records kept by the Oona Halloran family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1781 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Yarspire viaduct was decommissioned in 1841. ## Economy and trade

Roughly 45 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Yarspire. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Liesl Marchetti in 1784 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Yarspire after the flood relief of 1910. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the celebrated hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Oona Halloran keeps the toll records of 1791 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 20 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Liesl Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Oona Halloran and Sigrid Mollering households have disputed a fence line since 1857 without ever raising their voices. Children in Dormore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
