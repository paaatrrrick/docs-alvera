## Overview

Jorreach sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Jorreach is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its celebrated residents perfectly. The settlement of Jorreach grew up around a single warehouse in 1850, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Jorreach by the towpath from Ossfell, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. Roughly 63 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Ossfell. The cooperative founded by Petra Petrov in 1850 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Ossfell after the flood relief of 1824. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Edda Quist keeps the toll records of 1818 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Edda Quist and Dario Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1958 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Petra Petrov, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. The town counts perhaps 83 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
