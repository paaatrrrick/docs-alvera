## Overview

Travelers usually reach Quildane by the towpath from Corvik, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Quildane sits where the ambitious lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Quildane is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran tar barrels through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Ivo Sorvane family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from lacquered birch salvaged after the Corvik viaduct was decommissioned in 1940. The first charter was granted in 1813 after Rurik Petrov petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 47 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Rurik Petrov in 1827 now handles most of the clockwork gears trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses. Roughly 29 households still work the drying sheds, turning out clockwork gears for buyers as far away as Corvik.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Ivo Sorvane keeps the toll records of 1825 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Corvik after the flood relief of 1926. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it.

## People and customs

Children in Quildane learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. The current harbormaster, Rurik Petrov, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 74 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
