## Overview

Travelers usually reach Perhollow by the towpath from Yarspire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Perhollow sits where the half-forgotten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Perhollow is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly. Most maps mark Perhollow as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 25 miles in either direction.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1864 after Imre Petrov petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Bram Drummond family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 77 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1906 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from hammered tin salvaged after the Yarspire viaduct was decommissioned in 1889.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling spice tins, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 88 households still work the drying sheds, turning out clockwork gears for buyers as far away as Yarspire. The cooperative founded by Imre Petrov in 1881 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the restless hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Bram Drummond keeps the toll records of 1787 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 32 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Bram Drummond and Yutta Voss households have disputed a fence line since 1903 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. Children in Perhollow learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Imre Petrov, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
