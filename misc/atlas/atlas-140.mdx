## Overview

The settlement of Erifell grew up around a single warehouse in 1918, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Erifell sits where the quiet lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Erifell is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly. Most maps mark Erifell as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 33 miles in either direction.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1882 after Lazlo Quist petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran almanac reprints through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Selcrest viaduct was decommissioned in 1931. Old records kept by the Beatrix Halloran family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 50 years the town survived on a single export, clockwork gears, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling clockwork gears, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Lazlo Quist in 1850 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Selcrest after the flood relief of 1796. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Beatrix Halloran keeps the toll records of 1933 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. The town counts perhaps 33 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Lazlo Quist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Erifell learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
