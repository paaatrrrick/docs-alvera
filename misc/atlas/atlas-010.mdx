## Overview

Travelers usually reach Delvik by the towpath from Brenvik, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Delvik as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 79 miles in either direction. Delvik sits where the reclusive lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

A fire in 1921 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from fired clay salvaged after the Brenvik viaduct was decommissioned in 1958. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1962 after Joren Severin petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the lacquered birch workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses. The cooperative founded by Joren Severin in 1919 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Brenvik after the flood relief of 1797. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Liesl Sorvane keeps the toll records of 1945 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 82 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Joren Severin, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Liesl Sorvane and Otto Mollering households have disputed a fence line since 1845 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month.
