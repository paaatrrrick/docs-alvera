## Overview

Velvik is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly. Most maps mark Velvik as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 64 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Velvik by the towpath from Halreach, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Velvik sits where the patient lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

Old records kept by the Liesl Petrov family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 45 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from pressed kelp paper salvaged after the Halreach viaduct was decommissioned in 1826. The first charter was granted in 1942 after Otto Kessler petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. Roughly 17 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Halreach. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Otto Kessler in 1930 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Liesl Petrov keeps the toll records of 1793 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Halreach after the flood relief of 1806. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Liesl Petrov and Annik Holloway households have disputed a fence line since 1811 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Otto Kessler, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 89 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Velvik learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
