## Overview

Wrengate sits where the quiet lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Wrengate as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 61 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Wrengate by the towpath from Halspire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Wrengate grew up around a single warehouse in 1884, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The town hall was built from woven reed salvaged after the Halspire viaduct was decommissioned in 1844. ## Economy and trade

Roughly 67 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Halspire. Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. The cooperative founded by Sigrid Oduya in 1810 now handles most of the clockwork gears trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tilda Mollering keeps the toll records of 1877 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Halspire after the flood relief of 1907. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Sigrid Oduya, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. Children in Wrengate learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tilda Mollering and Petra Falkner households have disputed a fence line since 1870 without ever raising their voices.
