## Overview

Most maps mark Corgate as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 26 miles in either direction. The settlement of Corgate grew up around a single warehouse in 1904, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Corgate sits where the ambitious lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Corgate by the towpath from Ilygate, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran wool bales through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1897 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Ilygate viaduct was decommissioned in 1950. Old records kept by the Lazlo Falkner family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1811 after Joren Lindqvist petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. The cooperative founded by Joren Lindqvist in 1878 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Ilygate after the flood relief of 1841. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Lazlo Falkner and Anselm Greaves households have disputed a fence line since 1894 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 32 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Corgate learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Joren Lindqvist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
