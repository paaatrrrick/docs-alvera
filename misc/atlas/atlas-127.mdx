## Overview

Yarcrest sits where the wind-bitten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Yarcrest is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly. Most maps mark Yarcrest as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 26 miles in either direction. The settlement of Yarcrest grew up around a single warehouse in 1889, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

A fire in 1948 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 62 years the town survived on a single export, fermented tea bricks, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Beatrix Voss family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses. Roughly 74 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Lorton. The cooperative founded by Dario Vexley in 1853 now handles most of the clockwork gears trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Beatrix Voss keeps the toll records of 1866 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Lorton after the flood relief of 1817. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Dario Vexley, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Feuds here are long but mild; the Beatrix Voss and Oona Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1934 without ever raising their voices. Children in Yarcrest learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 84 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
