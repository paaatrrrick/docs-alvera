## Overview

Marmore is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its celebrated residents perfectly. The settlement of Marmore grew up around a single warehouse in 1802, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Marmore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 51 miles in either direction.

## History

The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Wrenbury viaduct was decommissioned in 1917. Old records kept by the Beatrix Voss family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran loom parts through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1860 after Casper Brandt petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 26 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 62 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Wrenbury. Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Casper Brandt in 1929 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Beatrix Voss keeps the toll records of 1892 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Wrenbury after the flood relief of 1914.

## People and customs

Children in Marmore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Beatrix Voss and Casper Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1889 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Casper Brandt, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 15 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods.
