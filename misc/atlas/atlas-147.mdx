## Overview

Most maps mark Ashfell as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 52 miles in either direction. Ashfell sits where the ambitious lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Ashfell grew up around a single warehouse in 1937, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Ashfell is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its stubborn residents perfectly.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Liesl Lindqvist family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1916 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 37 years the town survived on a single export, fermented tea bricks, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1783 after Vesna Marrow petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses. The cooperative founded by Vesna Marrow in 1831 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 54 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Ashvik.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Liesl Lindqvist keeps the toll records of 1851 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 38 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Liesl Lindqvist and Anselm Quist households have disputed a fence line since 1925 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Children in Ashfell learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Vesna Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
