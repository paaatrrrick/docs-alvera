## Overview

Travelers usually reach Jorshore by the towpath from Ashholm, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Jorshore sits where the rowdy lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Jorshore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 17 miles in either direction. The settlement of Jorshore grew up around a single warehouse in 1870, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Ashholm viaduct was decommissioned in 1928. Old records kept by the Annik Whitlock family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran fermented tea bricks through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1839 after Ivo Ashdown petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1831 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling spice tins, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Ivo Ashdown in 1871 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 90 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Ashholm.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Annik Whitlock keeps the toll records of 1924 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Ashholm after the flood relief of 1840. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Ivo Ashdown, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Jorshore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Annik Whitlock and Ivo Ashdown households have disputed a fence line since 1863 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 42 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
