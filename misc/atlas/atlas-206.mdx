## Overview

Most maps mark Galspire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 60 miles in either direction. The settlement of Galspire grew up around a single warehouse in 1937, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Galspire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Galspire by the towpath from Tamton, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

A fire in 1919 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Greta Kessler family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran tar barrels through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 83 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. ## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Vesna Ashdown in 1870 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses. Roughly 45 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Tamton.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Tamton after the flood relief of 1830.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 26 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Vesna Ashdown, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Greta Kessler and Beatrix Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1909 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. Children in Galspire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
