## Overview

The settlement of Vorreach grew up around a single warehouse in 1924, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Vorreach is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its stubborn residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Vorreach by the towpath from Marrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Vorreach as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 76 miles in either direction.

## History

For nearly 23 years the town survived on a single export, tar barrels, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1823 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran lamp oil through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from river-glass salvaged after the Marrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1847. The first charter was granted in 1781 after Tomas Kessler petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Tomas Kessler in 1872 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 33 households still work the drying sheds, turning out almanac reprints for buyers as far away as Marrun.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Beatrix Petrov keeps the toll records of 1928 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Marrun after the flood relief of 1919. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. Feuds here are long but mild; the Beatrix Petrov and Casper Severin households have disputed a fence line since 1824 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 72 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Tomas Kessler, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Vorreach learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
