## Overview

Fenholm sits where the wind-bitten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Fenholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 54 miles in either direction. Fenholm is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1831 after Dario Petrov petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 76 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1832 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Mireille Marchetti family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from lacquered birch salvaged after the Ashrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1844.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Dario Petrov in 1847 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 22 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Ashrun.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Ashrun after the flood relief of 1883. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Mireille Marchetti keeps the toll records of 1825 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Dario Petrov, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Mireille Marchetti and Liesl Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1787 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 26 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain.
