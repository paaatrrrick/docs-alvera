## Overview

Travelers usually reach Ulmholm by the towpath from Ulmton, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Ulmholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 48 miles in either direction. Ulmholm is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly.

## History

Old records kept by the Imre Halloran family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from fired clay salvaged after the Ulmton viaduct was decommissioned in 1927. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 22 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1919 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. Roughly 38 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Ulmton. The cooperative founded by Dario Brandt in 1923 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Ulmton after the flood relief of 1825. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Imre Halloran keeps the toll records of 1828 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Dario Brandt, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Ulmholm learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Imre Halloran and Halvard Severin households have disputed a fence line since 1923 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. The town counts perhaps 25 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
