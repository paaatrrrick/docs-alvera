## Overview

Torgate sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Torgate is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Torgate by the towpath from Fenford, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Fenford viaduct was decommissioned in 1943. A fire in 1895 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1797 after Sanna Drummond petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 14 years the town survived on a single export, fermented tea bricks, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran wool bales through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Sanna Drummond in 1924 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 34 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Fenford. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Anselm Falkner keeps the toll records of 1850 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Fenford after the flood relief of 1784. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Anselm Falkner and Anselm Greaves households have disputed a fence line since 1929 without ever raising their voices. Children in Torgate learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 38 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.
