## Overview

Most maps mark Ulmholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 45 miles in either direction. Ulmholm is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its restless residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Ulmholm by the towpath from Morstead, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Ulmholm sits where the half-forgotten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The town hall was built from whale-bone veneer salvaged after the Morstead viaduct was decommissioned in 1905. A fire in 1908 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. ## Economy and trade

Roughly 71 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Morstead. Apprenticeships in the lacquered birch workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Cosima Vexley in 1895 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Vesna Oduya keeps the toll records of 1899 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Morstead after the flood relief of 1860. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Children in Ulmholm learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 21 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Vesna Oduya and Oona Oduya households have disputed a fence line since 1885 without ever raising their voices.
