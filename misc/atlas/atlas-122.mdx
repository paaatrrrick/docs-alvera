## Overview

The settlement of Selshore grew up around a single warehouse in 1875, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Selshore is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Selshore by the towpath from Brenmara, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

Old records kept by the Joren Greaves family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1918 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 16 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from fired clay salvaged after the Brenmara viaduct was decommissioned in 1950.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Beatrix Marrow in 1959 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 83 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Brenmara. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Joren Greaves keeps the toll records of 1931 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose wax-sealed canvas roof was a gift from the town of Brenmara after the flood relief of 1937. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the meticulous hills behind it.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Joren Greaves and Yutta Oduya households have disputed a fence line since 1886 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Beatrix Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Selshore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
