## Overview

Velshore sits where the stubborn lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Velshore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 70 miles in either direction. Velshore is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly.

## History

The town hall was built from fired clay salvaged after the Eriholm viaduct was decommissioned in 1890. A fire in 1870 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Liesl Petrov family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

For nearly 49 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Liesl Petrov family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1796 after Annik Sorvane petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Annik Sorvane in 1869 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 24 households still work the drying sheds, turning out almanac reprints for buyers as far away as Eriholm. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling spice tins, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Eriholm after the flood relief of 1889. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Liesl Petrov keeps the toll records of 1942 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Annik Sorvane, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 48 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Liesl Petrov and Sanna Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1867 without ever raising their voices. Children in Velshore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides.
