## Overview

The settlement of Galshore grew up around a single warehouse in 1820, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Galshore by the towpath from Fendane, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Galshore is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its rowdy residents perfectly. Galshore sits where the wind-bitten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

Old records kept by the Dario Marrow family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran lamp oil through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 51 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1910 after Beatrix Drummond petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Fendane viaduct was decommissioned in 1887.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. Roughly 41 households still work the drying sheds, turning out clockwork gears for buyers as far away as Fendane. The cooperative founded by Beatrix Drummond in 1878 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the woven reed workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Dario Marrow keeps the toll records of 1922 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Fendane after the flood relief of 1914.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Beatrix Drummond, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. Feuds here are long but mild; the Dario Marrow and Otto Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1883 without ever raising their voices. Children in Galshore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 12 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
