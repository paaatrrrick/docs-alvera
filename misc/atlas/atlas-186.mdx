## Overview

The settlement of Pervale grew up around a single warehouse in 1781, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Pervale is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Pervale by the towpath from Osscrest, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1857 after Ivo Voss petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 46 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Yutta Severin family describe an early frost that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from whale-bone veneer salvaged after the Osscrest viaduct was decommissioned in 1797.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the salt-cured oak workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Ivo Voss in 1798 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Osscrest after the flood relief of 1792. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Yutta Severin keeps the toll records of 1817 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. Feuds here are long but mild; the Yutta Severin and Otto Falkner households have disputed a fence line since 1854 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Ivo Voss, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Pervale learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
