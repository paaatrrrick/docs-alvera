## Overview

Ulmgate sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Ulmgate by the towpath from Lorcrest, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Ulmgate is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly.

## History

A fire in 1841 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1821 after Liesl Voss petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran glass floats through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Stellan Greaves family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Lorcrest viaduct was decommissioned in 1834.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Liesl Voss in 1815 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses. Roughly 70 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Lorcrest.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Lorcrest after the flood relief of 1821. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Stellan Greaves keeps the toll records of 1913 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Children in Ulmgate learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 35 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Stellan Greaves and Dario Vexley households have disputed a fence line since 1871 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.
