## Overview

Most maps mark Quilcrest as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 40 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Quilcrest by the towpath from Quilreach, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Quilcrest is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 70 years the town survived on a single export, loom parts, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Petra Mollering family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1795 after Liesl Falkner petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1916 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Liesl Falkner in 1861 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 51 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Quilreach. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Petra Mollering keeps the toll records of 1872 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Quilreach after the flood relief of 1962. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Petra Mollering and Sanna Oduya households have disputed a fence line since 1877 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. The current harbormaster, Liesl Falkner, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 59 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
