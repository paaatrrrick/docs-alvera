## Overview

Vorreach sits where the rowdy lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Vorreach is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Vorreach by the towpath from Yarfell, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

The town hall was built from bog iron salvaged after the Yarfell viaduct was decommissioned in 1963. Old records kept by the Liesl Marchetti family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran almanac reprints through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1864 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 32 years the town survived on a single export, tar barrels, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 13 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Yarfell. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Stellan Severin in 1862 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of clockwork gears that crosses.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Liesl Marchetti keeps the toll records of 1929 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Yarfell after the flood relief of 1917.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Liesl Marchetti and Folke Quist households have disputed a fence line since 1936 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Stellan Severin, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. The town counts perhaps 77 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Vorreach learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
