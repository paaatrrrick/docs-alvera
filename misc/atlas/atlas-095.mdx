## Overview

Travelers usually reach Selvik by the towpath from Ilyshore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Selvik grew up around a single warehouse in 1938, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Selvik sits where the rowdy lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Selvik is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1869 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1805 after Sigrid Petrov petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Ilyshore viaduct was decommissioned in 1811.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Sigrid Petrov in 1821 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 61 households still work the drying sheds, turning out clockwork gears for buyers as far away as Ilyshore. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, clockwork gears, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Ilyshore after the flood relief of 1902. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Petra Severin keeps the toll records of 1929 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Children in Selvik learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 52 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Sigrid Petrov, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season.
