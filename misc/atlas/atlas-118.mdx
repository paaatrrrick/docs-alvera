## Overview

Most maps mark Velbury as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 68 miles in either direction. The settlement of Velbury grew up around a single warehouse in 1882, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Velbury is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Velbury by the towpath from Tamrun, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

For nearly 83 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Halvard Sorvane family describe a still and windless month that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Tamrun viaduct was decommissioned in 1939. The first charter was granted in 1938 after Maren Petrov petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 35 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Tamrun. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Maren Petrov in 1871 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Halvard Sorvane keeps the toll records of 1898 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Tamrun after the flood relief of 1887. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Maren Petrov, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. The town counts perhaps 34 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Halvard Sorvane and Stellan Voss households have disputed a fence line since 1851 without ever raising their voices.
