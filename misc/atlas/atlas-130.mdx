## Overview

Most maps mark Torford as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 17 miles in either direction. Torford sits where the reclusive lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Torford grew up around a single warehouse in 1934, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Torford by the towpath from Osswick, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

A fire in 1817 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Annik Quist family describe a still and windless month that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from lacquered birch salvaged after the Osswick viaduct was decommissioned in 1832.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Halvard Oduya in 1792 now handles most of the tar barrels trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 23 households still work the drying sheds, turning out almanac reprints for buyers as far away as Osswick. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Annik Quist keeps the toll records of 1885 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose blue slate roof was a gift from the town of Osswick after the flood relief of 1866.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 82 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Annik Quist and Bram Quist households have disputed a fence line since 1809 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. Children in Torford learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
