## Overview

Travelers usually reach Jorvale by the towpath from Perstead, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Jorvale as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 15 miles in either direction. The settlement of Jorvale grew up around a single warehouse in 1808, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

For nearly 28 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Cosima Lindqvist family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1876 after Vesna Voss petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran almanac reprints through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Perstead viaduct was decommissioned in 1794.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of glass floats that crosses. Roughly 29 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Perstead. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose blue slate roof was a gift from the town of Perstead after the flood relief of 1790. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Cosima Lindqvist keeps the toll records of 1836 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Vesna Voss, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Cosima Lindqvist and Casper Voss households have disputed a fence line since 1837 without ever raising their voices. Children in Jorvale learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 60 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
