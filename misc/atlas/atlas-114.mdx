## Overview

The settlement of Selholm grew up around a single warehouse in 1862, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Selholm is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly. Selholm sits where the prosperous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Selholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 13 miles in either direction.

## History

A fire in 1862 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1836 after Folke Ashdown petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Yarton viaduct was decommissioned in 1781. For nearly 23 years the town survived on a single export, dye cakes, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Liesl Quist family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 72 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Yarton. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Yarton after the flood relief of 1886. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Liesl Quist keeps the toll records of 1802 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 77 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Selholm learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Folke Ashdown, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. Feuds here are long but mild; the Liesl Quist and Sigrid Holloway households have disputed a fence line since 1786 without ever raising their voices.

## Getting there
