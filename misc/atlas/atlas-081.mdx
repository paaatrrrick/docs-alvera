## Overview

The settlement of Galhollow grew up around a single warehouse in 1885, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Galhollow by the towpath from Selcrest, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Galhollow as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 72 miles in either direction.

## History

Old records kept by the Vesna Falkner family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1880 after Imre Falkner petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran clockwork gears through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1907 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 39 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling lamp oil, copper wire, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Imre Falkner in 1890 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 43 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Selcrest.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Vesna Falkner keeps the toll records of 1857 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Selcrest after the flood relief of 1905. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 71 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. Feuds here are long but mild; the Vesna Falkner and Greta Halloran households have disputed a fence line since 1900 without ever raising their voices. Children in Galhollow learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
