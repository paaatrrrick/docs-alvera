## Overview

The settlement of Marmore grew up around a single warehouse in 1835, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Marmore by the towpath from Perspire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Marmore is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its rowdy residents perfectly.

## History

Old records kept by the Liesl Whitlock family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 65 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from bog iron salvaged after the Perspire viaduct was decommissioned in 1863. A fire in 1897 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1786 after Folke Halloran petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 17 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Perspire. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Folke Halloran in 1962 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of dye cakes that crosses.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Liesl Whitlock keeps the toll records of 1896 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose wax-sealed canvas roof was a gift from the town of Perspire after the flood relief of 1813.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. The town counts perhaps 88 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Liesl Whitlock and Anselm Falkner households have disputed a fence line since 1821 without ever raising their voices. Children in Marmore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
