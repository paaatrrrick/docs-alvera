## Overview

Marshore sits where the prosperous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Marshore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 81 miles in either direction. The settlement of Marshore grew up around a single warehouse in 1792, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

For nearly 38 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1817 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Tomas Severin family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from hammered tin salvaged after the Marmore viaduct was decommissioned in 1922. The first charter was granted in 1875 after Stellan Vexley petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. Roughly 38 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Marmore. The cooperative founded by Stellan Vexley in 1802 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the restless hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tomas Severin keeps the toll records of 1863 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Marmore after the flood relief of 1901. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Stellan Vexley, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Marshore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 66 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tomas Severin and Stellan Whitlock households have disputed a fence line since 1864 without ever raising their voices.
