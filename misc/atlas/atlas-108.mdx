## Overview

Travelers usually reach Galrun by the towpath from Wrenvale, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Galrun grew up around a single warehouse in 1826, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Galrun as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 90 miles in either direction. Galrun is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 30 years the town survived on a single export, clockwork gears, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from river-glass salvaged after the Wrenvale viaduct was decommissioned in 1921. ## Economy and trade

Roughly 51 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Wrenvale. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Maren Lindqvist in 1809 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 9 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Imre Lindqvist keeps the toll records of 1872 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 84 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. Children in Galrun learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Maren Lindqvist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.

## Getting there

Galrun sits where the prosperous lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.
