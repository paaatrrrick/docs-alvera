## Overview

Halstead is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly. Halstead sits where the patient lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Halstead as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 30 miles in either direction. The settlement of Halstead grew up around a single warehouse in 1932, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Tamshore viaduct was decommissioned in 1885. For nearly 26 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1930 after Ivo Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. ## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 84 households still work the drying sheds, turning out almanac reprints for buyers as far away as Tamshore. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Ivo Marchetti in 1954 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Stellan Greaves keeps the toll records of 1843 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Tamshore after the flood relief of 1866.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 79 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Stellan Greaves and Mireille Greaves households have disputed a fence line since 1818 without ever raising their voices. Children in Halstead learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides.
