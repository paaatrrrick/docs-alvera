## Overview

Most maps mark Roscrest as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 19 miles in either direction. Roscrest is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Roscrest by the towpath from Dorcrest, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

For nearly 53 years the town survived on a single export, tar barrels, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Rurik Voss family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1865 after Folke Halloran petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Dorcrest viaduct was decommissioned in 1827.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 43 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Dorcrest. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of glass floats that crosses. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Folke Halloran in 1889 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Dorcrest after the flood relief of 1826. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Rurik Voss keeps the toll records of 1947 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Children in Roscrest learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Folke Halloran, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 90 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Rurik Voss and Edda Marchetti households have disputed a fence line since 1827 without ever raising their voices.
