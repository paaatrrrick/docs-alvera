## Overview

Most maps mark Tamreach as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 50 miles in either direction. Tamreach is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its ambitious residents perfectly. The settlement of Tamreach grew up around a single warehouse in 1830, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1921 after Emeric Marrow petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1853 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Tilda Halloran family describe a hailstorm that stripped the orchards that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 36 years the town survived on a single export, lamp oil, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Emeric Marrow in 1823 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses. Roughly 23 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Erigate. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the fired clay workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Erigate after the flood relief of 1860. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tilda Halloran keeps the toll records of 1875 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the restless hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Emeric Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tilda Halloran and Annik Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1928 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 74 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Tamreach learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods.
