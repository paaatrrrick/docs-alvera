## Overview

Most maps mark Dorholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 34 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Dorholm by the towpath from Jorspire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Dorholm grew up around a single warehouse in 1927, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Dorholm is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its stubborn residents perfectly.

## History

Old records kept by the Tilda Kessler family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1843 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1805 after Bram Severin petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 18 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 57 households still work the drying sheds, turning out loom parts for buyers as far away as Jorspire. The cooperative founded by Bram Severin in 1829 now handles most of the clockwork gears trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Jorspire after the flood relief of 1873. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tilda Kessler keeps the toll records of 1955 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 30 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tilda Kessler and Folke Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1849 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Bram Severin, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the autumn king tides. Children in Dorholm learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
