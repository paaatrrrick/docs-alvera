## Overview

Quilfell sits where the rowdy lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Quilfell grew up around a single warehouse in 1928, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Quilfell by the towpath from Delgate, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

A fire in 1898 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Tilda Mollering family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1952 after Mireille Lindqvist petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 51 years the town survived on a single export, spice tins, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from lacquered birch salvaged after the Delgate viaduct was decommissioned in 1865.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 80 households still work the drying sheds, turning out dye cakes for buyers as far away as Delgate. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses. The cooperative founded by Mireille Lindqvist in 1799 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Delgate after the flood relief of 1936. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the ambitious hills behind it.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Mireille Lindqvist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 88 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Quilfell learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods.
