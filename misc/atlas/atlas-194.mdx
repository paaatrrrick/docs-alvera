## Overview

Brenbury is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly. Brenbury sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Brenbury grew up around a single warehouse in 1878, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

For nearly 39 years the town survived on a single export, clockwork gears, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Halvard Kessler family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Kelmire viaduct was decommissioned in 1853. A fire in 1929 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 73 households still work the drying sheds, turning out fermented tea bricks for buyers as far away as Kelmire. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Bram Sorvane in 1924 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Halvard Kessler keeps the toll records of 1932 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the patient hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose blue slate roof was a gift from the town of Kelmire after the flood relief of 1900.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 68 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Halvard Kessler and Vesna Halloran households have disputed a fence line since 1916 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. Children in Brenbury learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Bram Sorvane, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
