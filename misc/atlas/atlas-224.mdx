## Overview

The settlement of Fenvale grew up around a single warehouse in 1941, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Fenvale sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Travelers usually reach Fenvale by the towpath from Selvik, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1941 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Beatrix Brandt family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 22 years the town survived on a single export, copper wire, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1853 after Dario Lindqvist petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 87 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Selvik. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Dario Lindqvist in 1956 now handles most of the preserved lemons trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Beatrix Brandt keeps the toll records of 1855 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Selvik after the flood relief of 1792. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Dario Lindqvist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Children in Fenvale learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 29 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
