## Overview

Vorstead sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Vorstead grew up around a single warehouse in 1811, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Vorstead by the towpath from Fencrest, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Vorstead is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly.

## History

A fire in 1941 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1839 after Cosima Severin petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Annik Voss family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Fencrest viaduct was decommissioned in 1794.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 48 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Fencrest. The cooperative founded by Cosima Severin in 1922 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Fencrest after the flood relief of 1918. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Annik Voss keeps the toll records of 1881 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Feuds here are long but mild; the Annik Voss and Yutta Vexley households have disputed a fence line since 1818 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Cosima Severin, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 16 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Vorstead learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
