## Overview

Travelers usually reach Morreach by the towpath from Correach, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Morreach is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its ambitious residents perfectly. Most maps mark Morreach as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 30 miles in either direction.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Folke Oduya family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1923 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Correach viaduct was decommissioned in 1868. The first charter was granted in 1940 after Sanna Drummond petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the blue slate workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling copper wire, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses. The cooperative founded by Sanna Drummond in 1790 now handles most of the fermented tea bricks trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 85 households still work the drying sheds, turning out tar barrels for buyers as far away as Correach.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Folke Oduya keeps the toll records of 1830 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Correach after the flood relief of 1920.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Sanna Drummond, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. Children in Morreach learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Folke Oduya and Oona Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1880 without ever raising their voices.
