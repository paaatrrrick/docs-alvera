## Overview

Marholm sits where the wind-bitten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Marholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 38 miles in either direction. The settlement of Marholm grew up around a single warehouse in 1932, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Marholm by the towpath from Narshore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran wool bales through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1891 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. For nearly 49 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1882 after Vesna Marchetti petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 77 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Narshore. The cooperative founded by Vesna Marchetti in 1803 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling preserved lemons, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Narshore after the flood relief of 1923. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Beatrix Voss keeps the toll records of 1863 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 2 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Children in Marholm learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Beatrix Voss and Vesna Brandt households have disputed a fence line since 1846 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 53 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Vesna Marchetti, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
