## Overview

Most maps mark Ilygate as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 14 miles in either direction. Ilygate is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its celebrated residents perfectly. Ilygate sits where the reclusive lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The town hall was built from woven reed salvaged after the Torvik viaduct was decommissioned in 1859. For nearly 19 years the town survived on a single export, preserved lemons, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran fermented tea bricks through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. Old records kept by the Anselm Voss family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1937 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 16 households still work the drying sheds, turning out loom parts for buyers as far away as Torvik. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. Apprenticeships in the lacquered birch workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The cooperative founded by Imre Holloway in 1832 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Anselm Voss keeps the toll records of 1825 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Torvik after the flood relief of 1863.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. The current harbormaster, Imre Holloway, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Ilygate learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Anselm Voss and Lazlo Halloran households have disputed a fence line since 1876 without ever raising their voices.
