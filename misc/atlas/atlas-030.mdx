## Overview

Most maps mark Wrenholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 15 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Wrenholm by the towpath from Quilfell, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Wrenholm grew up around a single warehouse in 1863, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Quilfell viaduct was decommissioned in 1863. Old records kept by the Petra Oduya family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. A fire in 1857 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1881 after Petra Drummond petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran fermented tea bricks through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 55 households still work the drying sheds, turning out copper wire for buyers as far away as Quilfell. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses. The cooperative founded by Petra Drummond in 1887 now handles most of the clockwork gears trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Quilfell after the flood relief of 1911. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Petra Oduya keeps the toll records of 1963 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. Feuds here are long but mild; the Petra Oduya and Folke Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1936 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Petra Drummond, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Wrenholm learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 81 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
