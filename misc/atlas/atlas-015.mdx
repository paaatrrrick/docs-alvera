## Overview

Galfell is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its patient residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Galfell by the towpath from Kelvik, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Galfell grew up around a single warehouse in 1856, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. For nearly 37 years the town survived on a single export, tar barrels, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Greta Lindqvist in 1840 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 58 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Kelvik. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling copper wire, clockwork gears, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Maren Petrov keeps the toll records of 1875 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 6 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Kelvik after the flood relief of 1854.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Maren Petrov and Tilda Falkner households have disputed a fence line since 1888 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. The current harbormaster, Greta Lindqvist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Galfell learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
