## Overview

Travelers usually reach Ossmore by the towpath from Brencrest, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Ossmore grew up around a single warehouse in 1845, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Ossmore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 35 miles in either direction.

## History

For nearly 40 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1805 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The first charter was granted in 1875 after Stellan Sorvane petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. Old records kept by the Oona Ashdown family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from rope-fiber composite salvaged after the Brencrest viaduct was decommissioned in 1832.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Stellan Sorvane in 1912 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 90 households still work the drying sheds, turning out almanac reprints for buyers as far away as Brencrest. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of fermented tea bricks that crosses. Apprenticeships in the bog iron workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the rowdy hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Brencrest after the flood relief of 1914. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Oona Ashdown keeps the toll records of 1882 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Children in Ossmore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Oona Ashdown and Edda Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1910 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. The town counts perhaps 63 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Stellan Sorvane, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
