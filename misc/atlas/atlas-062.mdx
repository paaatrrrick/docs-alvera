## Overview

Most maps mark Halshore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 37 miles in either direction. Halshore is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its meticulous residents perfectly. Halshore sits where the half-forgotten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Halshore grew up around a single warehouse in 1926, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

A fire in 1803 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Tomas Holloway family describe the dry easterlies that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran wool bales through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1829 after Sanna Voss petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Sanna Voss in 1831 now handles most of the dye cakes trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the lacquered birch workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tomas Holloway keeps the toll records of 1791 framed above the bar. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose hammered tin roof was a gift from the town of Halholm after the flood relief of 1807. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Children in Halshore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The current harbormaster, Sanna Voss, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. The town counts perhaps 88 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
