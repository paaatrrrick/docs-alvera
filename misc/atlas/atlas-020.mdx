## Overview

Ilymire is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its rowdy residents perfectly. The settlement of Ilymire grew up around a single warehouse in 1792, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Ilymire sits where the quiet lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

The town hall was built from blue slate salvaged after the Wrenhollow viaduct was decommissioned in 1852. The first charter was granted in 1921 after Rurik Quist petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran dye cakes through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1821 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling almanac reprints, lamp oil, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Rurik Quist in 1951 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of preserved lemons that crosses. Roughly 54 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Wrenhollow.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Anselm Greaves keeps the toll records of 1782 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the restless hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose pressed kelp paper roof was a gift from the town of Wrenhollow after the flood relief of 1858.

## People and customs

Children in Ilymire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Feuds here are long but mild; the Anselm Greaves and Petra Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1815 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Rurik Quist, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
