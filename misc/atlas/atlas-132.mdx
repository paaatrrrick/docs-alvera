## Overview

Most maps mark Zelshore as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 78 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Zelshore by the towpath from Selbury, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. The settlement of Zelshore grew up around a single warehouse in 1872, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The town hall was built from river-glass salvaged after the Selbury viaduct was decommissioned in 1868. For nearly 55 years the town survived on a single export, lamp oil, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The first charter was granted in 1886 after Maren Vexley petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran fermented tea bricks through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling glass floats, fermented tea bricks, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses. Roughly 37 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Selbury. The cooperative founded by Maren Vexley in 1824 now handles most of the glass floats trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose salt-cured oak roof was a gift from the town of Selbury after the flood relief of 1936. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Imre Halloran keeps the toll records of 1796 framed above the bar. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the prosperous hills behind it.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Imre Halloran and Tomas Drummond households have disputed a fence line since 1883 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. The town counts perhaps 56 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Maren Vexley, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Zelshore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
