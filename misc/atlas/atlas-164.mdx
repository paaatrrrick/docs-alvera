## Overview

Delton is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly. The settlement of Delton grew up around a single warehouse in 1808, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Delton as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 54 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Delton by the towpath from Kelspire, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

For nearly 60 years the town survived on a single export, dye cakes, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Vesna Kessler family describe a long fog season that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The town hall was built from woven reed salvaged after the Kelspire viaduct was decommissioned in 1907. ## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of spice tins that crosses. Roughly 62 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Kelspire. The cooperative founded by Anselm Marrow in 1857 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the salt-cured oak workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the wind-bitten hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Vesna Kessler keeps the toll records of 1804 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Kelspire after the flood relief of 1886. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 76 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Vesna Kessler and Tomas Ashdown households have disputed a fence line since 1902 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. The current harbormaster, Anselm Marrow, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
