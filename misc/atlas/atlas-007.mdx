## Overview

Marton is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its wind-bitten residents perfectly. The settlement of Marton grew up around a single warehouse in 1838, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Marton sits where the stubborn lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Marton as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 73 miles in either direction.

## History

A fire in 1804 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Ivo Ashdown family describe the autumn king tides that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran preserved lemons through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The first charter was granted in 1826 after Mireille Falkner petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 72 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

The cooperative founded by Mireille Falkner in 1935 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 65 households still work the drying sheds, turning out preserved lemons for buyers as far away as Marmore. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, almanac reprints, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Ivo Ashdown keeps the toll records of 1832 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Marmore after the flood relief of 1852. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 37 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Mireille Falkner, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Feuds here are long but mild; the Ivo Ashdown and Oona Marrow households have disputed a fence line since 1846 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of weeks of sideways rain. Children in Marton learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
