## Overview

The settlement of Caldane grew up around a single warehouse in 1844, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Caldane as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 44 miles in either direction. Caldane is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Caldane by the towpath from Selwick, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

The town hall was built from pressed kelp paper salvaged after the Selwick viaduct was decommissioned in 1923. The first charter was granted in 1796 after Bram Holloway petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. A fire in 1816 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. ## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the pressed kelp paper workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling fermented tea bricks, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Bram Holloway in 1893 now handles most of the almanac reprints trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 84 households still work the drying sheds, turning out almanac reprints for buyers as far away as Selwick.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 4 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Halvard Marchetti keeps the toll records of 1784 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Selwick after the flood relief of 1962. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a still and windless month. Feuds here are long but mild; the Halvard Marchetti and Tilda Petrov households have disputed a fence line since 1860 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 80 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season.
