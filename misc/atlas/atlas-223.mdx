## Overview

Corholm sits where the reclusive lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Most maps mark Corholm as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 90 miles in either direction. Travelers usually reach Corholm by the towpath from Mormara, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

A fire in 1887 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran clockwork gears through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from whale-bone veneer salvaged after the Mormara viaduct was decommissioned in 1891. Old records kept by the Rurik Vexley family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 35 years the town survived on a single export, fermented tea bricks, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the river-glass workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling tar barrels, loom parts, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. The cooperative founded by Joren Ashdown in 1799 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Roughly 16 households still work the drying sheds, turning out loom parts for buyers as far away as Mormara. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of almanac reprints that crosses.

## Landmarks

Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Rurik Vexley keeps the toll records of 1802 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 7 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose fired clay roof was a gift from the town of Mormara after the flood relief of 1857. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the reclusive hills behind it.

## People and customs

Children in Corholm learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of the dry easterlies. The current harbormaster, Joren Ashdown, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 71 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Rurik Vexley and Oona Halloran households have disputed a fence line since 1866 without ever raising their voices.
