## Overview

Tamvale is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its quiet residents perfectly. Most maps mark Tamvale as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 29 miles in either direction. Tamvale sits where the patient lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

During the long embargo years, smugglers ran wool bales through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from wax-sealed canvas salvaged after the Jormire viaduct was decommissioned in 1917. For nearly 60 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. ## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the hammered tin workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 37 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Jormire. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of lamp oil that crosses. The cooperative founded by Anselm Brandt in 1919 now handles most of the lamp oil trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling spice tins, dye cakes, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tilda Quist keeps the toll records of 1896 framed above the bar. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose bog iron roof was a gift from the town of Jormire after the flood relief of 1900. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the stubborn hills behind it.

## People and customs

The town counts perhaps 56 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tilda Quist and Vesna Mollering households have disputed a fence line since 1815 without ever raising their voices. The current harbormaster, Anselm Brandt, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards. Children in Tamvale learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
