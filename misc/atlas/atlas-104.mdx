## Overview

Permara sits where the half-forgotten lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Permara is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its prosperous residents perfectly. Travelers usually reach Permara by the towpath from Dorfell, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Permara as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 33 miles in either direction.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1871 after Emeric Brandt petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. The town hall was built from salt-cured oak salvaged after the Dorfell viaduct was decommissioned in 1820. For nearly 27 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. ## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling loom parts, wool bales, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Roughly 60 households still work the drying sheds, turning out lamp oil for buyers as far away as Dorfell. The cooperative founded by Emeric Brandt in 1798 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the rope-fiber composite workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce.

## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the quiet hills behind it. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose river-glass roof was a gift from the town of Dorfell after the flood relief of 1862.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Emeric Brandt, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 37 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Permara learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost.
