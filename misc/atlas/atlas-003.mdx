## Overview

Jorshore sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. Jorshore is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its reclusive residents perfectly. The settlement of Jorshore grew up around a single warehouse in 1822, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Jorshore by the towpath from Haldane, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1831 after Annik Ashdown petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. For nearly 20 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. The town hall was built from pressed kelp paper salvaged after the Haldane viaduct was decommissioned in 1878. ## Economy and trade

Apprenticeships in the salt-cured oak workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of wool bales that crosses. The cooperative founded by Annik Ashdown in 1877 now handles most of the wool bales trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling spice tins, tar barrels, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose whale-bone veneer roof was a gift from the town of Haldane after the flood relief of 1906. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the restless hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Tilda Mollering keeps the toll records of 1812 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 5 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts.

## People and customs

Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. The current harbormaster, Annik Ashdown, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Children in Jorshore learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. The town counts perhaps 87 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tilda Mollering and Vesna Kessler households have disputed a fence line since 1799 without ever raising their voices.
