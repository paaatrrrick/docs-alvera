## Overview

Most maps mark Tammara as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 59 miles in either direction. The settlement of Tammara grew up around a single warehouse in 1920, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Tammara by the towpath from Wrenmara, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Tammara sits where the reclusive lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town.

## History

A fire in 1927 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. The town hall was built from woven reed salvaged after the Wrenmara viaduct was decommissioned in 1843. For nearly 72 years the town survived on a single export, loom parts, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. Old records kept by the Emeric Halloran family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran almanac reprints through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it.

## Economy and trade

The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of copper wire that crosses. The cooperative founded by Halvard Kessler in 1922 now handles most of the loom parts trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 35 households still work the drying sheds, turning out wool bales for buyers as far away as Wrenmara.

## Landmarks

Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose lacquered birch roof was a gift from the town of Wrenmara after the flood relief of 1784. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the celebrated hills behind it. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Emeric Halloran keeps the toll records of 1883 framed above the bar.

## People and customs

Feuds here are long but mild; the Emeric Halloran and Stellan Sorvane households have disputed a fence line since 1943 without ever raising their voices. The town counts perhaps 76 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Halvard Kessler, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a long fog season. Children in Tammara learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections.
