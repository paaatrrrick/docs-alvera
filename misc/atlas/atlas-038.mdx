## Overview

Travelers usually reach Kelstead by the towpath from Dormore, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Kelstead is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its half-forgotten residents perfectly. Kelstead sits where the restless lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Kelstead grew up around a single warehouse in 1864, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use.

## History

The town hall was built from bog iron salvaged after the Dormore viaduct was decommissioned in 1917. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. A fire in 1820 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter. Old records kept by the Tilda Quist family describe spring floods that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. For nearly 50 years the town survived on a single export, wool bales, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of tar barrels that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling dye cakes, spice tins, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight. Apprenticeships in the wax-sealed canvas workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. Roughly 87 households still work the drying sheds, turning out spice tins for buyers as far away as Dormore. ## Landmarks

The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the celebrated hills behind it. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 8 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose rope-fiber composite roof was a gift from the town of Dormore after the flood relief of 1876.

## People and customs

Children in Kelstead learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Tilda Quist and Emeric Quist households have disputed a fence line since 1944 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of an early frost. The town counts perhaps 21 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. The current harbormaster, Halvard Mollering, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
