## Overview

The settlement of Lorrun grew up around a single warehouse in 1790, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Travelers usually reach Lorrun by the towpath from Osswick, arriving at dusk when the lamplighters are working their way down the quay. Most maps mark Lorrun as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 72 miles in either direction. Lorrun is the kind of town that appears in ledgers more often than in stories, which suits its ambitious residents perfectly.

## History

Old records kept by the Liesl Greaves family describe weeks of sideways rain that forced the entire market underground into the cellars for a season. The first charter was granted in 1944 after Halvard Oduya petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran copper wire through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from river-glass salvaged after the Osswick viaduct was decommissioned in 1944. For nearly 78 years the town survived on a single export, almanac reprints, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 82 households still work the drying sheds, turning out loom parts for buyers as far away as Osswick. The cooperative founded by Halvard Oduya in 1835 now handles most of the spice tins trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the salt-cured oak workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of loom parts that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling spice tins, glass floats, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Liesl Greaves keeps the toll records of 1964 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Osswick after the flood relief of 1950.

## People and customs

The current harbormaster, Halvard Oduya, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture. The town counts perhaps 13 families as permanent, with a floating population of bargefolk that doubles the streets in high season. Children in Lorrun learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of a hailstorm that stripped the orchards.
