## Overview

Tamspire sits where the celebrated lowlands meet the old canal line, and it has never quite decided whether it is a port or a farming town. The settlement of Tamspire grew up around a single warehouse in 1895, and the warehouse is still there, leaning slightly, still in use. Most maps mark Tamspire as a minor crossing, but the people who live there will tell you it is the only sensible place to ford the river for 15 miles in either direction.

## History

The first charter was granted in 1948 after Otto Sorvane petitioned the regional assembly for the right to collect a bridge toll. During the long embargo years, smugglers ran spice tins through the reed channels north of town, and several prominent families quietly owe their fortunes to it. The town hall was built from whale-bone veneer salvaged after the Velmire viaduct was decommissioned in 1832. For nearly 71 years the town survived on a single export, glass floats, until the trade collapsed and the warehouses were converted to drying lofts. A fire in 1845 took the eastern quarter, and the rebuilt streets still follow the slightly odd angles the surveyors drew that winter.

## Economy and trade

Roughly 81 households still work the drying sheds, turning out glass floats for buyers as far away as Velmire. The cooperative founded by Otto Sorvane in 1894 now handles most of the copper wire trade and runs the only crane on the waterfront. Apprenticeships in the whale-bone veneer workshops remain the surest route to a steady living, and competition for them is fierce. A small but determined guild keeps the toll bridge in repair, funding the work through a levy on every barrel of glass floats that crosses. The market square fills twice a week with stalls selling wool bales, preserved lemons, and whatever the river barges brought in overnight.

## Landmarks

Locals insist the best view is from the grain stairs at low tide, looking back at the town with the half-forgotten hills behind it. Every visitor is eventually steered toward the tavern at the foot of the bridge, where Maren Quist keeps the toll records of 1820 framed above the bar. The museum occupies a former customs house and keeps an eccentric collection: toll tokens, barge figureheads, and a stuffed pike of improbable size. The walk along the old lock gates takes about an hour and passes 3 derelict windmills, each with its own resident colony of swifts. Visitors usually start at the bell tower, whose woven reed roof was a gift from the town of Velmire after the flood relief of 1916.

## People and customs

Children in Tamspire learn to scull before they learn to ride, and the school calendar still bends around the spring barge inspections. Feuds here are long but mild; the Maren Quist and Rurik Falkner households have disputed a fence line since 1916 without ever raising their voices. Newcomers are tolerated warmly and adopted slowly, usually after their first winter of spring floods. The current harbormaster, Otto Sorvane, inherited the post from a parent and grandparent before that, and treats the tide tables as a kind of scripture.
