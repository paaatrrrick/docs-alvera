## Summary

During weeks of sideways rain, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. At roughly 6:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Erimore stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The night crew at Erimore logged 6 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

Vesna Petrov traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Quilmore line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with rope-fiber composite, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1903 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 43 days earlier. ## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 82 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with wax-sealed canvas, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1900 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 29 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Sigrid Drummond has advocated since 1952. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Erimore yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 49 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
