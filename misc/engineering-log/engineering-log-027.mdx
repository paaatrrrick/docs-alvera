## Summary

The pressure readings from the Narton pumping station drifted upward for 6 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. The night crew at Narton logged 2 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. At roughly 9:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Narton stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 32 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Casper Brandt traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Mormire line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 63 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1809 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

fault-class: phantom-signal

## Root cause

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 24 days earlier. Casper Brandt traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Mormire line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 41 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Stellan Sorvane has advocated since 1910. We are adopting a rule from the Narton yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
