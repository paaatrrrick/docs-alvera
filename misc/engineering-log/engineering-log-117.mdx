## Summary

The night crew at Caldane logged 7 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. During spring floods, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The pressure readings from the Caldane pumping station drifted upward for 2 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 88 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with whale-bone veneer, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1898 that nobody documented. Stellan Kessler traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Pervik line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 53 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Caldane yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
