## Summary

A routine recalibration at Kelton was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1813. At roughly 9:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Wrenfell stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. During the autumn king tides, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 33 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Sanna Voss has advocated since 1831. We are adopting a rule from the Wrenfell yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
