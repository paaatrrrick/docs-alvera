## Summary

During the dry easterlies, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. At roughly 4:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Corwick stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The night crew at Corwick logged 5 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Root cause

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 87 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 57 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with lacquered birch, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1810 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 86 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Halvard Oduya has advocated since 1832. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Corwick yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
