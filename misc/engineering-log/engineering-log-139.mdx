## Summary

The pressure readings from the Yardane pumping station drifted upward for 4 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. At roughly 6:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Yardane stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The night crew at Yardane logged 6 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with bog iron, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1803 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 14 days earlier. Anselm Brandt traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Torford line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Root cause

Anselm Brandt traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Torford line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 20 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with rope-fiber composite, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1933 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Yardane yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 21 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
