## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 48 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 59 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1809 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 63 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Maren Whitlock has advocated since 1825. We are adopting a rule from the Narrun yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
