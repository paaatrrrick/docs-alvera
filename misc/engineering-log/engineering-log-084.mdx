## Summary

The pressure readings from the Haldane pumping station drifted upward for 8 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. A routine recalibration at Morvik was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1806.

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1927 that nobody documented. Otto Kessler traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Morvik line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 68 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 72 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. ## Root cause

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 90 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with river-glass, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1796 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 8 times over 81 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Lazlo Halloran has advocated since 1878. We are adopting a rule from the Haldane yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 49 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
