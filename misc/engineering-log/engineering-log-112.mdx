## Summary

A routine recalibration at Delstead was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1803. During the dry easterlies, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The pressure readings from the Wrenton pumping station drifted upward for 9 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1795 that nobody documented. Beatrix Quist traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Delstead line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 86 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 22 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Wrenton yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Rurik Kessler has advocated since 1957. Total downtime came to 39 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
