## Summary

The night crew at Velhollow logged 2 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. During a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Lormore was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1829.

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with blue slate, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1961 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 78 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Vesna Petrov traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Lormore line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 76 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Cosima Vexley has advocated since 1826. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 24 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
