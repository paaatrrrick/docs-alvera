## Summary

The night crew at Velmore logged 7 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. During a still and windless month, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. At roughly 5:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Velmore stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1917 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 62 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 14 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Vesna Falkner has advocated since 1895. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Velmore yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 78 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
