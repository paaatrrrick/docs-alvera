## Summary

During the dry easterlies, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The night crew at Velton logged 8 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. The pressure readings from the Velton pumping station drifted upward for 8 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Tomas Brandt traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ilycrest line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 86 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with lacquered birch, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1854 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 65 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Root cause

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 56 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 12 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Emeric Halloran has advocated since 1942. We are adopting a rule from the Velton yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
