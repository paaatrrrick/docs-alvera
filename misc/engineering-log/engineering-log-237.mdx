## Summary

The night crew at Ulmvale logged 6 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. During the dry easterlies, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The pressure readings from the Ulmvale pumping station drifted upward for 7 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Timeline

- Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 88 days earlier.
- Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with wax-sealed canvas, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1897 that nobody documented.
- A routine recalibration at Calton was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1939.
- At roughly 7:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Ulmvale stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.
- Lazlo Vexley traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Calton line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Investigation

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 82 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with rope-fiber composite, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1847 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 75 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Ulmvale yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 90 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Rurik Petrov has advocated since 1952.
