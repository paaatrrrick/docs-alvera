## Summary

At roughly 2:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Fenton stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The pressure readings from the Fenton pumping station drifted upward for 5 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. A routine recalibration at Brenton was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1855.

## Investigation

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. ## Root cause

Tilda Holloway traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Brenton line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 63 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 20 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with bog iron, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1893 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Fenton yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 86 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Anselm Severin has advocated since 1925.
