## Summary

A routine recalibration at Ilyshore was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1885. At roughly 7:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Fenfell stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The pressure readings from the Fenfell pumping station drifted upward for 8 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Dario Marchetti traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ilyshore line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 14 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 57 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with pressed kelp paper, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1896 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Fenfell yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Casper Halloran has advocated since 1871. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 30 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
