## Summary

A routine recalibration at Yarford was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1902. At roughly 5:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Tamstead stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The pressure readings from the Tamstead pumping station drifted upward for 6 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Anselm Petrov has advocated since 1820. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Tamstead yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 82 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
