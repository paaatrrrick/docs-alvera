## Summary

During an early frost, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The night crew at Kelton logged 8 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. The pressure readings from the Kelton pumping station drifted upward for 3 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Maren Severin traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Erimire line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with river-glass, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1936 that nobody documented. ## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 25 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with rope-fiber composite, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1930 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 45 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Mireille Holloway has advocated since 1934. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Kelton yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
