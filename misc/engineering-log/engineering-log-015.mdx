## Investigation

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 51 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with bog iron, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1803 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 79 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 7 times over 17 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Dario Voss traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Lorford line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1945 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Otto Oduya has advocated since 1799. Total downtime came to 75 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Calvik yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
