## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 14 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Mireille Voss traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Velspire line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with river-glass, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1827 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 23 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Fenmore yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Bram Oduya has advocated since 1954. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 42 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
