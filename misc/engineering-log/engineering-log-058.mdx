## Summary

During weeks of sideways rain, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The pressure readings from the Lorreach pumping station drifted upward for 2 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. The night crew at Lorreach logged 6 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 86 days earlier. Anselm Mollering traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ericrest line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1846 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 65 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 55 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Lorreach yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Ivo Quist has advocated since 1792. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 77 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
