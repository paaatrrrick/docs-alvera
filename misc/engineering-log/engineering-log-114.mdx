## Summary

A routine recalibration at Galmire was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1869. At roughly 4:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Corvik stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. During a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode.

## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 85 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with bog iron, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1811 that nobody documented. ## Root cause

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 31 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 7 times over 68 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1855 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 26 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. We are adopting a rule from the Corvik yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Folke Vexley has advocated since 1837. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
