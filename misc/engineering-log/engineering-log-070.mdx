## Summary

The night crew at Galholm logged 2 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. During weeks of sideways rain, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Wrenreach was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1833.

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1835 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 53 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Folke Ashdown traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Wrenreach line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 16 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 33 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Tomas Brandt has advocated since 1862. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Galholm yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
