## Summary

At roughly 6:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Cormore stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. During an early frost, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The pressure readings from the Cormore pumping station drifted upward for 9 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1799 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 63 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. ## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1886 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 88 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 16 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Cormore yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 52 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Dario Mollering has advocated since 1835.
