## Investigation

Oona Holloway traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Galmore line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with whale-bone veneer, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1944 that nobody documented. ## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with bog iron, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1822 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 89 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 42 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 88 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Otto Voss has advocated since 1884. We are adopting a rule from the Selmire yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
