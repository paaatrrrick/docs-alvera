## Summary

The pressure readings from the Kelvik pumping station drifted upward for 6 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. At roughly 3:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Kelvik stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. A routine recalibration at Delmara was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1870.

## Investigation

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 75 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1854 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 13 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Root cause

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 35 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with pressed kelp paper, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1900 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Kelvik yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Yutta Marrow has advocated since 1895.
