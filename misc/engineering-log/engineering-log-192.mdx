## Summary

The pressure readings from the Ossdane pumping station drifted upward for 6 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. At roughly 3:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Ossdane stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. A routine recalibration at Calmara was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1838.

## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 31 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 46 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1917 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Maren Whitlock has advocated since 1837. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Ossdane yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
