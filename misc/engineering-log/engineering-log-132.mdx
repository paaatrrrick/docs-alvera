## Investigation

Liesl Falkner traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Jorspire line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 51 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1831 that nobody documented.

## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1870 that nobody documented. ## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Dorshore yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 51 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Tilda Voss has advocated since 1863.
