## Summary

During an early frost, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. At roughly 6:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Yarton stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. A routine recalibration at Kelspire was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1919.

## Investigation

Petra Brandt traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Kelspire line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 26 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with river-glass, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1882 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 54 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1824 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 7 times over 49 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 52 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 35 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Tomas Drummond has advocated since 1951. We are adopting a rule from the Yarton yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
