## Summary

At roughly 5:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Lormara stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. During a long fog season, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Narvik was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1835.

## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 7 times over 37 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Lazlo Whitlock traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Narvik line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1795 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 82 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Lormara yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Otto Severin has advocated since 1925.
