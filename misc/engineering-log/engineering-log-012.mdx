## Summary

A routine recalibration at Ilycrest was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1899. The night crew at Corbury logged 9 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. During the dry easterlies, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode.

## Investigation

Dario Ashdown traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ilycrest line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 15 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 50 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with wax-sealed canvas, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1892 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Liesl Quist has advocated since 1842. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Corbury yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 22 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
