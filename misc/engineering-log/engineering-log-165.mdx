## Summary

During an early frost, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. At roughly 5:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Lorstead stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. A routine recalibration at Corwick was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1818.

## Root cause

Tomas Mollering traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Corwick line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 18 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 20 days earlier. ## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Rurik Whitlock has advocated since 1839. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Lorstead yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
