## Summary

During a still and windless month, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The pressure readings from the Perholm pumping station drifted upward for 3 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. At roughly 9:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Perholm stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 64 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 28 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1852 that nobody documented. ## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 68 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Folke Quist has advocated since 1944. We are adopting a rule from the Perholm yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
