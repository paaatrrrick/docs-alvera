## Summary

The night crew at Wrenholm logged 6 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. A routine recalibration at Yarhollow was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1893. At roughly 9:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Wrenholm stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Investigation

Tomas Oduya traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Yarhollow line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1882 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 31 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Wrenholm yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Folke Falkner has advocated since 1798. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 27 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
