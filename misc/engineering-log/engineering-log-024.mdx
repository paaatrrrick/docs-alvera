## Summary

At roughly 6:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Ashford stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. A routine recalibration at Jorvik was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1896. The pressure readings from the Ashford pumping station drifted upward for 3 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1955 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 89 days earlier. Casper Ashdown traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Jorvik line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 8 times over 71 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Ashford yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Edda Voss has advocated since 1928. Total downtime came to 31 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
