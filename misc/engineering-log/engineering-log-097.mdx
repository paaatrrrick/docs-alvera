## Summary

A routine recalibration at Tamholm was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1803. The night crew at Galholm logged 2 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. The pressure readings from the Galholm pumping station drifted upward for 2 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Petra Falkner traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Tamholm line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 51 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with whale-bone veneer, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1793 that nobody documented.

## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 37 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with rope-fiber composite, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1838 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 36 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 45 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. We are adopting a rule from the Galholm yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
