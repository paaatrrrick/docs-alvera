## Summary

During a still and windless month, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Narbury was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1960. The pressure readings from the Ashfell pumping station drifted upward for 6 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 79 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 72 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Lazlo Ashdown traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Narbury line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Mireille Drummond has advocated since 1796. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Ashfell yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 38 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
