## Summary

At roughly 7:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Delfell stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. During an early frost, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The pressure readings from the Delfell pumping station drifted upward for 5 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Maren Petrov traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Yarford line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 41 days earlier. ## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 66 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. We are adopting a rule from the Delfell yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Sanna Whitlock has advocated since 1826. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
