## Summary

A routine recalibration at Quilford was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1901. During a still and windless month, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The pressure readings from the Vorstead pumping station drifted upward for 2 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with blue slate, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1839 that nobody documented. Anselm Falkner traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Quilford line, subtly out of tolerance. ## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 48 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Vorstead yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
