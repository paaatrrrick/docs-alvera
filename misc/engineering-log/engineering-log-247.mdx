## Summary

At roughly 4:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Selcrest stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. During weeks of sideways rain, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Velmara was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1931.

## Investigation

Mireille Sorvane traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Velmara line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 61 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1855 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. ## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Folke Halloran has advocated since 1880. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 84 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
