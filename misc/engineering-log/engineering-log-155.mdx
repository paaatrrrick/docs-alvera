## Summary

A routine recalibration at Lorholm was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1929. The night crew at Tammore logged 7 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. At roughly 4:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Tammore stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Investigation

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 8 times over 17 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 53 days earlier. Greta Halloran traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Lorholm line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with rope-fiber composite, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1793 that nobody documented.

## Root cause

Greta Halloran traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Lorholm line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 54 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1964 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 30 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Tammore yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Dario Severin has advocated since 1823.
