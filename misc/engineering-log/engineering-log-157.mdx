## Summary

The pressure readings from the Lorstead pumping station drifted upward for 2 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. At roughly 9:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Lorstead stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. A routine recalibration at Delton was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1916.

## Investigation

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 81 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with blue slate, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1782 that nobody documented. Beatrix Petrov traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Delton line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with lacquered birch, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1960 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. ## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Imre Marchetti has advocated since 1885. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Lorstead yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 14 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
