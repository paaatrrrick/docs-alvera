## Summary

A routine recalibration at Ilymara was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1862. At roughly 6:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Torspire stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The night crew at Torspire logged 9 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1841 that nobody documented. Petra Kessler traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ilymara line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 74 days earlier. ## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 50 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with pressed kelp paper, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1886 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 51 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Sanna Oduya has advocated since 1908. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Torspire yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
