The night crew at Quilrun logged 6 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. During the dry easterlies, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. ## Investigation

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with lacquered birch, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1895 that nobody documented. Edda Lindqvist traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Narshore line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 31 days earlier. ## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 39 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Yutta Kessler has advocated since 1899.
