## Summary

A routine recalibration at Torhollow was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1858. The pressure readings from the Galfell pumping station drifted upward for 8 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. At roughly 6:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Galfell stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with wax-sealed canvas, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1944 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 7 times over 60 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 56 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. We are adopting a rule from the Galfell yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Ivo Oduya has advocated since 1945.
