## Summary

A routine recalibration at Morcrest was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1854. At roughly 3:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Jorspire stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The night crew at Jorspire logged 2 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

Ivo Falkner traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Morcrest line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with rope-fiber composite, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1961 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 80 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 83 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 46 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 55 days earlier. Ivo Falkner traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Morcrest line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Jorspire yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 16 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Cosima Greaves has advocated since 1853.
