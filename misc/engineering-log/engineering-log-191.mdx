## Summary

During the dry easterlies, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Eriford was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1831. At roughly 5:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Ossstead stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Root cause

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 41 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Ossstead yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 43 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Halvard Sorvane has advocated since 1831. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
