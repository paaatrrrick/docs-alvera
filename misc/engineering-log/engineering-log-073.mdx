## Summary

The night crew at Erigate logged 7 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. During an early frost, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Ilyfell was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1815.

## Investigation

Folke Kessler traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ilyfell line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with wax-sealed canvas, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1947 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 45 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 21 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 58 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 24 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Erigate yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
