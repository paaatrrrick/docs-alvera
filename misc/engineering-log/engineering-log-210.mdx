## Summary

A routine recalibration at Wrenshore was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1783. The night crew at Dorreach logged 9 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. The pressure readings from the Dorreach pumping station drifted upward for 4 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Tilda Mollering traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Wrenshore line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 26 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 90 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 75 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Bram Sorvane has advocated since 1941. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Dorreach yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
