## Summary

At roughly 6:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Calstead stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The night crew at Calstead logged 7 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. During an early frost, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode.

## Investigation

Edda Severin traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ulmvik line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 73 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 74 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with blue slate, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1905 that nobody documented.

## Root cause

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 28 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 55 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with woven reed, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1809 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Lazlo Brandt has advocated since 1963. Total downtime came to 45 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. We are adopting a rule from the Calstead yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
