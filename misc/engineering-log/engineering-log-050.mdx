## Summary

At roughly 4:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Quilton stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. A routine recalibration at Rosgate was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1819. The night crew at Quilton logged 9 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Tomas Marchetti has advocated since 1952. Total downtime came to 44 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Quilton yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
