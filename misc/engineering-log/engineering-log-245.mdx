## Summary

The night crew at Wrencrest logged 9 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. During a still and windless month, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Dordane was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1884.

## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with woven reed, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1860 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. ## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Casper Drummond has advocated since 1804. Total downtime came to 52 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
