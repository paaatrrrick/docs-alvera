## Summary

A routine recalibration at Ossholm was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1787. At roughly 2:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Tamrun stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The night crew at Tamrun logged 4 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1799 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 12 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. ## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 49 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Ivo Lindqvist traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ossholm line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 25 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Halvard Vexley has advocated since 1852.
