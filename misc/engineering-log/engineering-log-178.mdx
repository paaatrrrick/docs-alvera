## Summary

A routine recalibration at Perrun was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1819. The pressure readings from the Selfell pumping station drifted upward for 7 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. The night crew at Selfell logged 9 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 8 times over 84 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 37 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with river-glass, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1933 that nobody documented. Liesl Sorvane traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Perrun line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 38 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Selfell yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
