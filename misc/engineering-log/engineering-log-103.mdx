## Summary

The night crew at Narfell logged 5 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. At roughly 6:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Narfell stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The pressure readings from the Narfell pumping station drifted upward for 2 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 68 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 73 days earlier. Maren Marrow traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Selton line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with lacquered birch, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1932 that nobody documented.

## Root cause

Maren Marrow traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Selton line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 28 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 39 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Sanna Kessler has advocated since 1915. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
