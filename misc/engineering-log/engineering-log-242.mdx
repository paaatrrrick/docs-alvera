## Summary

A routine recalibration at Ashton was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1872. The night crew at Velhollow logged 4 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. ## Investigation

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 21 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1881 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 81 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 8 times over 30 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 24 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Ivo Kessler has advocated since 1959. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Velhollow yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
