## Summary

A routine recalibration at Kelwick was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1908. During a still and windless month, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. At roughly 9:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Marshore stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Timeline

- The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 7 times over 25 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.
- The night crew at Marshore logged 2 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.
- Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.
- The pressure readings from the Marshore pumping station drifted upward for 6 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1917 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 81 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 83 days earlier. Cosima Ashdown traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Kelwick line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Root cause

Cosima Ashdown traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Kelwick line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 76 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 67 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 44 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Casper Lindqvist has advocated since 1897. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Marshore yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
