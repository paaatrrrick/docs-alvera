## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 18 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Liesl Voss traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Erihollow line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 43 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1839 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 51 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Delford yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
