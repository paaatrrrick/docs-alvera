## Summary

The night crew at Vorvik logged 9 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. A routine recalibration at Ossreach was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1817. At roughly 6:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Vorvik stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 34 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 74 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with woven reed, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1803 that nobody documented. Stellan Ashdown traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ossreach line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 64 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Cosima Holloway has advocated since 1846. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
