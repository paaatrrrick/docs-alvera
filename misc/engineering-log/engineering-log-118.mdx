## Root cause

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 49 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Edda Holloway traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ulmshore line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with blue slate, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1846 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Osshollow yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 34 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Annik Ashdown has advocated since 1818. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
