## Summary

A routine recalibration at Wrenbury was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1802. The night crew at Caldane logged 5 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. ## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 21 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 57 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1923 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 78 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Ivo Voss has advocated since 1836. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Caldane yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
