## Summary

A routine recalibration at Perhollow was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1960. During the autumn king tides, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The night crew at Ilystead logged 7 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

Oona Whitlock traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Perhollow line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with lacquered birch, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1960 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 56 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

```text

station: ilystead

fault-class: contact-corrosion

first-logged: 13 June

recurrences: 9

owner: folke.severin

```

## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 29 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with whale-bone veneer, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1951 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 87 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 57 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. We are adopting a rule from the Ilystead yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
