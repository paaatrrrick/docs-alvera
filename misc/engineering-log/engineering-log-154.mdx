## Summary

A routine recalibration at Calcrest was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1926. The pressure readings from the Calgate pumping station drifted upward for 2 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. The night crew at Calgate logged 6 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

Beatrix Quist traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Calcrest line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 32 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with bog iron, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1957 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Root cause

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Beatrix Quist traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Calcrest line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with whale-bone veneer, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1787 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 73 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Tomas Ashdown has advocated since 1850. We are adopting a rule from the Calgate yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 44 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
