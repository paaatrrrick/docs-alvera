## Summary

A routine recalibration at Erimire was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1890. During a long fog season, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The night crew at Cormire logged 4 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with whale-bone veneer, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1834 that nobody documented. Folke Brandt traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Erimire line, subtly out of tolerance. ## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with lacquered birch, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1812 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 74 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 32 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Cormire yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 30 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Imre Marrow has advocated since 1960. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
