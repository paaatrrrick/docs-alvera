## Summary

A routine recalibration at Tormire was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1803. During spring floods, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The pressure readings from the Calholm pumping station drifted upward for 6 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Folke Ashdown traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Tormire line, subtly out of tolerance. ## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Tomas Marrow has advocated since 1905. Total downtime came to 59 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. We are adopting a rule from the Calholm yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
