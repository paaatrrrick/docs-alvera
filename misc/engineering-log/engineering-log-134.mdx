## Summary

A routine recalibration at Tormire was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1830. The pressure readings from the Dormara pumping station drifted upward for 9 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. During a still and windless month, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode.

## Investigation

Imre Brandt traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Tormire line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with whale-bone veneer, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1932 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 54 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. ## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Dormara yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 18 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
