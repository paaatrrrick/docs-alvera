## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with pressed kelp paper, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1927 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 80 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 25 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Liesl Greaves traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Erimore line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with rope-fiber composite, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1823 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 25 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Liesl Greaves traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Erimore line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Ossrun yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 69 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Folke Oduya has advocated since 1834. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
