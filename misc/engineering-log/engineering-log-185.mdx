## Root cause

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 23 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with whale-bone veneer, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1896 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Otto Oduya traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Galfell line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Bram Whitlock has advocated since 1905. We are adopting a rule from the Osston yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 32 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
