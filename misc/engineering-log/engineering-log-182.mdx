## Summary

A routine recalibration at Marvale was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1847. The night crew at Zelgate logged 3 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. At roughly 5:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Zelgate stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Investigation

Halvard Sorvane traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Marvale line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with bog iron, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1835 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 7 times over 21 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with river-glass, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1852 that nobody documented. Halvard Sorvane traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Marvale line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 50 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Zelgate yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Joren Oduya has advocated since 1946. Total downtime came to 22 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
