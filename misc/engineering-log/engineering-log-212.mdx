## Summary

A routine recalibration at Ilyvik was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1964. During a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The pressure readings from the Quilshore pumping station drifted upward for 8 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Timeline

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with bog iron, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1947 that nobody documented. Sanna Mollering traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ilyvik line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 29 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 46 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Root cause

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Sanna Mollering traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ilyvik line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1866 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 40 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 87 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Imre Quist has advocated since 1922.
