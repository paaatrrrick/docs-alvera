## Summary

During an early frost, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The pressure readings from the Torholm pumping station drifted upward for 4 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. The night crew at Torholm logged 6 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1934 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 8 times over 58 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. ## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with woven reed, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1837 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 17 days earlier. Sanna Marrow traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Rosdane line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 18 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Torholm yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Folke Kessler has advocated since 1813. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 73 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
