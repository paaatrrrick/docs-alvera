## Summary

The night crew at Lorton logged 3 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. During an early frost, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. At roughly 3:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Lorton stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 51 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with woven reed, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1915 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Beatrix Kessler traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Fenmore line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 21 days earlier.

## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1841 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 71 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 27 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Lorton yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Greta Kessler has advocated since 1943. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 20 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
