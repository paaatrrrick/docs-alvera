## Summary

At roughly 7:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Velton stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. A routine recalibration at Kelhollow was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1899. During a still and windless month, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode.

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1829 that nobody documented. Edda Quist traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Kelhollow line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 50 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 44 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Root cause

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Edda Quist traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Kelhollow line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 33 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 54 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Joren Brandt has advocated since 1821. Total downtime came to 81 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Velton yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
