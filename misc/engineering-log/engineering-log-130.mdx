## Summary

At roughly 6:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Ilydane stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. A routine recalibration at Ossvik was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1860. The night crew at Ilydane logged 2 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 36 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 18 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with pressed kelp paper, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1783 that nobody documented. Imre Quist traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ossvik line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Root cause

Imre Quist traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ossvik line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with woven reed, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1951 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Rurik Greaves has advocated since 1851. Total downtime came to 35 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. We are adopting a rule from the Ilydane yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
