## Summary

During the autumn king tides, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Halbury was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1784. At roughly 7:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Dordane stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 33 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with whale-bone veneer, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1885 that nobody documented. Dario Vexley traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Halbury line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 41 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Annik Lindqvist has advocated since 1842. We are adopting a rule from the Dordane yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 90 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
