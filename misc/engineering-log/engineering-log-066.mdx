## Summary

At roughly 4:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Jorvale stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The night crew at Jorvale logged 6 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. The pressure readings from the Jorvale pumping station drifted upward for 8 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Maren Holloway traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Permara line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 28 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1784 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 40 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. We are adopting a rule from the Jorvale yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
