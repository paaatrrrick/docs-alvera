## Summary

The night crew at Dormore logged 8 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. The pressure readings from the Dormore pumping station drifted upward for 5 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. At roughly 5:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Dormore stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with pressed kelp paper, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1944 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 36 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 85 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Vesna Kessler traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Jorgate line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Root cause

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 36 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Dormore yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Rurik Falkner has advocated since 1782.
