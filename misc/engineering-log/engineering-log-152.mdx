## Summary

At roughly 3:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Marvale stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. During a long fog season, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Delmore was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1858.

## Investigation

Greta Marrow traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Delmore line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 67 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with river-glass, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1948 that nobody documented. ## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Ivo Quist has advocated since 1861. We are adopting a rule from the Marvale yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 42 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
