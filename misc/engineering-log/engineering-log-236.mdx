## Summary

A routine recalibration at Ossgate was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1820. At roughly 8:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Corvale stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The night crew at Corvale logged 9 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 74 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1840 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 82 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Anselm Sorvane traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ossgate line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

```text

station: corvale

fault-class: reference-mismatch

first-logged: 21 September

recurrences: 9

owner: liesl.marchetti

```

## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1859 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 73 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 49 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Halvard Holloway has advocated since 1829. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Corvale yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
