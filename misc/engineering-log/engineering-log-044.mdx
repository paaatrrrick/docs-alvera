## Summary

The night crew at Ulmshore logged 3 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. The pressure readings from the Ulmshore pumping station drifted upward for 4 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. A routine recalibration at Fengate was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1888.

## Root cause

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 52 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1884 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 61 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Petra Voss has advocated since 1930. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 85 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Ulmshore yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
