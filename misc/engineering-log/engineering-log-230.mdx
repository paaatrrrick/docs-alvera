## Investigation

Maren Whitlock traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Perspire line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1929 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 7 times over 83 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 78 days earlier.

## Root cause

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 50 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. ## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Zelstead yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Stellan Drummond has advocated since 1899. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 68 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
