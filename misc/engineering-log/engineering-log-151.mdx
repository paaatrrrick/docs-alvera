## Summary

A routine recalibration at Jormire was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1928. The night crew at Morholm logged 3 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. During spring floods, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode.

## Investigation

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 74 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. ## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 18 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 40 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with woven reed, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1803 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Morholm yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 57 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Halvard Halloran has advocated since 1841.
