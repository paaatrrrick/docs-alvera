## Summary

At roughly 3:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Yarrun stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. A routine recalibration at Galcrest was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1881. During spring floods, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode.

## Root cause

Anselm Greaves traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Galcrest line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1887 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 38 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 39 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Yarrun yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 54 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Stellan Holloway has advocated since 1889.
