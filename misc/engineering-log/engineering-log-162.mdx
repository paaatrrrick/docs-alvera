## Summary

At roughly 9:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Fenspire stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The night crew at Fenspire logged 4 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. The pressure readings from the Fenspire pumping station drifted upward for 3 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1790 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 61 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 47 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Greta Halloran traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Brendane line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Dario Voss has advocated since 1865. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 81 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Fenspire yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
