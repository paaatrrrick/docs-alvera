## Summary

During the autumn king tides, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The pressure readings from the Halmire pumping station drifted upward for 4 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. The night crew at Halmire logged 2 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

Rurik Kessler traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Selgate line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 38 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1917 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 90 days earlier.

## Root cause

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 82 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 13 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1788 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Halmire yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 75 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
