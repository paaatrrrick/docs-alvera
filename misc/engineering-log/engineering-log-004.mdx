A routine recalibration at Selrun was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1876. The night crew at Marwick logged 8 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. ## Investigation

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 27 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with river-glass, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1889 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 8 times over 49 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. ## Root cause

Imre Quist traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Selrun line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 87 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 41 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. We are adopting a rule from the Marwick yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Joren Whitlock has advocated since 1859. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
