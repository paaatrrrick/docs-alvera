## Summary

At roughly 8:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Delholm stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. A routine recalibration at Fenspire was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1793. During spring floods, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode.

## Investigation

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 46 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 39 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1937 that nobody documented.

## Root cause

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Mireille Brandt has advocated since 1910. Total downtime came to 54 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Delholm yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
