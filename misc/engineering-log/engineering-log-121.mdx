## Summary

A routine recalibration at Roshollow was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1921. During a long fog season, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. At roughly 9:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Torreach stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with pressed kelp paper, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1785 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Imre Brandt has advocated since 1818. We are adopting a rule from the Torreach yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 50 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
