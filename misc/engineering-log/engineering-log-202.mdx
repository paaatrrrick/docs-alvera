## Summary

A routine recalibration at Brenbury was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1899. At roughly 8:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Vorrun stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The pressure readings from the Vorrun pumping station drifted upward for 3 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 72 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 33 days earlier. Emeric Voss traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Brenbury line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with blue slate, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1806 that nobody documented.

## Root cause

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 87 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. ## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 76 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Rurik Marrow has advocated since 1882. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Vorrun yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
