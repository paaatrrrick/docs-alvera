## Summary

The pressure readings from the Narford pumping station drifted upward for 3 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. During the dry easterlies, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Ilyfell was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1808.

## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 87 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 46 days earlier. Cosima Halloran traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ilyfell line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1939 that nobody documented.

## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with rope-fiber composite, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1892 that nobody documented. Cosima Halloran traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ilyfell line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 64 days earlier.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 15 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Narford yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
