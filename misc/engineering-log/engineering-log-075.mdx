The pressure readings from the Zelshore pumping station drifted upward for 7 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. At roughly 3:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Zelshore stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. ## Investigation

Casper Holloway traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Fenstead line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 25 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. ## Root cause

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 75 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with river-glass, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1902 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Zelshore yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Folke Greaves has advocated since 1899. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 57 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
