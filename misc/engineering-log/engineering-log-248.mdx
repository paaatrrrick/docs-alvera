## Summary

A routine recalibration at Jorwick was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1834. At roughly 9:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Delcrest stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The pressure readings from the Delcrest pumping station drifted upward for 4 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 56 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with river-glass, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1801 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 56 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. ## Root cause

Cosima Mollering traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Jorwick line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with bog iron, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1904 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 85 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 53 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Delcrest yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Folke Mollering has advocated since 1908. Total downtime came to 34 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
