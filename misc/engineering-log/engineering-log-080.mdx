## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 58 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with pressed kelp paper, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1948 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 85 days earlier. Halvard Mollering traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Delmore line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Root cause

Halvard Mollering traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Delmore line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 87 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 67 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with rope-fiber composite, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1861 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Erireach yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Tomas Petrov has advocated since 1876. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 88 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
