## Summary

A routine recalibration at Tamrun was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1932. During the dry easterlies, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. At roughly 5:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Kelwick stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Kelwick yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Bram Greaves has advocated since 1937. Total downtime came to 56 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned.
