## Summary

A routine recalibration at Corholm was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1948. During a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. At roughly 7:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Brenreach stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Investigation

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 47 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1871 that nobody documented. ## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 77 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Brenreach yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
