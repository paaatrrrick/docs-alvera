## Summary

A routine recalibration at Rosmore was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1941. The pressure readings from the Eristead pumping station drifted upward for 3 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. ## Root cause

Greta Whitlock traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Rosmore line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 8 times over 16 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with whale-bone veneer, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1789 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Eristead yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 52 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Yutta Ashdown has advocated since 1861.
