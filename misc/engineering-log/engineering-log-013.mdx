## Summary

A routine recalibration at Galgate was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1878. The night crew at Delstead logged 7 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. At roughly 8:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Delstead stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Root cause

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 38 days earlier. Ivo Falkner traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Galgate line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 39 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Delstead yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Rurik Sorvane has advocated since 1887. Total downtime came to 64 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
