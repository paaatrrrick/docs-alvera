## Summary

The night crew at Narvik logged 6 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. A routine recalibration at Vorspire was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1945. The pressure readings from the Narvik pumping station drifted upward for 2 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself.

## Investigation

Bram Petrov traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Vorspire line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with wax-sealed canvas, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1846 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 42 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. ## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with blue slate, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1808 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 19 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Narvik yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Edda Marrow has advocated since 1958. Total downtime came to 54 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
