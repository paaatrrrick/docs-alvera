## Summary

During a long fog season, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Delvale was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1881. At roughly 3:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Vorhollow stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 23 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Folke Oduya traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Delvale line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Vorhollow yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Tomas Vexley has advocated since 1882. Total downtime came to 27 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
