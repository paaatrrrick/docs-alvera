## Summary

At roughly 7:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Ossgate stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. A routine recalibration at Erivik was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1937. During a still and windless month, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode.

## Root cause

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 83 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Folke Oduya traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Erivik line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with fired clay, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1907 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Otto Quist has advocated since 1852. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 51 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. We are adopting a rule from the Ossgate yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
