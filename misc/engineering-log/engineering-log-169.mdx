## Summary

At roughly 4:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Ulmvale stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The night crew at Ulmvale logged 5 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. During an early frost, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode.

## Investigation

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 44 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with river-glass, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1895 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Greta Greaves traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Corstead line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Ulmvale yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Lazlo Petrov has advocated since 1843. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 34 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
