## Summary

At roughly 9:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Vorshore stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The night crew at Vorshore logged 7 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. During weeks of sideways rain, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode.

## Investigation

Annik Holloway traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Erifell line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with rope-fiber composite, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1885 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 61 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 15 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Anselm Holloway has advocated since 1917. Total downtime came to 38 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
