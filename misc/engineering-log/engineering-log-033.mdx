## Investigation

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 64 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Joren Voss traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Eriford line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 29 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with pressed kelp paper, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1787 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Calholm yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Annik Holloway has advocated since 1954.
