## Summary

The pressure readings from the Jorwick pumping station drifted upward for 8 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. During a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The night crew at Jorwick logged 9 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Investigation

Folke Quist traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Ashspire line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 75 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with bog iron, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1818 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 35 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 9 times over 83 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with wax-sealed canvas, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1901 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 46 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Jorwick yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Yutta Falkner has advocated since 1952. Total downtime came to 66 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
