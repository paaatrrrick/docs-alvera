## Summary

The pressure readings from the Brenmara pumping station drifted upward for 6 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. During the dry easterlies, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Dormire was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1893.

## Investigation

Annik Greaves traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Dormire line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1847 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 41 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 52 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Brenmara yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Maren Mollering has advocated since 1813. Total downtime came to 88 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
