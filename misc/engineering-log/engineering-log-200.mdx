## Summary

At roughly 9:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Kelshore stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. The pressure readings from the Kelshore pumping station drifted upward for 9 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. A routine recalibration at Quilmire was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1868.

## Timeline

- The night crew at Kelshore logged 9 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.
- Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.
- Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 44 days earlier.
- Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with woven reed, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1799 that nobody documented.
- During an early frost, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode.

## Investigation

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with bog iron, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1836 that nobody documented. Annik Severin traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Quilmire line, subtly out of tolerance. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 4 times over 28 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Root cause

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 15 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with river-glass, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1828 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Kelshore yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 19 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Edda Halloran has advocated since 1866.
