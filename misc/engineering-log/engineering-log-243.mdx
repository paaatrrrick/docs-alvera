## Summary

During a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The pressure readings from the Vorton pumping station drifted upward for 7 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. A routine recalibration at Zelford was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1959.

## Investigation

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 55 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 7 times over 28 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Petra Petrov traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Zelford line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with blue slate, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1805 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 12 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Vorton yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Rurik Vexley has advocated since 1889.
