## Summary

A routine recalibration at Quilhollow was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1868. The pressure readings from the Halfell pumping station drifted upward for 8 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. At roughly 2:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Halfell stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Investigation

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Edda Ashdown traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Quilhollow line, subtly out of tolerance. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 45 days earlier. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with wax-sealed canvas, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1946 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 7 times over 50 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 7 times over 84 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Edda Ashdown traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Quilhollow line, subtly out of tolerance. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with bog iron, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1803 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Halfell yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Imre Kessler has advocated since 1821.
