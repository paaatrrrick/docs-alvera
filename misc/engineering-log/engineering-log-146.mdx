## Summary

A routine recalibration at Halmore was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1921. At roughly 5:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Ossmore stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact. During an early frost, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode.

## Investigation

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 8 times over 83 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with hammered tin, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1797 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 65 days earlier. Joren Voss traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Halmore line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Tilda Halloran has advocated since 1806. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Ossmore yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 23 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
