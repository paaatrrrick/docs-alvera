## Summary

The night crew at Selton logged 9 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. A routine recalibration at Rosmire was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1905. At roughly 8:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Selton stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 5 times over 34 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with lacquered birch, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1805 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 76 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 74 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Selton yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
