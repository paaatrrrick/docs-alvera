## Summary

A routine recalibration at Galford was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1836. The pressure readings from the Halstead pumping station drifted upward for 3 hours before anyone thought to distrust the gauge itself. At roughly 6:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Halstead stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 84 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Halvard Severin has advocated since 1837. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. We are adopting a rule from the Halstead yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory.
