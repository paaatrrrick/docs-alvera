## Summary

A routine recalibration at Corholm was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1842. During the dry easterlies, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The night crew at Yarwick logged 3 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them.

## Root cause

Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with wax-sealed canvas, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1814 that nobody documented. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 47 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Maren Mollering traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Corholm line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Lessons and follow-up

Total downtime came to 90 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Rurik Greaves has advocated since 1853. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal.
