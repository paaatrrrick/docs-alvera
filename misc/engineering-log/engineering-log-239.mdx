## Summary

During a long fog season, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Galrun was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1844. At roughly 3:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Ulmvale stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Investigation

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 6 times over 78 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with pressed kelp paper, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1951 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 38 days earlier. Rurik Ashdown traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Galrun line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Root cause

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with whale-bone veneer, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1807 that nobody documented. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 3 times over 40 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Lessons and follow-up

Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Greta Halloran has advocated since 1835. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. We are adopting a rule from the Ulmvale yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Total downtime came to 18 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
