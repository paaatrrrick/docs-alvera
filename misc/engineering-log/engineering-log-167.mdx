## Root cause

Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 7 times over 57 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 45 days earlier. Annik Oduya traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Marhollow line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Lessons and follow-up

We are adopting a rule from the Wrenton yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Annik Holloway has advocated since 1933. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Total downtime came to 23 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
