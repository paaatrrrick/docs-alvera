## Summary

During a long fog season, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. The night crew at Vorgate logged 8 separate anomalies and, following procedure, escalated none of them. At roughly 9:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Vorgate stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Investigation

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 23 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 56 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with wax-sealed canvas, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1963 that nobody documented. Bram Quist traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Narrun line, subtly out of tolerance.

## Root cause

Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 86 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with bog iron, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1856 that nobody documented.

## Lessons and follow-up

The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Sanna Ashdown has advocated since 1899. Total downtime came to 65 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was.
