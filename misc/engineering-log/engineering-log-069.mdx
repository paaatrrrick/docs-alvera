## Summary

During a hailstorm that stripped the orchards, the signal cable along the towpath shorted intermittently, producing phantom readings that matched no known failure mode. A routine recalibration at Zelwick was performed against the wrong reference table, the one withdrawn in 1854. At roughly 5:40 in the morning, the relay tower at Jorford stopped acknowledging heartbeats, and the operators assumed the usual corroded contact.

## Investigation

Anselm Falkner traced the fault to a spare part cannibalized from the decommissioned Zelwick line, subtly out of tolerance. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years. Inspection revealed the junction box had been resealed with salt-cured oak, an improvisation dating to a repair in 1883 that nobody documented. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 51 days earlier. The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 7 times over 65 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes.

## Root cause

The maintenance log showed the same fault reported 2 times over 27 months, each time closed as resolved with no notes. Cross-checking the gauge against a portable reference took twenty minutes and should have happened 60 days earlier. Interviews established that the procedure existed, was correct, was printed, and was pinned behind a cabinet where nobody had seen it for years.

## Lessons and follow-up

The reference tables have been consolidated; the withdrawn editions were collected and, with some ceremony, burned. The deeper failure was organizational: the station treated recurring anomalies as weather rather than as signal. Total downtime came to 82 hours, most of it spent debating whose responsibility the gauge was. We are adopting a rule from the Jorford yards: any fault reported three times earns a named owner and a written theory. Spare parts now carry tags listing origin and tolerance, a practice Oona Marrow has advocated since 1803.
