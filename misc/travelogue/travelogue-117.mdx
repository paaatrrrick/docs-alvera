## Morning

a hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of glass floats. By evening we reached Vorwick, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Ivo Lindqvist. The lock keeper at Selwick waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and copper wire on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Selwick before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 88 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. A hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. Petra Whitlock told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. The local dialect has 2 words for drizzle and none for hurry.

## Evening
