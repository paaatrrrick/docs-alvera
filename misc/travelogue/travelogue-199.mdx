## Morning

We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Corwick waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Wrenmara, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Joren Greaves.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Corwick waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Wrenmara, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Joren Greaves. Lunch was bread and almanac reprints on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Halreach, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Corwick before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 36 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Evening

The lock keeper at Corwick waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We left Corwick before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 71 miles by the time the mist burned off.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Corwick has the most honest bridge I have seen. Bram Ashdown told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
