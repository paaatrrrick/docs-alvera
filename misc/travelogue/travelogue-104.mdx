## Morning

We left Dormire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 48 miles by the time the mist burned off. weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of glass floats. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Calford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Midday

We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Calford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and loom parts on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. You can tell a town that trades in loom parts by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Afternoon

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Calford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Rosmire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Folke Kessler.
