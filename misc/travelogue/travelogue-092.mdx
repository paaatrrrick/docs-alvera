## Morning

The lock keeper at Kelwick waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We left Kelwick before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 53 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Kelwick waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. An early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of fermented tea bricks. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Halvik, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

The local dialect has 7 words for drizzle and none for hurry. You can tell a town that trades in dye cakes by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Afternoon

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Halvik, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and tar barrels on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Things I noticed

- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Evening

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Halvik, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

Emeric Petrov told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
