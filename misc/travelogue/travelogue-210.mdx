## Morning

Lunch was bread and almanac reprints on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ilyton, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Margate before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 45 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Margate waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Calspire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Rurik Severin. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of glass floats. Lunch was bread and tar barrels on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Margate has the most honest bridge I have seen. The local dialect has 4 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
