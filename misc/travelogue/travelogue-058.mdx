## Morning

We left Rosshore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 21 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Mormire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Mormire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts.

You can tell a town that trades in clockwork gears by its smell before you can see its rooftops. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Mormire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. By evening we reached Corholm, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Folke Marrow.

## Things I noticed

- The local dialect has 2 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- You can tell a town that trades in almanac reprints by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Evening

By evening we reached Corholm, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Folke Marrow. The lock keeper at Rosshore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

You can tell a town that trades in wool bales by its smell before you can see its rooftops. The local dialect has 6 words for drizzle and none for hurry. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
