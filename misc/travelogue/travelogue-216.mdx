## Morning

The lock keeper at Galbury waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Calreach, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

a still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins. Lunch was bread and almanac reprints on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Galbury before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 55 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Calreach, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

The local dialect has 5 words for drizzle and none for hurry. Petra Quist told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

We left Galbury before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 34 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Galbury waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Evening

We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. By evening we reached Roshollow, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Yutta Brandt.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Galbury has the most honest bridge I have seen. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
