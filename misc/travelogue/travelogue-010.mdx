## Morning

Lunch was bread and spice tins on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins. We left Galdane before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 73 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Vorhollow, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of dye cakes. By evening we reached Yarwick, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Oona Mollering. The lock keeper at Galdane waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Galdane has the most honest bridge I have seen. You can tell a town that trades in lamp oil by its smell before you can see its rooftops. The local dialect has 7 words for drizzle and none for hurry.

## Things I noticed
