## Morning

The lock keeper at Halton waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and copper wire on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Brenstead, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. The local dialect has 4 words for drizzle and none for hurry. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Halton has the most honest bridge I have seen.

## Afternoon

By evening we reached Tamcrest, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Rurik Brandt. ## Evening

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Brenstead, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Halton has the most honest bridge I have seen. The local dialect has 3 words for drizzle and none for hurry. Tomas Kessler told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
