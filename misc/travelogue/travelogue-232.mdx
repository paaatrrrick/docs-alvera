## Morning

a hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins. Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Tamholm waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

By evening we reached Pervik, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Petra Greaves. Weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts. We left Tamholm before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 87 miles by the time the mist burned off.

The local dialect has 8 words for drizzle and none for hurry. You can tell a town that trades in fermented tea bricks by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Afternoon

We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Fenbury, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Things I noticed

- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Tamholm has the most honest bridge I have seen.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- Dario Halloran told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
