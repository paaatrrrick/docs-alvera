## Morning

We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Ashgate waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and spice tins on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins.

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Wrenspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The lock keeper at Ashgate waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. You can tell a town that trades in glass floats by its smell before you can see its rooftops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Ashgate has the most honest bridge I have seen.

## Afternoon

We left Ashgate before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 59 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of dye cakes. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.
