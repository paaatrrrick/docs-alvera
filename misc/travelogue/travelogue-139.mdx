## Morning

We left Ashfell before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 14 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Rosstead, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Vesna Holloway. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Kelbury, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

By evening we reached Rosstead, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Vesna Holloway. We left Ashfell before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 71 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Kelbury, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

You can tell a town that trades in copper wire by its smell before you can see its rooftops. The local dialect has 3 words for drizzle and none for hurry.

## Afternoon

Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Ashfell waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. A long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of glass floats.

## Things I noticed

- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Ashfell has the most honest bridge I have seen.
