## Morning

By evening we reached Lorton, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Maren Kessler. Lunch was bread and loom parts on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Calbury waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ossfell, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

We left Calbury before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 65 miles by the time the mist burned off. By evening we reached Lorton, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Maren Kessler. You can tell a town that trades in lamp oil by its smell before you can see its rooftops. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

## Evening

the autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts. We left Calbury before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 13 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

Stellan Greaves told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
