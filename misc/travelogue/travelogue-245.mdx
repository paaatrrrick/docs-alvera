## Morning

Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. A hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts. The lock keeper at Dorcrest waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Wrenton, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Stellan Lindqvist.

## Midday

We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints. By evening we reached Wrenton, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Stellan Lindqvist.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. You can tell a town that trades in spice tins by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Afternoon

We left Dorcrest before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 78 miles by the time the mist burned off. By evening we reached Wrenton, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Stellan Lindqvist. Lunch was bread and tar barrels on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.
