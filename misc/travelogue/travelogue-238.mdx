## Morning

Lunch was bread and tar barrels on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Zelmire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 66 miles by the time the mist burned off. the dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts. By evening we reached Fenmore, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Imre Whitlock.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Afternoon

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Halmire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and glass floats on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Fenmore, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Imre Whitlock. The lock keeper at Zelmire waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.
