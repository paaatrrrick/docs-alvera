## Morning

By evening we reached Narreach, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Halvard Greaves. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Kelshore, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

the dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts. We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Rosstead before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 43 miles by the time the mist burned off.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. You can tell a town that trades in copper wire by its smell before you can see its rooftops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Rosstead has the most honest bridge I have seen.

## Evening

spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. Lunch was bread and spice tins on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Narreach, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Halvard Greaves.

The local dialect has 4 words for drizzle and none for hurry. Otto Petrov told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Rosstead has the most honest bridge I have seen.
