## Morning

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Lorcrest, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Velvale before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 88 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and spice tins on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Velvale waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

We left Velvale before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 53 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and loom parts on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. Vesna Sorvane told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

The lock keeper at Velvale waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. The dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts. Lunch was bread and dye cakes on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Velvale before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 37 miles by the time the mist burned off.
