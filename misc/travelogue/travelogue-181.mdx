## Morning

The lock keeper at Brenvik waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ashmire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Midday

Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. a still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire. By evening we reached Ossford, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Sigrid Sorvane.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. The local dialect has 6 words for drizzle and none for hurry.

## Afternoon

Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Brenvik before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 27 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Things I noticed

- Halvard Sorvane told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- You can tell a town that trades in fermented tea bricks by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Brenvik has the most honest bridge I have seen.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Evening

The lock keeper at Brenvik waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.
