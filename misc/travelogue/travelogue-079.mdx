## Morning

The lock keeper at Ilyholm waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Fenmore, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

a hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts. By evening we reached Selford, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Folke Holloway. Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Tilda Lindqvist told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

We left Ilyholm before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 69 miles by the time the mist burned off.
