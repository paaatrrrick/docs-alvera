## Morning

We left Marholm before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 86 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. The lock keeper at Marholm waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Marholm before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 44 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and spice tins on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

You can tell a town that trades in lamp oil by its smell before you can see its rooftops. The local dialect has 4 words for drizzle and none for hurry. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Marholm has the most honest bridge I have seen.
