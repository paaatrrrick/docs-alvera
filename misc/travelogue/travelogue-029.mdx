## Morning

We left Narfell before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 29 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Narfell waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We left Narfell before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 38 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and glass floats on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Afternoon

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ossrun, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The lock keeper at Narfell waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Yargate, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Annik Quist. Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Things I noticed

- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Narfell has the most honest bridge I have seen.
- You can tell a town that trades in copper wire by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
