## Morning

Lunch was bread and glass floats on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Vorshore, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Greta Greaves. We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Kelwick before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 56 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Kelwick waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Kelwick before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 76 miles by the time the mist burned off.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Vorshore, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Greta Greaves. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Zeldane, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Evening

The lock keeper at Kelwick waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. the autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. We left Kelwick before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 58 miles by the time the mist burned off.
