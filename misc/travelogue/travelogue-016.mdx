## Morning

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Selvik, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Brenshore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 57 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins.

## Midday

Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Brenshore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Brenshore has the most honest bridge I have seen. Bram Holloway told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

The lock keeper at Brenshore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We left Brenshore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 21 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Things I noticed

- The local dialect has 8 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
- You can tell a town that trades in dye cakes by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Evening

The lock keeper at Brenshore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Quilmire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Tomas Kessler. We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.
