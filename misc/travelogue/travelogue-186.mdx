## Morning

Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Brenfell, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Selmore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 56 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Midday

We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales. We left Selmore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 53 miles by the time the mist burned off.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Selmore has the most honest bridge I have seen.

## Afternoon

The lock keeper at Selmore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. An early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales.

## Evening

Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Rosspire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Imre Severin. A long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of glass floats.
