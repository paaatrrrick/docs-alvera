## Morning

By evening we reached Ilyreach, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Bram Mollering. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Narfell waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Narfell before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 26 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Narfell waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Narfell has the most honest bridge I have seen. You can tell a town that trades in copper wire by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Afternoon

Lunch was bread and spice tins on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. A long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears. We left Narfell before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 58 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.
