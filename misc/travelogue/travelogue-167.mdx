## Morning

We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Jorrun waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Jorrun waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We left Jorrun before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 71 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

We left Jorrun before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 80 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. By evening we reached Halwick, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Annik Kessler.

## Evening
