## Morning

Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Dorcrest, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Liesl Petrov. The autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Lormire waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Wrenford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Lormire has the most honest bridge I have seen. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. You can tell a town that trades in spice tins by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Afternoon

We left Lormire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 74 miles by the time the mist burned off. Weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Wrenford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and loom parts on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.
