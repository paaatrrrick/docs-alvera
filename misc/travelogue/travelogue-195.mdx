## Morning

We left Calford before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 13 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Halmire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

Lunch was bread and fermented tea bricks on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Calford waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Halmire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. By evening we reached Torrun, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Bram Oduya.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Calford has the most honest bridge I have seen. Liesl Greaves told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

The lock keeper at Calford waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. The autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.
