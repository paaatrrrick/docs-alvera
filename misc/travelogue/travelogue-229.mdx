## Morning

The lock keeper at Marmire waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Brenmara, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. a hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of lamp oil. By evening we reached Vorford, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Imre Halloran.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Marmire has the most honest bridge I have seen. The local dialect has 8 words for drizzle and none for hurry. You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Afternoon

## Evening

We left Marmire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 28 miles by the time the mist burned off. the dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. You can tell a town that trades in wool bales by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
