## Morning

We left Velwick before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 27 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Calvik, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

Lunch was bread and copper wire on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Velwick waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Calvik, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. The local dialect has 4 words for drizzle and none for hurry. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Afternoon

By evening we reached Quilhollow, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Rurik Greaves. ## Evening

an early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Calvik, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Velwick has the most honest bridge I have seen. Imre Brandt told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. You can tell a town that trades in spice tins by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
