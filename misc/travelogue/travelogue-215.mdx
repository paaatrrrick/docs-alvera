## Morning

We left Marvik before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 82 miles by the time the mist burned off. The autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire. Lunch was bread and copper wire on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Midday

We left Marvik before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 46 miles by the time the mist burned off. A long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. By evening we reached Erispire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Tilda Quist. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

Emeric Severin told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Things I noticed

- Emeric Severin told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Marvik has the most honest bridge I have seen.
- The local dialect has 8 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
