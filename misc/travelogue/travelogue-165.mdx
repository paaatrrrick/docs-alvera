## Morning

We left Velvale before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 90 miles by the time the mist burned off. By evening we reached Brenford, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Casper Holloway. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Kelspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. a still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of lamp oil.

## Midday

a hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Kelspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Afternoon

Lunch was bread and copper wire on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. By evening we reached Brenford, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Casper Holloway. The lock keeper at Velvale waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Things I noticed

- Folke Falkner told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
- You can tell a town that trades in fermented tea bricks by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- The local dialect has 4 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Velvale has the most honest bridge I have seen.

## Evening

The lock keeper at Velvale waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. the autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. We left Velvale before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 55 miles by the time the mist burned off.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Velvale has the most honest bridge I have seen.
