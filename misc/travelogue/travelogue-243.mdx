## Morning

We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. An early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of lamp oil. By evening we reached Dorwick, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Folke Kessler. We left Narshore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 57 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Narshore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Dorwick, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Folke Kessler. We left Narshore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 50 miles by the time the mist burned off.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. The local dialect has 3 words for drizzle and none for hurry. Folke Mollering told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Narshore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Brenford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Narshore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 21 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Things I noticed

- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- You can tell a town that trades in loom parts by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- The local dialect has 7 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Narshore has the most honest bridge I have seen.
