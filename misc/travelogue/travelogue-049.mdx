## Morning

The lock keeper at Calshore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Galvale, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. Emeric Quist told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

Lunch was bread and almanac reprints on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. A hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears. We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Things I noticed

- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Calshore has the most honest bridge I have seen.
- Emeric Quist told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Evening

We left Calshore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 50 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Calshore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

Emeric Quist told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. You can tell a town that trades in wool bales by its smell before you can see its rooftops. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
