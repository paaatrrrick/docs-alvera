## Morning

Lunch was bread and almanac reprints on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. a hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears. We left Brenmara before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 85 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ashspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

By evening we reached Brenspire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Rurik Sorvane. weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ashspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and fermented tea bricks on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

You can tell a town that trades in wool bales by its smell before you can see its rooftops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Brenmara has the most honest bridge I have seen.

## Afternoon

We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Brenmara waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ashspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. By evening we reached Brenspire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Rurik Sorvane.

## Things I noticed

- Halvard Vexley told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
