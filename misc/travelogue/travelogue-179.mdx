## Morning

We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire. We left Delmire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 74 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Galvale, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Delmire waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and glass floats on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Delmire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 12 miles by the time the mist burned off.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. You can tell a town that trades in tar barrels by its smell before you can see its rooftops. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins. We left Delmire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 55 miles by the time the mist burned off. By evening we reached Ilyvik, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Liesl Oduya.
