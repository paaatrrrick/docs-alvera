## Morning

Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Eriholm waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ashholm, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. An early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales. We left Eriholm before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 27 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

The local dialect has 2 words for drizzle and none for hurry. Edda Severin told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Eriholm has the most honest bridge I have seen.
