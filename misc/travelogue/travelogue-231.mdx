## Morning

The lock keeper at Wrenford waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Velwick, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire. We left Wrenford before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 90 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Wrenford waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and glass floats on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. A still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins.

The local dialect has 3 words for drizzle and none for hurry. You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Afternoon

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Velwick, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. By evening we reached Wrenbury, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Imre Greaves.
