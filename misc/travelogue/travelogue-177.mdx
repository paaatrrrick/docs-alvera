## Morning

The lock keeper at Jorton waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. The autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins. We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

Lunch was bread and spice tins on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ashford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. An early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints.

Oona Ashdown told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Jorton has the most honest bridge I have seen. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Afternoon

By evening we reached Brenstead, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Ivo Vexley. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Jorton waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Evening

We left Jorton before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 26 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.
