## Morning

The lock keeper at Galcrest waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Vorstead, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears.

## Midday

We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Wrenton, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Rurik Marchetti. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Vorstead, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

The local dialect has 8 words for drizzle and none for hurry. You can tell a town that trades in lamp oil by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Afternoon

We left Galcrest before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 60 miles by the time the mist burned off. Weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears. By evening we reached Wrenton, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Rurik Marchetti.

## Things I noticed

- Petra Voss told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
- The local dialect has 4 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Galcrest has the most honest bridge I have seen.
