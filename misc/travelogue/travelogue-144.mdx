## Morning

We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Galmire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 66 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Galmire waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Marfell, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of fermented tea bricks. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Galmire has the most honest bridge I have seen. Bram Quist told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. You can tell a town that trades in spice tins by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
