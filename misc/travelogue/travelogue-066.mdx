## Morning

By evening we reached Galvale, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Rurik Lindqvist. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Eriford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Halcrest before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 50 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Halcrest waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and glass floats on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

The local dialect has 2 words for drizzle and none for hurry. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Halcrest has the most honest bridge I have seen.

## Afternoon

We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. A still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. ## Things I noticed

- You can tell a town that trades in almanac reprints by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- Annik Voss told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
