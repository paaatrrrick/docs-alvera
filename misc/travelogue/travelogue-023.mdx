## Morning

an early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Dorwick, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Galrun waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Dorwick, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. A long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire. We left Galrun before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 57 miles by the time the mist burned off.

Imre Voss told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Galrun has the most honest bridge I have seen. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

By evening we reached Velspire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Anselm Marrow.
