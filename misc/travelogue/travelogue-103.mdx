## Morning

an early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints. The lock keeper at Narbury waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and almanac reprints on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

By evening we reached Perhollow, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Tilda Holloway. We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. the autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts. We left Narbury before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 32 miles by the time the mist burned off.

The local dialect has 3 words for drizzle and none for hurry. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Halvard Sorvane told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
