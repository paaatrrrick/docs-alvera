## Morning

Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. An early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of fermented tea bricks. The lock keeper at Dorreach waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. A long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire. We left Dorreach before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 64 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

The local dialect has 7 words for drizzle and none for hurry. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Dorreach before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 39 miles by the time the mist burned off. By evening we reached Calton, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Annik Petrov. The lock keeper at Dorreach waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Things I noticed

- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Dorreach has the most honest bridge I have seen.
- You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Evening

We left Dorreach before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 39 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Nargate, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

You can tell a town that trades in fermented tea bricks by its smell before you can see its rooftops. The local dialect has 5 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
