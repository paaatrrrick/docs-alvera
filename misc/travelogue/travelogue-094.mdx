## Morning

We left Wrenholm before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 80 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Wrenholm waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. a still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints.

## Midday

Lunch was bread and fermented tea bricks on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Wrenholm waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of lamp oil.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. You can tell a town that trades in copper wire by its smell before you can see its rooftops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Wrenholm has the most honest bridge I have seen.
