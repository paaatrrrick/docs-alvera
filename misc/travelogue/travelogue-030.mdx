## Morning

Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Morwick waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. By evening we reached Corfell, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Maren Quist.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Morwick waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We left Morwick before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 62 miles by the time the mist burned off. the dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire. We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

Annik Lindqvist told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. You can tell a town that trades in almanac reprints by its smell before you can see its rooftops. The local dialect has 9 words for drizzle and none for hurry.

## Afternoon

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Brenwick, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. ## Things I noticed

- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Morwick has the most honest bridge I have seen.
