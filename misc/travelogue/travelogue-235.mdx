## Morning

The lock keeper at Halcrest waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Rosspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. By evening we reached Quilbury, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Otto Greaves. Lunch was bread and spice tins on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

an early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of glass floats. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Rosspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Halcrest before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 32 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

The local dialect has 3 words for drizzle and none for hurry. Stellan Sorvane told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Halcrest has the most honest bridge I have seen.
