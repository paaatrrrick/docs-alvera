## Morning

We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of fermented tea bricks. By evening we reached Perspire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Joren Quist. The lock keeper at Jorton waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

Lunch was bread and fermented tea bricks on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Jorton before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 61 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Jorton waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

You can tell a town that trades in glass floats by its smell before you can see its rooftops. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. The local dialect has 6 words for drizzle and none for hurry.

## Afternoon

The lock keeper at Jorton waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ashmire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Jorton before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 18 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Things I noticed

- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Jorton has the most honest bridge I have seen.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
- Annik Halloran told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
