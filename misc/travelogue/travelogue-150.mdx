## Morning

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Narshore, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The lock keeper at Kelvale waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Midday

a still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Narshore, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Kelvale before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 54 miles by the time the mist burned off. By evening we reached Brenholm, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Vesna Lindqvist.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Rurik Quist told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

We left Kelvale before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 24 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and dye cakes on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. ## Evening

We left Kelvale before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 78 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Kelvale has the most honest bridge I have seen.
