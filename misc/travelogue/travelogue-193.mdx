## Morning

We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Nardane before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 36 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Nardane waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Perton, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Nardane before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 48 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Perton, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The lock keeper at Nardane waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Nardane has the most honest bridge I have seen. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

We left Nardane before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 28 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Nardane waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and dye cakes on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Things I noticed

- Greta Oduya told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
