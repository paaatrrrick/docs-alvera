## Morning

Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Margate waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Calspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Margate before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 23 miles by the time the mist burned off. spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. You can tell a town that trades in copper wire by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Afternoon

We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Calspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and tar barrels on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Narmire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Bram Drummond.

## Things I noticed

- Sigrid Holloway told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- You can tell a town that trades in copper wire by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Margate has the most honest bridge I have seen.
