## Morning

The lock keeper at Wrenbury waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Delcrest, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Delcrest, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. By evening we reached Quilstead, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Halvard Falkner. Lunch was bread and fermented tea bricks on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints.

You can tell a town that trades in almanac reprints by its smell before you can see its rooftops. The local dialect has 4 words for drizzle and none for hurry. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Wrenbury has the most honest bridge I have seen.
