## Morning

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Selmore, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Lordane before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 50 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Lordane waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Selmore, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. a long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints.

The local dialect has 5 words for drizzle and none for hurry. You can tell a town that trades in dye cakes by its smell before you can see its rooftops. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Lordane waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We left Lordane before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 37 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Evening

By evening we reached Halrun, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Liesl Greaves. Lunch was bread and glass floats on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

The local dialect has 3 words for drizzle and none for hurry. You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops. Bram Kessler told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
