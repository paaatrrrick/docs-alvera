## Morning

We left Morton before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 57 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Morton waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ossford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

By evening we reached Cormire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Rurik Whitlock. a hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of dye cakes. Lunch was bread and loom parts on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Morton waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Vesna Severin told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. The local dialect has 8 words for drizzle and none for hurry.

## Things I noticed

- You can tell a town that trades in loom parts by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
