## Morning

The lock keeper at Rosshore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Narhollow, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Cosima Ashdown. Lunch was bread and loom parts on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. the autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins.

## Midday

a hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of tar barrels. We left Rosshore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 38 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and tar barrels on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Rosshore has the most honest bridge I have seen.
