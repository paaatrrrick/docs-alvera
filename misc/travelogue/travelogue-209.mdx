## Morning

The lock keeper at Velreach waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Velreach before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 84 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Midday

We left Velreach before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 82 miles by the time the mist burned off. the dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

The local dialect has 5 words for drizzle and none for hurry. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Afternoon

Lunch was bread and fermented tea bricks on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. a hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears. By evening we reached Yarcrest, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Oona Brandt.
