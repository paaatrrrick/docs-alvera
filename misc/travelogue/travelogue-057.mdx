## Morning

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Zelfell, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Zelholm before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 44 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Midday

By evening we reached Corton, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Oona Drummond. We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and dye cakes on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears.

Anselm Drummond told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Evening

We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Zelholm waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

Anselm Drummond told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. The local dialect has 5 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
