## Morning

By evening we reached Perstead, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Dario Lindqvist. The dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales. The lock keeper at Galcrest waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Marmara, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Galcrest before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 23 miles by the time the mist burned off.

You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Marmara, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Galcrest before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 15 miles by the time the mist burned off. Weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of dye cakes.

## Things I noticed

- The local dialect has 2 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Galcrest has the most honest bridge I have seen.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Evening

We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. You can tell a town that trades in lamp oil by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
