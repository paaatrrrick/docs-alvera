## Morning

We left Wrenmara before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 70 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Wrenmara waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Velcrest, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Edda Severin. spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of lamp oil.

## Midday

By evening we reached Velcrest, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Edda Severin. We left Wrenmara before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 14 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

You can tell a town that trades in almanac reprints by its smell before you can see its rooftops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Wrenmara has the most honest bridge I have seen. Lazlo Mollering told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

We left Wrenmara before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 63 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Selford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Things I noticed

- The local dialect has 8 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- You can tell a town that trades in wool bales by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Evening

a long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of glass floats. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.
