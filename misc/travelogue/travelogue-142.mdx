## Morning

Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Galbury, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Petra Vexley. We left Ulmstead before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 55 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Jormara, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Things I noticed

- Sigrid Holloway told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
- You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Evening

We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Ulmstead has the most honest bridge I have seen. You can tell a town that trades in almanac reprints by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
