## Morning

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Marwick, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts. By evening we reached Torvale, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Edda Falkner. The lock keeper at Tormore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

We left Tormore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 26 miles by the time the mist burned off. The autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of dye cakes. We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Tormore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

Edda Vexley told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. You can tell a town that trades in wool bales by its smell before you can see its rooftops. The local dialect has 9 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
