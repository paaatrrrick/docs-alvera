## Morning

We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. By evening we reached Velton, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Yutta Kessler. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

We left Perrun before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 24 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Perrun waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Perrun has the most honest bridge I have seen. You can tell a town that trades in copper wire by its smell before you can see its rooftops. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Corton, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The lock keeper at Perrun waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints.
