## Morning

The lock keeper at Ilyspire waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Midday

We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. A long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of fermented tea bricks.

The local dialect has 9 words for drizzle and none for hurry. You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Afternoon

The lock keeper at Ilyspire waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We left Ilyspire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 90 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ossmire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Evening

Lunch was bread and loom parts on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ossmire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales.

Folke Falkner told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Ilyspire has the most honest bridge I have seen. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
