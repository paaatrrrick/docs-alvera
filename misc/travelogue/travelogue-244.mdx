## Morning

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Torshore, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. a still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of glass floats. We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Tamhollow waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

Dario Greaves told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Afternoon

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Torshore, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. By evening we reached Wrencrest, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Edda Halloran. spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales. We left Tamhollow before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 52 miles by the time the mist burned off.
