## Morning

Lunch was bread and dye cakes on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Marfell, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Mireille Lindqvist. The lock keeper at Ilyrun waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Eriwick, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of fermented tea bricks. By evening we reached Marfell, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Mireille Lindqvist. The lock keeper at Ilyrun waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Ilyrun has the most honest bridge I have seen.

## Afternoon

We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. ## Things I noticed

- You can tell a town that trades in dye cakes by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- The local dialect has 9 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- Maren Whitlock told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
