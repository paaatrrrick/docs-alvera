## Morning

The lock keeper at Ashwick waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Calbury, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Maren Drummond. A still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. We left Ashwick before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 38 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Midday

Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. A still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of fermented tea bricks. We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops. The local dialect has 8 words for drizzle and none for hurry.

## Evening

We left Ashwick before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 40 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Ashwick waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

You can tell a town that trades in tar barrels by its smell before you can see its rooftops. The local dialect has 6 words for drizzle and none for hurry. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
