## Morning

The lock keeper at Selreach waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and glass floats on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Vordane, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales. By evening we reached Wrencrest, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Liesl Halloran. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Vordane, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

The local dialect has 8 words for drizzle and none for hurry. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Selreach has the most honest bridge I have seen. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Evening

Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Vordane, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Selreach has the most honest bridge I have seen. You can tell a town that trades in tar barrels by its smell before you can see its rooftops. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
