## Morning

The lock keeper at Seldane waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We left Seldane before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 86 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Velhollow, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Seldane before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 54 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Seldane waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Lorholm, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Edda Marrow.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Folke Lindqvist told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Evening

Lunch was bread and dye cakes on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. the autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of fermented tea bricks. We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

You can tell a town that trades in glass floats by its smell before you can see its rooftops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Seldane has the most honest bridge I have seen. The local dialect has 3 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
