## Morning

We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. A long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints. By evening we reached Tamreach, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Yutta Halloran. The lock keeper at Delrun waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

a long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Delrun before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 12 miles by the time the mist burned off. By evening we reached Tamreach, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Yutta Halloran.

You can tell a town that trades in tar barrels by its smell before you can see its rooftops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Delrun has the most honest bridge I have seen. You can tell a town that trades in lamp oil by its smell before you can see its rooftops. Ivo Voss told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
