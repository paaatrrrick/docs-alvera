## Morning

a still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Corcrest waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Corcrest waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Vorton, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

The local dialect has 6 words for drizzle and none for hurry. Cosima Sorvane told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. You can tell a town that trades in glass floats by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Afternoon

spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of dye cakes. We left Corcrest before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 82 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.
