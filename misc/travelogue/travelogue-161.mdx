## Morning

The lock keeper at Corford waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Corford before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 76 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Dorstead, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. An early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales. Lunch was bread and glass floats on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Corford waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Corford has the most honest bridge I have seen. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
