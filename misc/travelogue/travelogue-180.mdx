## Morning

Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Lorwick, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and loom parts on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Corspire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Yutta Mollering. Weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Calvale has the most honest bridge I have seen. Petra Brandt told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

We left Calvale before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 73 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Calvale waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Corspire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Yutta Mollering. Lunch was bread and tar barrels on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.
