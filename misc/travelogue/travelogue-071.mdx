## Morning

the dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears. We left Tambury before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 86 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Tambury waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Kelmore, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Casper Lindqvist.

## Midday

By evening we reached Kelmore, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Casper Lindqvist. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Torstead, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. You can tell a town that trades in almanac reprints by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Things I noticed

## Evening

Lunch was bread and copper wire on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

The local dialect has 5 words for drizzle and none for hurry. You can tell a town that trades in loom parts by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
