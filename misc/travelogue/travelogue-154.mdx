## Morning

By evening we reached Ashspire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Maren Lindqvist. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of dye cakes. Lunch was bread and tar barrels on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Lorcrest, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears. We left Selmire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 80 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Selmire waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. You can tell a town that trades in dye cakes by its smell before you can see its rooftops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Things I noticed

- Tilda Brandt told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
- The local dialect has 4 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Selmire has the most honest bridge I have seen.
