## Morning

the dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. Lunch was bread and fermented tea bricks on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Galholm before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 88 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Midday

Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Kelwick, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The lock keeper at Galholm waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

The local dialect has 8 words for drizzle and none for hurry. You can tell a town that trades in wool bales by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Things I noticed

- Annik Vexley told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
- The local dialect has 9 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Galholm has the most honest bridge I have seen.
- You can tell a town that trades in dye cakes by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Evening

a still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of glass floats. By evening we reached Quilspire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Tilda Ashdown.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Galholm has the most honest bridge I have seen. You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
