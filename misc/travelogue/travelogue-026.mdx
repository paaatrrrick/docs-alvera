## Morning

We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Vorreach, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Tammara, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Ivo Lindqvist.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Corton waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. You can tell a town that trades in lamp oil by its smell before you can see its rooftops. The local dialect has 5 words for drizzle and none for hurry.

## Afternoon

We left Corton before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 62 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and tar barrels on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Evening

Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. a long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire.
