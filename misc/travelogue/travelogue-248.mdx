## Morning

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Wrenrun, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears. The lock keeper at Brenwick waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Wrenrun, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Brenwick before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 49 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. A long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears.

Casper Quist told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Afternoon

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Wrenrun, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and almanac reprints on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Brenwick before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 32 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Things I noticed

- The local dialect has 8 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Brenwick has the most honest bridge I have seen.
