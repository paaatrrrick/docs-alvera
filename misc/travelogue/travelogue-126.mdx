## Morning

By evening we reached Brenspire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Bram Sorvane. weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of tar barrels. Lunch was bread and almanac reprints on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Erihollow, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

a still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. Lunch was bread and fermented tea bricks on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Quildane before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 25 miles by the time the mist burned off.

Mireille Severin told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. The local dialect has 8 words for drizzle and none for hurry.

## Afternoon

We left Quildane before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 36 miles by the time the mist burned off. By evening we reached Brenspire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Bram Sorvane. Lunch was bread and copper wire on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Things I noticed

- You can tell a town that trades in tar barrels by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- The local dialect has 3 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Evening

Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.
