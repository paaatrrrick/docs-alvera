## Morning

The lock keeper at Vorstead waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Galfell, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. A long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts.

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Galfell, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Vorstead before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 25 miles by the time the mist burned off.

The local dialect has 7 words for drizzle and none for hurry. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

By evening we reached Wrenholm, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Yutta Severin. ## Evening

By evening we reached Wrenholm, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Yutta Severin.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Vorstead has the most honest bridge I have seen. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. You can tell a town that trades in fermented tea bricks by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
