## Morning

By evening we reached Delstead, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Halvard Halloran. the dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of tar barrels. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Tamspire waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

We left Tamspire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 14 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Dorfell, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. a hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Tamspire has the most honest bridge I have seen. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. Beatrix Oduya told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Dorfell, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and dye cakes on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Things I noticed

- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
