## Morning

We left Velcrest before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 33 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Yarcrest, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. By evening we reached Vorgate, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Emeric Kessler.

## Midday

spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire. The lock keeper at Velcrest waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Yarcrest, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Velcrest has the most honest bridge I have seen. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. The local dialect has 4 words for drizzle and none for hurry.

## Evening

We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and spice tins on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. You can tell a town that trades in wool bales by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
