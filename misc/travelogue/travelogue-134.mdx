## Morning

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Vorton, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Jorcrest before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 59 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Jorcrest waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Brendane, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Emeric Lindqvist. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales.

The local dialect has 4 words for drizzle and none for hurry. Edda Marrow told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

By evening we reached Brendane, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Emeric Lindqvist. We left Jorcrest before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 51 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and dye cakes on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.
