## Morning

We left Tammara before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 83 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and tar barrels on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Midday

By evening we reached Veldane, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Petra Sorvane. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Wrenvik, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. The lock keeper at Tammara waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. You can tell a town that trades in glass floats by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Evening

We left Tammara before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 46 miles by the time the mist burned off. A long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire.

Liesl Ashdown told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. The local dialect has 3 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
