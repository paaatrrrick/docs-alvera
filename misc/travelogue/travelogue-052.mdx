## Midday

The lock keeper at Dorspire waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Halmore, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. The local dialect has 6 words for drizzle and none for hurry.

## Afternoon

Lunch was bread and fermented tea bricks on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Zelvik, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Yutta Severin. We left Dorspire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 36 miles by the time the mist burned off. an early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints.
