## Morning

Lunch was bread and glass floats on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Rosspire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 62 miles by the time the mist burned off. An early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales. By evening we reached Torstead, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Beatrix Severin.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Rosspire waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Torstead, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Beatrix Severin. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Dorvik, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of dye cakes.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Afternoon

We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Dorvik, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The lock keeper at Rosspire waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Things I noticed

- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Rosspire has the most honest bridge I have seen.
- The local dialect has 7 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- You can tell a town that trades in dye cakes by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- Sanna Halloran told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
