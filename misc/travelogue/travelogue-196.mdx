## Morning

Lunch was bread and dye cakes on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Delspire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Sanna Drummond. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Halton, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

We left Brenshore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 34 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Brenshore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Delspire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Sanna Drummond. Weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins.

The local dialect has 7 words for drizzle and none for hurry. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Sanna Quist told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Things I noticed

- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Brenshore has the most honest bridge I have seen.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
- You can tell a town that trades in loom parts by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
