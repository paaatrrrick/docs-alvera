## Morning

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Lorton, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Torton, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Annik Halloran. The lock keeper at Delmara waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

We left Delmara before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 67 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 6 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Lorton, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

The local dialect has 5 words for drizzle and none for hurry. Ivo Brandt told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

By evening we reached Torton, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Annik Halloran. an early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of dye cakes. ## Things I noticed

- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
- You can tell a town that trades in copper wire by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
