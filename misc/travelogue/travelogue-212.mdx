## Morning

We left Wrenreach before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 42 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Quilford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. an early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints. Lunch was bread and loom parts on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Quilford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Wrenreach before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 67 miles by the time the mist burned off. the dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. The local dialect has 4 words for drizzle and none for hurry.

## Afternoon

By evening we reached Ulmwick, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Imre Petrov.
