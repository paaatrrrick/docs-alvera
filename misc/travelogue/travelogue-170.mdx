## Morning

By evening we reached Selholm, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Anselm Lindqvist. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and copper wire on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and fermented tea bricks on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Selholm, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Anselm Lindqvist.

You can tell a town that trades in dye cakes by its smell before you can see its rooftops. Maren Brandt told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

By evening we reached Selholm, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Anselm Lindqvist. Lunch was bread and loom parts on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Roswick, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. an early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts.

## Evening

We left Ilyrun before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 24 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Roswick, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

The local dialect has 6 words for drizzle and none for hurry. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
