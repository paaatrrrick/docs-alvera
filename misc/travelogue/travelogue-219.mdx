## Morning

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ashford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. A still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of tar barrels. By evening we reached Morfell, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Tilda Drummond. Lunch was bread and copper wire on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

By evening we reached Morfell, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Tilda Drummond. We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and dye cakes on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Rosmara waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Otto Quist told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ashford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. By evening we reached Morfell, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Tilda Drummond. We left Rosmara before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 33 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Things I noticed

- You can tell a town that trades in fermented tea bricks by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- The local dialect has 6 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Rosmara has the most honest bridge I have seen.

## Evening
