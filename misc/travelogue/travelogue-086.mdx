## Morning

The lock keeper at Ilyreach waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. An early frost found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of lamp oil. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Halspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

We left Ilyreach before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 73 miles by the time the mist burned off. Weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins. The lock keeper at Ilyreach waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Ilyreach has the most honest bridge I have seen. You can tell a town that trades in almanac reprints by its smell before you can see its rooftops. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Afternoon

Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Ilyreach before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 74 miles by the time the mist burned off. By evening we reached Galwick, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Lazlo Marchetti.

## Evening

Lunch was bread and loom parts on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Ilyreach waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

The local dialect has 7 words for drizzle and none for hurry. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. Oona Marchetti told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
