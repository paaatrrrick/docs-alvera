## Morning

By evening we reached Dorvale, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Imre Holloway. a hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales. We left Velhollow before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 44 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Midday

We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and loom parts on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Velhollow waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

You can tell a town that trades in lamp oil by its smell before you can see its rooftops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Evening

The lock keeper at Velhollow waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Velhollow has the most honest bridge I have seen.
