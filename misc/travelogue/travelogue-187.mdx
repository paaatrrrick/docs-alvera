## Morning

The lock keeper at Ashhollow waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Morreach, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Liesl Quist. Lunch was bread and copper wire on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Dorbury, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Ashhollow before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 29 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Dorbury, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

Folke Marrow told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Ashhollow has the most honest bridge I have seen. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
