## Morning

We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. By evening we reached Marwick, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Beatrix Mollering. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of fermented tea bricks. We left Ashbury before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 20 miles by the time the mist burned off.

The local dialect has 2 words for drizzle and none for hurry. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Ashbury has the most honest bridge I have seen.

## Afternoon

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Roswick, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. The lock keeper at Ashbury waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and loom parts on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Evening

a still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales. We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Tilda Lindqvist told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
