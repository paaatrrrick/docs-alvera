## Morning

The lock keeper at Narhollow waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We left Narhollow before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 20 miles by the time the mist burned off. a hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Midday

We paid 5 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Calford, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The lock keeper at Narhollow waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Narhollow has the most honest bridge I have seen.
