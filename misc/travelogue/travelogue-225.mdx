## Morning

The lock keeper at Ilyspire waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. By evening we reached Nardane, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Sigrid Greaves. We left Ilyspire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 51 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and fermented tea bricks on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Velmire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Ilyspire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 60 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and almanac reprints on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. A still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of copper wire.

Anselm Sorvane told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Afternoon

Lunch was bread and loom parts on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Velmire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Ilyspire before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 19 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.
