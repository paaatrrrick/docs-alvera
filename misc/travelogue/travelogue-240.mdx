## Morning

We left Torreach before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 75 miles by the time the mist burned off. weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of lamp oil. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Wrenmire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

## Midday

We left Torreach before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 50 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Tamvik, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Beatrix Quist. a hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of fermented tea bricks.

You can tell a town that trades in almanac reprints by its smell before you can see its rooftops. Anselm Halloran told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

By evening we reached Tamvik, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Beatrix Quist.

## Things I noticed

- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
- The local dialect has 4 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Torreach has the most honest bridge I have seen.

## Evening

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Wrenmire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Torreach waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.
