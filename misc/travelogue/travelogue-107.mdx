## Morning

Lunch was bread and tar barrels on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Yarshore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Ulmmara, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Yarshore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 45 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Midday

By evening we reached Galmore, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Dario Kessler. The dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Yarshore has the most honest bridge I have seen. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
