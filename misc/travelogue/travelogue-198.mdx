## Morning

the dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints. The lock keeper at Zelstead waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and fermented tea bricks on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Zelstead waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We left Zelstead before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 21 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Tammore, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Zelstead has the most honest bridge I have seen.
