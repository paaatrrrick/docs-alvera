## Morning

By evening we reached Calgate, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Casper Halloran. the dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears. We left Yarmore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 54 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Yarmore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Midday

a still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Dorbury, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and almanac reprints on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

You can tell a town that trades in copper wire by its smell before you can see its rooftops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Evening

Lunch was bread and lamp oil on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The lock keeper at Yarmore waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We left Yarmore before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 73 miles by the time the mist burned off.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Yarmore has the most honest bridge I have seen. You can tell a town that trades in preserved lemons by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
