## Morning

We left Ossgate before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 26 miles by the time the mist burned off. By evening we reached Lorvik, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Casper Mollering. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Selmire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Selmire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The lock keeper at Ossgate waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears.

The local dialect has 6 words for drizzle and none for hurry. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.

## Evening

Lunch was bread and tar barrels on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. A still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. Annik Drummond told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
