## Morning

By evening we reached Galwick, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Oona Holloway. We left Brenrun before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 18 miles by the time the mist burned off. We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and spice tins on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

By evening we reached Galwick, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Oona Holloway. Lunch was bread and clockwork gears on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Brenrun before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 30 miles by the time the mist burned off.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Cosima Brandt told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Brenrun has the most honest bridge I have seen.

## Afternoon

weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears. We left Brenrun before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 76 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Wrenspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. By evening we reached Galwick, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Oona Holloway.
