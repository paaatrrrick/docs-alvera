## Morning

The lock keeper at Wrendane waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. By evening we reached Lormore, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Vesna Mollering.

## Midday

Lunch was bread and fermented tea bricks on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Wrendane before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 43 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Wrendane waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. You can tell a town that trades in glass floats by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Afternoon

By evening we reached Lormore, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Vesna Mollering. Spring floods found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins. We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Things I noticed

- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
- You can tell a town that trades in lamp oil by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- Maren Greaves told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Wrendane has the most honest bridge I have seen.
