## Morning

We left Brencrest before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 47 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and spice tins on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Calvik, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. A hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears.

## Midday

We left Brencrest before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 28 miles by the time the mist burned off. Lunch was bread and preserved lemons on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. A long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of dye cakes.

The local dialect has 5 words for drizzle and none for hurry. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Brencrest has the most honest bridge I have seen. You can tell a town that trades in spice tins by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Afternoon

weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins. We left Brencrest before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 63 miles by the time the mist burned off. By evening we reached Ilymire, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Emeric Severin. The lock keeper at Brencrest waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

## Things I noticed

- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- The local dialect has 2 words for drizzle and none for hurry.
- Tilda Mollering told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Evening

weeks of sideways rain found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of preserved lemons. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Calvik, which leans rather more than the postcards admit.

The local dialect has 9 words for drizzle and none for hurry. You can tell a town that trades in dye cakes by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
