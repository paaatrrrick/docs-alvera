## Morning

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Delshore, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Yarfell before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 64 miles by the time the mist burned off. A hailstorm that stripped the orchards found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of spice tins.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Yarfell waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and tar barrels on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We left Yarfell before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 33 miles by the time the mist burned off.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Yarfell has the most honest bridge I have seen. The local dialect has 9 words for drizzle and none for hurry. You can tell a town that trades in glass floats by its smell before you can see its rooftops.

## Evening

By evening we reached Torton, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Tilda Marchetti. We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and fermented tea bricks on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.
