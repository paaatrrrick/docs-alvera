## Morning

We paid 2 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. We left Halvik before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 25 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Galspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. A still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints.

## Midday

We paid 7 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. By evening we reached Velmara, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Sanna Whitlock. Lunch was bread and spice tins on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

Yutta Greaves told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.

## Afternoon

The lock keeper at Halvik waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We left Halvik before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 34 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Galspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. A long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of lamp oil.

## Evening

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Galspire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. The lock keeper at Halvik waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

You can tell a town that trades in copper wire by its smell before you can see its rooftops. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Halvik has the most honest bridge I have seen.
