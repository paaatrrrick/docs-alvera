## Morning

We left Zelford before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 20 miles by the time the mist burned off. In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Kelreach, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. Lunch was bread and glass floats on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

## Midday

By evening we reached Selreach, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Otto Quist. We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. A long fog season found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of wool bales.

I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Zelford has the most honest bridge I have seen. Vesna Marchetti told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

Lunch was bread and dye cakes on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. The dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints. We left Zelford before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 56 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Things I noticed

- You can tell a town that trades in lamp oil by its smell before you can see its rooftops.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Zelford has the most honest bridge I have seen.
- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
- Vesna Marchetti told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Evening

By evening we reached Selreach, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Otto Quist. The lock keeper at Zelford waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other.

You can tell a town that trades in fermented tea bricks by its smell before you can see its rooftops. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
