## Morning

The lock keeper at Roscrest waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. the dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of lamp oil. We paid 3 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. By evening we reached Zelgate, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Vesna Quist.

## Midday

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Vormire, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. the dry easterlies found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of clockwork gears. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Emeric Marrow told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
