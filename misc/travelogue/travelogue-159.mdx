## Morning

The lock keeper at Marvik waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 4 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. Lunch was bread and glass floats on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Midday

The lock keeper at Marvik waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. We paid 9 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism. By evening we reached Kelvik, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Sanna Marrow.

Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does. What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops. Oona Greaves told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous.

## Afternoon

The lock keeper at Marvik waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. The autumn king tides found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of loom parts. We left Marvik before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 51 miles by the time the mist burned off.
