## Morning

Lunch was bread and copper wire on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle. By evening we reached Wrenbury, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Ivo Marrow. We left Lorton before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 54 miles by the time the mist burned off.

## Midday

a still and windless month found us in open country, and we sheltered in a drying shed that smelled gloriously of almanac reprints. By evening we reached Wrenbury, footsore, and took a room above the cooperage from the obliging Ivo Marrow. We paid 8 small coins for passage on the chain ferry, operated by a child of stern professionalism.

Maren Vexley told me the canals are shrinking, then sold me a map on which they are noticeably generous. The local dialect has 2 words for drizzle and none for hurry.

## Afternoon

In the afternoon we passed the famous leaning granary of Osscrest, which leans rather more than the postcards admit. We left Lorton before light, the towpath frost crunching underfoot, and made 69 miles by the time the mist burned off. The lock keeper at Lorton waved us through with a breakfast roll in one hand and the tide tables in the other. Lunch was bread and wool bales on the wall above the weir, watching barges queue like patient cattle.

## Things I noticed

- Everyone here owns exactly one umbrella and lends it freely, a system that should not work and does.
- What the guidebooks miss is the sound of the canal at night, a conversation of ropes and hulls that never quite stops.
- I have begun to judge towns by their bridges, and Lorton has the most honest bridge I have seen.
